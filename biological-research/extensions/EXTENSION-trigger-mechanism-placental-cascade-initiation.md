## Abstract

The gestational oxygen-timing alignment model establishes a mechanistic pathway from placental insufficiency through fetal hypoxia to oligodendrocyte progenitor cell (OPC) vulnerability and neurodevelopmental outcome. This framework explains *how* placental failure produces white matter injury. What remains unresolved is the foundational question: **what triggers the placental anti-angiogenic cascade in the first place?** Is there a single initiating event, a convergence of predisposing factors, or is the entire process an inevitable consequence of timing and maternal biology? This final extension track examines the upstream determinants of alignment model activation — from defective spiral artery remodelling and the immunological paradox of pregnancy to genetic susceptibility, metabolic triggers, and environmental priming. The analysis distinguishes between processes wholly contained within the maternal system (immune maladaptation, vascular predisposition, metabolic dysfunction) and those requiring external inputs (paternal antigens, environmental exposures, infection). A unified trigger taxonomy is proposed, categorising initiating events by their point of origin, modifiability, and temporal relationship to the OPC vulnerability window. The document incorporates the multi-parity severity principle and the emerging understanding that trigger mechanisms may compound across pregnancies. Seventy-five references are cited spanning reproductive immunology, vascular biology, placental development, and the developmental origins of health and disease.

## 1. Introduction

### 1.1 The Missing First Domino

The gestational oxygen-timing alignment model [1] describes a causal chain:

```
Placental insufficiency → Fetal hypoxia → OPC maturation arrest → White matter injury → Neurodevelopmental outcome
```

Each link in this chain is supported by converging evidence from placental biology, fetal physiology, oligodendrocyte neuroscience, and clinical epidemiology. The chain explains *how* the injury occurs and *when* it matters most (GW 23–32).

What the chain does not explain is **why it starts.** What introduces the first anti-angiogenic reaction? Is the initiating event a single molecular trigger, a threshold effect of accumulating risk factors, or an inherent failure mode of human placentation? This question — the trigger mechanism — is the most fundamental open problem in the alignment model and arguably in the broader field of preeclampsia research.

### 1.2 Why the Trigger Matters

Understanding the trigger mechanism is not merely academic. It determines:

1. **Whether prevention is theoretically possible:** If the trigger is a single identifiable event, it may be blockable. If it is an emergent property of complex biological systems, prevention may require a fundamentally different approach.

2. **When prevention must act:** Triggers that operate before implantation require different interventions than those that operate in the second trimester.

3. **Which populations are targetable:** Triggers that are entirely maternal in origin implicate different screening strategies than those that depend on paternal genetic contribution or fetal-maternal immune interaction.

4. **Whether the alignment model has a natural upstream boundary:** If the trigger is purely stochastic, the alignment model describes the consequences of biological chance. If triggers are deterministic, the entire cascade — from molecular initiation to neurodevelopmental outcome — becomes a mechanistically unified and potentially interruptible process.

### 1.3 Scope

This document examines the trigger mechanism at four levels:

1. **Level 1 — The Placental Event:** What molecular and cellular processes constitute the initiation of placental insufficiency?
2. **Level 2 — The Maternal Substrate:** What aspects of the maternal biological system predispose to or directly cause the placental event?
3. **Level 3 — The Fetal-Maternal Interface:** What role does the interaction between maternal and fetal/paternal biology play in triggering the cascade?
4. **Level 4 — External Inputs:** What environmental, infectious, or behavioural factors can initiate or amplify the trigger?

## 2. Level 1 — The Placental Event: Defective Spiral Artery Remodelling

### 2.1 Normal Placentation

In successful human pregnancy, extravillous trophoblast (EVT) cells from the developing placenta invade the uterine decidua and myometrium during the first trimester (GW 6–18). These trophoblast cells remodel the maternal spiral arteries, replacing the endothelial lining and smooth muscle with trophoblast-derived cells and fibrinoid deposits [2, 3].

This remodelling transforms the spiral arteries from narrow, high-resistance, vasoresponsive vessels into wide, low-resistance, non-vasoresponsive conduits. The result is a high-flow, low-pressure maternal blood supply to the intervillous space — essential for adequate oxygen and nutrient delivery to the growing fetus throughout pregnancy [4].

The normal remodelled spiral artery has a diameter approximately 10-fold greater than its pre-pregnancy state and is no longer responsive to vasoconstrictive signals (sympathetic innervation, angiotensin II, endothelin-1). This loss of vasoresponsiveness is critical: it ensures that maternal blood flow to the placenta is maintained regardless of fluctuations in maternal systemic vascular tone [5].

### 2.2 Defective Remodelling as the Proximate Trigger

In preeclampsia, the trophoblast invasion of spiral arteries is **shallow** — typically limited to the decidual segments, failing to penetrate into the myometrial segments [6]. The result is:

- Spiral arteries that retain their muscular walls and endothelial lining
- Persistent vasoresponsiveness to constrictive signals
- Reduced luminal diameter → reduced maternal blood flow to the intervillous space
- Turbulent, high-velocity flow producing shear stress on the villous surface

This defective remodelling is the **proximate trigger** of the alignment model's cascade. But it is not the ultimate cause — it is itself a consequence of upstream factors that prevent normal trophoblast invasion.

### 2.3 The Two-Stage Model of Preeclampsia

Redman and Sargent proposed the influential two-stage model [7]:

**Stage 1 (Preclinical, GW 8–18):** Defective spiral artery remodelling → reduced uteroplacental blood flow → placental ischaemia

**Stage 2 (Clinical, typically >GW 20):** The ischaemic placenta releases anti-angiogenic factors (sFlt-1, soluble endoglin) and pro-inflammatory mediators into the maternal circulation → systemic endothelial dysfunction → clinical syndrome of preeclampsia (hypertension, proteinuria, organ damage)

The alignment model extends this framework by adding:

**Stage 3 (Fetal consequence):** The same placental ischaemia that produces maternal disease also produces fetal hypoxia → OPC vulnerability → neurodevelopmental outcome

Critically, the fetal consequences (Stage 3) begin to accumulate **before** the maternal disease becomes clinically apparent (Stage 2). By the time preeclampsia is diagnosed — typically after 20 weeks — the fetus may have already experienced weeks of suboptimal oxygenation during the OPC vulnerability window.

### 2.4 What Prevents Normal Trophoblast Invasion?

The question reduces to: **why do EVT cells fail to adequately invade and remodel the spiral arteries?** Several mechanisms have been identified:

#### 2.4.1 Decidual Natural Killer Cell Dysfunction

Uterine natural killer (uNK) cells are the predominant leucocyte population in the first-trimester decidua, comprising approximately 70% of decidual immune cells [8]. Contrary to their name, uNK cells in pregnancy are not primarily cytolytic. They secrete angiogenic factors (VEGF, PlGF, angiopoietin-2) and cytokines (IFN-γ, IL-8, IP-10) that actively promote trophoblast invasion and spiral artery remodelling [9, 10].

Deficient or dysfunctional uNK cell activity — through inadequate recruitment, impaired activation, or aberrant cytokine secretion — can result in insufficient support for trophoblast invasion, leading to shallow remodelling [11].

#### 2.4.2 Maternal Immune Rejection of Trophoblast

The fetal trophoblast is semi-allogeneic — it carries paternal antigens that are foreign to the maternal immune system. Successful trophoblast invasion requires immune tolerance at the maternal-fetal interface. This tolerance is mediated in part by:

- Expression of non-classical HLA molecules (HLA-C, HLA-E, HLA-G) on EVT cells, which engage inhibitory receptors on uNK cells and decidual macrophages [12]
- Regulatory T cells (Tregs) that suppress anti-fetal immune responses [13]
- Indoleamine 2,3-dioxygenase (IDO) expression, which creates local tryptophan depletion inhibiting T cell activation [14]

Failure of immune tolerance — through inadequate Treg induction, unfavourable HLA-C/KIR combinations, or inflammatory skewing of the decidual immune environment — can result in immune-mediated restriction of trophoblast invasion [15, 16].

#### 2.4.3 Endothelial Dysfunction of Spiral Arteries

Even with adequate trophoblast invasion, the spiral arteries must be receptive to remodelling. Pre-existing endothelial dysfunction in the spiral arteries — from chronic hypertension, diabetes, obesity, or other vascular disease — may impair the remodelling process by rendering the endothelium resistant to trophoblast-mediated replacement [17].

#### 2.4.4 Oxygen Tension Mismatch

The early first trimester (GW 0–10) is normally a low-oxygen environment, with intervillous pO₂ approximately 20 mmHg. Around GW 10–12, as spiral artery plugs dissolve and maternal blood begins flowing into the intervillous space, oxygen tension rises to approximately 60 mmHg [18]. This oxygen transition itself is a critical signal that regulates trophoblast behaviour:

- In low oxygen: EVT cells proliferate in a stem-like state
- At higher oxygen: EVT cells differentiate toward an invasive phenotype and begin spiral artery remodelling [19]

Premature exposure to high oxygen (e.g., from abnormally early dissolution of spiral artery plugs) can disrupt this programmed oxygen transition and impair trophoblast differentiation, paradoxically resulting in defective remodelling despite adequate oxygen availability. This is an intriguing parallel to the alignment model's core principle that oxygen timing, not just oxygen levels, determines biological outcome.

## 3. Level 2 — The Maternal Substrate: Why Some Women Are Predisposed

### 3.1 The Constitutional Predisposition

Preeclampsia is widely regarded as a "disease of first pregnancies" — nulliparity is the strongest demographic risk factor, with first pregnancies carrying a 3–7% risk compared to 1–3% in subsequent pregnancies [20]. However, this framing obscures a crucial distinction: preeclampsia may be a disease of **first exposure to a specific set of paternal antigens** (see Section 4), or it may reflect underlying maternal constitutional factors that are partially modified by the adaptive immune response to pregnancy.

### 3.2 Chronic Hypertension

Women with chronic hypertension have a 25–30% risk of developing superimposed preeclampsia [21]. The mechanism is straightforward: pre-existing endothelial dysfunction and vascular remodelling impair the capacity of spiral arteries to undergo pregnancy-associated transformation. The maternal vascular system is already compromised before placentation begins.

**Alignment model implication:** Chronic hypertension represents a purely maternal trigger — the anti-angiogenic cascade is initiated because the maternal vasculature cannot support normal placental development, regardless of fetal or paternal factors.

### 3.3 Metabolic Syndrome and Obesity

Maternal obesity (BMI ≥30) doubles the risk of preeclampsia, and metabolic syndrome (insulin resistance, dyslipidaemia, visceral adiposity) further amplifies this risk [22, 23].

The mechanisms connecting obesity to defective placentation include:

- **Chronic inflammation:** Adipose tissue releases pro-inflammatory cytokines (TNF-α, IL-6, leptin) that create a systemic inflammatory state hostile to immune tolerance at the maternal-fetal interface [24]
- **Insulin resistance:** Impairs endothelial function and nitric oxide bioavailability, reducing spiral artery vasodilation capacity
- **Oxidative stress:** Elevated basal oxidative stress reduces the capacity to buffer additional oxidative challenge from placental ischaemia
- **Hyperlipidaemia:** Elevated free fatty acids damage endothelium directly and promote atherosclerotic changes in spiral arteries [25]

### 3.4 Autoimmune Conditions

Systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE), antiphospholipid syndrome (APS), and other autoimmune conditions substantially increase preeclampsia risk [26, 27]. These conditions produce:

- Autoantibodies that damage placental vasculature (anti-phospholipid antibodies cause trophoblast apoptosis and complement activation at the placental surface)
- Immune dysregulation that disrupts the delicate tolerance required at the maternal-fetal interface
- Chronic endothelial activation that primes the maternal vasculature for the clinical syndrome of preeclampsia

**Alignment model implication:** Autoimmune triggers represent a maternal immune system that is constitutionally predisposed to reject or damage the trophoblast, regardless of the specific paternal antigen contribution.

### 3.5 Thrombophilia

Inherited thrombophilias (Factor V Leiden, prothrombin G20210A mutation, protein S deficiency, antithrombin deficiency) have been variably associated with preeclampsia risk [28]. The proposed mechanism is microthrombosis within the spiral arteries and intervillous space, reducing uteroplacental blood flow. However, large prospective studies have not consistently confirmed these associations, and thrombophilia screening for preeclampsia prediction is not currently recommended [29].

### 3.6 Renal Disease

Pre-existing renal disease — even mild chronic kidney disease — substantially increases preeclampsia risk [30]. The kidney is central to blood pressure regulation, fluid balance, and angiogenic factor clearance. Impaired renal function reduces sFlt-1 clearance, amplifying the anti-angiogenic signal even if placental sFlt-1 production is only modestly elevated.

### 3.7 The Maternal Age Paradox

Both very young maternal age (<18) and advanced maternal age (≥35) increase preeclampsia risk, creating a U-shaped relationship [31]. The mechanisms differ:

- **Young mothers:** Immunological immaturity may impair the development of appropriate tolerance to paternal antigens
- **Older mothers:** Accumulated vascular aging, endothelial dysfunction, and reduced immune adaptability

This paradox illustrates that the trigger mechanism is not a single factor but a balance of systems — immune, vascular, metabolic — that must all function adequately for normal placentation.

### 3.8 Maternal Stress as a Trigger Pathway

Maternal psychological stress, particularly chronic stress during the first trimester, has been associated with increased preeclampsia risk in several epidemiological studies [32, 33]. The proposed mechanisms include:

- **Hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis activation:** Chronic cortisol elevation impairs immune tolerance and promotes inflammatory skewing at the decidual interface
- **Sympathetic nervous system activation:** Elevated catecholamines cause vasoconstriction of uterine arteries, reducing early uteroplacental blood flow
- **Cortisol-mediated endothelial dysfunction:** Chronic hypercortisolaemia damages endothelial function, impairing spiral artery remodelling

This pathway is significant because it represents a modifiable trigger — stress reduction interventions in early pregnancy could theoretically reduce preeclampsia risk by improving conditions for trophoblast invasion. Extension Track 2 of this series examines maternal triggers in greater detail [34].

## 4. Level 3 — The Fetal-Maternal Interface: The Immunological Paradox

### 4.1 The Fundamental Problem of Pregnancy

Human pregnancy presents an immunological paradox: the mother must tolerate a semi-allogeneic tissue graft (the feto-placental unit) that expresses foreign (paternal) antigens, while maintaining immune competence against infection. The resolution of this paradox requires active immune modulation at the maternal-fetal interface — not merely immune suppression, but a precisely calibrated immune environment that promotes trophoblast invasion while preventing graft rejection [35].

### 4.2 The HLA-C/KIR Interaction

The interaction between fetal HLA-C antigens (inherited from the father and expressed on EVT cells) and maternal killer immunoglobulin-like receptors (KIR) on uNK cells has emerged as a critical determinant of trophoblast invasion success [36, 37].

**The Hiby hypothesis:** Hiby et al. (2004, 2010) demonstrated that specific combinations of maternal KIR genotype and fetal HLA-C group are associated with preeclampsia risk [38, 39]:

- Mothers homozygous for the KIR AA haplotype (which encodes predominantly inhibitory KIR receptors) who carry a fetus with HLA-C2 group antigens have the highest risk of preeclampsia
- The inhibitory KIR-HLA-C2 interaction results in excessive inhibition of uNK cells, preventing their normal secretion of angiogenic and invasion-promoting factors
- Mothers with the KIR BB haplotype (encoding the activating receptor KIR2DS1) are protected, presumably because activating KIR engagement with fetal HLA-C2 promotes appropriate uNK cell function

**Alignment model implication:** This is a trigger mechanism that requires BOTH maternal AND paternal genetic contribution. The mother's KIR genotype is necessary but not sufficient — the paternal HLA-C contribution through the fetus determines whether the unfavourable interaction occurs. This means the trigger is partially dependent on mate choice and cannot be predicted from maternal factors alone.

### 4.3 The Primipaternity Effect

The observation that preeclampsia is more common in first pregnancies has been reinterpreted as a "primipaternity" effect — it is not first pregnancy per se that is the risk factor, but first pregnancy **with a specific partner** [40]. Evidence supporting this interpretation:

- Preeclampsia risk resets when a woman changes partners (a new pregnancy with a new partner carries first-pregnancy-level risk)
- Longer duration of sexual cohabitation prior to conception is associated with reduced preeclampsia risk, suggesting that repeated mucosal exposure to paternal antigens (in seminal fluid) induces progressive immune tolerance [41]
- Use of barrier contraception (condoms) throughout a relationship before conception is associated with increased preeclampsia risk, consistent with reduced pre-conception antigen exposure [42]

**The immunological priming hypothesis:** Pre-conception exposure to paternal antigens through seminal fluid promotes the development of maternal regulatory T cells (Tregs) specific for paternal HLA antigens. These Tregs are then available to modulate the immune response at the maternal-fetal interface during pregnancy, promoting tolerance and facilitating trophoblast invasion [43].

### 4.4 Donor Gamete Pregnancies

Pregnancies conceived with donor oocytes have a markedly elevated risk of preeclampsia (approximately 20–40%), even in multiparous women [44, 45]. This is the strongest available evidence that fetal-maternal immune interaction is a trigger mechanism:

- Donor oocyte pregnancies are immunologically equivalent to first exposure to a completely novel set of fetal antigens
- There has been no pre-conception exposure to the "paternal" (donor) antigens through seminal fluid
- The maternal immune system encounters entirely foreign trophoblast for the first time

Similarly, pregnancies conceived through assisted reproduction technologies (ART) generally carry elevated preeclampsia risk, partially attributable to reduced pre-conception antigen exposure and partially to the underlying conditions that caused infertility [46].

### 4.5 The Father's Contribution to the Trigger

The paternal contribution to preeclampsia risk is not limited to HLA-C antigen donation. Epidemiological evidence suggests that some men are "preeclampsia-prone fathers" — their partners have elevated preeclampsia risk regardless of the woman's individual risk profile [47]:

- A man whose previous partner had preeclampsia confers elevated risk to his new partner
- Paternal family history of preeclampsia increases risk
- This paternal effect may be mediated through imprinted genes in the placenta that are predominantly expressed from the paternal allele (e.g., genes that promote placental growth and invasion may be paternally driven by evolutionary conflict — the father's genetic interest favours maximal placental extraction of maternal resources, while the mother's interest favours limiting placental invasion) [48]

**Parent-offspring conflict theory (Haig, 1993):** David Haig proposed that growth-promoting genes expressed from paternal alleles in the placenta are in evolutionary conflict with growth-restraining genes expressed from maternal alleles [49]. Preeclampsia may represent the maternal system "winning" this conflict — restraining placental invasion to protect maternal health, at the cost of fetal oxygen supply. This evolutionary framework positions preeclampsia not as a disease but as an extreme manifestation of a normal evolutionary tension.

## 5. Level 4 — External Inputs: Environmental and Infectious Triggers

### 5.1 Infection as a Trigger

Maternal infection — particularly chronic, low-grade infection — has been associated with increased preeclampsia risk through several mechanisms:

**Periodontal disease:** Chronic periodontal infection is associated with a 2–3-fold increase in preeclampsia risk [50]. The proposed mechanism involves systemic dissemination of inflammatory mediators (C-reactive protein, IL-6, TNF-α) and periodontal pathogens that activate the maternal immune system and compromise endothelial function.

**Urinary tract infection:** Recurrent UTI in pregnancy is associated with increased preeclampsia risk, potentially through systemic inflammatory activation and complement pathway activation that spills over to the maternal-fetal interface [51].

**Helicobacter pylori:** Several studies have reported associations between H. pylori seropositivity and preeclampsia, hypothesised to operate through molecular mimicry between H. pylori antigens and placental proteins, triggering cross-reactive immune responses [52].

**COVID-19 (SARS-CoV-2):** Emerging evidence from the COVID-19 pandemic demonstrated that SARS-CoV-2 infection during pregnancy significantly increases preeclampsia risk (OR approximately 1.5–2.0), potentially through ACE2 receptor-mediated placental damage and systemic endothelial activation [53]. This represents a natural experiment confirming that acute infection can trigger the placental anti-angiogenic cascade.

### 5.2 Environmental Exposures

Extension Track 3 of this series examines environmental exposures in detail [54]. Key triggers include:

- **Air pollution (PM2.5, NO₂):** Increases preeclampsia risk through systemic inflammation and oxidative stress, impairing spiral artery remodelling
- **Heavy metals (lead, cadmium, mercury):** Endocrine disruptors that impair trophoblast function and promote oxidative damage to the placental vasculature
- **Endocrine-disrupting chemicals (BPA, phthalates):** Interfere with progesterone signalling, which is essential for decidualisation and immune tolerance
- **Heat stress:** Extreme ambient temperature exposure has been associated with increased preeclampsia risk, potentially through dehydration-mediated haemoconcentration and thermal stress on the placental vasculature [55]

### 5.3 Nutritional Triggers and Deficiencies

Nutritional status in the periconceptional and early pregnancy period may influence the trigger mechanism:

**Calcium deficiency:** The WHO recommends calcium supplementation (1.5–2 g/day) for preeclampsia prevention in populations with low dietary calcium intake. Calcium deficiency increases parathyroid hormone and intracellular calcium, promoting vasoconstriction and smooth muscle reactivity [56].

**Vitamin D deficiency:** Low 25-hydroxyvitamin D levels have been associated with increased preeclampsia risk in multiple observational studies. Vitamin D has immunomodulatory effects (promoting Treg differentiation) and direct effects on trophoblast invasion and angiogenesis [57].

**Folate and B-vitamin deficiency:** Deficiency promotes hyperhomocysteinaemia, which damages endothelial function and may impair trophoblast invasion. Periconceptional folic acid supplementation may reduce preeclampsia risk, though trial evidence is inconsistent [58].

**Selenium deficiency:** Selenium is essential for glutathione peroxidase activity, a critical antioxidant enzyme. Selenium supplementation has shown promise in reducing preeclampsia risk in selenium-deficient populations [59].

**L-arginine and nitric oxide:** L-arginine is the substrate for endothelial nitric oxide synthase (eNOS), which produces the vasodilator nitric oxide. Deficiency may impair spiral artery vasodilation; supplementation trials have shown mixed results [60].

### 5.4 Altitude and Chronic Hypoxia

Living at high altitude (>2,500 metres) increases preeclampsia risk by 2–3-fold [61]. This represents a direct test of the alignment model's upstream trigger: chronic maternal hypoxia at altitude impairs the oxygen-dependent processes of trophoblast differentiation and spiral artery remodelling, even in the absence of any other risk factor.

Populations that have lived at high altitude for millennia (Andean, Tibetan) show evolutionary adaptations that partially protect against altitude-associated preeclampsia, including modified uterine artery blood flow, enhanced placental vascularisation, and genetic variants in hypoxia-response pathways (EPAS1, EGLN1) [62]. This confirms that the trigger mechanism is fundamentally an oxygen-sensing problem — consistent with the alignment model's core thesis.

## 6. A Unified Trigger Taxonomy

### 6.1 Classification Framework

Based on the preceding analysis, the trigger mechanisms for the placental anti-angiogenic cascade can be classified along three dimensions:

#### Dimension 1: Origin

| Origin | Examples | Modifiability |
|---|---|---|
| Purely maternal | Chronic hypertension, obesity, autoimmune disease, renal disease | Partially modifiable (pre-conception health optimisation) |
| Maternal-fetal interface | KIR/HLA-C mismatch, primipaternity, immune tolerance failure | Not modifiable (genetic), but predictable |
| Paternal contribution | Paternal HLA-C type, imprinted placental growth genes, "preeclampsia-prone father" effect | Not modifiable, potentially screenable |
| External/environmental | Infection, air pollution, nutritional deficiency, altitude | Modifiable |

#### Dimension 2: Timing

| Timing | Process | Intervention window |
|---|---|---|
| Pre-conception | Maternal vascular health, immune priming by seminal fluid, nutritional status | Months to years before pregnancy |
| GW 0–10 | Low-oxygen trophoblast proliferation, immune tolerance establishment | Periconceptional |
| GW 8–18 | Spiral artery remodelling, trophoblast invasion | First trimester — critical and largely inaccessible |
| GW 18–20+ | Placental ischaemia → anti-angiogenic response → clinical syndrome | Second trimester — aspirin window closes, monitoring begins |
| GW 23–32 | OPC vulnerability window — consequences of trigger are being written | Third trimester — delivery timing decisions |

#### Dimension 3: Determinism

| Category | Nature | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Deterministic | Will cause preeclampsia if present | Antiphospholipid syndrome with high titre antibodies |
| Probabilistic | Increases risk but not sufficient alone | Obesity, nulliparity, family history |
| Permissive | Creates conditions where other triggers can act | Immune tolerance deficiency + novel paternal antigens |
| Stochastic | Random variation in biological processes | Extent of early trophoblast invasion (varies even in healthy pregnancies) |

### 6.2 The Convergence Model

No single trigger is sufficient to explain all cases of preeclampsia. The convergence model proposes that the placental anti-angiogenic cascade is initiated when a **critical threshold of combined risk factors** is crossed:

```
Trigger Threshold = f(Maternal Vascular Health + Immune Tolerance Capacity + 
                      Placental Genetic Programming + Environmental Exposures + 
                      Stochastic Variation)
```

Different women cross this threshold through different combinations of factors:

- **Case A:** Healthy young woman with unfavourable KIR/HLA-C combination + first pregnancy with new partner → trigger is primarily immunological
- **Case B:** Obese woman with chronic hypertension + any pregnancy → trigger is primarily vascular/metabolic
- **Case C:** Healthy woman at sea level moves to high altitude during first trimester → trigger is primarily environmental
- **Case D:** Healthy woman with no identifiable risk factors → trigger may be stochastic variation in depth of trophoblast invasion

This convergence model explains why no single screening test has adequate sensitivity for preeclampsia prediction — the population of affected women is heterogeneous in their trigger mechanisms.

### 6.3 Integration with Aspirin Prophylaxis

The ASPRE trial demonstrated that low-dose aspirin (150 mg/day) initiated before 16 weeks' gestation reduces preterm preeclampsia by 62% in high-risk women [63]. Aspirin's mechanism of action in preeclampsia prevention is not fully understood, but likely involves:

- Inhibition of thromboxane A₂ synthesis (reducing platelet aggregation and vasoconstriction in the uteroplacental circulation)
- Anti-inflammatory effects (reducing decidual inflammation that impairs trophoblast invasion)
- Potential promotion of prostacyclin-mediated vasodilation in spiral arteries [64]

Notably, aspirin must be initiated before 16 weeks (and ideally before 12 weeks) to be effective — after spiral artery remodelling is complete (or has failed), aspirin cannot reverse the defective placentation [65]. This temporal requirement is consistent with the trigger taxonomy: aspirin operates at the Level 1 trigger (supporting spiral artery remodelling) and must act within the GW 8–18 remodelling window.

## 7. Is There a Single Initiating Event?

### 7.1 The Case for a Single Trigger

The most parsimonious model would identify a single molecular event that initiates the cascade. The strongest candidate is:

**Failure of the first wave of trophoblast invasion to remodel the myometrial segments of spiral arteries (GW 8–12)**

This is the earliest detectable difference between pregnancies that will develop preeclampsia and those that will not. All downstream events — placental ischaemia, sFlt-1 release, endothelial dysfunction, fetal hypoxia — follow causally from this single failure [6].

However, calling this a "single trigger" is somewhat misleading — the failure of trophoblast invasion is itself a multicausal event (as detailed in Sections 2–5). The single event (failed remodelling) has multiple possible upstream causes.

### 7.2 The Case Against a Single Trigger

Several observations argue against a simple single-trigger model:

1. **Heterogeneity of preeclampsia phenotypes:** Early-onset (<34 weeks) and late-onset (≥34 weeks) preeclampsia have different risk factor profiles, different placental pathology, and different outcomes — suggesting different trigger mechanisms for what is clinically labelled as the same disease [66]

2. **The HELLP syndrome distinction:** Haemolysis, Elevated Liver enzymes, Low Platelets syndrome shares features with preeclampsia but has distinct pathophysiology, suggesting a related but separate trigger mechanism [67]

3. **Preeclampsia without placental insufficiency:** Some cases of preeclampsia (particularly late-onset) occur without significant placental pathology, suggesting that the maternal constitutional response (excessive endothelial sensitivity to normal levels of anti-angiogenic factors) can produce the clinical syndrome without a primary placental trigger [68]

4. **The threshold effect:** The severity of the clinical syndrome is continuously distributed, not binary. This is consistent with a quantitative model where multiple factors contribute additively, rather than a single trigger that is either present or absent.

### 7.3 Resolution: The Trigger Is a Threshold, Not an Event

The most coherent synthesis of available evidence suggests that **the trigger is not a single event but a threshold effect:** when the combined burden of predisposing factors (maternal vascular, immunological, metabolic, genetic, environmental) exceeds the biological system's capacity to compensate, the placental anti-angiogenic cascade activates.

This threshold model has several important properties:

1. **It explains variable penetrance:** The same combination of risk factors may produce preeclampsia in one pregnancy and not another, depending on stochastic variation in early placentation
2. **It explains the spectrum:** Mild preeclampsia represents a threshold barely crossed; severe early-onset preeclampsia represents a threshold dramatically exceeded
3. **It explains aspirin's mechanism:** Aspirin lowers the composite risk burden (by reducing thrombosis and inflammation), potentially keeping the total below the activation threshold
4. **It explains multi-parity effects:** Each successive pregnancy adds cumulative maternal vascular damage to the risk burden, making the threshold easier to cross

## 8. Temporal Architecture of the Trigger

### 8.1 The Trigger Timeline

The trigger mechanism operates across a timeline that precedes and overlaps with the OPC vulnerability window:

```
Pre-conception (months-years before):
├── Maternal vascular health status established
├── Immune priming by seminal fluid (if applicable)
├── Nutrition, weight, chronic disease status
└── Environmental exposure history

GW 0–6 (Implantation and early development):
├── Trophoblast-decidual interaction begins
├── Immune tolerance mechanisms activated
├── Low-oxygen environment maintained
└── Early vascular remodelling initiated

GW 6–12 (First wave of remodelling):
├── EVT cells invade decidual spiral arteries
├── KIR/HLA-C interaction determines uNK response
├── Depth of invasion determined
└── TRIGGER THRESHOLD ASSESSED — remodelling fails or succeeds

GW 12–18 (Second wave of remodelling):
├── EVT cells should penetrate myometrial segments
├── If first wave was inadequate, second wave is also limited
├── Placental vascular bed capacity is determined
└── TRIGGER COMMITTED — placental architecture is fixed

GW 18–24 (Growing demand exceeds supply):
├── Fetal metabolic demand increases rapidly
├── Fixed placental capacity cannot match demand
├── Placental hypoxia develops
├── sFlt-1, sEng begin to rise
└── ANTI-ANGIOGENIC CASCADE INITIATED

GW 23–32 (OPC vulnerability window):
├── Fetal hypoxia now present during critical OPC development
├── OPC maturation arrest, death, depletion
├── Clinical preeclampsia typically diagnosed
├── Delivery decision being considered
└── ALIGNMENT MODEL ACTIVATED — damage being written

GW 32+ or delivery:
├── Delivery terminates placental insufficiency
├── Neonatal oxygen management becomes critical (Track 5)
└── OUTCOME DETERMINED by total hypoxic burden
```

### 8.2 The Inaccessible Window

A critical insight from this timeline is that the trigger mechanism operates primarily during GW 6–18 — a period when:

1. The pregnancy may not yet be confirmed (particularly GW 6–8)
2. No clinical signs of preeclampsia are present
3. Routine prenatal care has typically not yet begun
4. The biological events (trophoblast invasion, spiral artery remodelling) are occurring deep within the uterine wall and cannot be directly observed

This inaccessibility means that **by the time the trigger can be detected (through elevated sFlt-1, clinical signs, or placental Doppler abnormalities), it has already fired.** Prevention must therefore either:

- Act before the trigger window (pre-conception health, aspirin from GW 11–12)
- Or mitigate the consequences after the trigger fires (optimised delivery timing, neonatal management)

The alignment model occupies the downstream space — it describes the consequences of triggers that have already fired and cannot be reversed. The practical value of understanding the trigger mechanism is therefore primarily in **primary prevention** for future pregnancies, rather than treatment of established disease.

## 9. Multi-Parity and Trigger Compounding

### 9.1 Progressive Trigger Accumulation Across Pregnancies

The multi-parity severity observation (detailed in Track 5, Section 9) acquires additional mechanistic depth when analysed through the trigger framework.

Each preeclamptic pregnancy does not merely produce a neurodevelopmental outcome in one child — it also **modifies the maternal trigger substrate** for future pregnancies:

**Pregnancy 1 (Trigger threshold crossed by factor A):**
- Index event: first preeclamptic pregnancy
- Maternal consequence: endothelial damage, residual hypertension, renal microinjury
- Trigger substrate change: maternal vascular health worsened → threshold for next pregnancy lowered

**Pregnancy 2 (Trigger threshold crossed by factors A + B):**
- The original trigger factors (A) are still present
- New factor (B): accumulated vascular damage from Pregnancy 1
- Lower threshold → earlier onset, more severe preeclampsia
- Further maternal damage accumulates

**Pregnancy 3 (Trigger threshold crossed by factors A + B + C):**
- All previous factors + further accumulated damage (C)
- Threshold may be crossed so easily that early-onset severe preeclampsia is almost inevitable
- Fetal exposure to hypoxia is deeper and longer

### 9.2 The Irreversible Maternal Vascular Legacy

Women with a history of preeclampsia have demonstrably elevated long-term cardiovascular risk [69, 70]:

- 4-fold increased risk of later hypertension
- 2-fold increased risk of ischaemic heart disease
- 2-fold increased risk of stroke
- Elevated risk of chronic kidney disease

These are not merely associations — they reflect permanent structural changes to the maternal vasculature that accumulate with each preeclamptic pregnancy. This vascular legacy directly lowers the trigger threshold for subsequent pregnancies.

### 9.3 Immunological Memory: A Mitigating Factor?

Counterbalancing the vascular deterioration, the maternal immune system develops memory responses to paternal antigens that may **protect** against preeclampsia recurrence in subsequent pregnancies with the same partner [71]:

- Maternal Tregs specific for paternal HLA expand during first pregnancy and persist as memory cells
- Subsequent pregnancies with the same partner benefit from pre-existing immune tolerance
- This immunological protection explains why preeclampsia is less common in second pregnancies (with the same partner)

The net effect on the trigger threshold depends on the balance between:
- **Worsening:** Accumulated vascular damage (raises baseline risk)
- **Improving:** Enhanced immunological tolerance (lowers immune-mediated risk component)

In most women, the immunological benefit outweighs the vascular damage, and subsequent pregnancies are lower risk. But in women with severe vascular damage from early-onset preeclampsia, or in women who change partners (losing immunological priming), the trigger threshold may be lower in subsequent pregnancies, consistent with escalating severity.

## 10. Is the Trigger Mechanism Entirely Contained Within the Mother?

### 10.1 The Maternal Containment Question

A fundamental question for the alignment model is whether the trigger mechanism can be entirely attributed to maternal factors, or whether it inherently requires non-maternal (fetal/paternal/environmental) contributions.

### 10.2 Evidence for Maternal-Only Triggers

Several lines of evidence support triggers that are fully contained within the maternal system:

- **Chronic hypertension → preeclampsia superimposition:** The maternal vascular disease alone is sufficient to impair spiral artery remodelling
- **Autoimmune disease → placental damage:** Autoantibodies produced by the maternal immune system attack the placental vasculature regardless of fetal genotype
- **Obesity/metabolic syndrome → endothelial dysfunction:** Maternal metabolic state directly compromises the remodelling process

In these cases, the maternal system would produce placental insufficiency regardless of whom she conceived with or what genetic contribution the fetus carries.

### 10.3 Evidence Against Maternal-Only Triggers

However, other evidence clearly demonstrates that the trigger requires interaction between maternal and non-maternal factors:

- **KIR/HLA-C interaction:** Requires specific paternal HLA-C type to create the inhibitory combination
- **Primipaternity effect:** Risk depends on the specific partner
- **Donor oocyte effect:** Completely foreign fetal antigens dramatically increase risk regardless of maternal health
- **Paternal family history effect:** Some men confer risk across different partners

### 10.4 Resolution: The Trigger Is Multi-Factorial by Nature

The trigger mechanism cannot be reduced to either purely maternal or purely fetal-maternal factors. It is inherently multi-factorial:

- Some triggers are fully maternal (can be screened and potentially prevented pre-conception)
- Some triggers require fetal-maternal interaction (can be predicted if KIR/HLA-C typing is available)
- Some triggers are environmental (can be modified)
- Some triggers are stochastic (cannot be predicted or prevented, only mitigated post hoc)

This multi-factorial nature is what makes preeclampsia prediction so difficult and why no single screening test achieves both high sensitivity and high specificity. The alignment model's greatest practical value may therefore lie not in preventing the trigger (which is often unpredictable) but in **optimising the response** once it has fired: timed delivery, neonatal oxygen management, and developmental follow-up.

## 11. Predictive Screening and Early Detection

### 11.1 Current Screening Approaches

Given that the trigger mechanism is multi-factorial, screening strategies have evolved toward combining multiple markers:

**The FMF (Fetal Medicine Foundation) algorithm:** First-trimester screening combines maternal factors, mean arterial pressure (MAP), uterine artery pulsatility index (UtA-PI), serum PAPP-A, and serum PlGF at 11–13 weeks to generate a patient-specific risk for preeclampsia [72]. This algorithm has:
- Detection rate: ~90% for early-onset preeclampsia (<34 weeks)
- Detection rate: ~75% for preterm preeclampsia (<37 weeks)
- False positive rate: 10%

**sFlt-1/PlGF ratio:** Second/third trimester sFlt-1/PlGF ratio has high negative predictive value (>99%) for ruling out imminent preeclampsia and can confirm the anti-angiogenic cascade is active [73].

### 11.2 The Pre-Trigger Window

The alignment model emphasises that by the time current screening detects preeclampsia risk (11–13 weeks by FMF algorithm, >20 weeks by clinical diagnosis), the trigger has already fired or is in the process of firing. The only intervention with demonstrated pre-trigger efficacy is aspirin initiated before 16 weeks [63].

**The aspirin paradox:** Aspirin works by supporting the remodelling process during the trigger window (GW 8–18). It must be initiated before or during this window to be effective. Yet the screening that identifies who needs aspirin (the FMF algorithm) operates within this same window (GW 11–13). There is a narrow overlap — approximately 4–5 weeks — during which both screening AND intervention are possible. Delays in prenatal care access, particularly in underserved populations, can eliminate this window entirely [74].

### 11.3 Pre-Conception Risk Assessment

The trigger taxonomy suggests that some components of preeclampsia risk can be assessed before conception:

- Maternal blood pressure and vascular health
- BMI and metabolic status
- Autoimmune disease activity
- History of prior preeclampsia
- Family history (maternal and paternal)
- Potentially: KIR genotyping of the mother

Pre-conception risk assessment could allow aspirin prophylaxis to be initiated from the earliest point in pregnancy, maximising the available intervention window.

## 12. The Trigger Mechanism and the Alignment Model: Synthesis

### 12.1 Connecting Trigger to Outcome

The complete alignment model, incorporating the trigger mechanism as its upstream component, now describes a causal chain from molecular initiation to clinical outcome:

```
TRIGGER (Level 1–4 factors) 
→ Defective spiral artery remodelling (GW 8–18)
→ Placental ischaemia (GW 18+)
→ Anti-angiogenic cascade (sFlt-1 ↑, PlGF ↓)
→ Fetal hypoxia
→ OPC maturation arrest during vulnerability window (GW 23–32)
→ White matter injury pattern (timing-dependent)
→ Neurodevelopmental outcome (CP, ASD, SCZ spectrum)
→ [If OPC pool depleted: reduced lifelong myelin maintenance reserve]
→ [Potential contribution to age-related neurodegeneration]
```

Each link in this extended chain generates specific predictions and intervention opportunities. The trigger mechanism determines WHETHER the cascade fires and HOW SEVERELY; the timing alignment determines WHAT OUTCOME results from a given severity.

### 12.2 The Practical Hierarchy of Intervention

Given that trigger mechanisms are variably modifiable, the alignment model suggests a practical intervention hierarchy:

**Tier 1 — Prevent the trigger (ideal):**
- Pre-conception health optimisation (blood pressure, weight, nutrition)
- Aspirin prophylaxis from 12 weeks in high-risk women
- First-trimester screening (FMF algorithm) to identify women who need aspirin

**Tier 2 — Detect the trigger early:**
- sFlt-1/PlGF ratio monitoring in high-risk pregnancies
- Uterine artery Doppler surveillance
- Clinical vigilance for early signs of preeclampsia

**Tier 3 — Mitigate the consequences (if trigger fires):**
- Timed delivery to minimise fetal hypoxic exposure during OPC vulnerability window
- Maternal magnesium sulphate for fetal neuroprotection before preterm delivery [75]
- Optimised neonatal oxygen management (Track 5 of this series)
- Enhanced developmental surveillance and early intervention

### 12.3 The Unanswered Question

After examining the trigger mechanism across four levels, the most honest conclusion is: **we know what happens, we partially understand why, but we cannot yet predict who.** The trigger mechanism is a convergence of factors, some fixed (genetic), some modifiable (health, environment), and some stochastic (biological variation). No single test can capture this complexity.

The alignment model's contribution to the trigger problem is not to solve it, but to clarify **what is at stake when the trigger fires.** By connecting placental insufficiency directly to the permanent loss of neurodevelopmental potential through OPC vulnerability, the model provides the strongest possible motivation for investing in trigger prevention: the cost of a fired trigger is not merely a complicated pregnancy but a lifetime of neurological consequence.

## 13. Future Directions

### 13.1 Research Priorities

1. **Multi-omic trigger profiling:** Integration of maternal genomics (KIR genotype), transcriptomics (decidual gene expression), proteomics (circulating angiogenic factors), and metabolomics (metabolic state markers) to build a comprehensive trigger risk model

2. **Pre-conception risk calculators:** Development of validated tools that assess combined maternal vascular, immunological, and metabolic risk before pregnancy, enabling prospective intervention planning

3. **Partner-specific risk assessment:** Feasibility studies of KIR/HLA-C compatibility testing for couples with prior preeclampsia, providing counselling about recurrence risk

4. **Environmental trigger modification:** Interventional studies examining whether ambient air quality improvement, nutritional supplementation, or stress reduction in early pregnancy reduces preeclampsia incidence in high-risk populations

5. **Real-time placentation monitoring:** Development of non-invasive imaging or circulating biomarker panels that can detect defective spiral artery remodelling during the first trimester, before the trigger is committed

6. **Evolutionary analysis:** Comparative placentation research across primate species to understand why human placentation is uniquely vulnerable to the triggering mechanisms described in this framework

### 13.2 The Trigger-Intervention Mismatch

The most significant clinical challenge identified by this analysis is the temporal mismatch between trigger windows and intervention capability. The trigger fires at GW 8–18, but most women do not enter prenatal care until GW 8–12, and aspirin must be initiated before GW 16 to be effective. Closing this gap requires:

- Earlier engagement with prenatal care (ideally pre-conception)
- Universal first-trimester screening at GW 11–13 with immediate aspirin initiation for high-risk women
- Integration of preeclampsia risk assessment into routine pre-conception counselling

### 13.3 The Complete Framework

The extension tracks of the gestational oxygen-timing alignment framework published to date:

| Track | Topic | Key finding |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Paternal epigenetic transmission | Father's germline contributes via sperm epigenome and HLA-C |
| 2 | Colostrum as personalised neonatal repair | Mammary response to gestational injury as biomarker and repair mechanism |
| 3 | Environmental exposures | Air pollution, chemicals, altitude as external trigger inputs |
| 4 | Immune activation & myelination | MIA as a compounding factor on OPC vulnerability |
| 5 | Neonatal oxygen management | Cord transition and NICU care as the last intervention window |
| 6 | Trigger mechanism (this document) | Multi-factorial threshold model of cascade initiation |
| 7 | Preeclampsia as paternal compatibility signal | PE reframed as mate-pair immunogenetic incompatibility phenotype |

Together, these tracks extend the core alignment model from a narrow framework about placental insufficiency and OPC timing into a comprehensive theory of how neurodevelopmental outcomes are determined by the interaction of genetic, immunological, vascular, environmental, and clinical management factors — with oxygen timing as the unifying variable.
