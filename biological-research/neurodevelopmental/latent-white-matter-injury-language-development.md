# RESEARCH: Latent White Matter Injury and Language Development

## Theory Under Investigation

> "Early hypoxic/ischaemic insult (especially in the context of preeclampsia + birth complications) damages oligodendrocyte precursor cells during formation. The white matter damage is LATENT — it only becomes visible when the developmental program tries to USE those tracts."

---

## AREA 1: The Arcuate Fasciculus and Language Network White Matter

### 1.1 Anatomy and Connectivity of the Arcuate Fasciculus

The arcuate fasciculus (AF) is a bundle of axons that generally connects Broca's area (specifically Brodmann area 44 in the inferior frontal gyrus) and Wernicke's area (posterior superior temporal gyrus) in the brain. It is an association fiber tract connecting caudal temporal lobe and inferior frontal lobe (Carlson, 2012). However, this classical model is acknowledged as an oversimplification — research indicates more diffuse termination of the fibers than previously thought. While the main caudal source appears to be posterior superior temporal cortex, the rostral terminations are mostly in premotor cortex, part of Brodmann area 44 (Catani & Thiebaut de Schotten, 2008; Bernal & Ardila, 2009; Bernal & Altman, 2010).

The AF is part of the **dorsal stream** of language processing, which is implicated in sensory-to-motor mapping and processing complex syntax (Friederici, 2017). It runs parallel to the superior longitudinal fasciculus (SLF), from which it can be distinguished by its endpoints: the AF terminates in Broca's area (BA 44), while the SLF ends in the premotor cortex.

The language network also depends on:

- **Superior Longitudinal Fasciculus III (SLF III)**: Originates in the supramarginal gyrus and terminates in ventral premotor and prefrontal cortex (BA 6, 44, 46). It transfers somatosensory information related to language articulation (Makris et al., 2005; Wang et al., 2016).
- **Uncinate fasciculus**: Links anterior temporal lobe regions with inferior frontal gyrus. It has the longest development period, continuing until age 30 (Lebel et al., 2008). In 12-year-old males born preterm, abnormalities in the left anterior uncinate correlated with verbal IQ, full-scale IQ, and Peabody Picture Vocabulary Test scores (Constable et al., 2008).
- **Corpus callosum**: The largest white matter structure in the brain. Its trunk fibers interconnect motor cortex areas, with proportionately more dedicated to supplementary motor regions including Broca's area. Formation begins around week 12 of prenatal development (Rakic & Yakovlev, 1968). Myelination peaks in the first decade of life but continues through the thirties (Luders et al., 2010). Corpus callosum malformation has been linked to autism spectrum disorders (Wegiel et al., 2018).

### 1.2 Myelination Timeline of the Arcuate Fasciculus

**Critical finding**: The arcuate fasciculus of newborns is **unmyelinated**. The myelination process occurs gradually during childhood. Key developmental data:

- Myelin density increases between ages **3 and 10** (Friederici, 2017)
- A study comparing 6-year-olds to 3-year-olds found 6-year-olds had **stronger functional connectivity** of the arcuate fasciculus (Friederici, 2017)
- The density of myelination predicts the **accuracy and speed to which one can comprehend sentences** and alterations in structure can impact receptive language abilities (Carozza et al., 2025)
- As the AF matures and undergoes myelination, there is a corresponding increase in the ability to process syntax (Friederici, 2017)
- The AF is similarly undeveloped in non-human primates such as chimpanzees and macaques, supporting the theory that the AF is a critical component in language (Friederici, 2017)

**General white matter myelination timeline** (from Myelin Wikipedia):
- Myelination begins in the third trimester, around **week 26 of gestational age**
- Myelination "corresponds with the development of cognitive and motor skills, including language comprehension, speech acquisition, crawling and walking"
- Myelination continues through adolescence and into early adulthood; myelin sheaths can be added in grey matter regions throughout life

### 1.3 Specific White Matter Tracts That Must Myelinate for Speech/Language

| Tract | Function | Myelination Window |
|-------|----------|-------------------|
| Arcuate fasciculus | Complex syntax processing; connects posterior temporal cortex to BA 44 | Unmyelinated at birth; gradual myelination ages 3-10+ |
| SLF III | Language articulation; somatosensory transfer | Childhood through adolescence |
| Uncinate fasciculus | Name/word retrieval; temporal-frontal memory links | Longest development period — continues until age 30 |
| Corpus callosum (trunk) | Interhemispheric coordination of motor speech areas including Broca's area | First decade peak; continues through thirties |

### 1.4 Clinical Evidence of AF Disruption Affecting Language

- **Conduction aphasia**: Historically linked to AF damage — characterized by difficulty with repetition and phonemic paraphasias. Patients otherwise exhibit relatively normal language control (Adams, 1969; Bernal & Ardila, 2009)
- **Specific language impairment**: Children with SLI have particular difficulty with syntactic and hierarchical structures of language. AF damage is implicated as a possible cause (Friederici, 2017)
- **Dyslexia**: Decreases in the integrity of the arcuate fasciculus coincide with worsened reading ability in dyslexic subjects (Vandermosten et al., 2012)
- **Stuttering**: Bilateral AF deficits that reduce it by one-third or more relative to non-stutterers (Cieslak et al., 2015)
- **Progressive aphasia**: Lesions in the AF produce especially deficient syntax processing abilities, with worsened processing correlating with the degree of AF degradation (Friederici, 2017)

## AREA 2: "Latent Failure at Demand Spike" — Growing into Deficit

### 2.1 The Concept of "Growing into Deficit"

The concept that brain injury in early life may produce deficits that only become apparent later — when the developmental program attempts to deploy injured circuits — is well-documented in periventricular leukomalacia (PVL) literature and pediatric traumatic brain injury research.

**From PVL (Wikipedia / Rezaie & Dean, 2002; Fetters & Huang, 2007):**
> "It is often impossible to identify PVL based on the patient's physical or behavioral characteristics. The white matter in the periventricular regions is involved heavily in motor control, and so individuals with PVL often exhibit motor problems. However, since healthy newborns (especially premature infants) can perform very few specific motor tasks, early deficits are very difficult to identify. **As the individual develops, the areas and extent of problems caused by PVL can begin to be identified**; however, these problems are usually found after an initial diagnosis has been made."

> "The extent of signs is strongly dependent on the extent of white matter damage: **minor damage leads to only minor deficits or delays**, while significant white matter damage can cause severe problems with motor coordination or organ function."

**From cerebral hypoxia prognosis:**
> "The effects of certain kinds of severe generalized hypoxias may take time to develop."

**From pediatric TBI (Chevignard, Câmara-Costa & Dellatolas, 2020):**
> "Childhood traumatic brain injury (TBI) commonly occurs during brain development and can have direct, immediately observable neurologic, cognitive, and behavioral consequences. However, **it can also disrupt subsequent brain development, and long-term** [consequences emerge later]."

**From Anderson et al. (2012):**
> TBI in early childhood produces "high risk of persisting deficits" that become measurable only years later.

**From Anderson et al. (2009):**
> Intellectual outcomes from preschool TBI showed evolution of deficits over a 5-year prospective longitudinal study.

### 2.2 What "Failure at Demand Spike" Means for Language

The theory proposes that white matter tracts damaged at birth are **functionally silent because the neonate doesn't yet need them**. The arcuate fasciculus, for example, is unmyelinated at birth even in healthy infants. This creates a critical problem:

1. **Pre-injury window (birth-2 years)**: The damaged tracts are not yet functionally required. Single-word language can proceed relatively normally because it relies on ventral stream (uncinate fasciculus) and simpler cortical circuits.
2. **Demand spike (~2-3 years)**: The developmental program calls upon the arcuate fasciculus for complex syntax, sentence comprehension, and productive multi-word speech. Damaged OPCs cannot generate adequate myelination.
3. **Visible deficit (~2-4+ years)**: Language delays, syntactic difficulties, and comprehension problems emerge — not because of a new injury, but because existing damage is now "unmasked" by functional demand.

This is consistent with the PVL literature showing "predictable manner" of deficit emergence: "some abnormal neurological signs are visible by the third trimester of pregnancy, and definitive signs of cerebral palsy are visible by six to nine months of age" (Dubowitz et al., 1985). For language (which develops later than motor skills), the unmasking would occur later — in the 2-4 year window when complex syntax requires myelinated dorsal stream pathways.

### 2.3 Evidence for the Latent-Then-Manifest Pattern

**Direct evidence**: The PVL literature consistently describes deferred presentation. The term "growing into deficit" (also "growing into the lesion") is used in pediatric neurology to describe how children with early brain injuries appear to worsen or develop new problems as developmental demands increase — they are growing into the functional requirements that the damaged circuits should serve.

**Indirect evidence**: The uncinate fasciculus study by Constable et al. (2008) demonstrated that at 12 years old, children born preterm showed white matter microstructural differences — and these correlated with verbal IQ. The injury occurred at birth; the functional consequence was measurable over a decade later.

## AREA 3: Oligodendrocyte Precursor Cell (OPC) Vulnerability

### 3.1 Maturation-Dependent Vulnerability Window

This is the area with the strongest direct evidence, primarily from the laboratory of **Stephen A. Back** and collaborators.

**Back et al. (2002)** — "Selective vulnerability of late oligodendrocyte progenitors to hypoxia-ischemia" (J Neurosci):
> "The mechanism of perinatal white matter injury involved **maturation-dependent vulnerability in the oligodendrocyte lineage**. The timing of appearance of late OL progenitors was the major developmental factor that accounted for the susceptibility of the neonatal white matter to injury. **Late OL progenitors were the major OL lineage stage killed by apoptosis, whereas early OL progenitors and more mature OLs were highly resistant.**"
>
> The death rate was dramatic: "the death of **72 ± 6% of this OL stage**" in ischemic tissue.
>
> "Because we showed recently that late OL progenitors populate human cerebral white matter during the high risk period for PVL... **maturation-dependent vulnerability of OL progenitors to hypoxia-ischemia may underlie the selective vulnerability to PVL of the white matter in the premature infant.**"

**Back et al. (2001)** — cited in the 2002 paper: "Late oligodendrocyte progenitors coincide with the developmental window of vulnerability for human perinatal white matter injury" (J Neurosci). This established that the developmental window when late OL progenitors populate human white matter aligns with the period of maximal PVL risk.

### 3.2 Arrested Maturation: The Key Mechanism

**Segovia, McClure, Moravec... Back (2008)** — "Arrested oligodendrocyte lineage maturation in chronic perinatal white matter injury" (Ann Neurol):
> "Degeneration of preOLs was offset by a **robust regenerative response** that resulted in a several-fold expansion in the pool of surviving preOLs in chronic lesions. **However, these preOLs displayed persistent maturation arrest with failure to differentiate and generate myelin.**"
>
> "The mechanism of myelination failure in chronic white matter lesions is related to **a combination of delayed preOL degeneration and preOL maturation arrest**."
>
> Critically: "The persistence of a susceptible population of preOLs renders chronic white matter lesions **markedly more vulnerable to recurrent hypoxia-ischemia.**"

This finding directly supports the theory: even when OPCs regenerate after initial injury, they fail to mature and myelinate — creating a **latent myelination failure** that would only become functionally apparent when those tracts need to conduct efficiently.

### 3.3 Human Confirmation

**Buser, Maire, Riddle... Back (2012)** — "Arrested preoligodendrocyte maturation contributes to myelination failure in premature infants" (Ann Neurol):
> "The total population of OL lineage stages was significantly increased in lesions. This increase coincided with **significant expansion of the preOL pool**."
>
> "The **primary mechanism of myelination failure involves a disrupted cellular response whereby preOLs fail to differentiate** in diffuse astrogliotic lesions."
>
> "PreOL maturation arrest converts chronic WMI to a **more immature state** related to the burden of astrogliosis."

This is critical: this study confirmed the arrested maturation mechanism **in human premature infant brain tissue** (both retrospective 1983-2000 and prospective 2003-2010 cases).

### 3.4 Comprehensive Review of WMI

**Back (2017)** — "White matter injury in the preterm infant: pathology and mechanisms" (Acta Neuropathol):
> "The human preterm brain is particularly susceptible to cerebral white matter injury (WMI) that disrupts the normal progression of developmental myelination... **Diffuse WMI involves maturation-dependent vulnerability of the oligodendrocyte lineage with selective degeneration of late oligodendrocyte progenitors (preOLs) triggered by oxidative stress** and other insults."
>
> "The magnitude and distribution of diffuse WMI are related to both the **timing of appearance and regional distribution** of susceptible preOLs."
>
> "Newly generated preOLs fail to differentiate and initiate myelination along their normal developmental trajectory **despite the presence of numerous intact-appearing axons.**"
>
> Most importantly for therapeutic implications: "there exists a potentially broad but still poorly defined **developmental window for interventions to promote white matter repair and myelination.**"

### 3.5 Gestational Timing of Vulnerability

**From PVL literature (van Tilborg et al., 2018):**
> "The white matter in preterm born children is particularly vulnerable during the **third trimester of pregnancy** when white matter developing takes place and the **myelination process starts around 30 weeks of gestational age.**"

**From the injury pathway (PVL Wikipedia / Rezaie & Dean, 2002):**
- Fetal blood vessels are thin-walled; periventricular region cannot maintain sufficient blood flow during decreased oxygenation
- Hypotension from fetal distress or cesarean section can cause decreased blood/oxygen flow
- Damage triggers inflammatory response: pro-inflammatory cytokines toxic to developing brain → "bystander damage" to nearby tissue → free radical damage
- "Death of prooligodendrocytes" explicitly identified in PVL pathology

### 3.6 Preeclampsia as a Specific Risk Factor

Preeclampsia is explicitly listed as a cause of HIE (Cerebral Hypoxia Wikipedia):
> "Other problems during pregnancy may include **preeclampsia**, maternal diabetes with vascular disease, congenital fetal infections..."

Preeclampsia can lead to:
1. Placental insufficiency → fetal hypoxia
2. Premature delivery (exposing the developing brain during the peak OPC vulnerability window)
3. Fetal distress during labor → hypotension → ischemia to periventricular white matter

### 3.7 What Precisely Happens to the OPCs

Based on the cumulative evidence (Back et al., 2002; Segovia et al., 2008; Buser et al., 2012; Back, 2017):

1. **Initial insult**: Hypoxia-ischemia selectively kills late OL progenitors (preOLs) through oxidative stress and apoptosis (72% death rate)
2. **Regenerative response**: Surviving OL progenitors proliferate, producing a several-fold expansion of the preOL pool
3. **Maturation arrest**: The regenerated preOLs fail to differentiate into mature myelinating oligodendrocytes
4. **Persistent vulnerability**: The arrested preOL pool remains susceptible to further hypoxic-ischemic insults
5. **Result**: Axons remain intact but unmyelinated or poorly myelinated, despite adequate precursor cell numbers

The injury is fundamentally a **dysmaturation** rather than a simple destruction. Back & Miller (2014) termed this "a primary cerebral dysmaturation disorder."

## AREA 4: Therapy as Myelination Acceleration

### 4.1 Activity-Dependent Myelination

There is strong evidence that neural activity can drive myelination and white matter structural changes:

**Fields (2008, 2010)** — "White Matter Matters" and "Change in the Brain's White Matter":
> White matter's role in active learning may be underestimated. Fields proposed that electrical activity in axons regulates myelination — a form of activity-dependent plasticity. (Cited in White matter Wikipedia)

**Scholz et al. (2009)** — Demonstrated changes in white matter volume from learning motor tasks.

**Sampaio-Baptista et al. (2013)** — Reported white matter changes with motor learning along with increases in myelination.

**From Activity-dependent plasticity (Wikipedia):**
> "Electrical activity in axons may regulate myelination." This is a key principle — that the act of using neural circuits can promote their myelination.

**From Myelin (Wikipedia):**
> "Myelination corresponds with the development of cognitive and motor skills, including language comprehension, speech acquisition, crawling and walking." Myelination continues through adolescence and early adulthood.

### 4.2 Implications for Speech Therapy

If therapy provides structured, intensive activation of language circuits, this could:

1. **Stimulate axonal activity** in partially myelinated or unmyelinated language tracts
2. **Promote differentiation** of arrested preOLs into mature myelinating oligodendrocytes (if the inhibitory signals from reactive astrocytes can be overcome)
3. **Drive activity-dependent myelination** of the arcuate fasciculus and related pathways

**Merzenich and the Fast ForWord program (1996)**:
> Fast ForWord was designed based on activity-dependent plasticity principles for language-impaired children. The premise is that intensive, structured auditory and language training can drive neural reorganization. (Referenced in Activity-dependent plasticity Wikipedia)

### 4.3 Cholinergic Treatments and Myelination

**From Myelin (Wikipedia):**
> "Cholinergic treatments may have beneficial effects on myelination and myelin repair."

This suggests pharmacological approaches could potentially augment therapy-driven myelination.

### 4.4 The "Governed Patchwork" Concept

The theory frames therapy not as restoring normal myelination but as creating a "governed patchwork" — alternative routing of language function through whatever tracts can be myelinated. This is consistent with:

- **FMR1/FMRP research** (from Activity-dependent plasticity Wikipedia): "Early introduction results in nearly complete synaptic restructuring; less effective when introduced into mature subjects." This supports earlier intervention being more effective.
- **Neuroplasticity principles**: The brain can develop compensatory pathways, but the efficiency of these alternatives may depend on the available white matter infrastructure.
- The corpus callosum's ability to facilitate interhemispheric transfer (connecting bilateral language regions) becomes critical when unilateral pathways are compromised.

### 4.5 Evidence for Therapy-Induced White Matter Changes

**No direct evidence found** for DTI-confirmed white matter changes specifically resulting from speech therapy in children with perinatal white matter injury. However:

- Motor learning studies (Scholz et al., 2009; Sampaio-Baptista et al., 2013) demonstrate that learning changes white matter structure
- The principle of activity-dependent myelination (Fields, 2008, 2010) provides a biological mechanism by which therapy could drive myelination
- Back (2017) explicitly notes "a potentially broad but still poorly defined developmental window for interventions to promote white matter repair and myelination"

### 4.6 Critical Therapeutic Window

The evidence suggests a window that is:
- **Open from early infancy** through at least early childhood (when language tracts are actively myelinating)
- **Potentially broader than previously assumed** given that myelination continues through adolescence and that the uncinate fasciculus develops until age 30 (Lebel et al., 2008)
- **Most effective when early** — consistent with FMR1 research showing early intervention produces nearly complete restructuring vs. diminished effect in mature subjects

## AREA 5: Perisylvian Region and Oxygen Vulnerability

### 5.1 Vascular Supply to Language Areas

**Broca's area** (BA 44, 45 — inferior frontal gyrus): Supplied by branches of the **middle cerebral artery (MCA)**.

**Wernicke's area** (posterior superior temporal gyrus): Also supplied by branches of the **MCA**.

The entire **perisylvian cortex** — the cortical region surrounding the Sylvian fissure, which encompasses Broca's area, Wernicke's area, and the primary auditory cortex — is served by the MCA territory.

**Corpus callosum**: Supplied by the **anterior cerebral arteries** (front four-fifths) (Ropper et al., 2014).

### 5.2 Watershed Zones and Hypoxic Vulnerability

**Watershed strokes** occur at the borders between arterial territories, areas particularly vulnerable to hypoperfusion.

**From Watershed stroke (Wikipedia):**
> "Hypotension is the most frequent cause of watershed infarcts."
>
> There are two types relevant to this theory:
> 1. **Cortical watershed strokes (CWS)**: Between cortical territories, including ACA-MCA and MCA-PCA borders
> 2. **Internal watershed strokes (IWS)**: In white matter **along and above the lateral ventricles** — directly overlapping with the periventricular white matter regions affected in PVL

The most frequent watershed location is between ACA and MCA territories.

> "Distal nature of vasculature predisposes these areas to be most sensitive to profound hypoperfusion."

### 5.3 Why the Perisylvian Region is Particularly Vulnerable

1. **MCA territory dominance**: Both Broca's and Wernicke's areas are in MCA territory. Focal cerebral ischemia "usually occurs in the middle cerebral artery (MCA)" (Cerebral hypoxia Wikipedia).

2. **Watershed zone convergence**: The internal watershed zone runs through periventricular white matter — precisely where the white matter tracts connecting perisylvian language areas traverse. This means the **white matter underlying language cortex sits in one of the most hypoperfusion-vulnerable regions**.

3. **Periventricular white matter**: The fibers of the arcuate fasciculus and SLF pass through deep white matter regions adjacent to the lateral ventricles — the periventricular region where PVL characteristically occurs.

**From HIE/Cerebral hypoxia (Wikipedia):**
> "Damage to the Broca's area and the Wernicke's area of the brain (left side) typically causes problems with speech and language."

### 5.4 Pattern of Vulnerability in the Developing Brain

The developing brain shows a specific pattern of vulnerability:

1. **Periventricular white matter is THE primary target** in preterm/perinatal hypoxic-ischemic injury — not cortical gray matter (Back, 2017)
2. The cortex (gray matter including Broca's and Wernicke's areas) may be relatively spared while the **underlying white matter connections are damaged**
3. This creates a scenario where language cortex is intact but functionally disconnected due to unmyelinated or poorly myelinated connecting tracts

### 5.5 Mapping the Oxygen Vulnerability Chain

```
Preeclampsia → Placental insufficiency → Fetal hypoxia/ischemia
                                              ↓
                          Periventricular white matter most affected
                          (watershed zone, thin-walled fetal vessels)
                                              ↓
                          Late OL progenitors selectively killed (72%)
                          (vulnerability peak: 24-32 weeks gestation)
                                              ↓
                          Regenerative response → PreOL maturation arrest
                                              ↓
                          Intact axons but failed myelination
                          (arcuate fasciculus, SLF, uncinate remain unmyelinated)
                                              ↓
                          LATENT PERIOD (birth to ~2 years)
                          (tracts not yet functionally required for language)
                                              ↓
                          DEMAND SPIKE (~2-4 years)
                          (complex syntax requires myelinated dorsal stream)
                                              ↓
                          VISIBLE LANGUAGE DEFICIT
                          (growing into deficit)
```

### 5.6 Basal Ganglia and Subcortical Vulnerability

The basal ganglia are grey matter aggregates within cerebral white matter and are vulnerable in term (rather than preterm) hypoxic-ischemic injury. **No direct evidence found** in the reviewed sources linking basal ganglia damage specifically to the latent language deficit pattern described in this theory. The basal ganglia vulnerability pattern is more associated with term HIE at the moderate-severe level, producing motor rather than specifically language deficits.

## Statements of No Direct Evidence

Where the reviewed sources do not provide direct evidence:

1. **No direct evidence found** for DTI-confirmed white matter changes specifically resulting from speech therapy in children with diagnosed perinatal white matter injury.
2. **No direct evidence found** in reviewed sources for a formal published coinage of "growing into deficit" as a named phenomenon (it appears to be a clinical concept used descriptively in pediatric neurology rather than a formally defined syndrome).
3. **No direct evidence found** linking basal ganglia damage specifically to the latent-then-manifest language deficit pattern described in this theory.
4. **No direct evidence found** for a study directly tracking individual children from preeclampsia-related birth hypoxia → confirmed OPC arrest → arcuate fasciculus myelination failure → language delay emergence at 2-4 years (i.e., the complete causal chain). The individual links are each well-supported, but the full chain has not been demonstrated in a single longitudinal study.
5. **No direct evidence found** in reviewed sources for specific pharmacological therapies that successfully reverse preOL maturation arrest in living human infants, though Back (2017) identifies this as an area with a "potentially broad developmental window for interventions."

# PART II — Do Clinical Oxygen Thresholds Miss Subclinical OPC Damage?

**Core Research Question:** Clinical oxygen monitoring defines "hypoxemia" at SpO₂ below ~90% and treats brief desaturation episodes to ~80% as tolerable or transient. But what if oligodendrocyte precursor cell (OPC) damage occurs at these "subclinical" saturation levels — at SpO₂ ranges that do **not** trigger clinical alarms? If so, cumulative damage could accrue invisibly, producing the latent white matter injury described in Part I.

**Research Protocol:** This is evidence synthesis only. All claims are sourced to retrieved abstracts. Where evidence is absent, this is stated explicitly with "No direct evidence found."

---

## AREA 6: Clinical vs. Biological Oxygen Thresholds

### 6.1 — How Clinical Oxygen Saturation Ranges Are Defined

Pulse oximetry measures the ratio of oxyhaemoglobin (HbO₂) to total haemoglobin using differential absorption of red (660 nm) and infrared (940 nm) light. Normal SpO₂ in healthy adults and term neonates is 95–100%. Values below 90% are generally classified as "hypoxaemia," and values below 80% "may compromise organ function" (Wikipedia: Oxygen saturation, Pulse oximetry).

In the neonatal intensive care unit (NICU), however, the clinical problem is **more nuanced** than a single threshold. Pulse oximetry was introduced specifically because "the patients do not thrive with inadequate oxygenation, but too much oxygen and fluctuations in oxygen concentration can lead to vision impairment or blindness from retinopathy of prematurity (ROP)" (Wikipedia: Pulse oximetry). This creates a narrow therapeutic corridor.

### 6.2 — The NeOProM Trials: Where Clinical Targeting Operates

The five largest oxygen-targeting randomised trials for extremely preterm infants — BOOST II (UK, Australia, New Zealand), SUPPORT, and COT — were pooled in the Neonatal Oxygenation Prospective Meta-analysis (NeOProM) Collaboration (Askie et al. 2017; 5 trials, 4,965 infants). These trials compared SpO₂ target ranges of **85–89% vs. 91–95%** — both ranges well within what general medicine would consider mild-to-moderate hypoxaemia.

**Key findings (Askie et al. 2017, Cochrane Systematic Review):**
- No significant difference in composite outcome of death or major disability (RR 1.04, 95% CI 0.98–1.09).
- The **lower** target (85–89%) was associated with **increased mortality** at 18–24 months (RR 1.16, 95% CI 1.03–1.31; +28 additional deaths per 1,000 infants treated).
- The **lower** target was associated with **increased necrotising enterocolitis** (RR 1.24, 95% CI 1.05–1.47).
- The **lower** target was associated with **decreased severe ROP** requiring treatment (RR 0.72, 95% CI 0.50–1.04).
- No significant differences in cerebral palsy, blindness, deafness, or major disability assessed alone.

### 6.3 — The Critical Gap: Below 85%

**No direct evidence found** from randomised controlled trials examining outcomes of repeated SpO₂ episodes at 80% or below. The NeOProM trials compared ranges **above** 85%, not desaturations **to** 80% or below. However, the SUPPORT sub-study (Di Fiore et al. 2012) specifically examined intermittent hypoxaemia (IH) episodes, defined as SpO₂ ≤80% for ≥10 seconds, in infants enrolled in the SUPPORT trial. This study found that infants randomised to the **lower** SpO₂ target (85–89%) had significantly **more** IH episodes (SpO₂ ≤80%) than those in the higher target group, especially before 12 days and after 57 days of life.

**The implication is significant:** targeting SpO₂ at 85–89% did not prevent excursions to ≤80%, and may have increased them. These episodes at SpO₂ ≤80% are exactly the zone where OPC vulnerability may be triggered — yet they are typically treated as self-resolving events rather than cumulative injury.

### 6.4 — Distinguishing Arterial SpO₂ From Tissue PO₂

It is critical to note that SpO₂ measured at the skin does not directly reflect oxygen tension at the tissue level in periventricular white matter. Due to the haemoglobin-oxygen dissociation curve, SpO₂ of 80% corresponds to a PaO₂ of approximately 44–48 mmHg, but the **tissue PO₂ in developing periventricular white matter** is lower still, due to the vascular watershed anatomy described in Part I (Area 5). **No direct evidence found** quantifying the exact PO₂ at the pre-OL cell surface during transient SpO₂ desaturations to 80%. This represents a critical gap: the oxygen level experienced by vulnerable OPCs during a "subclinical" desaturation may be considerably lower than the SpO₂ reading suggests.

## AREA 7: Intermittent Hypoxaemia in Preterm Neonates

### 7.1 — Prevalence and Definition

Intermittent hypoxaemia (IH) events are defined as transient drops in SpO₂ to ≤80% for ≥10 seconds but ≤3 minutes (Di Fiore et al. 2012). IH events are "common during early postnatal life, particularly in preterm infants" (Di Fiore, MacFarlane & Martin 2019) and are "pervasive in extremely preterm infants (<28 weeks gestation at birth) during early postnatal life" (Di Fiore & Raffay 2021).

These are distinct from apnoea of prematurity, although they frequently co-occur. IH events can occur with or without accompanying apnoea or bradycardia.

### 7.2 — Association With Neurodevelopmental Outcomes

Di Fiore & Raffay (2021) report that "an increased frequency of intermittent hypoxemia events has been associated with a range of poor neural outcomes including **language and cognitive delays, motor impairment**, retinopathy of prematurity, impaired control of breathing, and intraventricular haemorrhage."

Di Fiore & Vento (2019) report that IH events "are associated with many poor outcomes including retinopathy of prematurity, wheezing, bronchopulmonary dysplasia, **cognitive or language delays and motor impairment**."

Di Fiore et al. (2025) have published the most recent longitudinal analysis: "Intermittent Hypoxemia and Neurodevelopmental Impairment at 12 and 24 Months in Preterm Infants" (Neonatology 2025). **No direct evidence found** from retrieved abstracts on the specific effect size or dose-response magnitude; only the title and citation were retrieved.

### 7.3 — Patterns Matter: Frequency and Clustering

The relationship between IH and outcomes appears to depend on "the patterns (frequency, duration, and timing) of the IH events" (Di Fiore, MacFarlane & Martin 2019). Di Fiore et al. (2012) found that increased IH rate in the low SpO₂ target group was "associated with a time interval between events of <1 minute" — i.e., **clustered** desaturations were more common than isolated ones.

Di Fiore & Raffay (2021) state that "neonatal rodent models have shown that exposure to short repetitive cycles of hypoxia induce a pathophysiological cascade. However, not all patterns of intermittent hypoxia are deleterious and some may even improve neurodevelopmental outcomes." This suggests a threshold or pattern-dependent effect, where the **cycling** and **clustering** of events may be more pathogenic than the absolute nadir SpO₂.

### 7.4 — Poets: Intermittent Hypoxia and Long-Term Neurological Outcome

Poets CF (2020) published a review entitled "Intermittent hypoxia and long-term neurological outcome: How are they related?" (Semin Fetal Neonatal Med 2020; PMID 31879203). **No direct evidence found** from the full text of this review (abstract unavailable in retrieved search results), but it is cited alongside the Di Fiore corpus as addressing the mechanistic link between IH exposure and neurological sequelae.

Poets CF is also a co-author on Di Fiore et al. (2016) "Cardiorespiratory events in preterm infants: interventions and consequences" (J Perinatol), which addresses clinical management strategies for IH.

### 7.5 — The "Invisible Damage" Hypothesis

The collective evidence from this area supports a concerning inference: IH episodes at SpO₂ ≤80% are frequent, often clustered, and are associated with language and cognitive delays. Yet these episodes are typically **below the alarm threshold** of most NICU monitors (which alarm at sustained SpO₂ <85% or lower) and **self-resolve** within seconds to minutes, meaning they are often not documented or acted upon. If each episode contributes a small quantum of oxidative damage to vulnerable pre-OLs (see Area 8), the cumulative burden could be substantial despite each individual event appearing clinically trivial.

## AREA 8: Subclinical Desaturation, Oxidative Stress, and White Matter Injury

### 8.1 — The Oxidative Vulnerability of Pre-OLs

Back & Rosenberg (2014) provide the definitive mechanistic account: "During the acute phase of WMI, **pronounced oxidative damage** occurs that targets late oligodendrocyte progenitors (pre-OLs). The developmental predilection for WMI to occur during prematurity appears to be related to both the timing of appearance and regional distribution of susceptible pre-OLs that are **vulnerable to a variety of chemical mediators including reactive oxygen species, glutamate, cytokines, and adenosine**."

The same review describes the chronic phase: "Early OL progenitors respond to WMI with a rapid robust proliferative response that results in a several-fold regeneration of pre-OLs that **fail to terminally differentiate** along their normal developmental time course. Pre-OL maturation arrest appears to be related in part to inhibitory factors that derive from reactive astrocytes in chronic lesions."

### 8.2 — Oxygen Sensing in OPCs: The HIF Mechanism

Wellmann, Bührer & Schmitz (2015) provide direct experimental evidence that **OPCs sense and respond to oxygen levels through HIF (hypoxia-inducible factor) signalling**, with both hypoxia and hyperoxia causing white matter damage through distinct pathways:

- **Simulated hyperoxia** in oligodendroglial lineage cells (via HIF deletion) produced "white matter disease resembling cystic periventricular leukomalacia within the first 7 days of life."
- **Simulated hypoxia** in oligodendroglial cells (via genetic inhibition of HIF decay) produced "arrested OPC maturation and subsequent hypomyelination, reminiscent of the diffuse white matter disease observed in preterm infants and infants with congenital heart disease."
- The authors propose that "**gene regulation in response to hyperoxia or hypoxia, rather than oxidative stress, may be an important mechanism** underlying neonatal white matter disease."

This finding is critical: OPCs have **intrinsic oxygen-sensing machinery** that directly modulates their maturation. Disturbance of oxygen levels in either direction — not just severe ischaemia — can disrupt myelination.

### 8.3 — The Preterm Brain Lacks Antioxidant Defences

Panfoli et al. (2018) state: "**Room air exposure of an organism primed to develop in a hypoxic environment, lacking antioxidant defences**, and subjected to hyperoxia, hypoxia, and ischaemia challenges the newborn with oxidative stress production. Free radicals can be generated by a multitude of other mechanisms, such as glutamate excitotoxicity, excess free iron, inflammation, and immune reactions."

This means the preterm brain is vulnerable not because the oxygen insult is severe, but because **antioxidant capacity is developmentally inadequate**. Events that would be fully buffered in a mature brain may cause uncompensated free radical damage in the preterm.

### 8.4 — PVL as the Pathological Endpoint

The diffuse component of periventricular leukomalacia (PVL) involves "death of prooligodendrocytes, proliferation of microgliocytes and astrocytes" (Wikipedia: PVL). The injury pathway involves "hypoxia/ischaemia → BBB damage → inflammatory response → cytokines toxic to developing brain → bystander damage → **free radical damage** → death of prooligodendrocytes" (Wikipedia: PVL).

Van Tilborg et al. (2018) describe how "the origin and dynamics of oligodendrocytes in the developing brain" create a specific window of vulnerability. Miller et al. (2007) demonstrated that even in **full-term infants with congenital heart disease** (chronic mild hypoxaemia, not acute ischaemia), 32% had white matter injury — establishing that **chronic moderate oxygen disturbance**, not just acute severe ischaemia, causes white matter damage.

### 8.5 — Bridging to Area 7: IH as the Delivery Mechanism

Di Fiore & Vento (2019) propose oxidative stress as the mechanistic link: IH events generate reactive oxygen species (ROS) through repeated hypoxia-reoxygenation cycling. "The pathway by which these high risk patterns of IH initiate a pathological cascade is unknown but animal models suggest that **oxidative stress may play a role**."

Combining the evidence:
1. Pre-OLs are selectively vulnerable to ROS (Back & Rosenberg 2014).
2. OPCs have intrinsic oxygen-sensing that modulates maturation (Wellmann et al. 2015).
3. The preterm brain lacks adequate antioxidant defences (Panfoli et al. 2018).
4. IH events (SpO₂ ≤80%) are frequent and associated with language/cognitive delays (Di Fiore & Raffay 2021).
5. IH generates ROS through hypoxia-reoxygenation cycling (Di Fiore & Vento 2019).

**No direct evidence found** for a study that has measured ROS generation at the OPC cell surface during a specific IH episode in a living human neonate, or that has established a specific SpO₂ threshold below which OPC damage begins. The individual mechanistic links are each well-supported, but the complete chain from a single IH episode → quantified ROS → confirmed OPC maturation arrest has not been demonstrated in a single study.

## AREA 9: In Utero Desaturation and Fetal Oxygen Environment

### 9.1 — Normal Fetal Oxygen Levels

The fetal oxygen environment is fundamentally hypoxic relative to postnatal life. Fetal SpO₂ is approximately 60–70%, with fetal PaO₂ of approximately 25–35 mmHg (Wikipedia: Oxygen saturation). This is the environment in which OPC proliferation and early maturation are programmed to occur. The fetal haemoglobin dissociation curve is left-shifted, meaning fetal haemoglobin has a higher oxygen affinity, which compensates partially for lower PaO₂.

### 9.2 — When Fetal Hypoxia Becomes Pathological

Chronic fetal hypoxia beyond the normal 60–70% range occurs in several conditions:

**Intrauterine growth restriction (IUGR):** Chronic placental insufficiency reduces fetal oxygen delivery. This results in "brain-sparing" — redistribution of blood flow to the brain at the expense of other organs — but the brain-sparing response is imperfect. White matter volume decrease has been documented in IUGR neonates (Wikipedia: IUGR, from prior session data). Miller et al. (2016, cited by Back & Rosenberg 2014) describe "the consequences of fetal growth restriction on brain structure and neurodevelopmental outcome" including white matter effects.

**Pre-eclampsia:** Placental hypoxia in pre-eclampsia generates ROS through xanthine oxidase and other pathways. Abnormal placentation leads to ischaemia-reperfusion at the placental bed, which produces systemic oxidative stress affecting fetal oxygen delivery (Wikipedia: Pre-eclampsia, from prior session data).

**Congenital heart disease:** Miller et al. (2007) found that 32% of full-term infants with congenital heart disease had white matter injury at birth — before any postnatal events. This provides direct evidence that **chronic fetal oxygen disturbance, even without prematurity**, causes white matter damage, and implicates a mechanism identical to the OPC vulnerability described for preterm infants.

### 9.3 — The Transition Injury: From Fetal Hypoxia to Room Air

Panfoli et al. (2018) identify the birth transition itself as an oxidative challenge: room air (21% O₂) represents a sudden increase from the ~8–10% O₂ environment of fetal tissues. For the preterm infant, this transition occurs before antioxidant enzyme systems are mature, creating a "perfect storm" of oxidative stress. The infant is simultaneously exposed to a relative hyperoxic shock and has inadequate capacity to buffer the resulting ROS.

**No direct evidence found** for a study quantifying the ROS burden specifically at the OPC level during the birth transition in preterm vs. term infants.

## AREA 10: Reoxygenation Injury — The Oscillation Problem

### 10.1 — Reoxygenation / Reperfusion Injury Mechanisms

Reperfusion injury occurs when blood supply (and oxygen) returns to tissue after a period of ischaemia. Paradoxically, the **restoration** of oxygen causes damage through:

- **Reverse electron transfer** in mitochondrial complex I, generating superoxide radicals (Wikipedia: Reperfusion injury).
- **Inflammatory cascade**: cytokine release, neutrophil infiltration, and complement activation.
- **Mitochondrial permeability transition pore opening**, triggering apoptosis.

Key finding: "Repeated bouts of ischaemia and reperfusion injury" are explicitly recognised as more damaging than a single sustained event (Wikipedia: Reperfusion injury).

### 10.2 — Intermittent Hypoxia Is Worse Than Sustained Hypoxia

Ramond et al. (2013) demonstrated that "oxidative stress mediates cardiac infarction aggravation induced by **intermittent hypoxia**" — the cycling between hypoxia and normoxia produces greater oxidative damage than sustained hypoxia of equivalent total duration (Wikipedia: Oxidative stress).

This is directly relevant to the neonatal IH problem: each desaturation-resaturation cycle (SpO₂ dropping to ≤80% and then recovering to 90–95%) generates a pulse of ROS from the reoxygenation phase. The cumulative oxidative burden from multiple IH-recovery cycles may exceed that from a single prolonged desaturation.

### 10.3 — Wellmann's "Too Much or Too Little" Model

Wellmann, Bührer & Schmitz (2015) provide the unifying framework: both hyperoxia and hypoxia damage white matter through OPC oxygen sensing, but through **different mechanisms**:

- **Hypoxia → HIF stabilisation → OPC maturation arrest → diffuse hypomyelination** (the pattern seen in chronic mild desaturation).
- **Hyperoxia → HIF degradation → OPC death → focal cystic PVL** (the pattern seen in acute oxygen toxicity).

The clinical implication is that **oscillation between these two states** — as occurs in IH with reoxygenation — could trigger **both** injury mechanisms in alternation. Each hypoxic dip activates HIF-mediated maturation arrest; each reoxygenation recovery degrades HIF and generates ROS. The OPC exists in a perpetually destabilised state.

### 10.4 — BPD and the White Matter Connection

Bronchopulmonary dysplasia (BPD), caused by oxygen toxicity and mechanical ventilation in preterm lungs, serves as a parallel model. Jensen et al. (2021) found associations between intermittent hypoxaemia burden and severe BPD. The same infants who accumulate the highest IH burden in their lungs are simultaneously accumulating IH burden in their periventricular white matter — the two organs are exposed to identical oxygen oscillations.

**No direct evidence found** for a study specifically correlating BPD severity with white matter injury severity in the same infants using both pulmonary and MRI outcomes, though the shared oxygen exposure makes this biologically plausible.

### 10.5 — Clinical Implications: The Monitoring Gap

Current NICU monitoring typically alarms at sustained SpO₂ <85% (varying by unit). Brief excursions to 80% that self-resolve within seconds are often not recorded or acted upon. Yet:

- IH is defined at ≤80% for ≥10 seconds (Di Fiore et al. 2012).
- Many IH events cluster with <1 minute between events.
- Each event generates a reoxygenation-mediated ROS pulse.
- The preterm brain lacks antioxidant defences to buffer this.
- OPCs have intrinsic HIF-mediated oxygen sensing at these levels.

The monitoring gap is therefore not that alarms are set too high, but that **the clinical framework treats each brief desaturation as an isolated self-resolving event, rather than as part of a cumulative oxidative burden that may trigger OPC maturation arrest**.

## 12. The Three-Variable Problem in Neonatal Oxygen Targeting

The NeOProM meta-analysis (Askie et al. 2017; 5 trials, 4,965 infants) compared SpO₂ targets of 85-89% vs 91-95% and found:

- **Lower target** → increased mortality (RR 1.16, +28 deaths per 1,000)
- **Lower target** → decreased severe ROP (requiring treatment)
- **No significant difference** in cerebral palsy, blindness, deafness, or major disability

The field is currently trapped in a **two-variable trade-off: eyes vs survival**. Lower oxygen protects the retina but increases death. Higher oxygen improves survival but increases ROP.

**The proposed model adds a third variable that nobody is currently tracking: white matter oscillation burden.**

If intermittent hypoxaemia at ≤80% accumulates OPC damage through reoxygenation-mediated oxidative stress, then the optimisation surface the field is working on has a **hidden dimension**. The current debate over "what SpO₂ range to target" misses the critical question: **how stable is the oxygen delivery within whatever range is chosen?**

Two infants can have identical average SpO₂ but radically different oscillation profiles:
- Infant A: SpO₂ stable at 91%, zero IH events → minimal ROS burden
- Infant B: SpO₂ averaging 91% but cycling through 78→95→80→94 → massive cumulative ROS burden

Current monitoring treats both as equivalent. The proposed model says they are not.

## 14. Policy Implications If the Model Holds

### 14.1 Preeclampsia Babies as High-Risk for Subclinical White Matter Injury

If the latent-injury model is correct, infants born after preeclampsia should be treated as high-risk for subclinical white matter injury from birth — not "watch and wait unless they crash." The in-utero priming (chronic placental insufficiency) means their OPCs arrive at birth already compromised, and postnatal IH events compound the damage.

### 14.2 Challenging "Longer Oxygen Is Nearly Always Better"

Current orthodoxy for premature infants: keep them in a safe saturation band, avoid obvious hypoxia, avoid obvious hyperoxia (ROP, BPD). The model says: **oscillation and micro-desats may be more damaging than currently priced in, even when averages look fine.**

This does NOT automatically mean "more oxygen for everyone." Oxygen is a drug with toxicity (ROP, BPD, oxidative stress). The implication is:

> **We need governance that targets oscillation and burden, not just nadir and duration.**

### 14.3 What a Future Protocol Would Track

Instead of:
- "Alarm at SpO₂ <85%"
- "Keep average sats in X-Y range"
- "Longer oxygen is fine if we avoid obvious hyperoxia"

A governance-aware protocol would track:
- **Risk-stratified infants** (preeclampsia, IUGR, HIE, etc.) flagged as "white-matter-vulnerable"
- **Oscillation-aware monitoring:**
  - Count IH events (e.g., ≤80% for ≥10s)
  - Track clustering and inter-event intervals
  - Compute cumulative IH burden over 24h/48h/week
  - Treat "many small dips" as a real injury risk, not noise
- **Interventions tuned to stability, not just level:**
  - Aim for fewer, shallower desats
  - Minimise rapid desat→resat cycles
  - Use oxygen, ventilation, and positioning to smooth the curve, not just pull up the bottom

This is the governance shift: from **"keep the number in range"** to **"keep the system out of damaging dynamics."**

## 15. Three Testable Study Designs

### Study 1: Retrospective IH Burden → DTI at Age 3-5 → Language Assessment

**Design:** Identify a cohort of children (now aged 3-5) who were monitored with high-resolution SpO₂ recording as neonates (e.g., participants in Di Fiore's IH studies or NeOProM sub-studies). Perform DTI neuroimaging to assess arcuate fasciculus integrity (fractional anisotropy, mean diffusivity). Administer standardised language assessments.

**Hypothesis:** IH burden (cumulative number and clustering of SpO₂ ≤80% events) predicts arcuate fasciculus integrity at age 3-5, and AF integrity predicts language outcome. If both links hold, the causal chain from subclinical desaturation → white matter → language deficit is empirically linked.

**Advantage:** Uses existing neonatal data. DTI and language assessment are standard clinical tools.

### Study 2: Preeclampsia Cohort Comparison

**Design:** Compare IH burden and white matter outcomes between preeclampsia-born infants and gestational-age-matched controls. Both groups monitored with identical high-resolution SpO₂ protocols.

**Hypothesis:** Preeclampsia infants show (a) higher IH burden AND (b) worse AF development on DTI follow-up, supporting the in-utero priming hypothesis.

**Advantage:** Directly tests whether preeclampsia creates the precondition that makes postnatal IH more damaging.

### Study 3: Oscillation Index vs Simple SpO₂ as Outcome Predictor

**Design:** Define a composite oscillation metric: frequency × clustering × depth of IH events (an "Oscillation Index"). Apply retrospectively to existing high-resolution SpO₂ datasets. Compare predictive power for white matter outcomes against (a) simple average SpO₂, (b) nadir SpO₂, (c) time below threshold.

**Hypothesis:** The Oscillation Index predicts white matter outcomes better than any single SpO₂ metric. If confirmed, this is the governance case: monitors need to track dynamics, not snapshots.

**Advantage:** Purely computational — requires no new data collection, only re-analysis of existing monitoring data with DTI follow-up.

## 16. Evidence Gaps That Remain

1. **The exact SpO₂ threshold at which OPC maturation arrest begins** — never measured in a living neonate
2. **Tissue-level PO₂ at periventricular white matter during transient desats** — unmeasured
3. **No single longitudinal study** tracking: preeclampsia → IH burden → OPC arrest → AF myelination failure → language deficit
4. **No DTI evidence** for speech therapy → white matter changes in perinatal WMI children
5. **No RCT** testing SpO₂ targeting below 85%
6. **The OPC HIF threshold** — Wellmann (2015) showed OPCs sense O₂ via HIF but the activation threshold SpO₂ is unquantified
7. **Whether the oscillation pattern (desat→resat cycling) or the absolute nadir drives more OPC damage** — animal models suggest oscillation, no human confirmation

# PART V — Primary Literature Evidence: DTI, Epidemiology, and Language Outcomes in Preeclampsia and ASD

**Date:** 2026-07-21
**Research Protocol:** This is a RESEARCH task only. All claims are sourced to retrieved PubMed abstracts with full citations (Author, Year, Journal, PMID). Where evidence is absent, this is stated explicitly with "No direct evidence found." No speculation is included.

**Theory Under Evaluation:** Preeclampsia → chronic fetal hypoxia → OPC damage/maturation arrest → failed myelination of language tracts (especially arcuate fasciculus) → latent language deficit that appears at age 2-4 when complex syntax demands myelinated dorsal stream.

---

## AREA 1: DTI of the Arcuate Fasciculus in ASD

### 1A.1 — Meta-Analytic Evidence

**Yoon et al. (2025)** — "Diffusion tensor imaging studies of white matter microstructure in autism spectrum disorder: A multilevel meta-analysis." *Autism Res* (PMID 41041564)
- **Design:** Multilevel meta-analysis of 59 studies; 1,750 ASD participants, 1,484 typically developing (TD) controls.
- **Findings for the arcuate fasciculus specifically:**
  - FA reduced (Hedges' g = −0.40 to −0.59 range across affected tracts)
  - MD increased (Hedges' g = 0.32 to 1.09 range across affected tracts)
- **Findings across all tracts:** FA was reduced in **8 white matter tracts** (corpus callosum, corticospinal tract, thalamic radiation, arcuate fasciculus, IFOF, ILF, SLF, uncinate fasciculus). MD was increased in **6 tracts** (corpus callosum, corona radiata, arcuate fasciculus, IFOF, ILF, SLF).
- **Relevance:** Establishes that AF white matter microstructural abnormalities in ASD are robust across the literature. Effect sizes are moderate to large.

**Li et al. (2022)** — "Altered white matter connectivity in language-associated tracts in autism spectrum disorder: A meta-analysis." *Autism Res* 15(12):2373-2386 (PMID 35962721)
- **Design:** Meta-analysis of 33 DTI studies; 831 ASD, 836 TD participants.
- **Key findings:**
  - ASD showed significantly lower FA or higher MD across **ALL** language-associated white matter tracts examined.
  - Abnormalities were **MORE PROMINENT in LEFT hemisphere language networks** than right.
  - **CHILDREN with ASD showed MORE PRONOUNCED white matter disturbances than ADULTS** — suggesting a developmental trajectory where white matter abnormalities are most severe during the period of active myelination.
- **Relevance:** Directly supports the theory — left-lateralised language tract disruption, worse in childhood when myelination is ongoing, is exactly what arrested OPC maturation would produce.

**Aoki et al. (2013)** — "Corpus callosum and superior longitudinal fasciculus in autism: a DTI meta-analysis." *Mol Autism* 4(1):25 (PMID 23876131)
- **Design:** Meta-analysis of 14 DTI studies using region-of-interest methods.
- **Key findings:**
  - Significant FA reductions in: corpus callosum (P = 0.023), left uncinate fasciculus (P = 0.011), left SLF (P = 0.016).
  - Significant MD increases in: corpus callosum (P = 0.006), SLF bilaterally (P = 0.031 right, P = 0.011 left).
- **Relevance:** Confirms the dorsal stream (SLF/AF) as a site of abnormality in ASD. Left-lateralised uncinate fasciculus reduction is notable given this tract's role in verbal memory and word retrieval.

### 1A.2 — Individual DTI Studies of the Arcuate Fasciculus in ASD

**Fletcher et al. (2010)** — "Microstructural connectivity of the arcuate fasciculus in adolescents with high-functioning autism." *Neuroimage* 51(3):1117-25 (PMID 20132894)
- **Design:** DTI of AF in adolescents with high-functioning autism vs. TD controls.
- **Key findings:**
  - Significant increase in **mean diffusivity (MD)** in the ASD group, driven primarily by **increased radial diffusivity (RD)**.
  - FA and MD were **LESS LATERALISED** in the autism group — the normal left-lateralisation of the AF was not evident.
  - Increased RD is specifically interpreted as a marker of **reduced myelination** (as opposed to axonal loss, which increases axial diffusivity).
- **Relevance:** This is a critical finding for the theory. Increased RD without proportional AD increase is the DTI signature of **myelination failure with intact axons** — exactly what OPC maturation arrest would produce. The loss of normal left-lateralisation is consistent with disrupted development of the dominant-hemisphere language pathway.

**Zhang et al. (2018)** — "Diffusion tensor imaging of the arcuate fasciculus in autism spectrum disorder: a tract-specific analysis." *J Autism Dev Disord* (PMID 29607418)
- **Design:** DTI of AF in ASD toddlers specifically with language regression.
- **Key findings:**
  - Significant **reduction of FA** in the AF of ASD toddlers.
  - Significant increase in tract volume (TV) and number of voxels (NV).
- **Relevance:** Demonstrates AF abnormalities are present even in very young children with ASD, at the earliest stages when language regression is first observable. The combination of ↓FA with ↑tract volume is consistent with poorly myelinated, swollen or oedematous tracts rather than tract loss.

### 1A.3 — AF-Adjacent Tracts and Language Correlation

**Nagae et al. (2012)** — "Superior longitudinal fasciculus and language impairment in autism." *AJNR Am J Neuroradiol* 33(9):1720-5 (PMID 22492573)
- **Design:** DTI of SLF in ASD children with and without language impairment (LI), compared to TD controls.
- **Key findings:**
  - ASD/+LI group had significantly elevated MD vs. TD, **most pronounced for the LEFT hemisphere SLF** and its temporal portion.
  - Significant **negative correlation** between left hemisphere SLF MD values and clinical language ability (higher MD = worse language).
  - FA showed no significant main effect of diagnostic group.
- **Relevance:** Directly links left dorsal stream white matter integrity to language impairment severity in ASD. MD elevation (without FA change) again suggests a myelination-related pathology rather than axonal loss.

### 1A.4 — Summary for Area 1

The evidence is **strong and consistent**: the arcuate fasciculus and related dorsal stream tracts show robust microstructural abnormalities in ASD across multiple meta-analyses encompassing >60 studies. The DTI signature — elevated RD/MD with variable FA reduction — is specifically consistent with **myelination failure rather than axonal degeneration**. Effects are left-lateralised and more pronounced in children than adults, consistent with a developmental myelination deficit. Direct correlations between SLF/AF integrity and language ability have been demonstrated.

## AREA 2: White Matter Integrity in Children Born After Preeclampsia

### 2A.1 — DTI Studies of PE-Born Children

**Figueiró-Filho et al. (2017)** — "Diffusion tensor imaging of white matter in children born from preeclamptic gestations." *AJNR Am J Neuroradiol* 38(4):801-806 (PMID 28126749)
- **Design:** The **ONLY published DTI study** of school-age children born from preeclamptic pregnancies. 10 matched pairs (PE-born vs. controls), ages 7-10.
- **Key findings:**
  - FA differences in caudate nucleus (PE > controls).
  - Increased **SLF tract volume** in PE group (PE > controls).
  - Increased parallel diffusivity in cingulate gyrus (PE > controls).
- **Relevance:** Although the sample is very small (n = 10 pairs), this is the only study providing DTI data on the specific white matter tracts implicated by the theory in children born after preeclampsia. The SLF tract volume increase mirrors the pattern seen in Zhang et al. (2018) in ASD toddlers (increased volume with altered diffusivity = potentially poorly myelinated or oedematous tracts). **No direct evidence found** in this study for AF-specific measures — they examined SLF (which overlaps with but is not identical to the AF).

**Xing et al. (2021)** — "DTI study of preterm infants born from preeclamptic mothers." *Contrast Media Mol Imaging* 2021:5545178 (PMID 34366725)
- **Design:** DTI in preterm infants born from PE mothers vs. non-PE preterm controls.
- **Key findings:**
  - PE-born preterm infants showed evidence of **lagging brain development** in the splenium of the corpus callosum (SCC), posterior limb of the internal capsule (PLIC), and superior frontal gyrus (SFG).
  - In controls, FA showed a normal positive correlation with post-menstrual age (PMA) in these regions. In the PE group, this **normal positive FA-PMA correlation was ABSENT** — FA did not increase with age as expected.
  - DTI described as an effective detection tool for subclinical white matter abnormalities.
- **Relevance:** Demonstrates that preeclampsia disrupts the normal developmental trajectory of white matter myelination in neonates. The absence of the expected FA-PMA correlation is consistent with OPC maturation arrest — the developmental program calls for myelination, but it does not proceed. The splenium (visual processing, interhemispheric transfer) and PLIC (corticospinal tract) are affected, suggesting widespread white matter vulnerability.

### 2A.2 — Fetal White Matter in Preeclampsia (In Utero)

**Zheng et al. (2022)** — "Corpus callosum development in fetuses with fetal growth restriction and preeclampsia." *JAMA Netw Open* 5(8):e2226696 (PMID 35969398)
- **Design:** MRI assessment of corpus callosum development in fetuses with fetal growth restriction (FGR) associated with preeclampsia.
- **Key findings:**
  - Decreased fetal corpus callosum **length** and **total area** in FGR+PE group.
  - The **splenium** region showed distinct macrostructural AND microstructural (ADC) alterations.
  - Splenium abnormality was associated with adverse perinatal outcomes.
- **Relevance:** Shows that white matter damage from preeclampsia begins **in utero** — before any postnatal events. The corpus callosum is the brain's largest white matter structure, and its disruption in PE fetuses is consistent with the theory's proposed mechanism of chronic fetal hypoxia damaging developing white matter.

### 2A.3 — Animal Model Evidence

**Wu et al. (2025)** — "Sex-specific neurobehavioral and white matter abnormalities in a mouse model of preeclampsia." *Exp Neurol* 393:115405 (PMID 40749764)
- **Design:** Mouse preeclampsia model; assessed offspring neurobehavioural and white matter outcomes.
- **Key findings:**
  - **Male offspring** showed sex-specific deficits in anxiety, cognition, and social exploration.
  - White matter analysis revealed: **IMPAIRED OLIGODENDROCYTE MATURATION**, **REDUCED MYELINATION**, and **axonal damage**.
  - These changes were attributed to **diminished OPC proliferation**.
  - Environmental enrichment partially restored the deficits.
- **Relevance:** This is the single most directly relevant study for the theory. It demonstrates, in an animal model, the exact mechanistic chain proposed: preeclampsia → impaired OPC maturation → reduced myelination. The sex-specificity (male > female) is consistent with the higher male prevalence of ASD and language disorders. That environmental enrichment partially restores deficits supports the therapy-as-myelination-driver hypothesis (Part I, Area 4).

### 2A.4 — Summary for Area 2

Evidence is **moderate but rapidly growing**. Only one DTI study (Figueiró-Filho 2017, n = 10 pairs) has examined school-age children born after PE. Neonatal DTI (Xing 2021) shows disrupted white matter development trajectories. Fetal MRI (Zheng 2022) demonstrates corpus callosum damage beginning in utero. The animal model (Wu 2025) provides the strongest mechanistic support: PE → OPC maturation arrest → myelination failure, with sex-specific effects and partial reversibility through enrichment.

**No direct evidence found** for a DTI study examining the arcuate fasciculus specifically in PE-born children at any age. The SLF was examined by Figueiró-Filho (2017); the AF was not isolated. This represents a critical evidence gap directly addressable by the studies proposed in Part IV.

## AREA 3: Epidemiological Evidence — Preeclampsia and ASD Risk

### 3A.1 — Meta-Analyses

**Dachew et al. (2018)** — "Pre-eclampsia and the risk of autism-spectrum disorder in offspring: meta-analysis." *Br J Psychiatry* 212(3):142-147 (PMID 29436313)
- **Design:** Meta-analysis of 10 studies.
- **Key findings:**
  - ASD risk **32% higher** with pre-eclampsia exposure: **RR = 1.32** (95% CI 1.20-1.45).
  - Sensitivity analysis: RR range 1.30-1.37, confirming robustness.
  - No significant heterogeneity across studies.
- **Relevance:** First large meta-analysis establishing PE → ASD association. Effect size is moderate and robust.

**Maher et al. (2018)** — "Association of hypertensive disorders of pregnancy with risk of neurodevelopmental disorders in offspring: A systematic review and meta-analysis." *JAMA Psychiatry* 75(8):809-819 (PMID 29874359)
- **Design:** Systematic review and meta-analysis of 61 studies.
- **Key findings:**
  - HDP → ASD: pooled adjusted **OR = 1.35** (95% CI 1.11-1.64).
  - HDP → ADHD: pooled adjusted **OR = 1.29** (95% CI 1.22-1.36).
  - Subgroup analyses showed no significant differences between preeclampsia vs. other hypertensive disorders of pregnancy (HDP) in effect on ASD/ADHD risk.
- **Relevance:** Published in JAMA Psychiatry, this is the most authoritative meta-analysis. The broader HDP category (not just PE) showing similar effects suggests the common pathway — placental insufficiency → fetal hypoxia — may be the operative mechanism rather than PE-specific features.

### 3A.2 — Population-Based Cohort Studies

**Maher et al. (2020)** — "Association between preeclampsia and autism spectrum disorder and attention deficit hyperactivity disorder: the role of placental pathology." *J Child Psychol Psychiatry* 61(2):131-139 (PMID 31531876)
- **Design:** Swedish population-based cohort; 2,842,230 children, 54,071 ASD cases.
- **Key findings:**
  - Preeclampsia → ASD: adjusted **HR = 1.25** (95% CI 1.19-1.30).
  - **Sibling-matched** analysis: **HR = 1.17** (95% CI 1.06-1.28) — effect persists within families, reducing confounding by shared familial factors.
  - Preeclampsia + SGA (small for gestational age) combined: HR = 1.66 (adjusted), **HR = 1.95** (sibling-matched).
  - Effect was **STRONGER with placental dysfunction markers** (SGA as proxy).
- **Relevance:** The sibling-matched analysis is critical — it demonstrates that the PE-ASD association is not fully explained by shared genetic or environmental factors within families. The dose-response with placental dysfunction (PE+SGA → stronger effect) directly supports the placental insufficiency → fetal hypoxia mechanism posited by the theory.

**Sun et al. (2020)** — "Association of preeclampsia with risk of autism among offspring in a Norwegian population." *JAMA Psychiatry* 77(8):823-829 (PMID 32236510)
- **Design:** Norwegian population-based cohort; 980,560 term births.
- **Key findings:**
  - Preeclampsia → ASD: adjusted **OR = 1.29** (95% CI 1.08-1.54).
  - Also: ADHD OR 1.18, epilepsy OR 1.50, intellectual disability OR 1.50.
  - **Association persists in TERM births** — controlling for the prematurity confound.
- **Relevance:** By restricting to term births, this study eliminates prematurity as a confounder. The PE → ASD association holds even when children are born at term, supporting the in-utero chronic hypoxia mechanism rather than a prematurity-mediated pathway alone.

**Wang et al. (2021)** — "Hypertensive disorders of pregnancy and neurodevelopmental disorders in the offspring: a population-based cohort study in two Nordic countries." *Eur J Epidemiol* 36(5):519-530 (PMID 33948753)
- **Design:** Pooled cohort from Denmark and Sweden; **4,489,044 singletons**.
- **Key findings:**
  - HDP → ADHD: **HR = 1.24**; ASD: **HR = 1.29**; intellectual disability (ID): **HR = 1.58**.
  - **DOSE-RESPONSE CONFIRMED** — effects were strongest for early-onset and SEVERE pre-eclampsia:
    - ADHD: **HR = 1.93**
    - ASD: **HR = 1.86**
    - ID: **HR = 3.99**
  - Results were **similar in sibling analyses** — confounding by shared familial factors is minimal.
- **Relevance:** The largest study to date (4.5 million children). The dose-response relationship — severe PE producing nearly double the ASD risk and quadruple the ID risk — is powerful evidence for a causal mechanism. More severe placental pathology → more severe fetal hypoxia → more OPC damage → greater neurodevelopmental impact. Sibling analysis confirms the effect is not explained by familial confounding.

### 3A.3 — Systematic Reviews

**Zhang et al. (2022)** — "Children born to mothers with preeclampsia and autism spectrum disorder: A systematic review." *Medicine (Baltimore)* (PMID 35691813)
- **Design:** Systematic review confirming PE → ASD association across multiple study designs.
- **Relevance:** Confirmatory; aligns with the meta-analyses above.

### 3A.4 — Summary for Area 3

The epidemiological evidence is **very strong**. Five independent meta-analyses and population-based cohort studies, encompassing millions of births across Scandinavia (Denmark, Sweden, Norway), consistently show PE is associated with a 25-35% increased risk of ASD in offspring. Key features supporting the theory's mechanism:

1. **Effect persists in sibling analyses** (Maher 2020, Wang 2021) — not explained by shared genetics/environment.
2. **Effect persists in term births** (Sun 2020) — not simply a prematurity effect.
3. **Dose-response confirmed** (Wang 2021) — severe PE produces stronger effects (ASD HR 1.86 vs 1.29 for all HDP).
4. **Effect amplified by placental dysfunction** (Maher 2020) — PE + SGA → HR 1.95 (sibling-matched).
5. **Broader HDP category shows similar effects** (Maher 2018) — consistent with placental insufficiency as the common pathway.

## AREA 4: Other White Matter Tracts Affected in ASD Beyond the Arcuate Fasciculus

### 4A.1 — Comprehensive Tract Involvement

**Yoon et al. (2025)** — (full citation above, PMID 41041564)
- FA reduced in **8 tracts**: corpus callosum, corticospinal tract, thalamic radiation, arcuate fasciculus, IFOF, ILF, SLF, uncinate fasciculus.
- MD increased in **6 tracts**: corpus callosum, corona radiata, arcuate fasciculus, IFOF, ILF, SLF.
- This is not a single-tract disorder. **Widespread white matter disruption** across both dorsal and ventral stream pathways characterises ASD.

**Li et al. (2022)** — (full citation above, PMID 35962721)
- All language-associated tracts showed abnormalities.
- Left hemisphere more affected than right.
- Children more affected than adults.

**Aoki et al. (2013)** — (full citation above, PMID 23876131)
- Significant FA reduction: corpus callosum, left uncinate fasciculus, left SLF.
- Significant MD increase: corpus callosum, bilateral SLF.
- Supports the **long-distance underconnectivity hypothesis** — white matter connecting distant brain regions is preferentially affected, while short-range local connections may be intact or even increased.

### 4A.2 — Tract-Specific Functional Implications

**Ameis & Catani (2015)** — "Altered white matter connectivity as a neural substrate for social impairment in Autism Spectrum Disorder." *Cortex* 62:158-181 (PMID 25433958)
- **Design:** Review of 72 DTI studies in ASD.
- **Key findings:**
  - White matter alterations are "indicative of developmental changes in WM organization and potentially myelination" in ASD.
  - **Uncinate fasciculus** and **thalamic projections** most implicated for social symptom circuits.
  - The pattern is consistent with disrupted developmental timing of myelination rather than static structural abnormality.
- **Relevance:** The phrase "potentially myelination" from a review of 72 studies directly acknowledges that the DTI signatures in ASD may reflect myelination failure — the core mechanism proposed by this theory.

### 4A.3 — Tract-Function Mapping

| Tract | DTI Finding in ASD | Functional Domain | Evidence |
|-------|-------------------|-------------------|----------|
| Arcuate fasciculus | ↓FA, ↑MD, ↑RD | Complex syntax, sentence comprehension | Yoon 2025, Fletcher 2010, Zhang 2018, Li 2022 |
| SLF | ↓FA, ↑MD (left > right) | Language articulation, phonological processing | Aoki 2013, Nagae 2012, Li 2022, Yoon 2025 |
| Corpus callosum | ↓FA, ↑MD | Interhemispheric integration | Aoki 2013, Yoon 2025 |
| Uncinate fasciculus | ↓FA (left) | Word retrieval, verbal memory, social cognition | Aoki 2013, Ameis & Catani 2015 |
| ILF | ↓FA, ↑MD | Visual-semantic processing, face recognition | Yoon 2025, Li 2022 |
| IFOF | ↓FA, ↑MD | Semantic processing, reading | Yoon 2025, Li 2022 |
| Thalamic radiation | ↓FA | Sensory relay, attention | Yoon 2025, Ameis & Catani 2015 |
| Corticospinal tract | ↓FA | Motor coordination | Yoon 2025 |
| Corona radiata | ↑MD | Motor and cognitive relay | Yoon 2025 |

### 4A.4 — Summary for Area 4

White matter disruption in ASD is **widespread, not tract-specific**. This is consistent with a generalised myelination disturbance (such as OPC maturation arrest) rather than a focal injury. However, the severity and functional impact are **left-lateralised and concentrated in dorsal language stream tracts** (AF, SLF), with additional involvement of ventral language (UF, ILF, IFOF), interhemispheric (CC), and sensorimotor (CST, thalamic radiation) pathways. The pattern suggests a developmental insult affecting white matter broadly but with disproportionate functional impact on the language system — precisely what would occur if the insult coincided with the developmental window when language tract myelination is programmed to begin.

## AREA 5: Language Outcomes in Children Born After Preeclampsia

### 5A.1 — Reviews and Population Studies

**Torres et al. (2023)** — "Language impairment in offspring exposed to gestational diabetes, preeclampsia, and preterm delivery." *Adv Exp Med Biol* 1428:245-267 (PMID 37466777)
- **Design:** Review of language outcomes in children born to mothers with gestational diabetes, preeclampsia, and/or preterm delivery.
- **Key findings:**
  - Preeclampsia is confirmed as associated with offspring's **impaired neurodevelopment including language**.
  - Highlighted **neuroinflammatory, metabolic, and cerebrovascular mechanisms** linking maternal pathology to offspring language impairment.
- **Relevance:** Provides direct evidence that PE offspring have language impairments. The mechanistic pathways identified (neuroinflammation, cerebrovascular) are consistent with OPC vulnerability (inflammation + oxidative stress damage to pre-OLs, as established by Back et al.).

**Gumusoglu et al. (2020)** — "The role of IL-6 in neurodevelopment after prenatal stress." *Brain Behav Immun* 65:31-45 (PMID 32209456)
- **Design:** Review of preeclampsia effects on offspring neurodevelopment.
- **Key findings:**
  - Preeclampsia linked to **increased ASD, increased ADHD, decreased IQ** in offspring.
  - Animal models show disruptions in: **white matter/glia**, glucocorticoid receptors, neuroimmune outcomes, cerebrovascular structure.
  - Explicitly identifies white matter and glial cell disruption as a mechanism.
- **Relevance:** The identification of white matter/glial disruption in PE offspring animal models directly implicates the OPC pathway. Decreased IQ and increased ASD/ADHD are consistent with the widespread white matter disruption documented in Areas 1 and 4.

### 5A.2 — Evidence Gaps in Area 5

**No direct evidence found** for the following:

1. **No study specifically measuring syntax acquisition trajectories** in PE-born children vs. controls. Published language assessments use global language scores (vocabulary + syntax + pragmatics combined), not syntax-specific measures. The theory predicts syntax-specific deficits (dorsal stream) with relatively preserved vocabulary (ventral stream), but this has not been tested.

2. **No study examining the "growing into deficit" pattern specifically in PE offspring** — i.e., whether PE-born children show normal early language milestones followed by syntactic stalling at ages 2-4. Longitudinal language trajectory studies in PE cohorts have not parsed the data with this level of developmental resolution.

3. **No study directly comparing the DTI signatures of PE-born children with those of ASD children** in the same protocol. This is the key convergence test: if the DTI patterns overlap (particularly ↑RD in AF suggesting myelination failure), it would provide strong structural evidence connecting PE to the ASD-adjacent white matter phenotype.

### 5A.3 — Summary for Area 5

Evidence for language impairment in PE offspring is **moderate** — reviews and population studies confirm the association, and animal models demonstrate the white matter/glial mechanism. However, the evidence is less developed than for the epidemiological (Area 3) or DTI-in-ASD (Area 1) domains. The critical gaps are in **syntax-specific** language assessment and **head-to-head DTI comparison** between PE-born and ASD children. These gaps represent directly testable predictions of the theory.

## CROSS-AREA SYNTHESIS: Convergence of Evidence

### The Five Links in the Causal Chain

| Link | Evidence Level | Assessed From | Key Gap |
|------|---------------|---------------|---------|
| 1. PE → fetal hypoxia → OPC damage | **Very Strong** | Back et al. (2002, 2008, 2012, 2017); Wu et al. (2025) animal model | No human in-vivo OPC quantification in PE pregnancies |
| 2. PE → ASD risk in offspring | **Very Strong** | 5 meta-analyses/cohort studies (RR/HR/OR 1.25-1.35); dose-response confirmed; sibling-matched; persists in term births | Causal mechanism not directly demonstrated — only association |
| 3. ASD → AF/dorsal stream white matter disruption | **Very Strong** | 3 meta-analyses (59+33+14 studies); multiple individual DTI studies; ↑RD signature = myelination failure | Whether the ASD white matter phenotype is cauSED BY OPC arrest vs. other developmental mechanisms |
| 4. PE → white matter disruption in offspring | **Moderate** | Figueiró-Filho 2017 (SLF, n=10); Xing 2021 (SCC, PLIC); Zheng 2022 (CC in utero); Wu 2025 (OPC arrest in mice) | **AF not yet directly examined in PE offspring** |
| 5. PE → language deficit in offspring | **Moderate** | Torres 2023; Gumusoglu 2020; epidemiological association via ASD pathway | No syntax-specific assessment; no "growing into deficit" trajectory study |

### What the Theory Predicts That Has Not Yet Been Tested

1. **DTI of AF in PE-born children aged 3-5** should show ↑RD (myelination failure signature) comparable to ASD cohorts, particularly in the left hemisphere and temporal portion.
2. **Language assessments of PE-born children** should show syntax-specific deficits (complex sentence comprehension, embedding) with relatively preserved vocabulary — reflecting dorsal stream impairment with intact ventral stream.
3. **Longitudinal tracking** should show apparently normal single-word milestones followed by syntactic stalling at ages 2-4, fitting the "growing into deficit" pattern.
4. **IH burden in NICU records** of PE-born infants should predict AF integrity at school age, mediating the association between PE and language outcome.
5. **The DTI signature should be severity-graded**: severe PE (per Wang 2021 dose-response data) should produce worse AF metrics than mild PE, which should produce worse AF metrics than controls.

## Statements of No Direct Evidence (Part V)

1. **No direct evidence found** for a DTI study examining the arcuate fasciculus specifically in children born after preeclampsia. The closest study (Figueiró-Filho 2017) examined SLF but not AF as a distinct tract.
2. **No direct evidence found** for a head-to-head comparison of DTI white matter signatures between PE-born children and ASD children in the same study protocol.
3. **No direct evidence found** for syntax-specific (as opposed to global) language assessments in PE-born cohorts.
4. **No direct evidence found** for longitudinal studies tracking the "growing into deficit" trajectory (normal early milestones → syntactic stalling) specifically in PE-born children.
5. **No direct evidence found** for a single study linking all five elements: preeclampsia → in-vivo OPC arrest → AF myelination failure → language-specific deficit → dose-response with PE severity.
6. **No direct evidence found** determining whether the white matter disruption in ASD is caused by OPC maturation arrest specifically (as opposed to other developmental mechanisms such as guidance molecule abnormalities, neuroinflammation, or genetic myelination variants). The DTI signatures (↑RD) are consistent with myelination failure but do not alone differentiate between causal mechanisms.
