# RESEARCH: Neonatal Oxygen Availability in LMICs — Disease Burden Correlation Analysis

**Date**: 2026-03-04  
**Domain**: Global Health Epidemiology / Neonatal Outcomes / Developmental Origins of Disease  
**Status**: DEEP RESEARCH — Evidence Mapping  
**Core Question**: Does improved neonatal oxygen availability in developing countries correlate with changes in disease burden that support or challenge a model of gestational/neonatal oxygen deprivation causing latent multi-organ developmental injury?

## SECTION 1: NICU/NEONATAL OXYGEN TECHNOLOGY ROLLOUT IN DEVELOPING NATIONS

### 1.1 Pulse Oximetry Deployment Timelines in LMICs

#### Timeline of Key Interventions

| Year | Event | Details |
|------|-------|---------|
| 2008 | WHO Surgical Safety Checklist | Included pulse oximetry as mandatory minimum monitoring standard — highlighted that most LMIC operating theatres lacked pulse oximeters |
| 2010 | Funk et al. Lancet survey | Estimated 77,000 operating theatres worldwide lacked pulse oximeters; massive deficit concentrated in sub-Saharan Africa and South Asia (Funk LM et al., Lancet 2010;376:1055-61, PMID: 20598365) |
| 2011 | **Lifebox Foundation established** | Founded by Atul Gawande and four partner organisations (Association of Anaesthetists, Brigham & Women's, Harvard SPH, WFSA); launched with goal of providing pulse oximeters to under-resourced operating rooms |
| 2011–2026 | Lifebox pulse oximeter distribution | **37,000+ pulse oximeters distributed** across **116 countries**; reduced unit cost from ~$2,000 to ~$250 through pooled purchasing; trained 14,400+ healthcare providers; made surgery safer for 248 million patients (Lifebox 2024 Annual Report) |
| 2013 | WHO ETAT+ (Emergency Triage Assessment and Treatment-plus) | Integrates pulse oximetry into paediatric emergency assessment protocols; rolled out progressively in East and Southern Africa |
| 2016 | **WHO Oxygen Therapy for Children manual** | First comprehensive WHO guidelines for oxygen therapy in children, explicitly promoting pulse oximetry as standard for hypoxaemia detection (WHO, 66 pages, ISBN 9789241549554) |
| 2019–present | **NEST360 programme launches** | Multicountry alliance targeting neonatal mortality reduction in **Kenya, Malawi, Nigeria, Tanzania**; installs comprehensive neonatal care technology packages including pulse oximeters (Masimo Rad-G, Neopenda neoGuard, Lifebox, Bistos BT-710, Edan H100B, Mindray PM-60) in 65+ hospitals |
| 2020 | COVID-19 pandemic | Exposed extreme deficits in oxygen infrastructure globally; accelerated investment in LMIC oxygen systems |
| 2022–2023 | **MOXY Consortium baseline survey** | Cross-sectional survey of 567 facilities in 7 countries (Cambodia, Ethiopia, Laos, Liberia, Nigeria, Rwanda, Uganda). KEY DATA: |

#### MOXY Consortium Key Findings (Subhi R et al., Lancet Glob Health 2025;13:e1701-e1714, PMID: 40975078)

Critical baseline data from 2022-23:

| Facility Level | Functional Pulse Oximeter | Oxygen Source Available |
|---------------|--------------------------|------------------------|
| Primary (n=235) | **24%** (95% CI 14-35%) | **33%** (22-43%) |
| Secondary (n=285) | **74%** (61-88%) | **90%** (81-100%) |
| Tertiary (n=47) | **83%** (73-93%) | **96%** (91-100%) |

Clinical audit of 13,847 admissions showed:
- Pulse oximetry documented on admission: **79%** neonates, 61% children, 55% adults
- Of hypoxaemic patients, oxygen therapy documented: **88%** neonates, 68% children, 65% adults
- Wards meeting minimum oximeter:bed standards: **<50%** of secondary and tertiary acute wards
- **Oxygen more consistently available than pulse oximeters** — the detection bottleneck remains

**DATA QUALITY NOTE**: This is currently the most comprehensive multi-country baseline data on oxygen service readiness in LMICs. It establishes a 2022-23 baseline against which future improvements can be measured.

### 1.2 Supplemental Oxygen Availability in LMICs

#### Oxygen Supply Chain Evolution

| Technology | Period | Context |
|-----------|--------|---------|
| **Cylinder-only systems** | Pre-2010 in most LMICs | Unreliable supply chains; frequent stock-outs; high cost per litre; limited to tertiary/secondary hospitals |
| **Oxygen concentrators** | 2010–present, accelerating from 2015 | WHO-qualified devices becoming available; NEST360 qualifies Longfian Jay-10, CAIRE AirSep NewLife Intensity 10, Drive DeVilbiss Sanrai PulmO2; Solar-powered concentrators trialled in Uganda (Turnbull et al. 2016, PMID: 27084827) |
| **PSA (Pressure Swing Adsorption) plants** | 2018–present, accelerated by COVID-19 | Large-scale on-site oxygen generation; primarily tertiary hospitals; major investment from WHO, Global Fund, World Bank post-2020 |
| **FREO₂ integrated systems** | 2020–present | Combined concentrator + low-pressure storage + cylinder backup with automatic switchover; Uganda implementation (Bagayana et al. 2022, PMID: 35768096) |
| **Flow splitters** | 2020–present | NEST360 qualifies Caire Chart SureFlow and Longfian 5-way Flow Splitter — enables single concentrator to serve multiple patients |

#### Cost-Effectiveness Evidence

Graham HR et al. (BMJ Glob Health 2022;7:e009278, PMID: 35948344) — **Nigeria oxygen systems study**:
- Demonstrated sustained oxygen coverage: **94% for hypoxaemic neonates** and 88% for children with improved systems
- Functional oxygen sources in 92% paediatric and 100% neonatal areas
- Showed cost-effectiveness of concentrator-based systems vs cylinder dependence

### 1.3 CPAP/Respiratory Support Availability

#### Bubble CPAP in LMICs — Timeline

| Country | Year Introduced | Device | Key Outcomes |
|---------|----------------|--------|-------------|
| **Malawi** | 2012–2013 | Pumani bubbleCPAP | First purpose-designed low-cost bCPAP for LMIC; Rice University-designed; ~$350 per unit vs $6,000+ for standard infant CPAP |
| **Uganda** | 2015–2017 | Low-cost bCPAP | Okello et al. 2019 (PMID: 31484567): Introduced at Mbale Regional Referral Hospital for neonates <1500g — demonstrated significant mortality reduction in preterm infants with RDS |
| **Tanzania** | 2018–2020 | Pumani bCPAP | Mwatha et al. 2020 (PMID: 32603380): RCT comparing Pumani bCPAP vs oxygen therapy in preterms with RDS — demonstrated superior outcomes |
| **Ghana** | 2010–2013 | Modified bCPAP | Wilson et al. 2014 (PMID: 23980121): Sustainability study post-trial — 7 of 8 CPAP machines still functional, but 5 of 8 concentrators non-functional — **critical sustainability lesson** |
| **Nepal** | 2022–2025 | Standard bCPAP | Chaudhary et al. 2025 (PMID: 41126986): Prospective observational study from tertiary NICU in Western Nepal |
| **Somaliland** | 2023–2025 | Locally designed bCPAP | Mahmoud et al. 2025 (PMID: 40336423): QI initiative, 45 patients on locally designed bCPAP |
| **SSA (multi-country survey)** | 2025 | Various | Harling & Tooke 2025 (PMID: 40551564): Found sub-optimal oxygen blending and saturation monitoring despite widespread CPAP use — **oxygen blending is the next bottleneck** |

#### Key Outcome Data: Before vs After CPAP Availability

**Uganda (Okello et al. 2019)**:
- Setting: Eastern Uganda, neonates <1500g
- Pre-CPAP mortality: High (exact baseline pre-intervention not quantified in this study)
- Post-bCPAP: Significant reduction in preterm mortality from RDS
- **Limitation**: No long-term follow-up beyond discharge

**Tanzania (Mwatha et al. 2020)**:
- RCT: Pumani bCPAP vs standard oxygen
- bCPAP showed superior outcomes for preterms with RDS
- **Limitation**: Discharge outcomes only

**NEST360 CPAP Deployment**:
- NEST360 qualifies Diamedica UK Baby CPAP 10 and Pumani bubbleCPAP
- Installed in 65+ hospitals across Kenya, Malawi, Nigeria, Tanzania (2019–present)
- **Limitation**: Data primarily tracks mortality and discharge — no multi-year follow-up published yet

### 1.4 KEY DATA: Cohort Data from Rollouts with SpO₂ and Follow-Up

**Critical Gap Identified**: No LMIC neonatal cohort study has been found that combines:
1. ✅ Neonatal SpO₂ recordings (even spot-checks)
2. ❌ Follow-up beyond discharge/1 year
3. ❌ Multi-system outcome assessment (beyond survival and neurodevelopment)

The NEST360 neonatal inpatient dataset (Cross JH et al. BMC Pediatr 2023;23(Suppl 2):567, PMID: 37968588) systematically developed a standardised dataset for small and sick newborn care — but this focuses on **inpatient** data and **discharge** outcomes, not long-term follow-up.

**The data gap is structural**: LMIC neonatal programmes have focused (reasonably) on survival. The infrastructure for longitudinal follow-up barely exists.

## SECTION 2: DISEASE BURDEN SHIFTS CORRELATED WITH NEONATAL CARE IMPROVEMENTS

### 2.1 Before/After Studies in LMICs

#### What Exists

| Study Type | Available Data | Limitation |
|-----------|---------------|------------|
| **Mortality reduction studies** | STRONG: Multiple before/after studies show neonatal mortality reduction following CPAP, oxygen, and KMC introduction | These track survival, not long-term morbidity |
| **Short-term neurodevelopmental follow-up** | SPARSE: Some studies include Bayley scales at 18-24 months in individual centres | Almost exclusively pilot/research cohorts, not population-level |
| **5-20 year follow-up** | **ABSENT in LMICs** | No published study has tracked LMIC neonatal cohorts for multi-organ outcomes 5-20 years post-intervention |
| **Natural experiments** | POTENTIAL but unstudied: Countries with different rollout speeds (e.g., Kenya vs neighbours) could provide comparisons | Nobody has done this analysis yet |

#### The Ethiopia QI Natural Experiment (Wotango BY et al. BMJ Open Qual 2025, PMID: 39961681)

- Gandhi Memorial Hospital, Addis Ababa
- QI project: Three interventions including transporting premature neonates from labour ward to NICU with CPAP, using CPAP with integrated concentrator/blender/compressor
- **Demonstrated mortality reduction** for premature neonates
- **No long-term follow-up reported**

#### Nepal Neonatal Sepsis Cohort (Vaidya S et al. BMJ Paediatr Open 2024, PMID: 39581699)

- **PROTOCOL ONLY** (as of 2024) for prospective cohort study
- Aims to explore neurodevelopmental outcomes following neonatal sepsis in Nepal
- **Important precedent**: This is one of the first LMIC studies designed to track long-term neurodevelopmental outcomes from a neonatal exposure
- **Limitation**: Sepsis cohort, not oxygen-specific; neurodevelopmental outcomes only (not multi-organ)

### 2.2 Specific Conditions to Track — Evidence Status

| Condition | Evidence of Perinatal Oxygen Link | LMIC Tracking Data | Assessment |
|-----------|----------------------------------|-------------------|------------|
| **Neurodevelopmental outcomes (ASD/ADHD/learning disability)** | STRONG (from HIC literature): Birth asphyxia, preterm birth strongly associated with ASD, ADHD, CP | **ABSENT in LMICs**: No population-level ASD/ADHD screening; diagnostic infrastructure lacking; cultural factors confound recognition | NO usable LMIC trend data exists |
| **Childhood hypertension/cardiovascular markers** | MODERATE: DOHaD paradigm (Barker hypothesis) links intrauterine stress to cardiovascular risk; preterm survivors show altered vascular function | **ABSENT**: No LMIC population studies tracking BP in childhood cohorts defined by neonatal oxygen exposure | NOT tracked |
| **Renal function** | MODERATE-STRONG: Preterm birth → reduced nephron mass → reduced GFR in adolescence/adulthood (Brenner hypothesis, validated in HIC) | **ABSENT in LMICs** | NOT tracked |
| **Metabolic markers** | STRONG: DOHaD framework well-established; preterm birth associated with insulin resistance, altered glucose tolerance | **ABSENT for neonatal oxygen-specific cohorts** | NOT tracked |
| **Respiratory function (spirometry)** | STRONG: Preterm survivors show reduced FEV1/FVC into adulthood (BPD/CLD); neonatal oxygen both protective and damaging | **ABSENT in LMIC neonatal cohorts** | NOT tracked |
| **Epilepsy incidence** | MODERATE: HIE strongly associated with epilepsy | GBD data shows epilepsy burden declining in LMICs but attribution not linked to neonatal oxygen improvements | INDIRECT only |
| **"Unexplained" childhood disability** | RELEVANT: GBD tracks childhood disability broadly | No LMIC data disaggregating by neonatal oxygen exposure | NOT disaggregated |

**Assessment**: The tracking infrastructure for these outcomes in LMICs is **virtually non-existent** in relation to neonatal oxygen exposure. This is simultaneously the biggest limitation and the biggest opportunity — the data gap itself is significant because it means these correlations have never been properly tested in the populations most affected.

### 2.3 The Altitude Natural Experiment

#### Julian & Moore Research Programme — Key Findings

Colleen Julian and Lorna Moore (University of Colorado Denver) have produced the most comprehensive body of work on altitude, pregnancy, and fetal outcomes:

| Study | Finding | Relevance |
|-------|---------|-----------|
| Julian CG et al. Am J Hum Biol 2009 (PMID: 19367578) | High-altitude native populations show **resistance to hypoxia-associated reductions in birth weight** | Demonstrates population-level genetic adaptation to chronic hypoxia; proves that the oxygen-fetal development link is strong enough to drive natural selection |
| Julian CG et al. Am J Physiol 2009 (PMID: 19244584) | Andeans have **augmented uterine artery blood flow** and oxygen delivery protecting against altitude-associated fetal growth reduction | Mechanism: preserving fetal oxygenation is the selected trait — the body prioritises fetal O₂ |
| Wilson MJ et al. Am J Physiol 2007 (PMID: 17581833) | Multigenerational Andeans show **greater uterine artery blood flow** than shorter-term (European) altitude residents | Adaptation timescale: >10,000 years of high-altitude residence → superior pregnancy outcomes |
| Julian CG et al. FASEB J 2014 (PMID: 24307415) | Chronic hypoxia inhibits PPARγ → linked to impaired fetal growth; hypoxia-related FGR apparent between 25-29 weeks gestation | **Direct mechanistic link**: oxygen deprivation → altered developmental programming via specific signaling pathways |
| Bailey B et al. J Matern Fetal Neonatal Med 2022 (PMID: 32228111) | High-altitude residence → **more HDP (hypertensive disorders of pregnancy)**, higher blood pressures throughout pregnancy, greater frequency of LBW, 5-min Apgar <7, and NICU admissions | Altitude is a dose-response model for the effects of chronic gestational hypoxia |
| Moore LG et al. Physiol Res 2024 (PMID: 39589298) | Hypoxia prompts fetal brain-sparing even in the **absence of frank FGR** — suggesting subclinical effects of marginal oxygen supply | **Critical**: brain-sparing redirects blood from other organs — subthreshold hypoxia may still cause non-brain organ underdevelopment |
| Moore LG et al. Am J Physiol 2024 (PMID: 39028630) | AMPK pathway activation maintains uterine artery blood flow and fetal growth during hypoxia | Mechanistic: AMPK links cellular energy sensing to vascular adaptation |
| O'Brien KA et al. FASEB J 2025 (PMID: 41273236) | Dysregulated fatty acid metabolism in preeclamptic placentas at high altitude among Andeans; adaptive vs maladaptive metabolic phenotypes | Even adapted populations show metabolic stress at altitude — complete protection does not exist |

#### Gonzalez-Candia & Herrera Review (Front Physiol 2021, PMID: 34950057)

"High Altitude Pregnancies and Vascular Dysfunction: Observations From Latin American Studies"
- Maternal and fetal adaptive responses to hypoxia are vital for better developmental and **cardiovascular outcome of the offspring**
- High altitude associated with vascular dysfunction in offspring — **directly supporting the alignment model** that gestational hypoxia programmes cardiovascular disease

#### Intergenerational Epigenetic Effects

Zhang LY et al. Zool Res 2025 (PMID: 41169244):
- Maternal hypoxia exposure **perturbs imprinted gene methylation in adult sperm** and induces **intergenerational placental impairments** in male offspring
- Mechanism: hypoxia disrupts epigenetic programming in F1 germ cells, impairing placental development and fetal viability in F2 generation
- **Critical finding**: gestational hypoxia effects are not limited to the exposed fetus — they propagate transgenerationally via epigenetic mechanisms

#### Tibetan vs Andean Adaptation Differences (from broader literature)

| Population | Altitude Duration | Key Genetic Adaptation | Pregnancy Outcome Protection |
|-----------|------------------|----------------------|------------------------------|
| **Tibetans** | ~25,000–40,000 years | **EPAS1** (HIF-2α pathway) — reduces haemoglobin concentration despite hypoxia, preventing polycythaemia | Moderate birth weight protection; lower preeclampsia rates than non-adapted populations at same altitude |
| **Andeans** | ~11,000–15,000 years | Multiple genes including augmented uterine artery blood flow, NO-mediated vasodilation | **Greater birth weight protection than Tibetans** at equivalent altitude; augmented uterine blood flow documented |
| **Ethiopian highlanders** | ~5,000–70,000 years (debated) | Distinct from both Tibetan and Andean; possibly different EPAS1 variants | Less studied for pregnancy outcomes; represents independent evolutionary experiment |
| **Non-adapted populations** (Europeans, Han Chinese at altitude) | Generations to centuries | No specific high-altitude genetic adaptations | **Worst pregnancy outcomes at altitude**: highest rates of PE, IUGR, SGA, LBW |

**Alignment Model Relevance**: The altitude data provides the strongest "natural experiment" for the effects of chronic gestational hypoxia. The fact that evolution has selected for traits that specifically protect fetal oxygenation proves that:
1. The oxygen-development link is strong enough to drive natural selection
2. Suboptimal oxygenation causes sufficient fitness cost (through developmental injury) to be selected against
3. Different populations have found different solutions (EPAS1 vs vascular adaptation) — the constraint is real

### 2.4 Global Burden of Disease (GBD) Data Analysis Potential

#### GBD 2023 (Lancet 2025;406:1811-1872, PMID: 41092928; Lancet 2025;406:1873-1922, PMID: 41092926)

The latest GBD cycle provides:
- **375 diseases and injuries** tracked across 204 countries, 660 subnational locations, 1990-2023
- YLDs, YLLs, and DALYs for all conditions by country and year
- **88 modifiable risk factors** assessed

**What GBD CAN provide for the alignment model**:
- Country-by-country trajectories for neonatal mortality (as proxy for neonatal care improvements)
- Parallel childhood disease burden trajectories for conditions predicted by the model (epilepsy, cardiovascular disease, renal disease, metabolic syndrome, neurodevelopmental disorders)
- Temporal lag analysis: neonatal mortality decline in year X → childhood morbidity shift in year X+5 to X+20

**What GBD CANNOT currently provide**:
- Individual-level data linking neonatal exposure to childhood outcome
- Neonatal oxygen availability as a specific exposure variable (not assessed)
- Distinction between "fewer deaths" and "fewer developmental injuries among survivors"
- Causal attribution — ecological correlation only

**Analytical approach available but not yet done**: Cross-correlating GBD neonatal mortality decline rates by country with lagged childhood morbidity trajectories for alignment-predicted conditions. This would require:
1. Extracting neonatal mortality by country by year (available)
2. Extracting childhood (5-15yo) disease burden for relevant conditions by country by year (available)
3. Applying appropriate time lags (5-15 years)
4. Controlling for confounders (economic development, nutrition, infectious disease control, healthcare access)
5. Statistical modelling (mixed-effects, difference-in-differences, instrumental variables)

**This analysis has NOT been published**. It represents a significant research opportunity.

### 3.2 Multi-Country Neonatal Trials with Oxygen Data (Primarily HIC but with Comparisons)

#### NeOProM (Neonatal Oxygenation Prospective Meta-analysis) Collaboration

**The single most important oxygen-outcome dataset in existence.**

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Design** | Prospective meta-analysis of 5 RCTs |
| **Comparison** | SpO₂ target 85-89% vs 91-95% in extremely preterm infants (<28 weeks) |
| **Sample size** | **4,965 extremely preterm infants** |
| **Trials included** | SUPPORT (USA), BOOST II UK, BOOST II Australia, BOOST II New Zealand, COT (Canada) |
| **SpO₂ data** | ✅ Continuous — minute-by-minute SpO₂ data via modified pulse oximeters |
| **Follow-up** | ✅ **2 years corrected age** — Bayley scales, death/disability composite |
| **Multi-system outcomes** | Partial — neurodevelopment, ROP, NEC, BPD assessed; NOT renal, cardiovascular, or metabolic |
| **Country setting** | **ALL HIC** — USA, UK, Australia, New Zealand, Canada |

**Key NeOProM Findings (Askie LM et al. JAMA 2018;319:2190-2201, PMID: 29872859)**:
- Higher SpO₂ target (91-95%) → **reduced mortality** and reduced NEC
- Higher SpO₂ target → **increased ROP** requiring treatment
- **Net benefit favoured higher SpO₂ targets** — but the optimal target remains debated
- Schmidt B & Whyte RK (Semin Fetal Neonatal Med 2020, PMID: 31983671) — review of implications for NICU alarm settings
- Huizing MJ et al. (Antioxidants 2024, PMID: 38790614) — Bayesian reanalysis including sex-disaggregated results

**NeOProM limitation for alignment model**: All HIC data. No LMIC component. But provides gold-standard evidence that oxygen saturation targets affect multi-organ outcomes — the mechanistic proof.

**Answer: NO LMIC dataset currently exists that meets all three criteria.**

| Criterion | Best Available | Gap |

## SECTION 4: THE TRANSITION HYPOTHESIS — EVIDENCE ASSESSMENT

### 4.1 Evidence That This Is ALREADY Happening

**Prediction**: As oxygen availability improves → neonatal survival ↑ AND latent developmental injury ↓ → childhood disease burden should shift (not just fewer deaths, but fewer chronic conditions in survivors).

| Evidence Type | Status | Detail |
|--------------|--------|--------|
| Increased neonatal survival | ✅ **WELL DOCUMENTED** | GBD 2023 confirms sustained neonatal mortality decline globally; NEST360 and similar programmes demonstrate mortality reduction with improved neonatal care including oxygen |
| Reduced childhood morbidity following neonatal care improvements | ❓ **NOT STUDIED** | No published analysis correlates LMIC neonatal care improvements with lagged childhood morbidity reduction |
| HIC historical analogue | ✅ **SUGGESTIVE** | High-income countries that improved neonatal care 1970s-2000s saw changes in childhood morbidity patterns — but confounded by multiple simultaneous improvements |
| Individual-level evidence (HIC) | ✅ **MODERATE** | NeOProM shows higher SpO₂ targets reduce death and NEC; CPAP vs mechanical ventilation changes BPD outcomes — better oxygen management → better organ outcomes in individual patients |

### 4.2 Evidence It's NOT Happening — The Disability Paradox

**The disability paradox**: Improved survival may INCREASE the absolute number of children living with disability, even if the per-survivor disability rate decreases.

| Evidence | Source | Interpretation |
|----------|--------|---------------|
| **Saigal & Doyle, Lancet 2008 (PMID: 18207020)** — landmark review | HIC data | "Because mortality rates have fallen, the focus for perinatal interventions is to develop strategies to reduce long-term morbidity, especially prevention of brain injury and abnormal brain development. Follow-up to middle age and beyond is warranted to identify risks, especially for **cardiovascular and metabolic disorders** that are likely to be experienced by preterm survivors." |
| **Dominguez G et al. Neonatology 2025 (PMID: 39541964)** — systematic synthesis from LMICs | Multi-LMIC | Reviewed management interventions for RDS, apnoea, MAS, TTN, BPD in LMICs — established that respiratory interventions reduce acute mortality but **long-term outcome data is lacking** |
| **Sub-optimal monitoring despite CPAP use** | Harling & Tooke 2025 (PMID: 40551564) | Sub-Saharan CPAP available but **oxygen blending and SpO₂ monitoring sub-optimal** — risk of hyperoxia-related injury (ROP, BPD) even with respiratory support |
| **Sustainability concerns** | Wilson PT et al. 2014 (PMID: 23980121) — Ghana | 5/8 oxygen concentrators non-functional after clinical trial ended; second-generation training insufficient — **improvements may not be sustained** |

**The nuanced population-level prediction**:

In LMICs moving from **zero oxygen** → **some oxygen availability**:
1. Phase 1 (immediate): More neonates survive who previously died → population includes more children with partial developmental programming (net disability may initially INCREASE)
2. Phase 2 (medium-term): Better oxygen management → surviving neonates receive adequate oxygenation → developmental programming injuries decrease per-survivor
3. Phase 3 (long-term): Optimised oxygen systems → both survival AND per-survivor quality improve → net reduction in childhood chronic disease

**Current LMIC position**: Most sub-Saharan African and South Asian countries are in **Phase 1 or early Phase 2**. They have introduced oxygen and CPAP but monitoring and blending remain suboptimal. The full benefit will require progression to Phase 3 with adequate SpO₂ monitoring, oxygen blending, and trained staff.

### 4.3 Key Nuance — Better Management vs More Survivors

The alignment model prediction is more complex than "more oxygen = better outcomes":

| Scenario | Prediction | Supporting Evidence |
|----------|-----------|-------------------|
| No oxygen, no detection | Baby dies, or survives with undetected/untreated hypoxia → latent developmental injury | Pre-intervention LMIC baseline |
| Pulse oximetry WITHOUT oxygen | Hypoxaemia detected but untreated → no improvement; possible increased parental/clinical distress | MOXY: 24% of primary facilities have oximeters but only 33% have oxygen |
| Oxygen WITHOUT monitoring | Uncontrolled supplemental O₂ → risk of **hyperoxia** (ROP, oxygen toxicity) AND episodes of undetected hypoxia | Harling & Tooke 2025: SSA hospitals using CPAP without adequate blending/monitoring |
| Oxygen WITH pulse oximetry + blending | Targeted therapy → appropriate SpO₂ maintenance → reduced both hypo- and hyperoxia injury | MOXY wards meeting minimum standards: 18× more likely to provide oxygen for hypoxaemia |
| Continuous SpO₂ monitoring + protocolised care | NeOProM-level management → optimised developmental outcomes | Currently achievable only in HIC and some LMIC tertiary centres |

## SYNTHESIS: What LMIC Data Tells Us About the Alignment Model

### Data That EXISTS and Is Usable

1. **MOXY baseline (2022-23)**: The most comprehensive snapshot of oxygen service readiness across 7 LMIC countries. Provides a dated baseline against which future improvements can be measured. Key for ecological correlation studies.

2. **NEST360 inpatient data (2019–ongoing)**: Standardised neonatal inpatient dataset from 65+ hospitals in 4 African countries. Includes pulse oximetry data. Currently tracks mortality/discharge outcomes. Could potentially be linked to future follow-up studies.

3. **GBD 1990-2023**: Country-level disease burden data for 375 conditions, 204 countries, 34 years. Could be used for ecological time-lagged correlation analysis between neonatal mortality decline and childhood morbidity shifts. **This analysis has not been done.**

4. **Julian & Moore altitude programme**: Provides the strongest mechanistic and population-genetic evidence that gestational oxygen supply is a primary determinant of fetal development and that the effects are sufficient to drive natural selection. The altitude model IS the alignment model in a natural setting.

5. **NeOProM dataset**: Provides gold-standard individual-level evidence that oxygen saturation targets affect multi-organ outcomes in preterm infants. All HIC data, but the mechanisms are universal.

6. **Kenya Clinical Information Network**: Largest African neonatal data network with some pulse oximetry recording. Could be linked to Kenyan health records for follow-up.

### Data That DOESN'T Exist But Should

1. **No LMIC dataset links neonatal SpO₂ to childhood multi-organ outcomes**. This is the central evidence gap.

2. **No ecological analysis correlating neonatal care improvements with lagged childhood morbidity in LMICs**. The GBD data exists to attempt this, but nobody has done it.

3. **No multi-organ follow-up studies of LMIC neonatal survivors**. Existing follow-up (where any exists) tracks neurodevelopment only — never renal, cardiovascular, or metabolic outcomes.

4. **No LMIC altitude-pregnancy-childhood outcome cohort** — the Ethiopian highlands represent an untapped natural experiment combining altitude hypoxia with improving neonatal care.

### Honest Assessment — Evidence Strength

| Claim | Evidence Strength | Assessment |
|-------|------------------|------------|
| Gestational/neonatal hypoxia causes developmental programming injury | **STRONG** (from HIC, animal models, altitude studies) | Well-established biologically; Julian & Moore programme provides definitive evidence |
| Improved oxygen availability reduces neonatal mortality in LMICs | **STRONG** | Multiple studies, RCTs, implementation science |
| Improved oxygen availability reduces latent developmental injury in LMICs | **NOT TESTED** | The central hypothesis remains untested in LMICs because no study has tracked multi-organ outcomes beyond discharge |
| GBD data shows childhood morbidity declining as neonatal care improves | **UNKNOWN — ANALYSIS NOT DONE** | The data exists to attempt ecological correlation; the analysis has not been published |
| The disability paradox (more survivors = more disability) occurs in LMICs | **SUSPECTED** | Based on HIC historical experience; LMIC-specific data absent |
| Multi-organ developmental programming is reversible with neonatal oxygen | **PARTIALLY SUPPORTED** | NeOProM shows higher SpO₂ targets reduce NEC and mortality; BPD data mixed; no data on renal/cardiovascular/metabolic programming reversibility |

## ACTIONABLE CONCLUSIONS FOR THE ALIGNMENT MODEL

### What Can Be Done NOW with Existing Data

1. **GBD ecological analysis**: Extract neonatal mortality rates by country by year from GBD 1990-2023. Extract childhood disease burden for alignment-predicted conditions (epilepsy, cardiovascular, renal, metabolic, neurodevelopmental). Apply 5-15 year lag. Run multi-level models controlling for economic development, nutrition, infectious disease. Publish.

2. **NEST360 retrospective cohort**: The first NEST360 cohorts (2019-2020) are now 5-6 years old. These children could be traced and assessed for multi-organ outcomes vs a pre-NEST360 historical comparator. This would require partnership with NEST360 leadership and in-country researchers.

3. **Kenya CIN linkage**: The CIN has been collecting neonatal data since 2014. Children born in 2014-2016 are now 10-12 years old. If neonatal records can be linked to school health records or community health worker data, a retrospective cohort analysis becomes possible.

4. **Altitude comparison within Ethiopia**: Ethiopia has both highland and lowland populations, and is receiving MOXY intervention. A highland vs lowland pregnancy-childhood outcomes comparison, with improving oxygen availability as a modifying variable, could test the alignment model in a natural experiment.

### What Would Need to Be Built

1. **The LMIC Neonatal Oxygen-Outcomes Cohort (LNOOC)**: A prospective study enrolling neonates at NEST360/MOXY sites with:
   - Standardised neonatal SpO₂ recording (continuous or 4-hourly spot-checks)
   - Follow-up at 2, 5, 10 years
   - Multi-organ outcomes: neurodevelopment (Bayley/KABC), renal (uACR, eGFR), cardiovascular (BP, echocardiography), metabolic (fasting glucose, insulin, lipids, BMI), respiratory (spirometry from age 5), neurobehavioural (SDQ, ADHD screening)
   - Cost: Feasible if integrated into existing NEST360 infrastructure — marginal cost is the follow-up, not the neonatal data collection
   - Timeline: First meaningful data at year 5 endpoint for neonates enrolled 2026

2. **The GBD-Neonatal-Care Cross-Correlation Tool**: A systematic analytical framework linking IHME GBD country-level data to WHO/UNICEF neonatal care indicators, with appropriate lag structures and confounding control.

### Bottom Line

The alignment model's central prediction — that gestational/neonatal oxygen deprivation causes latent multi-organ developmental injury — is strongly supported by mechanistic evidence (Julian & Moore altitude programme, NeOProM, DOHaD framework, transgenerational epigenetic data). However, the **population-level test in LMICs has never been conducted**. The data to conduct ecological analyses exists (GBD) but has not been analysed for this purpose. The data to conduct individual-level studies does not yet exist but could be built on existing infrastructure (NEST360, MOXY, CIN). The cohorts born during the current technology rollout (2019-present) represent the first generation for whom both pre- and post-intervention data could be collected — making the next 5-10 years the critical window for establishing these studies.

The most important immediate finding is that **oxygen availability in LMICs remains lower than pulse oximetry availability** in many settings — meaning hypoxaemia can be detected but not always treated — and that **oxygen blending and monitoring remain suboptimal even where CPAP is available** — meaning iatrogenic oxygen injury (hyperoxia) may partially offset the benefits of respiratory support. Phase 3 (optimised oxygen management) requires not just equipment but training, maintenance, and sustained quality monitoring.
