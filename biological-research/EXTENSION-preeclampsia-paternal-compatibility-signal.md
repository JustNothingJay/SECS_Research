## Abstract

The gestational oxygen-timing alignment model identifies placental insufficiency as the upstream determinant of fetal organ-specific injury, with preeclampsia (PE) as the dominant clinical expression of placental failure. Extension Track 6 catalogues the *triggers* of PE — defective spiral artery remodelling, KIR/HLA-C mismatch, immune priming failure — as disparate mechanisms converging on a common cascade. This extension reframes the central question: **preeclampsia may not originate primarily from the mother. It may originate primarily from the sperm.** The primipaternity effect, the "preeclampsia-prone father" observation, the donor gamete risk amplification, the seminal fluid priming requirement, and the KIR/HLA-C interaction all point toward a single conclusion: PE is a compatibility signal — the maternal immune system's assessment that the feto-placental unit constructed from this specific paternal contribution is insufficiently compatible to permit full trophoblast invasion. This document synthesises evidence from reproductive immunology, evolutionary biology, MHC/HLA diversity theory, and parent-offspring conflict to propose that preeclampsia is not a maternal disease but a **mate-pair incompatibility phenotype** with the paternal genome as the dominant variable. If correct, this reframing has profound implications: PE risk is predictable from paternal genotype before conception, the "best" reproductive matches are those where paternal and maternal HLA profiles complement rather than duplicate each other, and offspring of complementary pairings are less likely to inherit the narrow immune repertoires that perpetuate PE risk across generations.

## 1. Introduction

### 1.1 The Conventional Framing

Preeclampsia is conventionally classified as a maternal hypertensive disorder — a disease of pregnancy, managed by obstetricians, understood as a maternal condition that happens to affect the fetus. The clinical focus is on the mother: her blood pressure, her proteinuria, her organ function, her magnesium sulphate, her delivery timing.

This framing is understandable — the mother presents with symptoms, and the mother is at immediate risk. But it obscures the most important question: **why does this pregnancy trigger preeclampsia when the same woman's previous pregnancy (with a different partner) did not?**

### 1.2 The Evidence That PE Comes from the Sperm

Track 6 (Carpenter 2026f) catalogues the evidence in mechanistic terms. Here, it is restated as a single convergent argument:

| Observation | What It Tells Us |
|---|---|
| **Primipaternity effect** — PE risk resets with partner change | Risk is partner-pair specific, not woman-specific |
| **Seminal fluid priming** — longer sexual cohabitation before conception reduces PE risk | The maternal immune system must learn tolerance for *this specific partner's* antigens |
| **Barrier contraception** — condom use throughout relationship increases PE risk | Physical prevention of antigen exposure → immune tolerance failure |
| **Donor gamete pregnancies** — 20–40% PE rate even in multiparous women | Total novelty of paternal antigens = maximum immune rejection risk |
| **"Preeclampsia-prone fathers"** — a man whose prior partner had PE confers elevated risk to his new partner | Something about *his* genetic contribution — not hers — drives PE |
| **Paternal family history** — men whose mothers had PE have partners with higher PE risk | The PE-prone genotype transmits through the *paternal* line |
| **KIR/HLA-C interaction** — maternal KIR-AA + fetal HLA-C2 = highest risk | The fetal HLA-C2 is *inherited from the father* — the incompatibility is determined at conception |
| **Imprinted placental genes** — paternal alleles drive placental invasion | The paternal genome sets the invasion aggressiveness of the placenta |

**Collective conclusion:** Every major line of PE epidemiological evidence implicates the paternal contribution. The mother's body is the *arena* in which PE is expressed, but the *source* of the incompatibility is the sperm.

### 1.3 The Reframing

This extension proposes that preeclampsia is not a maternal disease but a **compatibility phenotype** — the reproductive system's assessment that a specific sperm-egg combination has produced a feto-placental unit whose paternal antigens are insufficiently tolerable for full immune accommodation. The maternal immune system restricts trophoblast invasion not because it is malfunctioning, but because it is *correctly identifying* an immunogenetic mismatch.

If this reframing is correct, PE is an evolutionary mechanism, not a pathology — albeit one with devastating consequences in the context of modern pregnancy expectations where every pregnancy is expected to reach term without complication.

## 2. The HLA System and Mate Compatibility

### 2.1 MHC/HLA Diversity: The Evolutionary Imperative

The major histocompatibility complex (MHC) — HLA in humans — is the most polymorphic gene system in the vertebrate genome. This extreme diversity is maintained by balancing selection: individuals heterozygous at HLA loci have broader pathogen recognition capacity and superior immune function [1, 2].

The consequence for reproduction is direct: **the best immunological outcome for offspring is HLA heterozygosity** — inheriting different HLA alleles from each parent, producing the widest possible immune repertoire. This requires that the parents themselves have *different* HLA profiles.

### 2.2 MHC-Disassortative Mating

Multiple studies across species and human populations have demonstrated MHC-disassortative mating preferences — individuals preferring mates with MHC profiles different from their own:

| Study | Species | Finding |
|---|---|---|
| Wedekind et al. (1995) | Human | Women preferred the body odour of men with dissimilar MHC genotypes (sweaty T-shirt experiment) |
| Wedekind & Füri (1997) | Human | Replication of MHC-disassortative odour preference; reversed in women using oral contraceptives |
| Ober et al. (1997) | Human (Hutterite) | Couples shared fewer HLA haplotypes than expected by chance; fetal loss increased in HLA-similar couples |
| Yamazaki et al. (1976) | Mouse | MHC-disassortative mating preference demonstrated in controlled breeding |
| Penn & Potts (1999) | Multiple | Meta-analysis confirming MHC-disassortative mate choice across vertebrates |

**Key insight:** Humans appear to have evolved mate selection mechanisms that favour HLA-dissimilar partners — presumably because offspring of HLA-complementary pairings have broader immune repertoires and better fitness.

### 2.3 What Happens When Compatibility Fails

When MHC/HLA-similar individuals reproduce, their offspring are more HLA-homozygous — narrower immune repertoire, reduced pathogen defence. But the reproductive consequence may begin before the offspring's immune function is tested:

**Hypothesis:** The maternal immune system uses the trophoblast — the first fetal tissue to make contact with maternal blood — as a **compatibility assay.** If the paternal HLA antigens expressed on trophoblast cells are too similar to the mother's own HLA (insufficient foreignness to trigger the activating KIR pathway) or too novel (insufficient prior immune education to mount a tolerance response), the result is restricted trophoblast invasion — the upstream pathology of preeclampsia.

In this framing, PE is the immune system's answer to the question: *is this pairing compatible enough to warrant full placental investment?*

## 3. The Spectrum of Compatibility

### 3.1 The Goldilocks Zone

The compatibility model proposes a spectrum of paternal antigen foreignness with an optimal range:

| Compatibility Zone | Paternal HLA Relative to Maternal | Immune Response | Pregnancy Outcome |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Too similar** (HLA-homologous mating) | Paternal HLA alleles overlap with maternal → fetal trophoblast not foreign enough | uNK cells insufficiently activated (inhibitory KIR dominates) → poor cytokine/angiogenic factor secretion → inadequate spiral artery remodelling | Elevated PE risk, IUGR, recurrent miscarriage |
| **Optimal (complementary)** | Paternal HLA alleles different from maternal → fetal trophoblast recognised as appropriately foreign | Activating KIR engagement → proper uNK function → cytokine/angiogenic factor release → complete spiral artery remodelling | Normal placentation, adequate fetal oxygen supply |
| **Too foreign** (no prior immune priming) | Completely novel paternal antigens (donor gametes, first intercourse → immediate conception) | Maternal immune system has no Treg memory for these antigens → exaggerated immune response → trophoblast invasion restricted as "graft rejection-lite" | Markedly elevated PE risk (donor gamete pregnancies: 20–40%) |

### 3.2 Seminal Fluid as the Compatibility Training System

The primipaternity effect and the seminal fluid exposure data reveal an elegant biological system:

1. **Initial exposure:** Seminal fluid deposits paternal antigens (soluble HLA, TGF-β, prostaglandins) on the cervical mucosa
2. **Immune education:** Maternal dendritic cells capture paternal antigens and present them to T cells → generation of paternal-antigen-specific regulatory T cells (Tregs) [Robertson, 2005]
3. **Tolerance building:** Repeated exposure over months to years builds a robust Treg population specific to this partner's HLA antigens
4. **Pregnancy application:** At implantation, the maternal immune system has pre-existing tolerance for the paternal antigens expressed by the trophoblast → appropriate immune modulation → normal trophoblast invasion

**This system is a pre-conception compatibility test.** The immune system uses repeated seminal fluid exposure to assess whether it can build adequate tolerance for this partner's antigens. The duration of cohabitation before conception — the reduction in PE risk with longer sexual relationship — represents the time required for this compatibility assessment.

When this system is bypassed — barrier contraception throughout relationship, IVF with donor gametes, short relationship before conception — the compatibility assessment is incomplete, and PE risk rises.

### 3.3 The "Preeclampsia-Prone Father" Reinterpreted

Some men are "PE-prone" — their partners have elevated PE risk regardless of the woman's individual profile [Dekker, 2002]. The compatibility model reinterprets this:

A "PE-prone father" carries a combination of:
- HLA-C alleles that are statistically more likely to produce unfavourable KIR interactions across a range of female KIR genotypes
- Imprinted paternal alleles that programme excessively aggressive placental invasion (triggering a stronger maternal immune restriction)
- Antigenic profiles that are inherently difficult for the female immune system to develop tolerance toward

This is not a pathology of the man — it is a probability distribution. Some paternal genotypes are *compatible with fewer potential partners* than others. The man is not "diseased" — he is immunogenetically less universally compatible.

## 4. Parent-Offspring Conflict and the Evolutionary Logic

### 4.1 Haig's Conflict Theory

David Haig (1993) proposed that imprinted genes in the placenta reflect evolutionary conflict between maternal and paternal genetic interests [3]:

- **Paternal alleles** (e.g., IGF2) promote placental growth and resource extraction — the father's genetic interest is served by maximal nutrient transfer to this specific offspring, even at maternal cost
- **Maternal alleles** (e.g., H19, PHLDA2) restrain placental growth — the mother's genetic interest is served by conserving resources across all current and future pregnancies

Preeclampsia, in Haig's framework, is what happens when the maternal restraint system "wins" too aggressively — restricting placental invasion to protect maternal health, but compromising fetal oxygen supply in the process.

### 4.2 The Compatibility Extension of Haig

This extension proposes that the intensity of parent-offspring conflict is modulated by **mate compatibility:**

- **Complementary pairings** (HLA-dissimilar, adequate immune priming) → paternal-maternal conflict is *negotiated* effectively → balanced placental invasion → adequate fetal oxygenation
- **Incompatible pairings** (HLA-similar, inadequate priming, "PE-prone" paternal genotype) → paternal-maternal conflict is *unresolved* → maternal immune system over-restricts placental invasion → fetal hypoxia → PE

In this framing, PE is not the maternal system malfunctioning — it is the maternal system *correctly refusing to fully accommodate* a feto-placental unit that it judges to be insufficiently compatible. The "disease" is the consequence of proceeding with a pregnancy despite the immunological mismatch, in a species where reproductive choice is no longer governed by MHC-disassortative mate selection.

### 4.3 What "Best Match" Means

The user's original insight — "best matches may be those that complement each other where their offspring are less likely to produce crossover" — maps precisely onto this evolutionary framework:

- **Complementary HLA profiles** → offspring inherit a diverse HLA repertoire → broader immune function → reduced likelihood that their own children will face KIR/HLA-C compatibility issues (because diverse parents produce diverse HLA combinations in offspring)
- **Similar HLA profiles** → offspring inherit a narrow HLA repertoire → reduced immune diversity → **increased probability that their own reproductive pairings will face the same compatibility issues** — because their narrow HLA comes from a pool already shown to be incompatible

This is the "crossover" the user identified: **PE risk is heritable not as a single gene but as an immune repertoire problem that perpetuates itself across generations when incompatible pairings produce immunogenetically narrow offspring.**

The paternal family history data supports this: men whose mothers had PE are more likely to have partners who develop PE — because these men inherited the narrow HLA profile that made their mother's pregnancy problematic, and they are now contributing that same narrow profile to a new pregnancy.

## 5. The Sperm as the Primary Variable

### 5.1 Everything Else Is Secondary

If PE is fundamentally a compatibility phenotype driven by the paternal genetic contribution, then the conventional risk factors — maternal age, BMI, hypertension, diabetes, renal disease, autoimmune conditions — are *modulators* of a primary incompatibility, not causes in their own right.

| Factor | Conventional Role | Compatibility Model Reinterpretation |
|---|---|---|
| Maternal obesity | "Risk factor for PE" | Chronic inflammation raises the immune system's baseline reactivity → lower threshold for rejecting borderline-compatible trophoblast |
| Maternal hypertension | "Risk factor for PE" | Pre-existing endothelial dysfunction → spiral artery remodelling already compromised → less margin for immunological restriction |
| Maternal autoimmunity | "Risk factor for PE" | Dysregulated immune tolerance → reduced capacity to generate Tregs for paternal antigens |
| Maternal age | "Risk factor for PE" | Immunosenescence → reduced Treg generation capacity → slower/incomplete tolerance building |
| Gestational diabetes | "Comorbidity" | Metabolic stress compounds the already-restricted placental function |
| First pregnancy | "Risk factor for PE" | First exposure to *this specific partner's* antigens — primipaternity, not primiparity |
| Multiple gestation | "Risk factor for PE" | Double antigenic load from twins → immune system must accommodate twice the foreign trophoblast |

**The common thread:** Every "maternal risk factor" either raises the immune system's reactivity (making it harder to tolerate even a compatible pairing) or reduces its tolerance-building capacity (making it harder to accommodate a borderline pairing). None of them is the *cause* — the cause is the compatibility mismatch from the sperm. They determine whether a borderline mismatch tips into clinical PE.

### 5.2 The Threshold Model

This produces a threshold model of PE:

$$\text{PE risk} = f(\text{paternal HLA incompatibility}) + g(\text{immune priming deficit}) + h(\text{maternal inflammatory burden})$$

Where:
- $f$ = the degree of paternal HLA incompatibility (determined at conception — the sperm)
- $g$ = the immune priming deficit (modifiable — duration and method of pre-conception antigen exposure)
- $h$ = the maternal inflammatory/metabolic burden (partially modifiable — weight, chronic disease management)

**PE occurs when the sum exceeds the maternal immune system's tolerance threshold.** In a perfectly compatible pairing with adequate priming and no inflammatory burden, PE cannot occur. In a maximally incompatible pairing (donor gametes, no priming, maternal comorbidities), PE is near-inevitable.

## 6. Implications for the Alignment Model

### 6.1 PE as the Upstream Determinant — Reframed

The alignment model's causal chain runs: PE → placental insufficiency → fetal hypoxia → OPC maturation arrest → neurodevelopmental injury.

With the compatibility reframing, this extends to: **paternal genome incompatibility → PE → placental insufficiency → fetal hypoxia → OPC maturation arrest → neurodevelopmental injury.**

The father's DNA — specifically his HLA profile, his imprinted placental genes, and his antigenic presentation characteristics — is the most upstream variable in the entire causal chain. The injury to the fetus's brain begins, conceptually, at the moment of fertilisation.

### 6.2 Why Some Families Have Multiple Affected Children

The compatibility model explains why some couples have multiple children with neurodevelopmental differences:

- If the incompatibility is genetic (KIR/HLA-C mismatch), it is present in **every pregnancy** with the same partner
- The primipaternity tolerance effect may provide partial protection for later pregnancies (explaining why first-born children are often more affected), but the underlying genetic incompatibility remains
- If the father's imprinted genes programme aggressive placental invasion, every placenta built from his sperm will have the same invasion characteristics
- Successive pregnancies with increasing maternal inflammatory burden (accumulated from each prior preeclamptic pregnancy) may worsen outcomes — connecting to Track 5's multi-parity severity effect

### 6.3 Why Changing Partners Changes Outcomes

Women who had PE with one partner but uncomplicated pregnancies with a new partner are not demonstrating that their body "fixed" itself — they are demonstrating that **the new paternal genome is more compatible with their immune system.** The new partner's HLA profile falls within the Goldilocks zone for this specific woman's KIR genotype.

## 7. Predictive and Preventive Implications

### 7.1 Pre-Conception Compatibility Screening

If PE is a compatibility phenotype, it is **predictable before conception:**

| Screening Approach | What It Measures | When Available |
|---|---|---|
| **KIR genotyping of the mother** | KIR-AA (high risk) vs. KIR-AB/BB (lower risk) | Pre-conception — single blood test |
| **HLA-C typing of the father** | HLA-C1 vs. HLA-C2 group — C2 + maternal KIR-AA = highest risk | Pre-conception — single blood test |
| **HLA-C typing of the couple** | Degree of HLA overlap — high similarity = elevated risk | Pre-conception — paired blood tests |
| **Paternal PE family history** | Men whose mothers had PE — elevated risk transmission | Pre-conception — family history |
| **Relationship duration before conception** | Proxy for immune priming adequacy | Anamnestic — no test required |

**The most powerful predictor would be the KIR/HLA-C combination:** a woman with KIR-AA paired with a man carrying HLA-C2 alleles represents the highest-risk combination. This is deterministic at conception — it does not change with lifestyle modification.

### 7.2 Immune Priming Interventions

If seminal fluid exposure builds immune tolerance for paternal antigens, then deliberate immune priming strategies could reduce PE risk in identified high-risk pairings:

- **Extended unprotected sexual activity before conception** — the simplest intervention, already epidemiologically supported
- **Paternal antigen desensitisation** — experimental concept: intradermal or sublingual administration of paternal HLA antigens prior to conception to generate Tregs
- **Treg expansion therapy** — ex vivo expansion of paternal-antigen-specific Tregs and re-infusion prior to or early in pregnancy

These are speculative but mechanistically coherent with the compatibility model.

### 7.3 Aspirin Timing and the Compatibility Window

Low-dose aspirin initiated before 16 weeks' gestation reduces PE risk by approximately 60% in high-risk populations [ASPRE trial — Rolnik et al., 2017]. The compatibility model provides a mechanistic explanation: aspirin must be initiated while spiral artery remodelling is still ongoing (before 16 weeks) to mitigate the downstream consequences of the immune restriction. After 16 weeks, the remodelling window has closed, and the restriction is permanent.

**Compatibility model prediction:** Aspirin effectiveness should correlate with the degree of KIR/HLA-C incompatibility. In severe mismatches (KIR-AA + HLA-C2), aspirin may be insufficient — the immunological restriction is too fundamental for a cyclooxygenase inhibitor to overcome. In borderline mismatches, aspirin may be sufficient to tip the balance toward adequate remodelling.

## 8. The Generational Perpetuation of Incompatibility

### 8.1 The Crossover Problem

The user's original insight — that offspring of complementary matches are less likely to "produce crossover" — describes a generational selection mechanism:

**Generation 1:** Father and mother with complementary HLA profiles → wide-repertoire offspring → these offspring are compatible with a broad range of partners → PE risk in Generation 2 is low

**Generation 1:** Father and mother with overlapping HLA profiles → narrow-repertoire offspring → these offspring are compatible with fewer partners → PE risk in Generation 2 is elevated → their own offspring (Generation 3) inherit even narrower repertoires if they also pair with similar-HLA partners

This is a **positive feedback loop** in populations with assortative mating — if people tend to pair with genetically similar partners (same ethnic background, same geographical origin, same socioeconomic group), HLA diversity narrows over generations, and PE risk accumulates.

### 8.2 Population-Level Predictions

| Population Characteristic | Predicted PE Risk | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| Small, endogamous populations (genetic bottleneck) | Elevated | Reduced HLA diversity → more HLA-homologous pairings |
| First-generation immigrant pairings (large genetic distance) | Reduced | Maximum HLA diversity → complementary pairings |
| Couples from same small village/family group | Elevated | Shared HLA haplotypes → compatibility zone missed |
| Couples from different ethnic backgrounds | Potentially reduced (if HLA-dissimilar) | Complementary HLA — though total immune novelty could push into "too foreign" zone |
| IVF with donor gametes (unknown-HLA donor) | Markedly elevated | No pre-conception immune priming + potentially extreme HLA dissimilarity |

### 8.3 The Evolutionary Paradox

If PE is an evolutionary compatibility mechanism, why hasn't natural selection eliminated the incompatible allele combinations?

**Answer:** Because the same alleles that produce PE in one pairing may be protective in another context:

- HLA-C2, which drives PE risk with KIR-AA mothers, may provide superior pathogen defence in certain infectious environments
- KIR-AA, which increases PE risk, may provide better immune surveillance against certain cancers
- The evolutionary "solution" is not to eliminate these alleles but to maintain diversity and rely on mate selection (MHC-disassortative preference) to avoid incompatible pairings

In modern human societies, mate selection is driven by social, economic, and cultural factors rather than MHC-disassortative preference. The contraceptive pill — which reverses MHC odour preference (Wedekind, 1995) — may be literally disrupting the evolved mate compatibility assessment system, contributing to incompatible pairings that would not have occurred under ancestral conditions.

## 10. Testable Predictions

### 10.1 Immediately Testable (Existing Cohorts)

1. **KIR/HLA-C prospective prediction:** In IVF cohorts where both parental genotypes are available, KIR-AA mothers with HLA-C2 donor sperm should have significantly higher PE rates than KIR-BB mothers with the same HLA-C2 donors. This may already be calculable from existing assisted reproduction databases.

2. **Partner-change natural experiment:** In populations where women have children with multiple partners, partner HLA typing should show that the non-PE pregnancies had more HLA-dissimilar fathers than the PE pregnancies.

3. **Paternal family history validation:** Men whose mothers had PE should carry HLA profiles that are statistically *less compatible* across a range of potential maternal KIR genotypes — i.e., their HLA-C alleles should disproportionately be C2 group.

### 10.2 Novel Study Designs Required

4. **Couple HLA similarity and PE dose-response:** A prospective cohort of 1,000+ couples with HLA typing at booking should show a gradient: HLA similarity → PE risk in a continuous dose-response, not just the binary KIR-AA/HLA-C2 interaction.

5. **Seminal fluid priming RCT:** High-risk couples (KIR-AA + HLA-C2) randomised to extended unprotected intercourse (3+ months) vs. barrier contraception before conception, with PE as primary outcome. Ethical challenges are obvious, but the prediction is clear.

6. **Population HLA diversity and PE epidemiology:** Cross-national comparison of population-level HLA diversity (measured by expected heterozygosity) against national PE prevalence. Populations with lower HLA diversity should have higher PE rates.

### 10.3 Long-Term / Generational

7. **Three-generation HLA-PE study:** Prospective tracking of HLA profiles across three generations, testing whether narrow-HLA couples produce offspring at higher PE risk than diverse-HLA couples.

8. **Oral contraceptive use and mate HLA similarity:** Retrospective study of women who met their partner while using vs. not using hormonal contraception, with HLA typing of both partners. OC users should have partners with greater HLA similarity (per Wedekind's preference reversal finding).

## 11. Connections to Other Extension Tracks

### 11.1 Track 1 — Paternal Epigenetic Transmission

Track 1 examines how a father's own fetal experience marks his germline. This Track 7 provides the upstream explanation: if a father was born from an incompatible pairing (his mother had PE), his own genome carries the HLA configuration that made that pairing difficult. He transmits not only any epigenetic marks from his own fetal hypoxia but the **genetic incompatibility architecture itself** to the next generation.

### 11.2 Track 2 — Colostrum as Personalised Repair

Track 2 proposes that colostrum composition is programmed by gestational experience. If PE originates from the sperm (paternal incompatibility), then colostrum composition is ultimately programmed by the *degree of paternal incompatibility* — the mammary gland integrates the inflammatory and hypoxic signals generated by the immune system's struggle with incompatible trophoblast.

### 11.3 Track 6 — Trigger Mechanisms

Track 6 catalogues PE triggers as a taxonomy. This Track 7 reinterprets that taxonomy: the "triggers" are not independent causes but **modulators of a primary compatibility variable.** The paternal HLA contribution is the necessary condition; the triggers determine whether a borderline incompatibility becomes clinical PE or remains subclinical.

## 12. Significance Statement

This extension proposes a fundamental reframing of preeclampsia: from maternal disease to **mate-pair incompatibility phenotype** with the paternal genome as the primary determinant. The evidence that PE risk is partner-specific, resets with partner change, is elevated with novel paternal antigens, and transmits through the paternal family line is individually strong and collectively overwhelming. Within the alignment model, this reframing positions the father's DNA — specifically his HLA profile, imprinted placental genes, and antigenic presentation characteristics — as the most upstream variable in the entire causal chain linking placental insufficiency to fetal brain injury. The "best" reproductive matches are those where paternal and maternal HLA profiles complement each other, producing offspring with broad immune repertoires who are themselves compatible with a wider range of future partners. Preeclampsia may be evolution's imperfect solution to the problem of immunological compatibility in a species where haemochorial placentation requires the mother to tolerate deep invasion by semi-foreign tissue — a solution rendered increasingly dysfunctional by modern disruptions to MHC-disassortative mate selection.
