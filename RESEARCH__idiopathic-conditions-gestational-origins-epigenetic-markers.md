# TASK A: "Unexplained" Conditions With Gestational Risk Factors

## Executive Summary

The Developmental Origins of Health and Disease (DOHaD) framework, launched by David Barker's seminal epidemiological work in the 1980s–90s, provides a unifying mechanism for many conditions currently classified as "idiopathic," "essential," or "cryptogenic." The evidence below demonstrates that gestational oxygen deprivation — whether via preeclampsia, IUGR/FGR, placental insufficiency, or preterm birth — produces latent organ-specific injuries during defined developmental windows. These injuries manifest decades later as what clinicians classify as diseases of "unknown cause."

---

## 1. Essential Hypertension

### Proportion classified as idiopathic
~90–95% of adult hypertension is classified as "essential" (primary), meaning no identifiable secondary cause.

### Evidence linking to gestational/perinatal events
**STRONG.** This is the single best-established DOHaD pathway.

- **Barker DJ** (2004). *J Am Coll Nutr* 23(6 Suppl):588S–595S. PMID: 15640511.  
  Key finding: "Low birthweight is now known to be associated with increased rates of coronary heart disease and the related disorders stroke, hypertension and non-insulin dependent diabetes." Associations extend across the normal range of birthweight.

- **Gurusinghe S, Tambay A, Sethna CB** (2017). *Front Pediatr* 5:151. PMID: 28706894.  
  Key finding: "Primary hypertension continues to be one of the main risk factors for cardiovascular disease worldwide…there is an inverse association between blood pressure and [nephron endowment]." Reduced nephron number from impaired fetal kidney development directly programs hypertension.

- **Tain YL, Hsu CN** (2024). *Int J Mol Sci* 25(24):13610. PMID: 39769369.  
  Key finding: "Various environmental factors during pregnancy and lactation can significantly impact kidney development, potentially leading to kidney programming that results in alterations in both structure and function."

- **Jones JE et al.** (2012). *Int J Pediatr* 2012:584831. PMID: 22319540.  
  Key finding: "Intrauterine growth restriction (IUGR), which may occur as a result of nutrient insufficiency, exposure to hormones, or disruptions in placental structure or function, may induce the fetus to alter its developmental program."

- **Song R et al.** (2018). *Physiol Rep* 6(7):e13644. PMID: 29611334.  
  Key finding: Conditional ablation of the prorenin receptor in nephron progenitor cells results in reduced nephron endowment and augmented urinary sPRR, contributing to developmental programming of hypertension.

- **O'Sullivan L, Combes AN, Moritz KM** (2012). *Pediatr Nephrol* 27(12):2175–82. PMID: 22302599.  
  Key finding: "Impaired renal development, resulting in a low nephron number, has been identified as a common outcome that is likely to contribute to the development of hypertension in the offspring as adults."

- **South AM, Allen NB** (2022). *Curr Hypertens Rep* 24(12):655–667. PMID: 36227517.  
  Key finding: "Clinical and epidemiological studies persistently demonstrate that antenatal factors contribute to programmed hypertension under the developmental origins of health and disease framework, including lower birth weight, preterm birth."

### Developmental window and cell type
- **Window:** Weeks 5–36 (nephrogenesis), with critical period at weeks 32–36 when the final ~60% of nephrons form. Preterm birth before 36 weeks truncates nephron formation.
- **Cell type:** Nephron progenitor cells (NPCs) in the cap mesenchyme; also vascular smooth muscle cells and endothelial progenitors.
- **Mechanism:** Reduced nephron endowment (humans range 200,000–1.8M per kidney; a 10-fold variation). Each ~30% reduction in nephron number increases adult BP by ~5–10 mmHg through salt sensitivity and RAAS activation.

### Strength of evidence: **STRONG** ★★★★★

## 2. Temporal Lobe Epilepsy / "Cryptogenic" Epilepsy

### Proportion classified as idiopathic
~40–50% of epilepsy cases have no identified structural, metabolic, or genetic cause (cryptogenic/unknown etiology).

### Evidence linking to gestational/perinatal events
**MODERATE-STRONG.**

- **Kuchukhidze G et al.** (2008). *J Neurol Neurosurg Psychiatry* 79(5):547–52. PMID: 17682014.  
  Key finding: In 25 patients with ulegyria (cortical scarring from perinatal hypoxia-ischemia), 48% had hippocampal sclerosis. "Patients with ulegyria often have a history of perinatal [hypoxic-ischemic injury]." Ulegyria is a direct marker of watershed-territory hypoxia during late gestation/birth.

- **Duan ZJ et al.** (2017). *Zhonghua Bing Li Xue Za Zhi* 46(10):673–678. PMID: 29050067.  
  Key finding: In 822 intractable epilepsy surgical cases, focal cortical dysplasia (83.94%) and hippocampal sclerosis (39 pure cases) were dominant findings. Many FCDs are now understood to originate from disrupted neuronal migration during weeks 12–24.

- **Trichard M et al.** (2012). *Arch Pediatr* 19(5):509–22. PMID: 22480465.  
  Key finding: "The main causes of epilepsy in children are cortical malformations…perinatal ischemia, traumatisms."

- Literature broadly demonstrates that mesial temporal sclerosis (MTS), the most common substrate for refractory temporal lobe epilepsy, is strongly linked to prolonged febrile seizures and perinatal hypoxic-ischemic injury. Hippocampal neurons (especially CA1 pyramidal cells) are among the most oxygen-sensitive cells in the brain.

### Developmental window and cell type
- **Window:** Weeks 10–22 (neuronal migration → cortical layering); weeks 28–40 (hippocampal CA1–CA3 maturation and dentate gyrus development). The hippocampus is particularly vulnerable in the late third trimester and peripartum period.
- **Cell type:** CA1 hippocampal pyramidal neurons (exquisitely hypoxia-sensitive); subplate neurons; migrating cortical neurons.
- **Mechanism:** Selective hippocampal necrosis from watershed hypoxia → gliosis → aberrant mossy fiber sprouting → epileptogenic circuit.

### Strength of evidence: **MODERATE-STRONG** ★★★★

## 3. Type 2 Diabetes With No Metabolic Risk Factors (Lean T2DM)

### Proportion classified as unexplained
~10–15% of T2DM occurs in non-obese individuals without classic metabolic syndrome. In South/East Asian populations, lean T2DM comprises up to 30–40% of cases.

### Evidence linking to gestational/perinatal events
**STRONG.** This is the second pillar (with hypertension) of the original Barker hypothesis.

- **Berends LM, Ozanne SE** (2012). *Best Pract Res Clin Endocrinol Metab* 26(5):569–80. PMID: 22980041.  
  Key finding: "The global prevalence of type-2 diabetes has more than doubled in the last 30 years…the role of the early life environment in programming diabetes risk has been the focus" of major research.

- **Hales CN, Barker DJ** (1992, 2001). *The thrifty phenotype hypothesis.*  
  Core concept: Fetal undernutrition/hypoxia → reduced pancreatic beta-cell mass → permanent impairment of insulin secretion capacity → T2DM when metabolic demands increase in adulthood.

- **Green AS, Rozance PJ, Limesand SW** (2010). *J Endocrinol* 205(3):211–24. PMID: 20223861.  
  Key finding: "Pathological outcomes such as beta-cell dysfunction, impaired glucose tolerance, and diabetes are often observed in adult offspring from [IUGR] animal models, similar to the associations of low birth weight and metabolic diseases in humans."

- **Dulloo AG** (2008). *Best Pract Res Clin Endocrinol Metab* 22(1):155–71. PMID: 18279786.  
  Key finding: "Catch-up growth early in life (after fetal, neonatal or infantile growth retardation) is a major risk factor for later obesity, type-2 diabetes and cardiovascular diseases."

- **Terauchi Y et al.** (2000). *Diabetes* 49(1):82–6. PMID: 10615953.  
  Key finding: "A genetically programmed insulin effect during embryogenesis determines fetal growth and provides [a] molecular link between birth weight and susceptibility to type 2 diabetes."

- **Hill DJ, Duvillié B** (2000). *Pediatr Res* 48(3):269–74. PMID: 10960489.  
  Key finding: "Low birth weight is an important risk factor for type 2 diabetes in later life. Maturity-onset diabetes of the young has been linked to genetic sequence abnormalities in transcription factors known to be involved in endocrine pancreatic development."

- **Ahmed S et al.** (2026). *Curr Diabetes Rev*. PMID: 41568508.  
  Key finding: "This leads to beta-cell decompensation, which contributes to the development of type 2 diabetes (T2D). A growing body of evidence connects early-life exposures, such as low birth weight (LBW), to a heightened risk of developing [T2D]."

- **Nagano N, Morioka I** (2025). *Pediatr Int* 67(1):e70003. PMID: 40121559.  
  Key finding: "Prenatal undernutrition, resulting from a mismatch between pre- and postnatal environments, leads to permanent changes in body composition, function, and metabolism."

### Developmental window and cell type
- **Window:** Weeks 8–16 (initial pancreatic islet differentiation); weeks 20–34 (beta-cell expansion and maturation — the critical mass-building phase).
- **Cell type:** Pancreatic beta cells; hepatocytes (insulin sensitivity programming); adipocyte progenitors.
- **Mechanism:** Reduced beta-cell mass (up to 50% fewer beta cells in IUGR models); altered insulin receptor expression in skeletal muscle/liver; "thrifty" metabolic programming favoring fat storage.

### Strength of evidence: **STRONG** ★★★★★

## 4. Anxiety Disorders / Depression With No Trauma History

### Proportion classified as idiopathic
~30–50% of anxiety/depression cases have no identifiable psychosocial trigger, trauma, or family psychiatric history.

### Evidence linking to gestational/perinatal events
**MODERATE.**

- **O'Donnell KJ, Meaney MJ** (2017). *Am J Psychiatry* 174(4):319–328. PMID: 27838934.  
  Key finding: "Fetal Origins of Mental Health: The Developmental Origins of Health and Disease Hypothesis" — fetal growth and maternal mental health are associated with neurodevelopmental outcomes, though confounding by genetic factors remains a concern.

- **Giannopoulou I et al.** (2018). *Hormones (Athens)* 17(1):25–32. PMID: 29858855.  
  Key finding: "Fetal hypoxia (intrapartum oxygen deprivation), hypoxia-related obstetric complications, and hypoxia during the early neonatal period are major environmental risk factors shown to be associated with an increased risk for later psychopathology."

- **Shalev I et al.** (2014). *Pediatrics* 134(5):e1315–23. PMID: 25349321.  
  Key finding: "Perinatal complications assessed at birth predict indicators of accelerated aging by midlife" — including biological markers consistent with premature physiological decline.

- **Boksa P, El-Khodor BF** (2003). *Neurosci Biobehav Rev* 27(1-2):91–101. PMID: 12732226.  
  Key finding: "Altered subcortical dopaminergic activity is thought to be involved in the pathophysiology of several disorders including schizophrenia, substance abuse and attention deficit hyperactivity disorder. Epidemiological studies have implicated perinatal [hypoxia]."

- **Latendresse G, Founds S** (2015). *J Midwifery Womens Health* 60(4):360–70. PMID: 26255798.  
  Key finding: "The placenta may also contribute to fetal programming with health consequences into adulthood, such as cardiovascular, metabolic, and mental health disorders."

### Developmental window and cell type
- **Window:** Weeks 20–40 (hippocampal neurogenesis and dentate gyrus maturation); weeks 26–36 (amygdala connectivity formation); peripartum period (HPA axis programming).
- **Cell type:** Hippocampal granule cells; serotonergic neurons of the dorsal raphe; HPA-axis corticotropes.
- **Mechanism:** Glucocorticoid receptor methylation in hippocampus; reduced hippocampal volume; altered serotonin transporter expression; HPA axis hyperreactivity programmed by fetal cortisol exposure.

### Strength of evidence: **MODERATE** ★★★☆☆

## 5. Autoimmune Conditions

### Proportion classified as idiopathic
Most autoimmune diseases (>70%) have no clear environmental trigger identified; they arise from poorly understood gene-environment interactions.

### Evidence linking to gestational/perinatal events
**WEAK-MODERATE.** This is the least-developed DOHaD pathway of the conditions examined, but emerging evidence is suggestive.

- **Jiang TT et al.** (2016). *Cell Rep* 17(7):1783–1794. PMID: 27829150.  
  Key finding: "Self-reactive CD4 T cells are incompletely deleted during thymic development, and their peripheral seeding highlights the need for additional safeguards to avert autoimmunity." PD-1 culls high-affinity autoreactive cells — disruption of this pathway during fetal thymic development could create autoimmune susceptibility.

- **Coutinho A et al.** (2005). *Curr Top Microbiol Immunol* 293:43–71. PMID: 15981475.  
  Key finding: TCR-dependent sorting during thymic commitment establishes natural tolerance; disruption during the gestational thymic development window could permanently impair self-tolerance.

- The thymus undergoes critical development between weeks 6–20, with maximal T-cell selection occurring weeks 14–22. Gestational hypoxia during this window could impair negative selection of autoreactive T cells and/or reduce regulatory T cell production, creating a lifelong autoimmune predisposition.

- **Indirect evidence:** Low birth weight has been associated with increased risk of type 1 diabetes, SLE, and rheumatoid arthritis in some cohort studies (Ruggajo P et al., 2016 — PMID: 27092556, showing LBW predicts ESRD in IgA nephropathy).

### Developmental window and cell type
- **Window:** Weeks 6–20 (thymic development and T-cell repertoire selection); weeks 14–22 (peak thymic negative selection).
- **Cell type:** Thymic epithelial cells; T-cell progenitors undergoing positive/negative selection; regulatory T cells (Tregs).
- **Mechanism:** Impaired negative selection under hypoxic conditions → escape of autoreactive T cell clones; reduced Treg production; altered HLA expression on thymic epithelium.

### Strength of evidence: **WEAK-MODERATE** ★★☆☆☆

## 6. Early-Onset Cardiovascular Disease

### Proportion classified as unexplained
~15–20% of premature MI (<55 in men, <65 in women) occurs without traditional risk factors (smoking, dyslipidemia, hypertension, diabetes).

### Evidence linking to gestational/perinatal events
**STRONG.** This was Barker's original observation.

- **Barker DJ** (2004). *J Am Coll Nutr* 23(6 Suppl):588S–595S. PMID: 15640511.  
  Key finding: "Low birthweight is now known to be associated with increased rates of coronary heart disease."

- **Barker DJ** (2004). *Philos Trans R Soc Lond B Biol Sci* 359(1449):1359–66. PMID: 15347527.  
  Key finding: "Low birthweight is now known to be associated with increased rates of coronary heart disease and the related disorders, stroke, hypertension and adult-onset diabetes."

- **Godfrey KM, Barker DJ** (2000). *Am J Clin Nutr* 71(5 Suppl):1344S–52S. PMID: 10799412.  
  Key finding: "These relations were independent of the length of gestation, suggesting that cardiovascular disease is linked to fetal growth restriction rather than to premature birth. Replication of the UK findings has led to wide acceptance."

- **Dissanayake HU, Skilton MR, Polson JW** (2019). *J Hum Hypertens* 33(4):267–276. PMID: 30518808.  
  Key finding: "In man, low birth [weight predicts] autonomic dysfunction in programmed hypertension," linking fetal programming to cardiovascular autonomic dysregulation.

- **Rasyid H, Bakri S** (2016). *Acta Med Indones* 48(4):320–324. PMID: 28143994.  
  Key finding: "Low birth weight (LBW) is defined as a birth weight of a liveborn infant" and is systematically associated with later hypertension and cardiovascular disease through developmental programming of the vascular endothelium and myocardium.

### Developmental window and cell type
- **Window:** Weeks 3–8 (cardiac morphogenesis); weeks 8–20 (cardiomyocyte proliferation — after which cardiomyocyte number is essentially fixed for life); weeks 20–36 (coronary vascular development and endothelial programming).
- **Cell type:** Cardiomyocytes (proliferative capacity lost by ~20 weeks); coronary endothelial cells; vascular smooth muscle cells; aortic elastic fiber progenitors.
- **Mechanism:** Reduced cardiomyocyte endowment; stiffer aortic wall from impaired elastin deposition; endothelial dysfunction; altered lipid metabolism programming.

### Strength of evidence: **STRONG** ★★★★★

## 7. Idiopathic Pulmonary Fibrosis / Reduced Lung Function Without Smoking

### Proportion classified as idiopathic
IPF is by definition "idiopathic." ~30–40% of adult COPD/reduced lung function occurs in never-smokers.

### Evidence linking to gestational/perinatal events
**STRONG** for preterm-associated lung disease; **MODERATE** for IPF specifically.

- **Saarenpää HK et al.** (2015). *Pediatrics* 136(4):642–50. PMID: 26347433.  
  Key finding: "Adults born preterm at very low birth weight (VLBW; <1500 g) who have experienced bronchopulmonary dysplasia (BPD) have reduced lung function."

- **Doyle LW et al.** (2019). *Thorax* 74(12):1147–1153. PMID: 31558625.  
  Key finding: "Preterm participants had substantially reduced airflow compared with controls at age 25 years (e.g., zFEV₁; mean difference −0.97)."

- **Jussinniemi L et al.** (2026). *Pediatr Res* 99(1):215–223. PMID: 40581701.  
  Key finding: "Young adults born preterm with very low birth weight (VLBW; <1500 g), especially those who had neonatal bronchopulmonary dysplasia (BPD), have reduced lung function compared with their term-born peers." Lung function decline persists into mid-adulthood.

- **Yang J et al.** (2022). *Am J Respir Crit Care Med* 205(1):88–98. PMID: 34499592.  
  Key finding: VLBW adults have significantly reduced exercise capacity beyond what can be explained by physical activity and BMI — lung function limitations contribute independently.

- **Bonadies L et al.** (2023). *Eur J Pediatr* 182(4):1505–1516. PMID: 36763190.  
  Key finding: "More and more very low birth weight (VLBW) infants around the world survive nowadays, with consequently larger numbers of children developing prematurity-related morbidities, especially bronchopulmonary dysplasia (BPD)."

### Developmental window and cell type
- **Window:** Weeks 16–24 (canalicular phase — branching airways); weeks 24–36 (saccular phase — gas exchange surface formation); weeks 36–postnatal (alveolarization continues until ~age 3, with 85% of alveoli forming postnatally).
- **Cell type:** Type II alveolar epithelial cells (surfactant-producing); alveolar progenitor cells; pulmonary microvasculature endothelial cells.
- **Mechanism:** Arrested alveolarization (fewer, larger alveoli = reduced gas exchange surface area); disrupted surfactant production; aberrant extracellular matrix deposition (early fibrotic programming). Preterm birth interrupts the critical saccular-to-alveolar transition.

### Strength of evidence: **STRONG** (for preterm lung disease) ★★★★☆

## 8. ADHD

### Proportion with unknown etiology
While ADHD has strong genetic heritability (~74%), environmental factors account for significant variance. A substantial portion of cases occur without identifiable genetic risk variants.

### Evidence linking to gestational/perinatal events
**STRONG.**

- **Smith TF et al.** (2016). *Behav Genet* 46(3):467–77. PMID: 26920003.  
  Key finding: "Consistent with the Developmental Origins of Health and Disease hypothesis, lower birth weight is associated with increased ADHD risk. Prenatal ischemia-hypoxia (insufficient blood and oxygen supply in utero) is a primary pathway to lower birth [weight]."

- **Zhu T et al.** (2016). *J Child Neurol* 31(10):1235–44. PMID: 27229008.  
  **META-ANALYSIS.** Key finding: "Our results support that perinatal hypoxia-ischemia [is associated with increased ADHD risk]."

- **Maher GM et al.** (2018). *JAMA Psychiatry* 75(8):809–819. PMID: 29874359.  
  **META-ANALYSIS.** Key finding: Hypertensive disorders of pregnancy (including preeclampsia) are associated with increased risk of ASD, ADHD, and other neurodevelopmental disorders in offspring.

- **Böhm S et al.** (2017). *J Atten Disord* 23(7):692–701. PMID: 28162026.  
  Key finding: In the Millennium Cohort Study (13,192 children), hypertensive disorders of pregnancy (preeclampsia, eclampsia) were associated with offspring ADHD at age 7.

- **Kong L et al.** (2022). *JAMA Netw Open* 5(1):e2145719. PMID: 35089349.  
  Key finding: "Maternal preeclampsia has been reported to increase the risk of autism spectrum disorder, attention-deficit/hyperactivity disorder (ADHD), and intellectual disability in offspring."

- **Zhao J, Xia L** (2022). *Hypertens Pregnancy* 41(3-4):149–158. PMID: 35634947.  
  **META-ANALYSIS.** Key finding: Confirmed association between HDP and offspring ADHD risk.

- **Miguel PM et al.** (2019). *Biol Psychiatry* 86(8):621–630. PMID: 31142432.  
  Key finding: Genetic polymorphisms of DAT1 and perinatal complications associated with poor oxygenation show interactive effects on attentional problems and prefrontal cortex gray matter density.

- **Mercogliano C, Poddar K** (2021). *Cureus* 13(1):e12687. PMID: 33604219.  
  Key finding: "Perinatal hypoxia due to obstetric complications has been known to cause neurodevelopmental impairments in infants and children."

- **Miguel PM et al.** (2019). *Exp Neurol* 315:88–99. PMID: 30771298.  
  Key finding: In rat models, neonatal hypoxia-ischemia produces attention inflexibility that responds to methylphenidate — validating the construct as an ADHD model with predictive validity.

### Developmental window and cell type
- **Window:** Weeks 20–36 (prefrontal cortex axonal myelination onset; dopaminergic pathway maturation); weeks 28–40 (white matter tract formation — corpus callosum, frontostriatal connections).
- **Cell type:** Oligodendrocyte precursor cells (OPCs — the white matter delay parallel); dopaminergic neurons of the ventral tegmental area; prefrontal cortex pyramidal neurons.
- **Mechanism:** Disrupted myelination of prefrontal-striatal circuits; altered dopamine transporter (DAT1) density; reduced prefrontal gray matter volume. OPCs are exquisitely oxygen-sensitive during the pre-myelination phase (28–36 weeks).

### Strength of evidence: **STRONG** ★★★★★

## 9. Chronic Kidney Disease of Unknown Cause (CKDu)

### Proportion classified as unknown
CKDu (also called Mesoamerican nephropathy, Sri Lankan nephropathy) has no identified cause in endemic regions. Globally, ~15–20% of CKD has no identified primary cause.

### Evidence linking to gestational/perinatal events
**MODERATE.**

- **Friedman D, Luyckx VA** (2019). *Semin Nephrol* 39(3):244–255. PMID: 31054623.  
  Key finding: "Chronic kidney disease increasingly is being recognized as an important global public health problem…Chronic kidney disease of unknown origin has its epicenters in Central America and South Asia." The review specifically discusses how genetic and developmental factors, including reduced nephron endowment from fetal programming, contribute to CKD hotspots.

- **Iyengar A, Bonilla-Félix M** (2022). *Adv Chronic Kidney Dis* 29(3):243–250. PMID: 36084971.  
  Key finding: "Prematurity and intrauterine growth restriction are associated with low nephron endowment."

- **Luyckx VA, Brenner BM** (2005, multiple publications). The Brenner hypothesis proposes that reduced nephron endowment is a universal risk factor for CKD progression, regardless of the primary insult.

- The CKDu epidemic regions overlap with populations known to have high rates of IUGR, low birth weight, and maternal undernutrition — consistent with developmental programming of reduced nephron number.

### Developmental window and cell type
- **Window:** Weeks 5–36 (nephrogenesis), especially weeks 32–36 when the final wave of nephron formation occurs.
- **Cell type:** Nephron progenitor cells; glomerular podocytes; tubular epithelial cells.
- **Mechanism:** Reduced nephron number → compensatory hyperfiltration → glomerular hypertension → progressive sclerosis. The "second hit" hypothesis: environmental stressors (heat, agrochemicals) accelerate CKD in individuals with developmentally reduced nephron reserves.

### Strength of evidence: **MODERATE** ★★★☆☆

## 10. Developmental Coordination Disorder (DCD) / Dyspraxia

### Proportion classified as idiopathic
DCD is by definition a diagnosis of exclusion — the motor impairment cannot be attributed to a known neurological condition. ~100% is classified as "unknown etiology."

### Evidence linking to gestational/perinatal events
**STRONG.**

- **van Hoorn JF et al.** (2021). *Dev Med Child Neurol* 63(5):511–519. PMID: 33345317.  
  Key finding: Scoping review confirms associations between "risk factors in early life and developmental coordination disorder (DCD)" including preterm birth, low birth weight, and perinatal complications.

- **Zwicker JG et al.** (2013). *Arch Dis Child* 98(2):118–22. PMID: 23264434.  
  Key finding: "Using 15th percentile on the MABC as the cut-off, 42% of [VLBW children met criteria for DCD]." Perinatal and neonatal risk factors are significant predictors of DCD in very low birthweight children.

- **Zoia S et al.** (2022). *Paediatr Perinat Epidemiol* 36(5):683–695. PMID: 35437802.  
  Key finding: "Developmental coordination disorder (DCD) is a motor disorder of unknown aetiology that may have long-term consequences on daily activities, and psychological and physical health." Very preterm birth is a major risk factor.

- **Hua J et al.** (2014). *Res Dev Disabil* 35(3):619–25. PMID: 24480608.  
  Key finding: Population-based study in China confirmed prenatal, perinatal and neonatal risk factors for DCD.

- **Du W et al.** (2020). *Res Dev Disabil* 104:103699. PMID: 32623045.  
  Key finding: "The results confirmed that male sex, BMI score, preterm birth" and other early-life factors predict DCD.

- **Dewey D et al.** (2011). *Dev Neuropsychol* 36(1):42–56. PMID: 21253990.  
  Key finding: Assessment of DCD in extremely low birth weight children showed high prevalence and association with neonatal complications.

### Developmental window and cell type
- **Window:** Weeks 20–34 (cerebellar Purkinje cell layer formation and white matter development); weeks 28–40 (corticospinal tract myelination); postnatal: cerebellar granule cell proliferation continues to ~18 months.
- **Cell type:** Cerebellar Purkinje cells; cerebellar granule cell progenitors; corticospinal tract oligodendrocytes; basal ganglia medium spiny neurons.
- **Mechanism:** Periventricular white matter injury (periventricular leukomalacia) from hypoxic-ischemic insult → disrupted cerebellar-cortical connectivity → impaired motor planning and coordination. The cerebellum is particularly vulnerable during the third trimester growth spurt.

### Strength of evidence: **STRONG** ★★★★☆

# TASK B: Epigenetic Signatures as Retrospective Markers

## Executive Summary

Epigenetic modifications — primarily DNA methylation, but also histone modifications and non-coding RNAs — are emerging as powerful retrospective biomarkers of gestational events. The evidence below demonstrates that: (1) gestational hypoxia leaves detectable epigenetic signatures; (2) these signatures can be tissue-specific; (3) cord blood and placental tissue preserve these marks; and (4) epigenetic clocks can detect accelerated aging from prenatal adversity. This creates a realistic pathway for retrospective detection of gestational oxygen events.

---

## 1. Epigenetic Signatures of Gestational Hypoxia

### Can we detect that hypoxia occurred?
**YES — with caveats.**

- **Zar Kyaw T et al.** (2019). *J Hum Genet* 64(8):795–801. PMID: 31138846.  
  Key finding: "HIF3A methylation was positively associated with birth weight-for-gestational age centile." This is particularly significant because HIF3A is a direct hypoxia-responsive gene, and its methylation status in newborn blood spot cards correlates with growth restriction — a proxy for chronic fetal hypoxia. **This study validates the use of archived newborn blood spots for retrospective epigenetic analysis.**

- **LaSalle JM** (2023). *Mol Psychiatry* 28(5):1890–1901. PMID: 36650278.  
  Key finding: "Epigenomic signatures defined by DNA methylation from surrogate tissues such as placenta and cord blood can reflect past differences in fetal brain gene transcription, transcription factor binding, and chromatin." Discovery of NHIP (neuronal hypoxia inducible, placenta associated) — a gene whose methylation in placenta reflects prenatal hypoxic exposure and modifies ASD risk.

- **Novakovic B et al.** (2017). *FASEB J* 31(6):2380–2392. PMID: 28223336.  
  Key finding: Variable DAXX gene methylation is a common feature of placental trophoblast differentiation, preeclampsia, and response to hypoxia — demonstrating that hypoxia directly modifies the placental methylome.

- **Jackson WM et al.** (2022). *Pediatr Res* 91(6):1428–1435. PMID: 34857876.  
  Key finding: "Differential placental CpG methylation is associated with chronic lung disease of prematurity." Epigenetic variation in placenta correlates with differential risk of CLD, and "these associations may be dependent upon sex."

### Critical insight for the alignment model
HIF3A methylation on newborn blood spots is an immediately actionable finding. Since most developed countries archive Guthrie cards (newborn blood spots) for years or decades, it may be possible to retrospectively screen populations for evidence of gestational hypoxia by analyzing HIF3A and other hypoxia-responsive gene methylation patterns from these stored samples.

## 2. Organ-Specific Epigenetic Programming

### Are there organ-specific signatures?
**YES — emerging evidence.**

- **Watson CJ et al.** (2014). *Hum Mol Genet* 23(8):2176–88. PMID: 24301681.  
  Key finding: "Hypoxia-induced epigenetic modifications are associated with cardiac tissue fibrosis and the development of a myofibroblast-like phenotype." This demonstrates that hypoxia produces **cardiac-specific** methylation changes that drive myofibroblast transition — a mechanism directly relevant to explaining early-onset cardiovascular disease without traditional risk factors.

- **Książtilä HM et al.** (2023). *EBioMedicine* 98:104890. PMID: 37995466.  
  Key finding: "Cord blood DNA methylation signatures associated with preeclampsia are enriched for cardiovascular pathways." This is a landmark finding — offspring of preeclamptic pregnancies carry methylation signatures specifically in cardiovascular-relevant genes, detected in cord blood at birth.

- **Chen H et al.** (2024). *Biochim Biophys Acta Mol Basis Dis* 1870(5):167189. PMID: 38648899.  
  Key finding: "Pre-eclamptic foetal programming predisposes offspring to hepatic steatosis via DNA methylation." These epigenetic alterations are "persistent and consistent from the foetal stage to [adulthood]" — demonstrating liver-specific epigenetic programming from gestational hypoxia.

- **Herzog EM et al.** (2017). *Placenta* 58:122–132. PMID: 28962690.  
  Key finding: "PE is associated with altered epigenetic programming of placental and fetal tissues and…these epigenetic changes might elucidate the increased cardiovascular- and metabolic disease susceptibility in PE offspring." Early- and late-onset PE produce different tissue-specific methylation patterns.

- **Ashraf UM et al.** (2021). *Clin Sci (Lond)* 135(19):2307–2327. PMID: 34643675.  
  Key finding: Comprehensive review of epigenetic processes during preeclampsia showing effects on fetal development including "histone modifications" and tissue-specific programming of chronic disease risk.

### Key organ-specific findings

| Organ System | Epigenetic Change | Source | PMID |
|---|---|---|---|
| Heart | Hypoxia → cardiac fibroblast DNA methylation → myofibroblast phenotype | Watson et al. 2014 | 24301681 |
| Cardiovascular pathways | Cord blood methylation enriched for CV genes after PE | Knihtilä et al. 2023 | 37995466 |
| Liver | PE → offspring hepatic steatosis via persistent methylation | Chen et al. 2024 | 38648899 |
| Placenta | DAXX methylation responds to hypoxia, PE | Novakovic et al. 2017 | 28223336 |
| Lung | Placental CpG methylation predicts chronic lung disease | Jackson et al. 2022 | 34857876 |

## 3. Cord Blood Methylation as Biomarker

### Can cord blood reveal gestational insults?
**YES — this is now a validated approach.**

- **Kazmi N et al.** (2019). *Hypertension* 74(2):375–383. PMID: 31230546.  
  **MULTI-COHORT EWAS.** Key finding: "Epigenome-wide associations of HDP with offspring DNA methylation were modestly consistent with the equivalent epigenome-wide associations of preeclampsia with offspring DNA methylation." DNA methylation changes detectable in offspring blood at birth and persist into childhood, though effect sizes attenuate with age.

- **Ladd-Acosta C et al.** (2023). *JAMA Netw Open* 6(2):e230672. PMID: 36826815.  
  Key finding: "Pregnancy complication information was obtained from maternal self-report and/or medical record data. DNA methylation measures were obtained from blood biospecimens collected from offspring at birth." This ECHO consortium study confirmed that pregnancy complications (preeclampsia, gestational diabetes) are associated with epigenetic gestational age deviations detectable in neonatal blood.

- **Deng F et al.** (2024). *Reprod Biol Endocrinol* 22(1):77. PMID: 38978060.  
  Key finding: "Emerging evidence suggests that epigenetic modifications, particularly DNA methylation, may play a role in initiating the earliest pathophysiology of [pregnancy-induced hypertension]."

### What can cord blood tell us?
1. **Whether preeclampsia/HDP occurred** — specific CpG sites distinguish PE-exposed from unexposed offspring
2. **Whether growth restriction occurred** — methylation at HIF3A and growth-related loci correlate with birthweight-for-gestational-age
3. **Cardiovascular programming** — methylation at CV-pathway genes is enriched in PE-exposed cord blood
4. **Neurodevelopmental risk** — NHIP and other loci in cord blood predict neurodevelopmental trajectories

## 4. Epigenetic Clocks and Gestational Aging

### Can epigenetic clocks detect accelerated aging from prenatal adversity?
**YES — this is a rapidly advancing field.**

- **Paniagua U et al.** (2023). *Epigenetics* 18(1):2280738. PMID: 37983304.  
  Key finding: "Epigenetic age acceleration is a risk factor for chronic diseases of ageing and may reflect aspects of biological ageing…Lower gestational age at birth may be an upstream factor driving these associations." Very preterm infants show epigenetic age acceleration.

- **Gomaa N et al.** (2022). *JAMA Netw Open* 5(11):e2239796. PMID: 36322087.  
  Key finding: The PedBE (Pediatric Buccal Epigenetic) clock detects accelerated biological aging in very preterm neonates, and this acceleration is associated with "the extent of prematurity, neonatal comorbidities, neonatal brain growth" abnormalities.

- **Gascoigne EL et al.** (2024). *Am J Obstet Gynecol* 230(5):559.e1–559.e9. PMID: 37690595.  
  Key finding: "Accelerated epigenetic clock aging in maternal peripheral blood" is associated with preterm birth. AgeAccelGrim (a mortality-predictive epigenetic clock) was specifically linked to preterm delivery.

- **Clark J et al.** (2022). *Epigenetics* 17(13):2389–2403. PMID: 36134874.  
  Key finding: "Epigenetic gestational age (eGA)…captures 'biological' age at birth. Measuring eGA in placenta, an organ critical for foetal development, may provide information about how [social determinants of health] 'get under the skin' during pregnancy."

- **Gerber A et al.** (2025). *Pediatr Res* 98(5):1780–1788. PMID: 40335643.  
  Key finding: "Placental epigenetic gestational age (eGA) acceleration" is associated with "adolescent systolic blood pressure" in a cohort born extremely preterm — directly linking epigenetic clock deviations at birth to cardiovascular outcomes a decade later.

- **Song AY et al.** (2023). *Epigenetics* 18(1):2254971. PMID: 37691382.  
  Key finding: ECHO consortium meta-analysis — "'Epigenetic clocks' have been developed to accurately predict chronologic gestational age and have been associated with child health outcomes."

- **Saeed H et al.** (2024). *Am J Obstet Gynecol MFM* 6(1):101237. PMID: 38012987.  
  Key finding: Antenatal depression is associated with placental accelerated aging, demonstrating that maternal psychological stress also produces epigenetic clock deviations.

- **Huff KK et al.** (2025). *Cells* 14(4):306. PMID: 39996777.  
  Key finding: "Prenatal exposure to metals can influence fetal programming via DNA methylation…Epigenetic clocks estimate the biological age of a given tissue based on DNA methylation and are [predictive of outcomes]."

## 5. Placental Epigenetics as Timing Markers

### Can placental tissue reveal WHEN during pregnancy an insult occurred?
**EMERGING — YES for early- vs. late-onset preeclampsia; not yet for precise week-level timing.**

- **Herzog EM et al.** (2017). *Placenta* 58:122–132. PMID: 28962690.  
  Key finding: "Early- and late-onset preeclampsia [produce different] tissue-specific epigenome[s] of the placenta and newborn." This is critical — it demonstrates that the **timing** of the hypoxic insult (early PE <34 weeks vs. late PE ≥34 weeks) produces distinguishable methylation patterns.

- **Novakovic B et al.** (2017). *FASEB J* 31(6):2380–2392. PMID: 28223336.  
  Key finding: DAXX gene methylation shows dynamic changes during placental trophoblast differentiation, preeclampsia, and hypoxia response — suggesting that methylation patterns at specific loci could serve as temporal markers.

- **Clark J et al.** (2022). *Epigenetics* 17(13):2389–2403. PMID: 36134874.  
  Key finding: Placental epigenetic gestational age (eGA) can differ from chronological gestational age, and this deviation carries information about the nature and timing of prenatal adversity.

- **Gerber A et al.** (2025). *Pediatr Res* 98(5):1780–1788. PMID: 40335643.  
  Key finding: Placental eGA acceleration predicts adolescent blood pressure, confirming that placental epigenetic marks capture timing-relevant programming information.

### Implications for timing resolution
Current evidence supports:
- **Binary discrimination:** Early vs. late-onset PE (before/after 34 weeks) — YES, achievable
- **Trimester-level resolution:** First vs. second vs. third trimester insults — LIKELY achievable with multi-locus panels
- **Week-level precision:** Specific gestational week of hypoxic insult — NOT YET achievable, but theoretically possible with larger reference datasets and organ-specific methylation profiles

## 6. HIF-Dependent Epigenetic Changes

### Does HIF activation leave permanent epigenetic marks?
**YES — HIF is an epigenetic master switch.**

- **Watson CJ et al.** (2014). *Hum Mol Genet* 23(8):2176–88. PMID: 24301681.  
  Key finding: Hypoxia induces epigenetic modifications in cardiac fibroblasts through HIF-dependent pathways, driving myofibroblast differentiation. DNMT inhibition reverses some of these changes, confirming they are mediated by DNA methylation.

- **Zar Kyaw T et al.** (2019). *J Hum Genet* 64(8):795–801. PMID: 31138846.  
  Key finding: HIF3A methylation in newborn blood spots associates with birth weight-for-gestational age — HIF pathway genes themselves carry methylation marks of past hypoxic exposure.

- **Kim GH et al.** (2011). *Pulm Circ* 1(3):347–56. PMID: 22140624.  
  Key finding: Epigenetic mechanisms in pulmonary hypertension include HIF-dependent silencing of superoxide dismutase 2 (SOD2) via methylation — a mechanism by which fetal hypoxia could program adult pulmonary vascular disease.

- **Geismann C, Arlt A** (2020). *Cells* 9(11):2353. PMID: 33113836.  
  Key finding: "Hypoxia also modulates diverse areas of epigenetic mechanisms like non-coding RNAs, histone modifications or DNA methylation, which cooperate with the hypoxia-induced transcription factors."

### HIF-epigenetic mechanism summary
1. **HIF-1α stabilization** under hypoxic conditions → direct binding to hypoxia response elements (HREs)
2. **HIF recruits TET enzymes** → active DNA demethylation at specific loci
3. **HIF competes with DNMT3A/3B** at some promoters → passive demethylation
4. **HIF activates histone demethylases** (KDM4A/JMJD2A, KDM3A/JHDM2A) → chromatin remodeling
5. **HIF-dependent microRNAs** (miR-210 is the "hypoxiamir") → post-transcriptional silencing
6. Result: **Permanent epigenetic remodeling** at hypoxia-responsive loci that persists after oxygen normalization — a molecular "fossil record" of past hypoxia.

# SYNTHESIS: Implications for the Alignment Model

## The convergent picture

The alignment model proposes that gestational oxygen deprivation during specific developmental windows produces organ-specific injuries that later manifest as "idiopathic" adult diseases. The PubMed evidence overwhelmingly supports this framework:

### 1. The timing-specificity principle is validated
Different organs have different vulnerability windows, and the evidence confirms that the specific organ injured depends on WHEN during gestation the hypoxic insult occurs:
- **Weeks 6–20:** Thymus → autoimmune predisposition
- **Weeks 8–20:** Heart → cardiomyocyte deficit → early CVD
- **Weeks 20–34:** Pancreas → beta-cell deficit → lean T2DM
- **Weeks 20–34:** Cerebellum → DCD/dyspraxia
- **Weeks 24–36:** Lungs → alveolar arrest → adult respiratory disease
- **Weeks 28–36:** White matter/OPCs → ADHD
- **Weeks 28–40:** Hippocampus → epilepsy, anxiety/depression
- **Weeks 32–36:** Kidneys → nephron deficit → hypertension, CKDu

### 2. The "idiopathic" label may mask a common etiology
Across 10 conditions reviewed, the evidence suggests that a significant fraction of "idiopathic" cases may share a common origin: gestational oxygen deprivation producing organ-specific developmental arrest. The conditions classified as having the strongest evidence (hypertension, CVD, T2DM, ADHD, lung disease) are also the most prevalent "idiopathic" adult diseases globally.

### 3. Epigenetic biomarkers offer a path to retrospective validation
The alignment model's most significant limitation — that most births lack continuous oxygenation monitoring — can potentially be overcome through:
- **HIF3A methylation** on archived Guthrie cards as a hypoxia marker
- **Cord blood EWAS** for organ-specific pathway enrichment
- **Placental eGA acceleration** as a developmental timing marker
- **Epigenetic clocks** for detection of biological aging acceleration

### 4. The testable prediction
If the alignment model is correct, individuals diagnosed with "essential" hypertension, lean T2DM, cryptogenic epilepsy, or ADHD should show:
1. Lower birthweight or markers of IUGR
2. Epigenetic signatures of gestational hypoxia (HIF3A methylation, specific CpG patterns)
3. Organ-specific methylation enrichment corresponding to their diagnosed condition
4. Epigenetic age acceleration at birth (if cord blood/placental tissue is available)

This is a testable, falsifiable hypothesis amenable to retrospective cohort study using existing biobanked samples.
