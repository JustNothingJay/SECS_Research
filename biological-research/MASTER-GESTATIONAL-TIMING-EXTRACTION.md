## Week-by-Week Blocks

### Weeks 4–8: Cardiac Septation Only
- **Active systems:** Cardiac septation (neural crest migration)
- **HIF role:** HIF-1α is ESSENTIAL — required for normal cardiac morphogenesis
- **Disruption outcome:** CHD (VSD, ASD, ToF) — immediate structural defects, NOT latent
- **Evidence:** Llurba Olive et al. 2018 — "O₂ is a Janus gas" required for development

### Weeks 8–12: Cardiac Closing, Immune Opening
- **Active systems:** Cardiac septation completing (3→8wk), Immune thymic seeding begins (8–9wk T-cells)
- **HIF role:** HIF-1α still dominant exclusively
- **Key event:** Thymocyte seeding — first T-cell precursors migrate to thymic anlage (formed at 6wk)
- **Disruption outcome:** Cardiac defects (late), early immune pathway disruption

### Weeks 12–16: Immune Expansion + Retinal Onset
- **Active systems:** Immune (DP thymocyte expansion), Retinal (RGC specification + retinal vascularisation begins at 14wk)
- **New entry:** HIF-2α begins rising alongside HIF-1α
- **Key events:** Paternal germline de novo methylation (12–22wk overlaps), male germline consolidation window opens
- **Disruption outcome:** Immune repertoire narrowing, retinal vascular maldevelopment

### Weeks 16–20: Critical Transition — Pre-Symptomatic PE Established
- **Active systems:** 3–5 (Immune + Retinal + Renal beginning + Cardiac myocyte proliferation)
- **CRITICAL:** Preeclamptic pathology (defective spiral artery remodelling) is ESTABLISHED by 18–20 wk — symptoms lag by weeks to months
- **Key events:** PE Stage 1 pathology complete; nephron generation accelerating; pancreatic β-cell programming beginning
- **Iron:** Maternal iron demand increasing — pseudohypoxia risk begins if deficient
- **Two-stage model of PE (Extension Track 6):** Stage 1 = preclinical defective remodelling GW 8–18; clinical symptoms lag to >20 wk

### Weeks 20–24: Five Systems Open, EOPE Can Begin
- **Active systems:** 5+ (WM preOLs entering, Hippocampal peak beginning, Immune continuing, Renal accelerating, Pancreatic opening)
- **EOPE onset zone opens** — clinical preeclampsia symptoms may first appear
- **Key events:** PreOL population beginning to dominate periventricular white matter; CA1 pyramidal neurons entering peak vulnerability; nephron generation at high rate
- **HIF complexity:** Both HIF-1α and HIF-2α now active with distinct tissue-specific roles

### Weeks 24–28: THE SUPER-WINDOW — ALL 10 SYSTEMS SIMULTANEOUS
- **Active systems:** **10/10 at maximum vulnerability**
- **Week 28 = "Ground Zero"**
- **What converges simultaneously at this point:**
  1. Maximum organ vulnerability — 9–10 systems in construction phase
  2. Maximum PHD counter-regulatory load — managing HIF across more tissues than any other time
  3. Maximum fetal iron demand — cofactor availability may be limiting PHD function
  4. HIF isoform complexity — all three isoforms active (HIF-1α, HIF-2α, HIF-3α)
  5. EOPE expressing clinical symptoms — placental insufficiency producing episodic hypoxia
  6. Autonomic convergent vulnerability (SIDS risk) — all three autonomic systems at peak
  7. Paternal germline consolidation window active
- **A single hypoxic event here produces:**
  - Speech delay (visible at 3 yr)
  - Motor deficit (visible at 5 yr)
  - Learning disability (visible at 7 yr)
  - Anxiety disorder (visible at 12 yr)
  - Hypertension (visible at 35 yr)
  - Metabolic syndrome (visible at 45 yr)

**Sources:** Paper 2 (Super-Window), gestational-HIF-dependency-developmental-timeline, SIDS paper

### Weeks 28–32: Super-Window Peak — WM at MAXIMUM Risk
- **PreOL → immature OL transition** = the most critical myelination event
- **Purkinje cells at peak vulnerability**
- **PreOL vulnerability statistics (Back et al. 2002):** 72 ± 6% death rate of preOLs in ischaemic tissue
- **"Full combination" injury** — all systems simultaneously affected
- **HIF switch becomes critical:** Acute vasospasm = HIF-1α dominant; Chronic PE = HIF-2α; Oscillating = both cycling = **WORST outcome**
- **PHD3 negative feedback at maximum:** Oscillation produces maximal molecular injury through HIF cycling

### Weeks 32–36: Closing Phase
- **Nephron window CLOSES at 36 wk** — zero new nephrons ever generated after this point
- **HbF → HbA transition begins** (~32 wk) — itself a vulnerability event (changing oxygen affinity)
- **Cortical subplate window closes** (~32 wk)
- **Remaining active windows:** WM (declining), Hippocampal (declining), Cerebellar (continuing), Pancreatic (declining), Pulmonary (continuing), Immune (continuing)
- **LOPE onset zone** — late-onset preeclampsia from placental senescence

### Weeks 36–40: Preparing for Birth
- **Most windows closed** — 4–5 systems still active (Cerebellar, Pulmonary, Hepatic, Cardiac myocytes completing)
- **Iron stores being consolidated** for postnatal life
- **PHD maturation** — preparing for the massive counter-regulatory challenge of birth

### Birth: Counter-Regulatory CRISIS
- **PaO₂ jumps 30 → 80–100 mmHg** — the most dramatic oxygen change in the lifespan
- **PHDs must activate massively** to degrade HIF-1α in the new high-O₂ environment
- **Preterm birth = TRIPLE vulnerability:**
  1. HIF dysregulation (immature PHD2)
  2. Oxidative injury (inadequate antioxidant enzymes)
  3. Developmental arrest (preOLs still in vulnerable stage)
- **The "Goldilocks Zone":** OPCs adapted to chronic hypoxia; abrupt hyperoxia → ROS → OPC injury; narrow optimal window especially for PE-exposed infants

**Sources:** gestational-HIF-dependency-developmental-timeline (Section 4), Extension Track 5

## 4C. Language-Critical Tracts — The Latent Failure at Demand Spike

| Tract | Function | Status at Birth | When Language NEEDS It | What Happens If Damaged |
|-------|----------|----------------|------------------------|-------------------------|

**The "Growing Into Deficit" Mechanism:**
1. Perinatal OPC injury → preOL maturation arrest (72% death rate + arrested survivors)
2. Tracts are unmyelinated at birth even in healthy infants → damage is SILENT
3. Developmental program calls on tracts at demand spike (2–4 years for language)
4. Arrested OPCs cannot generate adequate myelination → VISIBLE DEFICIT EMERGES
5. This is NOT a new injury — it is existing damage UNMASKED by functional demand

## 5B. The Autistic Regression Timeline — Mapped to Myelination Demand

| Age | Behavioural Observation | Myelination Event | Proposed Mechanism |
|-----|-------------------------|--------------------|--------------------|
| **6 months** | Gaze, smiles, vocalisations COMPARABLE to controls (Ozonoff 2010) | No language tracts yet required — CST myelinating normally | Damage is present but SILENT — tracts not needed yet |
| **6–12 months** | **Declining trajectories begin** (detectable prospectively) | Early association tract demands emerging | First signs of disconnect between intact cortex and undermyelinated connections |
| **12–18 months** | Single words may appear → then plateau or decline | SLF beginning to be needed for articulation | Ventral stream (simpler circuits) can support single words; dorsal stream (AF) not yet required |
| **15–24 months** | **Peak regression window** — loss of words, social withdrawal | **AF demand spike** — complex syntax requires myelinated dorsal stream | Arrested OPCs CANNOT generate AF myelination → language fails at demand spike |
| **2–4 years** | Language delay, social deficits become clinically apparent | AF, SLF, UF all expected to be actively myelinating | "Growing into deficit" — damage present since birth, visible only now |
| **4+ years** | Improvement begins (with therapy) | Activity-dependent myelination begins to rescue some connections | Therapy = intensive neuronal activity → drives OPC differentiation → partial myelination recovery |

**Key insight from Wolff et al. 2012 (DTI):** Infants who later developed ASD showed **HIGHER FA at 6 months** but **LOWER FA at 24 months** across 12/15 white matter tracts — consistent with an initial aberrant development followed by failure to myelinate normally.

**Key insight from Travers et al. 2012 (DTI meta-analysis):** White matter differences in ASD become **MORE pronounced with age**, not less — consistent with a maturation arrest model where the deficit worsens as tracts that should myelinate fail to do so.

# 7. EOPE vs LOPE DIFFERENTIAL OUTCOMES

| Feature | EOPE (<34 wk) | LOPE (≥34 wk) |
|---|---|---|
| **Pathology** | Defective spiral artery remodelling | Placental senescence |
| **HIF pattern** | Chronic, sustained HIF activation | Subacute, fluctuating HIF |
| **Primary HIF isoform** | HIF-2α (chronic hypoxia → isoform switch) | Mixed HIF-1α/HIF-2α |
| **Organ targeting** | Multi-organ — ALL systems in super-window | WM/cerebellar-biased (fewer systems open) |
| **WM injury** | Severe and diffuse | Moderate, more focal |
| **Nephron loss** | Significant (peak nephron generation during EOPE) | Minimal (window closing/closed) |
| **Cardiac programming** | Significant (myocyte window fully open) | Less significant |
| **HIF3A methylation** | Likely higher (longer hypoxic exposure) | Lower |
| **Offspring risk profile** | Multi-system: ASD + hypertension + CKD + T2DM + CVD | Primarily neurodevelopmental: ASD-like, cerebellar deficits |
| **Oscillating PE** | WORST outcome: both HIF-1α and HIF-2α cycling → maximal injury | Less relevant |

```
Hit 1 (Prenatal): Placental insufficiency → chronic fetal hypoxia → OPC maturation arrest
                                    ↓
        OPC population enters postnatal life COMPROMISED and VULNERABLE
                                    ↓
```

# 11. BIRTH TRANSITION VULNERABILITY

## 11A. The Oxygen Transition

| Parameter | Fetal | Postnatal (term) | Change |
|---|---|---|---|
| PaO₂ | 25–35 mmHg | 80–100 mmHg | **3–4× increase** |
| SpO₂ | 60–70% | 95–100% | +30–40% |
| Tissue pO₂ | 2–8 mmHg | 20–40 mmHg | 3–10× increase |
| HbF P50 | ~19 mmHg | HbA: ~27 mmHg | O₂ affinity drops (HbF→HbA transition begins ~32 wk) |

## 11B. Cord Clamping and the Ventilation Gap

- **DCC ≥60s evidence:** APTS trial (1,566 infants) — DCC reduced mortality 6.4% vs 9.0% in 24–27 wk subgroup; Cochrane 2024: mortality RR 0.73; Improved fine motor at 4 yr (Andersson 2015), especially boys
- **UCM WARNING:** PREMOD2 stopped early — IVH 22% vs 6% in 23–27 wk subgroup. **Do not use in <28 wk infants**
- **PBCC (physiological-based cord clamping):** Clamp only AFTER spontaneous breathing established

## 11C. FiO₂ and SpO₂ Targets

- Current recommendations: Start at FiO₂ 21–30% for preterm <35 wk
- SpO₂ targets: 60–65% at 1 min → 85–95% at 10 min (allowing physiological transition)
- **The Goldilocks Zone:** PE-exposed OPCs are adapted to chronic hypoxia → abrupt hyperoxia → ROS → OPC injury
- **NeOProM meta-analysis:** Lower SpO₂ (85–89%) → increased mortality RR 1.17; Higher SpO₂ (91–95%) → more ROP

## 11D. Intermittent Hypoxaemic Episodes (IHE)

- Defined as SpO₂ ≤80% for ≥10 seconds but ≤3 minutes
- **Duration threshold (Poets 2015):** Only prolonged episodes ≥1 minute significantly associated with outcomes (RR 1.53). Brief dips NOT independently harmful (RR 1.01)
- **Highest burden infants:** 3× cognitive/language risk (Poets 2020)
- Each desaturation-resaturation cycle generates ROS pulse from reoxygenation
- **Oscillation is WORSE than sustained hypoxia** (Ramond 2013) — generates more oxidative damage

## 11E. Caffeine Neuroprotection

- **CAP trial (Schmidt 2007):** 2,006 infants — caffeine reduced death/disability OR 0.77; CP reduced OR 0.58; cognitive delay reduced OR 0.81
- **11-year follow-up (Schmidt 2017):** Motor benefit persisted OR 0.66 (P=0.009)
- **Mechanisms:** Reduces IHE frequency, A₂A receptor antagonism, antioxidant, anti-inflammatory
- **Earlier initiation = better:** Within 2 hours of NICU admission recommended

## 11F. Wellmann's "Too Much or Too Little" Model

- **Hypoxia → HIF stabilisation → OPC maturation arrest → diffuse hypomyelination**
- **Hyperoxia → HIF degradation → OPC death → focal cystic PVL**
- **Oscillation between both** → triggers BOTH injury mechanisms in alternation → OPC in perpetually destabilised state

# 12. HIF ISOFORM SWITCHING & COUNTER-REGULATION

## 12A. HIF Isoform Developmental Phases

| Phase | Timing | Dominant Isoform | Role |
|---|---|---|---|
| Phase 1: Embryonic | 3–10 wk | HIF-1α exclusively | Essential for organogenesis; cardiac septation, angiogenesis |
| Phase 2: Fetal | 10–34 wk | HIF-1α dominant + HIF-2α rising | Tissue-specific differentiation; EPO switches to HIF-2α control |
| Phase 3: Transitional | 34+ wk | HIF-2α dominant | Preparing for birth; chronic adaptations |

## 12B. HIF Switch in Preeclampsia

| PE Pattern | Dominant HIF | Molecular Injury Type |
|---|---|---|
| Acute vasospasm | HIF-1α | Acute glycolytic shift, immediate stress response |
| EOPE (chronic) | HIF-2α | Chronic adaptation genes, vascular remodelling failure |
| LOPE (mild) | Mixed | Subdued response |
| **Oscillating PE** | **Both cycling** | **WORST outcome — maximal injury from HIF cycling** |

## 12C. PHD Counter-Regulation System

| Component | Function | Key Characteristic |
|---|---|---|
| **PHD2** | Dominant O₂ sensor; marks HIF-1α for degradation | KO = embryonic lethal; dominant throughout gestation |
| **PHD3** | HIF-1α-INDUCIBLE negative feedback amplifier | Creates oscillation sensitivity — hypoxia → HIF-1α → PHD3 upregulation → HIF-1α degradation → PHD3 falls → cycle repeats |
| **FIH** | Asparagine hydroxylase; fine-tunes HIF transcriptional activity | Different O₂ threshold than PHDs → graded response |
| **VHL** | E3 ubiquitin ligase; physical degrader of hydroxylated HIF-1α | Loss → VHL disease (renal cancer, haemangioblastoma) |

## 12D. PHD vs FIH — Different Thresholds, Different Genes

| O₂ Level | PHD Status | FIH Status | HIF Gene Activation |
|---|---|---|---|
| Normoxia | Active → HIF degraded | Active → HIF silenced | None |
| **Mild hypoxia** | **Partially inhibited** | **Still active** | N-TAD genes ONLY (partial HIF programme) |
| **Severe hypoxia** | **Fully inhibited** | **Also inhibited** | **N-TAD + C-TAD genes (FULL HIF programme)** |

**Clinical implication:** Mild placental insufficiency → partial HIF activation (different genes than severe insufficiency) → **qualitatively different injury**, not just quantitatively less.

## 12E. HIF3A Methylation Self-Amplifying Loop

```
Gestational hypoxia → HIF-1α accumulation
              ↓
HIF-1α → upregulates epigenetic enzymes (DNMTs, TETs)
              ↓
Epigenetic enzymes → methylate HIF3A promoter
              ↓
HIF3A methylation → reduced HIF-3α expression
              ↓
Loss of HIF-3α "brake" → HIF-1α/2α amplified
              ↓
MORE HIF → MORE epigenetic modification → MORE HIF3A silencing
              ↓
              SELF-AMPLIFYING INJURY LOOP
```

# 13. IRON–PHD–HIF–OPC CONVERGENCE TIMELINE

## 13A. Fetal Iron Timeline

| Gestational Window | Iron Demand | WHY Iron Matters for HIF |
|---|---|---|
| Wk 4–12 | Minimal | PHDs require Fe²⁺ as catalytic cofactor |
| Wk 12–20 | Increasing | Iron needed for haem synthesis (HbF production ramping) |
| Wk 20–28 | **HIGH** — pseudohypoxia risk begins if maternal iron deficient | PHDs need iron to function; iron-deficient PHDs cannot degrade HIF; RESULT = pseudohypoxia without actual O₂ reduction |
| Wk 28–36 | **MAXIMUM** — 80% of term iron stores transferred in this window | Quadruple iron demand (PHD function + myelin synthesis + Hb synthesis + cytochrome enzymes) |
| Wk 36–40 | Consolidation | Iron stores being finalised for postnatal life |
| Preterm birth | **ZERO further supply** — whatever was transferred = total stores | Preterm infants start with 20–50% of term iron stores → PHD impairment from birth → prolonged pseudohypoxia risk |

## 13B. The Iron-PHD-HIF-OPC Quadruple Hit at Weeks 28–32

At the super-window, iron is simultaneously needed for FOUR things:

1. **PHD enzymatic function** → HIF-1α counter-regulation → preventing developmental arrest
2. **Myelin lipid synthesis** → direct substrate for OPC maturation → myelination
3. **Haemoglobin synthesis** → oxygen carrying capacity → preventing actual hypoxia
4. **Cytochrome enzymes** → mitochondrial function → cellular energy for developmental processes

Iron deficiency at this point = QUADRUPLE hit: less O₂ carried, less O₂ sensed, less myelin substrate, less cellular energy.

## 13C. Global Scale of Iron Deficiency

- **40–60% of pregnancies globally** have suboptimal iron status (WHO 2023)
- This = MASSIVE population exposed to PHD impairment → pseudohypoxia
- Countries with highest iron deficiency rates should show highest rates of alignment-model-predicted conditions
- **This is the pseudohypoxia extension** — testable with existing epidemiological data

# 15. OSCILLATION, CYCLING, AND VARIABLE SYMPTOM EXPRESSION

## 15A. Molecular Oscillation Mechanisms

| Oscillation Type | Mechanism | Why It Makes Injury WORSE |
|---|---|---|
| **HIF-1α oscillation** | Hypoxic dip → HIF stabilisation → arrest + reoxygenation → PHD reactivation → HIF degradation → ROS from reoxygenation | Each cycle = maturation arrest + oxidative damage simultaneously |
| **PHD3 oscillation amplifier** | HIF-1α → induces PHD3 → PHD3 degrades HIF-1α → PHD3 expression falls → HIF-1α rises again → cycle repeats | Creates oscillatory dynamics with intrinsic instability |
| **HIF isoform cycling** | Acute events: HIF-1α; Chronic adaptation: HIF-2α; Oscillating PE: both cycling | Different gene sets activated in alternation → maximal molecular disruption |
| **IL-1β → HIF-1α → IL-1β loop** | Self-sustaining inflammatory-pseudohypoxic positive feedback | Once initiated, this cycle is SELF-PERPETUATING without external input |
| **IHE reoxygenation oscillation** | Each SpO₂ dip → hypoxia → HIF stabilisation; each recovery → ROS burst from reoxygenation | Intermittent > sustained for same total hypoxic burden (Ramond 2013) |
| **Wellmann's dual damage** | Hypoxia → HIF maturation arrest; Hyperoxia → HIF degradation + OPC death | Oscillation triggers BOTH pathways in alternation — worst possible scenario |

## 15B. How Oscillation Maps to Variable Symptom Expression in ASD

| Clinical Observation | Potential Oscillation Mechanism |
|---|---|
| "Good days and bad days" | Ongoing OPC maturation attempts vs. residual inflammatory/oxidative cycling |
| Skills appear then disappear | Partial myelination → conduction temporarily achieved → myelin fails → conduction lost |
| Regression followed by partial recovery | HIF cycling during vulnerability window → patchy myelination arrest → activity-dependent myelination partially rescues some circuits |
| Different symptoms at different ages | Different tracts myelinate on different schedules → demands exceed reserve at different times |
| Temperature sensitivity | Fever → metabolic O₂ demand increase → relative hypoxia in borderline-myelinated circuits → transient functional loss |
| Fatigue-associated worsening | Metabolic competition → reduced O₂/glucose to borderline circuits → conduction fails |
| Improvement with therapy but plateau | Activity-dependent myelination partially compensates → but arrested OPCs have ceiling → plateau when available OPCs exhausted |

## 15C. The "Forming vs Formed" Framework for Variable Expression

| Phase | OPC/Myelination Status | Symptom Character |
|---|---|---|
| **"Forming" (active myelination)** | OPCs proliferating, some differentiating, activity-dependent myelination active | Variable — good days/bad days; responsive to therapy; skill gains possible |
| **"Formed" (myelination complete or arrested)** | OPC pool depleted or arrested; myelin maintenance replaces formation | Stable — symptoms plateau; intervention relies on compensatory mechanisms; remyelination thinner and less efficient |

**Evidence for lifelong activity-dependent myelination:** de Faria et al. 2019 — "throughout life"; Fields 2020 — "across the lifespan"; BUT efficiency declines with age and OPC pool size

| Fetal (23–36 wk) | OPCs injured; preOLs arrested | Latent — no symptoms |
| Infancy (0–2 yr) | Reduced pool; some catch-up via activity-dependent myelination | Motor delay, potential regression |
| Childhood (2–12 yr) | Partially compensated; therapy drives activity-dependent myelination | Language delay → improvement; "forming" phase advantages |
| Adolescence (12–20 yr) | Frontal tracts completing myelination; improvement continues | Social/executive improvements; ADHD convergence |
| Adulthood (20–40 yr) | Cingulum/UF completing; OPC pool declining but adequate for maintenance | Social functioning improvements |
| Mid-life (40–60 yr) | **Smaller OPC pool reaches maintenance threshold earlier** | Accelerated cognitive decline begins |
| Late life (60+ yr) | **OPC pool depleted → myelin maintenance fails → demyelination** | **Dementia onset earlier than unaffected cohort** |

## 18B. Why Long-Term Memory Is Preserved While Short-Term Memory Fails

| Memory Type | Myelin Status | Why Affected/Spared |
|---|---|---|
| **Long-term memories** | Encoded in circuits myelinated decades ago; robust, mature myelin | Preserved — old myelin degrades slowly |
| **Short-term/working memory** | Requires ACTIVE myelination of new/modified connections | **IMPAIRED — current OPC pool too depleted to support new circuit myelination** |
| **Procedural memory** | Deep, heavily auto-myelinated circuits | Variably preserved — depends on circuit maturity |

## 18C. The Bartzokis Myelin Model of Alzheimer's Disease

- Late-myelinating regions (frontal, temporal association cortices) are FIRST to develop Alzheimer's pathology
- These are the same regions with the MOST protracted myelination windows
- Age-related myelin breakdown PRECEDES and PROMOTES amyloid deposition
- **Prediction:** Individuals with gestational OPC depletion should develop AD pathology ~10–20 years earlier

**Supporting evidence:**
- Chen et al. 2021: OPCs among most severely affected cell types in AD brain (single-nucleus RNA-seq)
- Nasrabady et al. 2018: WM degeneration is consistent and EARLY feature of AD
- Mot et al. 2024: Age-related OPC depletion in mouse models → cognitive deficits; OPC augmentation partially rescues

**Sources:** Extension Track 5 (Section 10 — Dementia and Lifelong Myelin Maintenance)

## Three-Level Cascade Model

```
Level 1 (Foundational): Spiral artery remodelling failure → Anti-angiogenic cascade → Chronic placental insufficiency
                                                                        ↓
Level 2 (Chronic Loading): Maternal HTN, Anaemia, Obesity, Renal disease, Age → Elevated baseline sFlt-1 → Reduced reserve
                                                                        ↓
Level 3 (Intermittent Triggers): Supine sleep, OSA, Stress, Infection, Exercise → Episodic hypoxic spikes during super-window
```

**The "Everyday Preeclampsia" Concept:** Clinical PE is the visible tail of a distribution. SUBCLINICAL anti-angiogenic activation is common. Individual triggers are each subclinical — they become pathological only when they STACK during the 24–32 wk super-window.

# APPENDIX C: COMPLETE FETAL OXYGEN PHYSIOLOGY

| Parameter | Fetal Value | Adult Value | Ratio |
|---|---|---|---|
| PaO₂ | 25–35 mmHg | 80–100 mmHg | ~30% of adult |
| SpO₂ | 60–70% | 95–100% | ~65% of adult |
| Tissue pO₂ | 2–8 mmHg | 20–40 mmHg | 10–15% of adult |
| Haemoglobin type | HbF (P50 ~19 mmHg) | HbA (P50 ~27 mmHg) | Higher O₂ affinity compensates |
| HbF → HbA transition | Begins ~32 wk | Complete by ~6 months postnatal | Transition itself = vulnerability |

**Key principle:** Low oxygen IS the developmental signal. Fetal tissue pO₂ of 2–8 mmHg is NECESSARY for HIF-1α-mediated organogenesis. The damage occurs not from hypoxia per se, but from ABNORMAL hypoxia — too deep, too long, oscillating, or in the wrong window.
