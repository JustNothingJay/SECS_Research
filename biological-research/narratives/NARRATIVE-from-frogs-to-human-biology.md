## Preface

This document is not a scientific paper. It is the story of a chain of thought — how years of studying biological systems under stress, combined with a deeply personal experience, led to a research hypothesis about gestational oxygen timing and human developmental injury.

The path ran from amphibian ecology through information theory, through a software system that models collapse as creation, through a neonatal intensive care unit, and into a body of biological research that now spans eight synthesis papers, 34 documents, and 900+ citations.

None of the ideas below are original in isolation. Every piece of evidence cited was published by someone else. What is original is the alignment — the recognition that these separate fields are describing the same underlying physics, and that nature had been demonstrating the mechanism for decades if you knew where to look.

## 1. The Frog as Sentinel

I had been studying amphibian population dynamics for years — not as a biologist, but as someone building a software system (SECS Sovereign) that models how complex systems survive mutation, entropy, and collapse. The frog was the mascot. The biology was the metaphor. Or so I thought.

Amphibians are the most severely affected vertebrate class on Earth. Thirty-two percent of all species are globally threatened. Forty-three percent are declining. The extinction rate is 211 times the background rate — and if currently endangered species are included, it rises to 25,000–45,000 times (McCallum, 2007). This is not a slow fade. This is a system under catastrophic entropy injection.

What caught my attention was not the decline itself, but the *mechanisms*. Specifically:

**The damage is indirect.** Atrazine — the most widely used herbicide in the United States — causes hermaphroditism in frogs at 0.1 parts per billion, thirty times below the EPA safe drinking water standard (Hayes et al., 2002). But atrazine doesn't poison the frog directly. It increases aromatase activity, converting androgens to oestrogens. It disrupts *hormonal information signalling* — it injects entropy into the developmental programme itself.

**The cascades are multi-step.** Rohr et al. (2008, *Nature*) showed that atrazine kills phytoplankton, which lets light penetrate, which grows periphyton, which feeds snails, which amplify trematode parasites, which cause limb deformities in frogs. The contaminant and the deformity are separated by four trophic levels. No one looking at the deformed frog would trace the cause back to a herbicide applied to a field upstream.

**Early warning signals exist but are ignored.** Fluctuating asymmetry — subtle left-right differences in body structure — was detectable in frog populations sixteen years before observable die-offs. Developmental entropy signals collapse a decade before anyone sees dead frogs.

**Recovery is asymmetric.** Entropy injection is fast; entropy reduction is thermodynamically uphill. It takes years to destroy population homeostasis and decades to restore it — if restoration is possible at all (Knapp et al., 2016).

These were not just ecological observations. They were *principles*.

## 2. The Chernobyl Frogs: Collapse as Creation

The finding that changed my thinking came from Car et al. (2023, *BMC Biology*): tree frogs in the Chernobyl Exclusion Zone showed impaired metabolism, small population sizes, and accumulated stop-gained mutations — direct evidence of genetic entropy under chronic radiation and chemical stress.

But they were alive.

They were not the same frogs that had lived there before the disaster. They were something new. Their genomes had absorbed mutations, routed around the lethal ones, and continued. They did not recover to their pre-contamination state. They became a novel population shaped by the constraints imposed by catastrophe.

In the software system I was building, I had been modelling exactly this process: how does a system survive mutation without rejecting all change? The answer I had arrived at — through pure abstract reasoning about collapse algebra and admissibility functions — was that the system needed to *decompose* each mutation into its structural contribution (novel valid paths, adaptive capacity) and its entropy payload (disorder that degrades existing paths). The system absorbs the first and discards the second.

The Chernobyl frogs were doing this at the genomic level.

This was the moment the metaphor stopped being a metaphor.

## 3. The Bridge: From Entropy to Oxygen

The software system I built models something called *monotonic contraction* — the principle that when a system absorbs stress, its state space contracts irreversibly. It does not return to its previous state. What survives inside the contracted space is more fit for the environment that caused the contraction, but the contraction itself cannot be undone.

The biological equivalent is developmental programming. A fetus exposed to environmental stress during a critical window does not "recover" — its developmental programme is permanently altered. The organism that emerges is functional, but architecturally different from what it would have been without the insult. The neonatal brain, for instance, can reorganise after injury so that functions normally handled by damaged areas are taken over by undamaged regions. The result is not a restoration to the original plan — it is a novel architecture that evolved under constraint (Johnston, 2009).

The question that linked the frog research to human biology was simple: **what is the contaminant, and what is the developmental programme it disrupts?**

For frogs, it was atrazine disrupting aromatase-mediated hormonal signalling.

For human fetuses, the answer had been sitting in front of me for years, because of what happened to my own family.

## 4. The Personal Case

My child was born following a pregnancy complicated by preeclampsia. Preeclampsia is fundamentally a placental disease — abnormal spiral artery remodelling leads to poor placental perfusion, which releases anti-angiogenic factors into the maternal circulation, causing hypertension and organ damage. The fetal effects are mediated through placental insufficiency: chronic hypoxia, fetal growth restriction, inflammatory exposure, oxidative stress, and altered developmental programming.

After birth, there were oxygen desaturations requiring intervention in the neonatal ICU. The transition from intrauterine to extrauterine life is the most complex physiological transition in human existence — a newborn at sixty percent oxygen saturation at one minute is normal, not hypoxic (Dawson et al.). The expected trajectory takes minutes, not seconds. But when that trajectory stalls or reverses, intervention is required.

My child is thriving. But the experience left me with questions that the clinical system could not answer:

- Why do children born from complicated pregnancies develop such *different* health problems?
- Why do those problems appear at such *different ages*?
- Why does no clinician connect them, even though they share the same prenatal origin?

The answer, I came to believe, is timing.

## 5. The Oxygen-Timing Insight

The connection between frogs and fetuses is this: **in both systems, the damage is not caused by a single toxic event. It is caused by disruption of an information-signalling pathway during a critical developmental window, and the specific window that is open when the disruption occurs determines the specific injury that results.**

For frogs, atrazine during the sexual differentiation window produces hermaphroditism. The same atrazine at a different developmental stage produces a different outcome — or none at all. The timing matters as much as the dose.

For human fetuses, the signalling pathway is HIF-1α — hypoxia-inducible factor 1-alpha — the master oxygen sensor that controls how cells respond to changes in oxygen availability. Every fetal organ system has a peak oxygen-demand window during development. These windows are staggered — the fetus cannot build all systems simultaneously. And between gestational weeks 23 and 36, nearly all windows overlap simultaneously in what I call the "super-window."

The specific timing of placental oxygen disruption determines which systems sustain injury. A hypoxic insult at week 24 damages different organ progenitors than the same insult at week 32.

This is why the children look different. This is why the problems appear at different ages. This is why no clinician connects them — because the injuries present as separate diseases, at separate times, to separate specialists. Speech delay at three. Learning difficulties at seven. Hypertension at thirty-five. Type 2 diabetes at fifty. All potentially traced back to which developmental windows were open when the placenta failed.

## 6. The Frog Principles in Human Biology

Every principle I had observed in amphibian ecology mapped onto the human gestational oxygen model:

### Indirect Damage

Just as atrazine damages frogs through aromatase disruption rather than direct toxicity, gestational hypoxia damages fetuses through HIF-1α counter-regulation failure rather than simple oxygen starvation. The PHD-VHL-FIH system that regulates HIF-1α depends on iron, ascorbate, and 2-oxoglutarate — all commonly deficient in pregnancy. Iron deficiency alone, affecting 40–60% of pregnancies globally, can trigger the same pathway without measurable oxygen deprivation. This is "pseudohypoxia" — HIF activation without true hypoxia — and it may be the most common gestational insult on Earth.

### Multi-Step Cascades

Just as the path from atrazine to frog deformity passes through four trophic levels, the path from placental insufficiency to childhood speech delay passes through HIF-1α stabilisation, oligodendrocyte precursor cell maturation arrest, failed myelination of specific white matter tracts, and delayed neural circuit integration. No one looking at the child with speech delay traces it back to a placental event at week 28.

### Early Warning Signals

Just as fluctuating asymmetry detects frog population collapse sixteen years early, epigenetic markers — HIF3A methylation, miR-210 elevation, BChE deficiency — may be detectable at birth on routine newborn blood spots (Guthrie cards), years before clinical symptoms emerge. The signal is there. We are not looking for it.

### Asymmetric Recovery

Just as contaminated frog populations do not return to their pre-contamination state, a brain that reorganises after developmental injury does not return to its original architecture. It compensates. It creates novel circuitry under constraint. The child is functional — often remarkably so — but the architecture is permanently different. This is not damage in the traditional sense. It is the biological equivalent of what the Chernobyl frogs demonstrated: the system absorbed the entropy and kept running, but it became something new.

## 7. The SIDS Extension

The most striking application of this framework came when I examined Sudden Infant Death Syndrome through the same lens.

SIDS has been mechanistically unexplained for decades because three systems whose failure produces death — cardiac sympathetic innervation, brainstem serotonergic arousal, and adrenal medullary catecholamine release — have been studied by different research communities using different methods. No one had proposed a single upstream mechanism that damages all three simultaneously.

HIF-1α is that mechanism. Bohuslavova et al. (2019) demonstrated that HIF-1α is required for sympathetic nervous system development. MacFarlane et al. (2016) showed that neonatal hypoxia alters brainstem serotonergic expression. Harrington et al. (2022) showed that butyrylcholinesterase deficiency — a marker of autonomic dysfunction — is measurable at birth in future SIDS victims.

The critical insight was timing. All three autonomic systems pass through their peak HIF-1α-dependent functional maturation during overlapping gestational windows. The convergent vulnerability window spans approximately weeks 24–32. A single hypoxic insult during this window can damage all three systems simultaneously — producing an infant who appears normal but lacks the triple autonomic backup required to survive a sleeping crisis at two to four months of age.

This is the frog principle applied directly: the same contaminant (oxygen disruption), during the same type of critical window (developmental maturation), producing the same type of outcome (latent vulnerability that manifests later under stress).

## 8. What I Learned from the Rejection

All eight papers were submitted to medRxiv in March 2026. All eight were rejected. The reason: "not a clinical research article with new data, therefore out of scope." The rejection was explicitly not a judgment on the merits of the work.

The papers are hypothesis and synthesis papers. They propose a framework and identify testable predictions. They do not contain new experimental data. medRxiv publishes clinical research with new data. I sent theoretical work to an empirical platform.

This is, in its own way, the same lesson the frogs teach: know which window you are in. The work is what it is. The channel was wrong.

## 9. What Remains

The hypothesis generates falsifiable predictions:

1. HIF3A methylation in archived newborn blood spots should differ between SIDS cases and controls.
2. Combined BChE deficiency and HIF3A methylation should identify the highest-risk infants.
3. Preeclampsia onset timing should predict which organ-specific outcomes appear in offspring.
4. LMIC populations transitioning from low to adequate neonatal oxygen management should show declining neurodevelopmental disease rates.
5. The gestational timing of documented hypoxic insults in confirmed SIDS cases should cluster within the 24–32 week convergent window.

These predictions are testable with existing biobanked samples, existing registry data, and existing clinical infrastructure. They require a collaborator with institutional access, not new technology.

The research sits publicly at https://github.com/JustNothingJay/SECS_Research. It started with frogs. It ended — for now — with a model of how gestational oxygen timing programmes human disease across the lifespan.

The frogs were never a metaphor. They were a proof of concept.
