# Adversarial Stress-Test: OPC Maturation Arrest → Autism Model

## Model Under Examination

**Claim:** Autism (or a significant subset of it) results from perinatal OPC maturation arrest causing latent white matter injury that manifests as staggered deficits when different tracts reach their myelination demand spikes.

**Core Mechanism:** Preeclampsia/birth complications → oxidative stress → arrested pre-oligodendrocyte (preOL) maturation → failed myelination of key white matter tracts → staggered emergence of social, language, and cognitive deficits aligned to tract-specific developmental demand windows (e.g., arcuate fasciculus → language 2–4 years; uncinate fasciculus → social cognition 4–8+ years).

---

## 1. Falsification Evidence: What Directly Contradicts the Model?

### 1.1 Grey Matter and Cortical Pathology Findings

The model positions white matter (WM) as the primary substrate. However, substantial evidence implicates grey matter (GM) and cortical architecture as central to autism neuropathology:

- **Stoner et al. (2014)** found focal patches of cortical disorganisation—disrupted laminar cytoarchitecture—in prefrontal and temporal cortex of autistic children at post-mortem. These patches showed abnormal expression of cortical layer markers, pointing to *prenatal cortical migration defects* rather than postnatal myelination failure. If the cortex itself is disorganised during the second trimester (well before myelination begins), the damage precedes and may be independent of any OPC injury. *(Stoner et al., NEJM and subsequent commentaries; cited from training knowledge — primary paper not directly fetchable.)*

- **Cortical overgrowth:** Wikipedia's "Mechanism of autism" article states: "Just after birth, the brains of children with autism tend to grow faster than usual, followed by normal or relatively slower growth in childhood." Brain overgrowth appears "most prominent in the frontal and temporal lobes" and hypotheses include "an excess of neurons that causes local overconnectivity" (Courchesne et al., 2007). This is a *neuronal excess* phenomenon, not a myelin deficit.

- **Limbic system findings:** "Smaller neurons that are more densely packed together" in the limbic system, and "fewer and smaller Purkinje neurons in the cerebellum" are described as characteristic neuropathological findings in autism (Chen et al., 2015, *Annual Review of Pathology*; cited via Wikipedia "Mechanism of autism"). These are neuronal/structural findings that have no clear connection to OPC arrest.

- **Increased astrocytes and microglia** in the cerebral cortex are consistently reported. The Wikipedia "Mechanism of autism" article notes "increased number of astrocytes and microglia in the cerebral cortex" — these glial changes suggest neuroinflammation and may reflect a different glial pathology than OPC-specific injury.

### 1.2 Syndromic Autism: Mechanisms That Bypass White Matter Entirely

Syndromic forms of autism account for up to 20% of cases and have clearly delineated mechanisms that are predominantly *not* white matter based:

**Fragile X Syndrome (FXS):**
- FXS is the most common single-gene cause of autism; 15–60% of individuals with FXS meet criteria for ASD (Wikipedia, "Fragile X syndrome").
- The pathophysiology is *synaptic and dendritic*, not myelination-related: FMRP protein binds approximately 4% of brain mRNA and transports it to synapses. Loss of FMRP causes "abnormal dendritic spines" — they are "more numerous and longer" (Bhatt et al., 2014, *Frontiers in Genetics*).
- The mGluR theory of FXS identifies exaggerated metabotropic glutamate receptor-dependent long-term depression (LTD) as the core mechanism (Bear et al., 2004, *Trends in Neurosciences*).
- GABA pathway downregulation is also implicated — an E/I imbalance mechanism, not a WM mechanism.

**Rett Syndrome:**
- Caused by *MECP2* mutations (95% de novo) on the X chromosome. The mechanism is fundamentally *transcriptional/epigenetic*: MECP2 is involved in transcriptional silencing, chromatin organisation, mRNA splicing (Wikipedia, "Rett syndrome").
- Neurotransmitter systems are the primary substrate: "lower brain levels of norepinephrine" (locus coeruleus), impaired dopamine system (substantia nigra) (Wikipedia, "Rett syndrome").
- Rett is explicitly *not* a white matter disorder. Signs overlap with both autism and cerebral palsy, but the mechanism is gene expression regulation affecting neurotransmitters.
- Crucially, Rett phenotypes are *partially rescuable in adult mice* by re-introducing functional MECP2, suggesting the pathology isn't irreversible structural WM damage.

**Tuberous Sclerosis Complex (TSC):**
- Caused by TSC1/TSC2 mutations → hamartin/tuberin complex → mTOR signalling dysregulation → tumour growth (Wikipedia, "Tuberous sclerosis").
- Approximately 50% of TSC patients have ASD, making it one of the most common genetic causes. ASD is more common with TSC2 mutations, earlier/more severe epilepsy, and higher tuber counts.
- The mechanism is *mTOR-mediated cell growth and cortical tuber formation*, not OPC arrest. The "cortical tubers" and "subependymal nodules" are hamartomatous growths, not myelination defects.
- While some WM involvement exists ("radial white matter tracts hyperintense on T2WI," "cerebral white matter radial migration lines"), this reflects neuronal *migration* defects, not myelination failure.

### 1.3 Synapse-Level Findings

The Wikipedia "Mechanism of autism" article states: "Synapse and dendritic spine growth may be disrupted in autism due to impaired neurexin–neuroligin cell-adhesion signalling or dysregulated synthesis of synaptic proteins. Disrupted synaptic development may also contribute to epilepsy."

De Rubeis et al. (2014, *Nature*) found that "synaptic, transcriptional and chromatin genes" were disrupted in autism. Loss-of-function mutations in genes including SHANK3, SCN2A, and PTEN relate to *synapse function and development*, not oligodendrocyte biology.

**Assessment:** The model's exclusive focus on WM cannot account for these clear GM, cortical, synaptic, and neurotransmitter findings. The model would need to argue these are either downstream of OPC arrest (implausible for prenatal cortical migration defects) or represent a separate subset of autism (possible but dramatically narrows the model's scope).

## 2. The Genetics Problem: Can 60–95% Heritability Be Reconciled?

### 2.1 Scale of the Genetic Evidence

- Heritability of autism is estimated at **60–95%** across studies, with the most recent large study (Sandin et al., 2017, *JAMA*) estimating **83%** (Wikipedia, "Heritability of autism").
- Twin concordance: monozygotic 36–95.7%, dizygotic 0–23.5% (Wikipedia, "Heritability of autism").
- **350–450 genes** predicted to increase susceptibility (Wikipedia, "Heritability of autism").
- **De novo mutations** contribute approximately **30%** of cases (CNVs + point mutations + missense) (Wikipedia, "Causes of autism"); de novo CNVs are approximately **4× more frequent** in autism than controls (Wikipedia, "Heritability of autism").
- Most autism cases are sporadic with no recent family history — potentially explained by spontaneous de novo mutations in sperm/egg (Wikipedia, "Heritability of autism").

### 2.2 What the Genes Actually Do

The critical challenge: most autism-associated genes affect **synapses, not oligodendrocytes**:

- **SHANK3:** Postsynaptic scaffolding protein at glutamatergic synapses.
- **CHD8:** Chromatin remodeller affecting transcription during neurodevelopment.
- **SCN2A:** Sodium channel subunit affecting neuronal excitability.
- **PTEN:** Tumour suppressor regulating mTOR/PI3K pathway; affects cell size and proliferation.
- **SHANK2:** Mutations "act in a dominant fashion and appear to cause hyperconnectivity between the neurons" (Wikipedia, "Heritability of autism") — directly *opposite* to the disconnection predicted by the OPC model.
- **NLGN3/NLGN4:** Neuroligins — postsynaptic cell adhesion molecules at synapses.
- **NRXN1:** Neurexin — presynaptic cell adhesion molecule.

Research cited on the Wikipedia "Autism" page states that autism-associated genes are "involved in key neuronal processes such as protein synthesis, synaptic activity, cell adhesion, and the formation and remodelling of synapses, as well as the regulation of excitatory and inhibitory neurotransmission."

Additionally: "Studies have identified lower expression of genes linked to the inhibitory neurotransmitter gamma-aminobutyric acid, alongside higher expression of genes associated with glial (e.g., astrocytes) and immune (e.g., microglia) cells" — while glial changes are present, they implicate *astrocytes and microglia*, not specifically OPCs.

### 2.3 The Bailey 1995 Finding

A 1995 British twin study concluded: **"obstetric hazards usually appear to be consequences of genetically influenced abnormal development, rather than independent aetiological factors"** (Bailey, 1995, cited via Wikipedia "Heritability of autism").

This directly challenges the OPC model's causal chain. If birth complications (preeclampsia, hypoxia) are *downstream* of the same genetic variants that cause autism — rather than independent causes of OPC injury — then the model's proposed mechanism may be confounding effect with cause.

### 2.4 Can the Model Accommodate Genetics?

The model could argue:
1. **Gene–environment interaction:** Genetic variants create OPC vulnerability, and perinatal insult triggers the arrest. This is plausible but would require identifying autism genes that specifically affect OPC resilience — no strong evidence for this currently exists.
2. **Genetic subset argument:** The OPC model applies only to the ~10–20% of autism associated with perinatal complications, not the 60–80% with strong genetic signatures. This is defensible but dramatically reduces scope.
3. **Epigenetic mediation:** Some autism-associated epigenetic changes (DNA methylation, chromosomes 15q and 7q as "epigenetic hotspots") could theoretically affect OPC gene expression. *No direct evidence found* linking autism epigenetic changes specifically to OPC maturation genes.

**Assessment:** The genetics problem is the single most serious challenge to the model. The overwhelming majority of identified autism genes relate to synaptic function, not myelination. The Bailey 1995 conclusion that obstetric hazards are consequences of genetic abnormality (not independent causes) directly undermines the model's causal arrow. The model can survive only if positioned as explaining a *subset* of autism, or if gene–OPC vulnerability interactions are demonstrated.

## 3. Evidence Against the Disconnection Model

### 3.1 The Connectivity Picture Is Mixed, Not Simply "Disconnected"

The OPC model predicts *underconnectivity* (reduced myelination → impaired long-range signal transmission). However, connectivity findings in autism are far more complex:

- The Wikipedia "Mechanism of autism" article describes functional connectivity studies that have found **"both hypo- and hyperconnectivity"** in autistic brains (Williams et al., 2006).

- **Developmental trajectory matters:** "In infancy, children at high risk for autism that were later diagnosed with autism were observed to have abnormally **high long-range connectivity** which then decreased through childhood to eventual long-range under-connectivity by adulthood" (O'Reilly et al., 2017, *PLOS ONE*, cited via Wikipedia "Mechanism of autism").

  This developmental trajectory is *problematic* for the OPC model. If autism *starts* with hyperconnectivity in infancy and transitions to underconnectivity, the initial state cannot be explained by failed myelination (which would predict underconnectivity from the beginning).

- **Local overconnectivity:** "Some studies have found local overconnectivity in the cerebral cortex and weak functional connections between the frontal lobe and the rest of the cortex" (Murias et al., 2007; cited via Wikipedia "Mechanism of autism"). The pattern is simultaneous *excess local* and *deficient long-range* connectivity — not a simple myelination deficit.

### 3.2 Fragile X: Mixed Connectivity Pattern

In Fragile X syndrome (the most common genetic cause of autism), Haberl et al. (2015) found **"structurally increased local connectivity and functionally decreased long-range connectivity"** (cited via Wikipedia "Fragile X syndrome").

Furthermore, the FXS mouse model shows cortical neurons receive "reduced sensory information (hyposensitivity)" but this is "accompanied by enhanced contextual information." This mixed pattern suggests the connectivity disruption in syndromic autism is not reducible to simple WM disconnection.

### 3.3 SHANK2 Hyperconnectivity

SHANK2 mutations — established autism risk genes — "act in a dominant fashion and appear to cause **hyperconnectivity** between the neurons" (Wikipedia, "Heritability of autism"). This is the *opposite* of what the OPC model predicts.

### 3.4 Brain Overgrowth and Excess Neurons

The early brain overgrowth in autism (macrocephaly in 15–20% of individuals), attributed to "an excess of neurons that causes local overconnectivity" (Courchesne et al., 2007), cannot be explained by OPC arrest. More neurons → more connections, which is the opposite of a myelination deficit producing disconnection.

### 3.5 Lateralisation Findings

An additional complexity: autistic brains show "preferential processing of information by the left hemisphere" vs. right hemisphere preference in neurotypicals (Wikipedia, "Mechanism of autism"). This lateralisation pattern is not predicted by, and may be independent of, myelination status.

**Assessment:** The connectivity data does not support simple disconnection. The developmental trajectory (hyperconnectivity in infancy → underconnectivity in adulthood), local overconnectivity, SHANK2 hyperconnectivity findings, and brain overgrowth all argue against the OPC model's straightforward prediction. The model would need to argue that WM-mediated underconnectivity coexists with, and is masked by, GM-mediated overconnectivity — a more complex picture than the model currently proposes.

## 4. The Preterm Paradox: Why Doesn't Confirmed WM Injury Reliably Produce Autism?

### 4.1 PVL → Cerebral Palsy, Not Autism

Periventricular leukomalacia (PVL) is the paradigmatic white matter injury of prematurity. The Wikipedia "Periventricular leukomalacia" article confirms:

- PVL involves "death of prooligodendrocytes [preOLs], proliferation of microgliocytes and astrocytes" — this is precisely the OPC arrest mechanism the model proposes.
- Injury pathway: hypoxia/ischemia → BBB damage → inflammatory response → cytokine toxicity → free radical damage → glial cell death.
- PVL affects 3–4% of infants <1500g and 4–10% born before 33 weeks.

**But the primary outcome is motor, not autistic:** 20–60% of PVL infants develop **cerebral palsy** (Wikipedia, "Periventricular leukomalacia"). The dominant clinical consequence of confirmed preOL destruction is *motor impairment*, not the social-communicative profile of autism.

This is the fundamental paradox: the model's core mechanism (preOL death) exists in a well-characterised clinical population (preterm infants with PVL), but the observed outcome (CP) is different from the predicted outcome (autism).

### 4.2 Some Evidence Does Link PVL to Autism

The PVL Wikipedia article cites five references linking periventricular WM abnormalities to autism:
- Blackmon 2015: periventricular WM abnormalities and restricted repetitive behaviour in ASD.
- Weber 2023: ASD-specific changes in WM connectome.
- Boddaert 2009: MRI findings in 77 children with non-syndromic autism.

And from prior session data: preterm birth is associated with executive function problems linked to "decreased myelinisation of frontal lobes," with a 25% decrease in WM values corresponding to a 60% increase in cognitive impairment risk (Wikipedia, "Preterm birth"). Volpe (2009) coined "encephalopathy of prematurity" to describe the WM injury pattern.

### 4.3 The Rate Problem

While preterm birth does modestly increase autism risk, the vast majority of preterm infants — even those with confirmed WM injury — do **not** develop autism. If OPC arrest were sufficient for autism, the autism rate in WM-injured preterm infants should be much higher than observed. The model needs to explain:

1. **Why CP, not autism?** If the same preOL death can cause either CP or autism, what determines the outcome? Is it tract-specific (motor tracts → CP; association tracts → autism)? This is plausible but requires direct evidence showing differential tract vulnerability.

2. **Dose-response:** Is there a threshold of OPC injury below which you get autism and above which you get CP? Or are they on a spectrum? *No direct evidence found* for a dose-response relationship between degree of WM injury and likelihood of autism vs. CP.

3. **The full-term autism majority:** Most autistic individuals were born at term without perinatal complications. The model must explain how OPC arrest occurs in the absence of prematurity or hypoxic-ischemic injury — perhaps through genetic OPC vulnerability or subclinical environmental insults. This remains speculative.

### 4.4 Brain Trajectory May Be Fundamentally Different

From the Preterm birth Wikipedia data: the brain trajectory in preterms may be "fundamentally distinctive," not just delayed. Forty-six percent of children born at 22–25 weeks had moderate-severe disabilities; 12% had disabling CP. The neurodevelopmental pattern of prematurity is heterogeneous and may not map cleanly onto autism phenotypes.

**Assessment:** The preterm paradox is a moderate-to-serious challenge. The model's core mechanism (preOL death) is well-documented in PVL, but PVL primarily produces CP, not autism. The model needs tract-specificity data to explain why some WM injuries produce motor deficits and others produce social-communicative deficits. The fact that some PVL-autism associations exist provides partial support, but the low rate of autism in WM-injured preterms suggests OPC arrest is neither necessary nor sufficient for autism in most cases.

## 5. Competing/Alternative Theoretical Models

### 5.1 Intense World Theory (Markram & Markram, 2010)

The Intense World Theory proposes that autism results from **hyper-functioning neural circuits** producing "more intense sensory perception, attention, memory, and emotional responses" (cited via Wikipedia "Autism," ref. 126: Markram & Markram, 2010, *Frontiers in Human Neuroscience*, PMC 3010743).

**Relationship to OPC model:** These theories are *opposing*. The OPC model proposes *under*-connectivity from failed myelination; the Intense World Theory proposes *over*-reactivity from excess connectivity and synaptic strength. If autistic brains are hyper-responsive, this suggests the neural circuits are *too connected*, not insufficiently myelinated. The VPA (valproic acid) rat model supporting the Intense World Theory shows hyper-connectivity in amygdala microcircuits — hyper-plasticity, not under-myelination.

### 5.2 Excitatory/Inhibitory (E/I) Imbalance

Multiple lines of evidence suggest E/I imbalance in autism:
- Fragile X pathophysiology directly implicates mGluR5-mediated exaggerated LTD (Bear et al., 2004). *No direct evidence found* that this relates to myelination.
- "Studies have suggested that excitatory–inhibitory networks may be imbalanced in autism" (Persico & Bourgeron, 2006; cited via Wikipedia "Mechanism of autism").
- GABA pathway downregulation is observed in FXS (Wikipedia, "Fragile X syndrome").
- Reduced NMDA-receptor function has been linked to "reduced social interactions, locomotor hyperactivity, self-injury, prepulse inhibition deficits, and sensory hypersensitivity" (Wikipedia, "Mechanism of autism").

**Relationship to OPC model:** E/I imbalance is a *synaptic* mechanism that can occur with intact myelination. It directly explains sensory hypersensitivity and seizure comorbidity (20–30% of autistic individuals have epilepsy) in ways the OPC model cannot easily accommodate.

### 5.3 Synaptic Pruning / Excess Synapses

The observation of early brain overgrowth followed by normalisation suggests abnormal synaptic pruning. Courchesne et al. (2007) proposed that excess neurons cause local overconnectivity. If the issue is *too many* synapses that fail to be pruned, this is fundamentally different from too few myelinated connections.

### 5.4 Social Motivation Theory

This theory proposes that reduced intrinsic reward from social interaction during critical periods leads to reduced social learning and subsequent autism features. It implicates the *dopaminergic reward system*, not white matter tracts. Evidence from Rett syndrome that dopamine pathways (substantia nigra) are disrupted provides some support for reward-circuit models of autism (Wikipedia, "Rett syndrome").

### 5.5 Maternal Immune Activation (MIA)

- The Wikipedia "Causes of autism" and "Mechanism of autism" articles describe maternal infection during pregnancy as a risk factor, with a 2021 meta-analysis of 36 studies supporting the association.
- Activation of the "maternal immune system during pregnancy...have been associated with an increased risk of autism." Pro-inflammatory cytokines (IFN-γ, IFN-α, TNF-α, IL-6, IL-17) "have been shown to promote autistic behaviours in animal models" (Wikipedia, "Mechanism of autism").
- MIA may affect brain development through multiple mechanisms: direct neuroinflammation, altered microglial activation, disrupted synaptic pruning, and *potentially* OPC injury.

**Relationship to OPC model:** MIA is partially compatible — inflammation could damage OPCs. However, MIA models in animals produce autism-like behaviours through *cytokine-mediated neuronal changes*, not specifically through OPC arrest. The MIA model's scope is broader and doesn't require a WM-specific mechanism.

### 5.6 Immune/Inflammatory Model

The Wikipedia "Mechanism of autism" article notes that IgA antibodies found in elevated levels in ASD populations "may attack proteins that support myelination of the brain, a process that is important for robust transmission of neural signal in many nerves." This is one of the few pieces of evidence that bridges immune dysregulation to myelination — but the mechanism is autoimmune attack on myelin proteins, not developmental OPC maturation arrest.

### 5.7 Multiple-Cause Framework

Happé, Ronald & Plomin (2006, *Nature Neuroscience*) argued it is "time to give up on a single explanation for autism." The Wikipedia "Mechanism of autism" article confirms: "No single type of theory is satisfactory on its own; social cognition theories poorly address autism's rigid and repetitive behaviours, while most of the nonsocial theories have difficulty explaining autism's social impairment and communication difficulties."

**Assessment:** Multiple alternative models (Intense World Theory, E/I imbalance, synaptic pruning, social motivation, MIA) explain autism features without requiring OPC arrest. Some (MIA, immune/inflammatory) are partially compatible with the OPC model. The consensus view (Happé et al., 2006) that autism has multiple causes via multiple mechanisms challenges any single-mechanism theory, including the OPC model.

## 6. What Would Strengthen the Model: Required Evidence and Testable Predictions

### 6.1 Critical Missing Evidence

The following evidence, if found, would substantially strengthen the OPC model:

1. **Post-mortem OPC quantification in autism:** Direct evidence of arrested preOL maturation or reduced mature oligodendrocyte density in autism brain tissue, particularly in the arcuate fasciculus, uncinate fasciculus, and cingulum. *No direct evidence found* — current post-mortem studies have focused on neuronal counts, cortical architecture (Stoner 2014), and glial markers, but not specifically on OPC maturation state in autism.

2. **Tract-specific myelination trajectories in autism:** Longitudinal DTI studies tracking fractional anisotropy (FA) in specific WM tracts (arcuate fasciculus, uncinate fasciculus, corpus callosum, ILF) across early development (0–8 years), demonstrating that myelination of these tracts falls behind at the precise developmental timepoints when corresponding deficits emerge. Some DTI studies exist showing reduced FA in autism (training knowledge: multiple meta-analyses), but *tract-specific temporal correlation with symptom emergence* has not been demonstrated.

3. **OPC maturation markers in neonatal screening:** Biomarkers of OPC arrest (e.g., NG2, O4, PDGFR-α in cord blood or neonatal MRI spectroscopy) that predict later autism diagnosis. *No direct evidence found.*

4. **Dose-response between WM injury severity and autism severity:** In preterm populations, demonstration that the degree of periventricular WM injury correlates with autism symptom severity (not just CP severity). Some suggestive evidence exists (Blackmon 2015, Weber 2023 — cited via PVL Wikipedia), but *no direct quantitative dose-response study found.*

5. **OPC-specific genetic vulnerability:** Identification of autism-associated genetic variants that specifically affect OPC maturation, survival, or myelination capacity. Given that most autism genes affect synapses (SHANK3, SCN2A, PTEN), finding variants that affect OPCs would bridge the genetics gap. *No direct evidence found.*

### 6.2 Testable Predictions the Model Must Make

1. **Temporal prediction:** Language regression/deficit should correlate with reduced arcuate fasciculus myelination at 2–4 years; social cognition deficits should correlate with reduced uncinate fasciculus myelination at 4–8 years. This is testable with longitudinal DTI + behavioural assessment.

2. **Preeclampsia/hypoxia prediction:** Children born after preeclampsia or perinatal hypoxia who develop autism should show greater WM tract-specific anomalies than those who develop only motor deficits (CP) or neither condition. This is testable in preterm cohort studies.

3. **Intervention prediction:** If the model is correct, activity-dependent myelination therapies (intensive language therapy, social skills training) should *specifically increase FA in the targeted tracts*, not just improve behaviour. This is testable with pre/post DTI.

4. **Tract specificity vs. global prediction:** The model predicts *selective* WM deficits in specific tracts, not global WM reduction. If autism WM deficits are evenly distributed rather than tract-specific, the model's key mechanistic claim (staggered deficits from staggered demand) would be falsified.

5. **No OPC arrest in syndromic autism prediction:** In Fragile X, Rett, and TSC-associated autism, the model would predict *either* (a) OPC arrest is present as a secondary feature explaining the autism component, or (b) these syndromic forms operate through different mechanisms and should show different WM profiles. Either finding is informative.

### 6.3 What Would Partially Validate the Model

Even if not a universal explanation, the OPC model could be validated as a pathway for a *subset* of autism if:

- A distinct **"perinatal WM injury"** subtype of autism is identified with: (a) history of perinatal complications, (b) tract-specific DTI abnormalities, (c) staggered symptom emergence tracking myelination windows, (d) better response to activity-dependent interventions.
- This subtype is demonstrated to have a different clinical trajectory, biomarker profile, and/or treatment response compared to "synaptic" or "genetic" subtypes.
- Prevalence of this subtype is estimated (even at 5–15% of autism cases, this would be clinically significant).

## Summary of Threat Assessment

| Area | Severity | Key finding |
|---|---|---|
| **Grey matter/cortical evidence** | **High** | Stoner 2014 cortical patches, brain overgrowth, Purkinje cell loss — non-WM pathologies in autism |
| **Genetics** | **Very High** | 60–95% heritability; 350+ genes mostly affecting synapses, not OPCs; Bailey 1995 argues obstetric hazards are *consequences* of genetic abnormality |
| **Connectivity** | **High** | Both hypo- AND hyperconnectivity; developmental trajectory starts with hyperconnectivity in infancy; SHANK2 causes hyperconnectivity |
| **Preterm paradox** | **Moderate-High** | PVL (confirmed preOL death) primarily → CP, not autism; some PVL-autism links exist but at low rates |
| **Alternative models** | **Moderate** | E/I imbalance, Intense World Theory, MIA explain features without requiring OPC arrest; partially compatible with some |
| **Model subset viability** | **Moderate-Positive** | Model could explain a specific subtype if tract-specific, longitudinal, and biomarker evidence is generated |

## Overall Verdict

The OPC maturation arrest model faces **serious, but not necessarily fatal, challenges**. The genetics problem (Section 2) is the most damaging: the overwhelming direction of genetic evidence points to synapses, not myelin, and the Bailey 1995 finding that obstetric hazards may be consequences rather than causes directly inverts the model's causal arrow. The connectivity evidence (Section 3) showing early hyperconnectivity and SHANK2-driven hyperconnectivity contradicts simple disconnection. The preterm paradox (Section 4) — that confirmed preOL death in PVL primarily produces CP rather than autism — challenges the idea that OPC arrest is a sufficient cause.

However, the model remains **viable as a subset hypothesis** (perhaps 5–20% of autism cases associated with perinatal complications) if reframed as:
1. One pathway among many (consistent with Happé et al. 2006 "multiple causes" framework);
2. Requiring gene–environment interaction (genetic OPC vulnerability + perinatal insult);
3. Predicting specific, testable tract-temporal correlations that distinguish this subtype.

The model's strongest asset is its *testability*: the predictions in Section 6.2 are concrete, falsifiable, and achievable with current neuroimaging and cohort study methods.
