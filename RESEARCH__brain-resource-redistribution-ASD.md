# RESEARCH: Brain Resource Redistribution, ASD Brain Differences, Physiological Triage, Myelination Costs, and Developmental Scaffolding

## AREA 1: Brain Resource Redistribution During Development

### 1a. Is there evidence that brain regions compete for metabolic resources during development?

**YES — strong evidence.**

- **Peters (2006, 2011)** — "The Selfish Brain: Competition for Energy Resources." The brain demands priority energy from the body via a "brain-pull" mechanism, competing with somatic tissues for glucose and ATP. The brain's share is defended at the expense of peripheral organs. (Cited 242/158 times)

- **Kuzawa et al. (2014), PNAS** — "Metabolic costs and evolutionary implications of human brain development." Documents that the human brain's glucose consumption peaks at ~66% of the body's resting metabolic rate during early childhood (~age 4–5), creating direct metabolic competition between brain growth and somatic body growth. Brain metabolic peak inversely correlates with the rate of body weight gain — when brain glucose demand is highest, body growth slows. (Cited 687)

- **Godfrey et al. (2012)** — "Fetal Liver Blood Flow Distribution: Imaging a physiological dilemma." Describes the resource allocation trade-off between brain growth and fat deposition in neonates — blood flow distribution in the fetus reflects a zero-sum competition between organs. (Cited 119)

- **Hill et al. (2010)** — "Similar patterns of cortical expansion during human development and evolution." Demonstrates that prenatal resources are focused on specific cortical regions, with some regions expanding dramatically more than others, implying differential resource allocation across cortical areas. (Cited 842)

- **Wright & Bourke (2016)** — "Further work on the shaping of cortical development and function by synchrony and metabolic competition." Directly addresses distribution of synaptic resources and growth-factor competition between brain regions. (Cited 13)

### 1b. Does any study document a "cerebral steal"-like phenomenon within the brain?

**YES — documented in pathological contexts. No direct evidence in normal development.**

- **Lee et al. (2000)** — Documented a "steal phenomenon from neighbouring brain regions" during epileptic seizures — ictal hyperperfusion in one region caused concurrent hypoperfusion in adjacent areas. (Cited 105)

- **Pranevicius & Pranevicius (2002)** — "Cerebral venous steal: blood flow diversion with increased tissue pressure." Described a "hyperperfusion zone around a hypoperfused ischemic core," formalizing intra-cerebral steal as a blood-flow diversion phenomenon. (Cited 51)

- **Yan et al. (2021)** — "Ipsilateral hypoperfusion caused by intracerebral steal phenomenon after carotid stenting." Documented intracerebral steal causing measurable hypoperfusion in one region when another was hyperperfused. (Cited 1)

- **Bellomo et al. (2025)** — BOLD-CVR steal phenomenon: documented steal between brain areas exhibiting different cerebrovascular reactivity. (Cited 11)

- **van Mook et al. (2005), Lancet Neurology** — "Cerebral hyperperfusion syndrome." (Cited 730)

**Gap:** No study was found documenting a steal-like redistribution mechanism operating during normal neonatal or developmental brain growth. The studies above are all from adult pathological contexts (stroke, seizure, surgical intervention).

### 1c. Is there evidence of regional metabolic variation across the developing brain?

**YES.**

- **Chugani (2018)** — "Imaging brain metabolism in the newborn." Volume-of-distribution values for glucose utilization across brain regions vary with age, with some regions showing much higher metabolic activity than others during specific developmental windows. (Cited 36)

- **Siddiqui et al. (2022)** — "Regional haemodynamic and metabolic coupling in infants." Used NIRS to map metabolic changes across healthy infant brain regions, documenting significant regional variation. (Cited 9)

- **York et al. (2026)** — "Spatiotemporal metabolic mapping." Found distinct metabolic profiles across brain regions during the postnatal period, with regional differences in metabolic trajectory.

## AREA 2: ASD Brain Differences — Which Regions Are Reduced, Which Are Enlarged?

### 2a. Corpus callosum in ASD

**CONSISTENTLY REDUCED — one of the most replicated findings in ASD neuroimaging.**

- **Frazier & Hardan (2009)** — Meta-analysis: "Total CC area was reduced in autism; magnitude of reduction was medium." (Cited 369)

- **Hardan et al. (2000)** — "Corpus callosum size in autism" — anterior subregions specifically smaller. (Cited 395)

- **Valenti et al. (2020)** — Review: "Reduced CC volume is one of the most consistent findings" in ASD. (Cited 61)

- **Vidal et al. (2006)** — "Mapping corpus callosum deficits in autism" — reduction in total callosal area and especially the anterior third. (Cited 338)

- **Wolff et al. (2015)** — "Altered corpus callosum morphology associated with autism over the first 2 years of life" — microstructural properties decreased longitudinally. (Cited 259)

- **Alexander et al. (2007)** — DTI of CC in autism. (Cited 799)

- **Barnea-Goraly et al. (2004)** — "White matter structure in autism" — reduced fractional anisotropy (FA) in CC, superior temporal tracts, occipitotemporal tracts, and near the amygdala. (Cited 989)

- **Shukla et al. (2010)** — "White matter compromise of callosal and subcortical fiber tracts" — reduced FA and increased radial diffusion across all CC segments. (Cited 192)

- **Keller et al. (2007)** — Reduced FA including corpus callosum. (Cited 384)

- **Travers et al. (2015)** — Decreased FA, increased mean diffusivity of CC in ASD, longitudinal study. (Cited 106)

- **Hardan et al. (2009)** — CC volume decreased in children with autism. (Cited 189)

- **Wegiel et al. (2018)** — "Deficit of corpus callosum axons, reduced axon diameter and decreased area are markers of abnormal development of interhemispheric connections in autistic subjects." (Cited in Wikipedia on Corpus Callosum)

**Wikipedia (Corpus callosum)** confirms: Myelination helps transfer of information between neurons; the process peaks in the first decade. The CC is the largest white matter structure in the brain. Agenesis/hypoplasia of CC has been correlated with autism spectrum disorders (refs 32, 33 on Wikipedia).

### 2b. Cerebellum and Purkinje cells in ASD

**PURKINJE CELL LOSS IS WELL-DOCUMENTED. CEREBELLAR VERMAL VOLUMES REDUCED.**

- **Fatemi et al. (2002)** — "Purkinje cell size is reduced in cerebellum of patients with autism." (Cited 461)

- **Skefos et al. (2014)** — "Regional alterations in Purkinje cell density in patients with autism." (Cited 226)

- **Whitney et al. (2009)** — "Evidence for a late developmental loss of Purkinje cells" in autism. (Cited 223)

- **van der Heijden et al. (2021)** — "Abnormal cerebellar development in ASD" — early Purkinje cell loss may be the defining culprit. (Cited 119)

- **Kern (2003)** — "Purkinje cell vulnerability and autism" — Purkinje cells are selectively vulnerable to metabolic stress and toxins. (Cited 237)

- **Wang et al. (2020)** — "Longitudinal changes of cerebellar thickness in autism spectrum disorder." (Cited 18)

- **Spisák et al. (2019)** — Purkinje cell number correlated with cerebrocerebellar circuit anomaly. (Cited 32)

### 2c. Hippocampus and amygdala in ASD

**ENLARGED — at least in early development.**

- **Schumann et al. (2004)** — "The amygdala is enlarged in children but not adolescents with autism; the hippocampus is enlarged at all ages." Amygdala enlargement is transient (normalizes by adolescence); hippocampal enlargement is persistent. (Cited 1172)

- **Groen et al. (2010)** — "Amygdala and hippocampus enlargement during adolescence in autism." (Cited 205)

- **Banker et al. (2021)** — "Hippocampal contributions to social and cognitive deficits in ASD" — despite enlargement, decreased long-range hippocampal connectivity across the brain. (Cited 303)

### 2d. Frontal–cerebellar inverse correlation (the "trade-off" finding)

**YES — a critical finding.**

- **Carper & Courchesne (2000), Brain** — "Inverse correlation between frontal lobe and cerebellum sizes in children with autism." When frontal lobe volume was enlarged, cerebellar volume was reduced, and vice versa. Direct quote: "decreased size of another, the correlation is clearly not an artefact of variance in overall brain [size]." This is the most direct evidence of a possible zero-sum trade-off between brain regions in ASD. (Cited 539)

### 2e. Early brain overgrowth followed by arrested/reduced growth

**WELL-DOCUMENTED — a defining trajectory in ASD.**

- **Courchesne et al. (2001)** — "Unusual brain growth patterns" — early overgrowth followed by arrested growth, with white matter overgrowth contrasting with cerebellar reduction. (Cited 2099)

- **Courchesne et al. (2003), JAMA** — "Evidence of brain overgrowth in the first year of life in autism." (Cited 1615)

- **Courchesne (2004)** — "Brain development in autism: early overgrowth followed by premature arrest of growth" — "increased frontal volumes and decreased cerebellar vermal volumes were linked." (Cited 481)

- **Courchesne et al. (2011)** — "Brain growth across the life span in autism" — early overgrowth during infancy followed by accelerated rate of decline in later years. (Cited 956)

- **Hazlett et al. (2011)** — "Early brain overgrowth in autism associated with increase in cortical surface area before age 2." (Cited 663)

- **Hua et al. (2013)** — Accelerated brain growth before age 2, white matter overgrowth. (Cited 106)

- **Lee et al. (2021)** — Longitudinal: "early cerebral overgrowth is followed by normalization of cerebral volumes." (Cited 79)

- **Yankowitz et al. (2020)** — "Evidence against the 'normalization' prediction" — volume remained larger even in later development. (Cited 33)

### 2f. Does any study explicitly frame ASD brain differences as resource "reallocation"?

**PARTIALLY — theoretical framing exists but no empirical metabolic resource study.**

- **Johnson (2017), Developmental Cognitive Neuroscience** — "Autism as an adaptive common variant pathway for human brain development." Explicitly proposes four types of whole-brain adaptation in autism, including: **"reorganization – the reallocation of functions to regions or networks as orchestrated by critical hubs."** Also proposes that hub regions (with higher metabolic rate) are "potentially more sensitive to pathogenic effects such as oxidative stress" and are "differentially sensitive to small changes in signal-to-noise ratio." Johnson frames autism not as a disorder but as "an adaptive common variant pathway" — the brain adapting to widespread mild neural processing atypicalities by reallocating functions. (Cited 160/81)

- **Mottron et al. (2015)** — Discusses "cortical reallocations following brain lesions" in the context of sex differences in brain plasticity. (Cited 108)

- **Wegiel et al. (2014)** — "Brain-region-specific alterations of trajectories of neuronal volume growth" — documented regional disproportions and desynchronization of growth between brain regions in autism. (Cited 105)

**Gap:** No published study directly measures metabolic resource competition between brain regions in ASD and frames one region's enlargement as occurring *at the metabolic expense* of another. The Carper & Courchesne (2000) inverse correlation is the closest empirical evidence, but the authors did not attribute it to metabolic competition — only noting the statistical anti-correlation. Johnson (2017) provides the theoretical framework for "reallocation" but without metabolic measurement data.

## AREA 3: Physiological Triage Principles — Cushing Reflex, Diving Reflex, and Preferential Perfusion

### 3a. Does the body perform triage — prioritizing brain perfusion over peripheral organs?

**YES — well-established.**

**Diving Reflex (Wikipedia):**
- The diving reflex "optimizes respiration by preferentially distributing oxygen stores to the heart and brain, enabling submersion for an extended time."
- Peripheral vasoconstriction occurs, "limiting blood flow to muscles, skin, and viscera, regions which are 'hypoxia-tolerant', thereby preserving oxygenated blood for the heart, lungs, and brain."
- "Blood shift" — blood flow is redistributed from extremities to the head and torso, concentrating flow "in a heart-brain circuit."
- Present in all air-breathing vertebrates studied. More pronounced in diving mammals but also present in human infants up to 6 months old.

**Cushing Reflex (Wikipedia):**
- A "last-ditch effort by the body to maintain homeostasis in the brain" when intracranial pressure rises.
- The body induces systemic hypertension via peripheral vasoconstriction to force blood past compressed cerebral arterioles — the body sacrifices peripheral perfusion to maintain cerebral blood flow.
- "The reflex mechanism can maintain normal cerebral blood flow and pressure under stressful situations such as ischemia."
- Notably: "its activity has also been seen in fetal life" — though "this activity has not been thoroughly investigated."

### 3b. Does the brain perform internal triage between its own regions?

**NO DIRECT EVIDENCE for normal developmental triage within the brain. Indirect evidence from pathology only.**

The diving reflex and Cushing reflex both operate at the **whole-body** level (periphery vs. brain), not at the **intra-cerebral** level (one brain region vs. another).

The cerebral steal studies in Area 1b demonstrate that intra-cerebral redistribution *can* occur in pathological states (seizure, stroke), but no study was found documenting this as a normal developmental mechanism.

**Gap:** No published research explicitly demonstrates that the developing brain performs internal metabolic triage — preferentially feeding some regions while starving others — as a normal developmental process. This remains an untested hypothesis.

### 3c. Is the Cushing reflex relevant to neonatal brain development?

**MINIMALLY — it is an emergency response, not a developmental mechanism.**

- The Cushing reflex responds to acute rises in intracranial pressure and "whenever a Cushing reflex occurs, there is a high probability of death in seconds to minutes."
- However, Ogilvy & Dubois (1987) documented the reflex in neonatal rabbits, and Wikipedia notes it "has also been seen in fetal life."
- Dickinson (1990) called it "the most powerful neural blood pressure stabilizing system."
- Its relevance as a developmental principle (rather than emergency mechanism) is **not established in any study found**.

## AREA 4: Myelination Costs — Grey Matter vs. White Matter Metabolic Demands

### 4a. What is the metabolic cost of myelination?

**DOCUMENTED — myelination is metabolically expensive but white matter is cheaper to maintain than grey matter.**

- **Harris & Attwell (2012)** — "The energetics of CNS white matter." White matter uses less energy than grey matter per unit volume; the main reason is the absence of synaptic signaling (which dominates grey matter energy cost). However, the *process* of myelination (oligodendrocyte differentiation, lipid synthesis, membrane wrapping) is acutely metabolically expensive. (Cited 598)

- **Yu et al. (2018)** — "Evaluating the gray and white matter energy budgets." Computed energy budgets for grey and white matter. (Cited 235)

- **Saab et al. (2013)** — "The role of myelin and oligodendrocytes in axonal energy metabolism." Oligodendrocytes provide metabolic support (lactate) to axons via myelin sheaths, making them critical for neuronal survival. (Cited 412)

- **Nave et al. (2023)** — "Expanding the function of oligodendrocytes to brain energy metabolism." (Cited 80)

### 4b. What happens to myelination under metabolic stress?

**MYELINATION IS SACRIFICED UNDER METABOLIC CONSTRAINT — oligodendrocytes prioritize survival over function.**

- **Tepavčević (2021)** — "Oligodendroglial energy metabolism and (re)myelination." Under nutrient deprivation, oligodendrocytes respond with: process retraction, reduced metabolic rate, compromised myelination — but preserved cell survival. Myelination is the first function sacrificed under metabolic stress. (Cited 70)

- **Rone et al. (2016)** — Oligodendrocytes reduce glycolytic rate under stress, directly impairing myelination capacity. (Cited 159)

- **Rao et al. (2017)** — Adult oligodendrocytes reduce metabolic rate under stress conditions. (Cited 61)

- **Blüml et al. (2014)** — "Metabolic maturation of white matter is altered in preterm infants" — compared WM and GM metabolic profiles, found altered maturation trajectories. (Cited 64)

- **Narine & Colognato (2022)** — "Current insights into oligodendrocyte metabolism and its power to sculpt the myelin landscape." Oligodendrocyte metabolic state directly shapes the pattern of myelination — which axons get myelinated depends partly on metabolic resource availability. (Cited 68)

### 4c. Do grey matter and white matter compete for resources?

**INDIRECT EVIDENCE — but no study directly demonstrates grey-white matter metabolic competition in development.**

The Kuzawa et al. (2014) study shows brain glucose consumption overall competes with body growth. Within the brain, grey matter (synaptic signaling) dominates energy use (Harris & Attwell, 2012), and myelination is the most metabolically costly component of white matter development — but no study was found that measures grey matter activity directly constraining or reducing available resources for concurrent myelination.

**Gap:** No study was found that explicitly quantifies metabolic competition between grey matter activity and white matter myelination during normal development, despite the logical inference that both draw on the same finite glucose/O₂ supply.

## AREA 5: Developmental Scaffolding — Transient Pathways, Pruning, and the Build-to-Demolish Pattern

### 5a. Are there transient neural pathways that serve as scaffolding during development?

**YES — extensively documented.**

- **Tau & Peterson (2010)** — "Normal development of brain circuits." Scaffolding cells (especially subplate neurons) are important early in development; many early neurons are transient, serving as guideposts/templates for permanent circuits before being eliminated. (Cited 1800)

- **Petanjek et al. (2023)** — "Dendritic spines: synaptogenesis and synaptic pruning." Documented that "retraction of exuberant number of axons and transitory projections parallels synaptogenesis" — transient overproduction is the norm, followed by activity-dependent pruning. (Cited 28)

### 5b. What is known about synaptic pruning — the "build then demolish" pattern?

**WELL-ESTABLISHED — the brain massively overproduces connections, then eliminates the majority.**

- **Riccomagno & Kolodkin (2015)** — "Sculpting neural circuits by axon and dendrite pruning." (Cited 423)

- **Low & Cheng (2006)** — "Axon pruning: essential step underlying developmental plasticity." (Cited 330)

- **Faust et al. (2021)** — "Mechanisms governing activity-dependent synaptic pruning" — transient elevation in spine density is normal; pruning selects which connections survive. (Cited 485)

- **Neniskyte & Gross (2017)** — "Errant gardeners: glial-cell-dependent synaptic pruning and neurodevelopmental disorders." Glial cells (microglia, astrocytes) actively prune synapses; excess connections are transient. Defects in pruning machinery are associated with neurodevelopmental disorders. (Cited 390)

### 5c. Is there evidence that scaffolding disruption affects final circuit architecture?

**YES — disrupted pruning is linked to neurodevelopmental disorders including ASD.**

- Neniskyte & Gross (2017) directly links pruning defects to neurodevelopmental disorders.
- Petanjek et al. (2023) notes that the timing of transitory projection retraction is critical — if scaffolding is removed too early or too late, final circuit architecture is altered.
- Johnson (2017) proposes that in autism, the timing and balance of these processes is disrupted, leading to an "alternative developmental trajectory" rather than the typical sequence.

**Gap:** No study was found that specifically measures whether metabolic resource constraints cause scaffolding to be removed prematurely (i.e., whether metabolic stress forces early pruning of transient pathways).
