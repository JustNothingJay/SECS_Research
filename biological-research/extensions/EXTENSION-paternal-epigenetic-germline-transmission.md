# Paternal Epigenetic Germ-Line Transmission

**Research Track 1 — Post-Publication Extension**
**Status:** Draft — not part of submitted medRxiv preprint
**Date:** 2026-03-05
**Author:** Jay Carpenter (Independent Researcher, Sydney, Australia)

---

## Abstract

The gestational oxygen-timing alignment model (Carpenter 2026, medRxiv preprint) proposes that transient placental hypoxia during specific fetal vulnerability windows disrupts HIF-1α-regulated developmental processes, with white-matter myelination (OPC maturation arrest) as the index case. The submitted paper focuses on the direct fetal-to-child pathway. This research track extends the model one generation deeper: if the affected fetus is male, his spermatogonial precursors are being epigenetically programmed during the same vulnerable window — meaning the gestational oxygen insult could mark his germline, transmitting developmental risk to his own future children.

This document synthesises evidence across five domains: (1) spermatogonial stem cell establishment timing, (2) paternal age effects and epigenetic drift, (3) Y chromosome epigenetic vulnerability, (4) transgenerational paternal studies (Överkalix, Dutch Hunger Winter), and (5) the sperm epigenome's sensitivity to oxygen disruption.

## 1. Spermatogonial Stem Cell Establishment During Male Fetal Development

Male germline development follows a tightly choreographed sequence that overlaps with the critical vulnerability windows identified in the alignment model:

| Gestational Window | Event | Epigenetic State |
|---|---|---|
| Weeks 4–6 | PGC migration from yolk sac to genital ridge | Genome-wide methylation erasure underway |
| Weeks 7–9 | PGCs colonise developing testis, become gonocytes | Near-complete demethylation |
| Weeks 10–11 | Methylation nadir — maximum epigenetic vulnerability | Lowest methylation, highest susceptibility |
| Weeks 12–22 | De novo methylation via DNMT3A/DNMT3L — paternal imprints established | Active re-methylation window |
| Weeks 22–38+ | Gonocyte-to-spermatogonia transition, mitotic quiescence | Imprints consolidating |

**Critical overlap:** The male germline epigenome is being *actively written* during weeks 12–22 GA — directly overlapping with the early portion of the alignment model's vulnerability super-window (weeks 20–36 for myelination). A male fetus exposed to placental insufficiency during this period could sustain epigenetic injury to his spermatogonial precursors that would not manifest until his own children are conceived decades later.

## 2. Evidence That the Father's Fetal Environment Programs His Germline

### 2.1 Animal Models

Radford et al. (2014) demonstrated that in utero undernutrition in mice altered DNA methylation in F1 sperm at >100 loci, and the metabolic phenotype transmitted to F2 offspring. Critically, the methylation changes were at **nucleosome-retaining regions** in sperm — the very regions that escape protamine replacement and can transmit epigenetic information to the embryo.

### 2.2 Dutch Hunger Winter

Veenendaal et al. (2013) found that offspring of men who were exposed *in utero* to the Dutch Hunger Winter famine had increased adiposity and poorer health outcomes — evidence of paternal germline transmission of fetal nutritional stress. Heijmans et al. (2008) showed periconceptional famine exposure was associated with reduced DNA methylation of the IGF2 gene six decades later, specifically through the paternal line.

### 2.3 Implication for the Alignment Model

If famine (a systemic oxygen-and-nutrient stressor) can permanently alter the paternal germline epigenome in utero, then placental hypoxia — which creates a more targeted but mechanistically similar insult through HIF-1α pathway activation — should be capable of the same. The alignment model predicts that the *specific loci affected* would depend on which genes are undergoing de novo methylation at the moment of HIF activation, consistent with the model's window-dependency principle.

**Key references:**
- Radford EJ, Ito M, Shi H, et al. In utero undernourishment perturbs the adult sperm methylome and intergenerational metabolism. *Science*. 2014;345(6198):1255903.
- Veenendaal MVE, Painter RC, de Rooij SR, et al. Transgenerational effects of prenatal exposure to the 1944-45 Dutch famine. *BJOG*. 2013;120(5):548-553.
- Heijmans BT, Tobi EW, Stein AD, et al. Persistent epigenetic differences associated with prenatal exposure to famine in humans. *Proc Natl Acad Sci USA*. 2008;105(44):17046-17049.

## 3. Paternal Age Effect: De Novo Mutations AND Epigenetic Drift

### 3.1 The Landmark Finding

Reichenberg et al. (2006) demonstrated that offspring of fathers aged ≥40 were **5.75 times** more likely to have ASD compared to offspring of fathers <30 (OR 5.75, 95% CI 2.65–12.46) in a cohort of 318,506 Israeli subjects. This was independent of maternal age.

### 3.2 The Dual Mechanism

The paternal age effect operates through two simultaneous channels:

**Channel 1 — De novo mutation accumulation:** Kong et al. (2012), sequencing 78 Icelandic trios, showed de novo mutations increase by ~2 per year of paternal age, with the father's age explaining ~97% of variation in de novo mutation count. Iossifov et al. (2014) found ~13% of ASD cases accounted for by de novo mutations, with strong paternal age correlation.

**Channel 2 — Epigenetic drift:** Jenkins et al. (2014) demonstrated age-associated DNA methylation changes at 139 regions in human sperm, many near genes implicated in schizophrenia, bipolar disorder, and autism (including DMPK, NRXN1). These changes are *epigenetic*, not mutational. Progressive methylation drift at developmental regulatory genes accumulates with age.

### 3.3 The "Head Start" Hypothesis

If the father's own fetal epigenetic programming was disrupted by gestational oxygen insult, this could establish an abnormal baseline from which further age-related epigenetic drift accelerates. The implication: a man whose own OPC/germline programming was disrupted in utero would reach a higher level of epigenetic damage at any given age than a man whose development was unperturbed. This could explain why paternal age is a risk factor for ASD independently of the genetic mutation channel — the epigenetic drift channel may carry the gestational programming legacy.

**Key references:**
- Reichenberg A, Gross R, Weiser M, et al. Advancing paternal age and autism. *Arch Gen Psychiatry*. 2006;63(9):1026-1032.
- Kong A, Frigge ML, Masson G, et al. Rate of de novo mutations and the importance of father's age to disease risk. *Nature*. 2012;488(7412):471-475.

## 4. Paternal Epigenetic Marks That Transmit to Offspring

Three classes of epigenetic carrier have been demonstrated in the paternal germline:

### 4.1 Retained Nucleosomes at Developmental Gene Promoters

Hammoud et al. (2009) made the landmark finding that human sperm retain nucleosomes (rather than protamines) at ~4% of the genome, and these retained nucleosomes are specifically located at **developmental gene promoters** — HOX gene clusters, imprinted loci, microRNA clusters, and genes critical for embryonic development. Paternal epigenetic information at these loci is transmitted *intact* to the embryo.

Brykczynska et al. (2010) showed that H3K4me3 (active) and H3K27me3 (repressive) histone modifications are retained in sperm at developmental loci, providing an epigenetic blueprint that persists to the embryo.

### 4.2 Direct Transgenerational Transmission via Histones

Siklenka et al. (2015) provided the most direct evidence: genetic disruption of KDM1A (histone demethylase) in developing mouse sperm caused altered offspring development, and the effects transmitted across **multiple generations** — even when the original mutation was removed. This is direct evidence that paternal histone modifications carry developmental information transgenerationally.

### 4.3 Small Non-Coding RNAs in Sperm

Gapp et al. (2014) demonstrated that traumatic stress in early life altered sperm small RNA profiles (miRNAs, piRNAs) and these changes were associated with altered offspring behaviour and metabolism. **Crucially, injection of sperm RNA from stressed males into control oocytes reproduced the phenotype** — proving RNA-mediated paternal epigenetic transmission.

Rodgers et al. (2015) showed stress exposure alters sperm miRNA content, and these miRNAs are sufficient to programme HPA axis dysregulation in offspring.

Dias & Ressler (2014) demonstrated that paternal odour fear conditioning altered DNA methylation at the relevant olfactory receptor gene in sperm, and F1 and F2 offspring showed enhanced sensitivity to the same odour — without direct exposure. Paternal epigenetic transmission of a specific environmental experience.

Chen et al. (2016) showed that tRNA-derived small RNA fragments (tsRNAs) in sperm from high-fat-diet males could reprogram offspring metabolism when injected into control zygotes.

## 5. Y Chromosome Epigenetic Vulnerability

### 5.1 Structural Limitation

The Y chromosome cannot undergo homologous recombination with the X across most of its length (Skaletsky et al. 2003). It has lost ~95% of its ancestral genes over 300 million years (Hughes & Page 2015). Critically, the surviving Y-linked genes are disproportionately **chromatin modifiers and transcriptional regulators** — including KDM5D and UTY (histone demethylases) (Bellott et al. 2014). These are epigenetic regulators, making Y-linked epigenetic function directly vulnerable to disruption.

### 5.2 No Female Backup

Dunford et al. (2017) demonstrated that loss of Y-linked chromatin regulators (KDM5D, UTY) in males contributes to sex-biased disease, because their X-linked homologues (KDM5C, UTX) escape X-inactivation in females, giving females a "backup" copy. Males have no such backup for Y-linked epigenetic regulators. This provides a mechanistic explanation for the 4:1 male predominance in ASD that is *epigenetic* rather than hormonal.

### 5.3 Y-Linked Genes in Neurodevelopment

The Y chromosome encodes NLGN4Y (neuroligin-4Y), a paralogue of the autism-associated gene NLGN4X, which is expressed in the developing male brain (Johansson et al. 2016). Y-linked chromatin modifiers (UTY, KDM5D) create sex-specific epigenetic landscapes in the developing brain that influence vulnerability to neurodevelopmental disorders (Loke et al. 2015).

### 5.4 Mechanistic Chain (Plausible but Undemonstrated)

If gestational hypoxia disrupts HIF-regulated epigenetic machinery during the fetal re-methylation window (weeks 12–22), and the Y chromosome's epigenetic regulators lack homologous repair capacity, then male germ cells may be disproportionately susceptible to permanent epigenetic damage. This could contribute to the 4:1 male predominance through a paternal transmission pathway — not just through the affected male fetus directly, but through his future sons. **This remains mechanistically plausible but experimentally undemonstrated.**

Cotton et al. (2015) provided the first genome-wide methylation map of the human Y chromosome, showing extensive DNA methylation variation — suggesting the Y carries functional epigenetic information susceptible to environmental modification.

## 6. Överkalix and Transgenerational Paternal Effects

### 6.1 The Överkalix Studies

Using historical records from the isolated Swedish parish of Överkalix, Bygren et al. (2001) found that **paternal grandfathers'** food supply during their pre-pubertal slow growth period (SGP, ages 9–12) predicted grandchildren's longevity. Feast during SGP led to increased diabetes mortality in grandchildren; famine during SGP led to decreased diabetes mortality.

Kaati et al. (2002) extended the analysis: paternal grandfather's feast produced a ~4x risk ratio for cardiovascular and diabetes mortality in grandchildren. The effect was **sex-specific**: paternal grandfather's nutrition affected grandsons through the male line; maternal grandmother's nutrition affected granddaughters.

### 6.2 The Definitive Publication

Pembrey et al. (2006) combined Överkalix data with ALSPAC (Avon Longitudinal Study of Parents and Children). Key findings:
- Paternal grandfather's food supply during SGP linked to grandson's mortality risk ratio of 1.67 (CI 1.07–2.59) for diabetes
- In ALSPAC: fathers who started smoking before age 11 had sons with significantly greater BMI at age 9
- The effect was **sex-linked** — fathers' early smoking increased sons' but not daughters' BMI
- Interpreted as evidence for **epigenetic inheritance through the male germline**

### 6.3 Generational Persistence

- Siklenka et al. (2015): histone modification disruption in mouse sperm transmitted effects for at least **3 generations** with gradually declining penetrance
- Gapp et al. (2016): paternal trauma effects persisted to F3 generation through sperm RNA changes, but could be partially reversed by environmental enrichment in F1
- Anway et al. (2005): endocrine disruptor (vinclozolin) caused transgenerational effects on male fertility for at least **4 generations** via altered DNA methylation in sperm

**Key references:**
- Bygren LO, Kaati G, Edvinsson S. Longevity determined by paternal ancestors' nutrition during their slow growth period. *Acta Biotheor*. 2001;49(1):53-59.
- Kaati G, Bygren LO, Edvinsson S. Cardiovascular and diabetes mortality determined by nutrition during parents' and grandparents' slow growth period. *Eur J Hum Genet*. 2002;10(11):682-688.
- Pembrey ME, Bygren LO, Kaati G, et al. Sex-specific, male-line transgenerational responses in humans. *Eur J Hum Genet*. 2006;14(2):159-166.
- Anway MD, Cupp AS, Uzumcu M, Skinner MK. Epigenetic transgenerational actions of endocrine disruptors and male fertility. *Science*. 2005;308(5727):1466-1469.

## 7. Two-Parent Epigenetic Convergence

### 7.1 The Understudied Frontier

Most DOHaD research examines maternal OR paternal effects independently. Very few studies have tested for interaction. This is the most significant gap in the literature.

Soubry et al. (2014) proposed a "dual-origin" model where both maternal and paternal epigenetic programming converge on offspring outcomes, noting that imprinted genes (with parent-of-origin-specific methylation) are particularly relevant because disruption of EITHER parental imprint at the same locus would affect offspring.

Lane et al. (2014) reviewed evidence that BOTH parents' preconception environments programme offspring health, and called for studies examining their interaction.

### 7.2 The Closest Published Evidence

Sandin et al. (2016), in a Danish-Swedish cohort of >5.7 million children, found that both maternal and paternal age independently predicted ASD risk, and **the effects were additive** — greatest risk when BOTH parents were older. A large age gap between parents (in either direction) independently increased risk. This is the closest published evidence for a "two-parent convergence" effect, though interpreted through demographic rather than epigenetic framing.

### 7.3 Assortative Mating for Adversity

If both parents experienced adverse gestational environments (plausible if both were born in the same community, SES stratum, or time period), their epigenetic programming could converge additively or synergistically on offspring risk. This has been theoretically proposed but **not empirically tested in a controlled design.**

**Key references:**
- Soubry A, Hoyo C, Jirtle RL, Murphy SK. A paternal environmental legacy: evidence for epigenetic inheritance through the male germline. *Bioessays*. 2014;36(4):359-371.
- Lane M, Robker RL, Robertson SA. Parenting from before conception. *Science*. 2014;345(6198):756-760.
- Sandin S, Schendel D, Magnusson P, et al. Autism risk associated with parental age and with increasing difference in age between the parents. *Mol Psychiatry*. 2016;21(5):693-700.

## 8. Sperm Epigenome and Oxygen Sensitivity

### 8.1 Systemic Hypoxia

Chronic intermittent hypoxia (CIH) in mice — modeling sleep apnoea — caused increased sperm DNA fragmentation, altered sperm morphology, and elevated oxidative stress markers (Zheng et al. 2021). This parallels the oscillation damage pattern described in the alignment model's Section 4.4 (the molecular amplifier).

### 8.2 Varicocele as a Natural Testicular Hypoxia Model

Varicocele is the most clinically relevant model of **chronic testicular hypoxia** — the dilated pampiniform plexus creates venous stasis and reduced oxygen delivery.

- Tian et al. (2021) confirmed varicocele creates a genuinely hypoxic environment with **elevated HIF-1α expression** in spermatic cord blood — directly linking varicocele to the HIF pathway central to the alignment model
- Bahreinian et al. (2015) directly demonstrated **DNA hypomethylation** in sperm from varicocele patients — global reduction in methylation that could affect imprinted loci and developmental gene promoters
- Guo et al. (2020) showed that in utero environmental exposure could predispose to varicocele, suggesting an **intergenerational cycle**: gestational insult → testicular vascular abnormality → testicular hypoxia → sperm epigenetic damage → offspring effects

### 8.3 Environmental Sensitivity

Paul et al. (2008) showed that even a single mild transient scrotal heat stress caused DNA damage in sperm and reduced blastocyst formation — demonstrating the extreme sensitivity of the spermatogenic epigenome to environmental stress. Dada et al. (2012) confirmed that heat stress, oxidative stress, and environmental toxicants all alter sperm DNA methylation, histone modifications, and small RNA content.

**Key references:**
- Zheng J, Chen X, Liu Q, et al. The effects of chronic intermittent hypoxia on sperm quality and oxidative stress in a mouse model. *Sleep Breath*. 2021;25(2):835-843.
- Tian RH, Yang G, Wang J, et al. Varicocele is associated with spermatic hypoxia, increased MMP-9 expression, and impaired sperm function. *Andrology*. 2021;9:1252-1262.
- Bahreinian M, Tavalaee M, Abbasi H, et al. DNA hypomethylation predisposes sperm to DNA damage in individuals with varicocele. *Syst Biol Reprod Med*. 2015;61(4):179-186.

## 9. Synthesis: The Paternal Germ-Line Extension of the Alignment Model

### 9.1 The Core Proposition

The alignment model's two-variable framework — (1) does the placenta fire the anti-angiogenic cascade, and (2) which fetal developmental windows are open when it fires — gains a third temporal dimension when the fetus is male:

**(3) Is the male germline undergoing de novo methylation when the HIF response hits?**

If the answer to all three is yes, the gestational oxygen insult creates a **dual legacy**:
- **Direct:** disrupted development of the fetus himself (OPC arrest, organ vulnerability)
- **Indirect:** altered epigenetic programming of his spermatogonial precursors, creating a latent second-generation risk that manifests only when he becomes a father

### 9.2 The Timeline Convergence

| Event | Gestational Weeks | Overlap with Alignment Model |
|---|---|---|
| PGC methylation nadir | 10–11 | Before super-window but during early placental development |
| Male germline re-methylation | 12–22 | Overlaps with early OPC specification (weeks 12–16) |
| OPC super-window | 20–36 | Late germline re-methylation and consolidation |
| Preeclampsia typical onset | 20–34 | Full overlap with both germline and OPC windows |

The temporal coincidence is striking: preeclampsia's typical onset window (weeks 20–34) exactly spans both the OPC maturation window AND the period when the male germline epigenome is being consolidated. A single systemic event — placental anti-angiogenic cascade — hits both systems simultaneously.

### 9.3 The "Subset of a Subset" Structure

The submitted paper argues that neurodevelopmental outcome depends on window alignment (not all gestational insults produce the same result). The paternal transmission pathway adds another filter:

1. **Not all gestational insults affect the germline** — only those occurring during weeks 12–22 (re-methylation window)
2. **Not all germline effects transmit** — only those at nucleosome-retaining regions (Hammoud 2009) survive protamine replacement
3. **Not all transmitted marks produce phenotype** — depends on maternal epigenetic state and conventional genetic background

This produces the "subset of a subset" pattern: paternal germline transmission is a narrower channel than direct fetal effects, which is itself a narrower channel than total gestational insults. The funnel explains why transgenerational autism is observed but not universal.

### 9.4 Testable Predictions

1. **Male offspring of preeclamptic pregnancies should show altered sperm methylation profiles as adults** — specifically at nucleosome-retaining developmental loci. This is testable with existing methylation array technology (e.g., EPIC array on sperm samples from adult men born after preeclamptic pregnancies vs. normotensive controls).

2. **The paternal age effect on ASD should be stronger in men who were themselves born after complicated pregnancies** — their epigenetic baseline is already disrupted, so age-related drift reaches pathological thresholds sooner.

3. **Varicocele patients should show HIF-1α-associated methylation patterns similar to those predicted by in utero hypoxic exposure** — because both involve the same pathway (HIF-mediated epigenetic disruption).

4. **Transgenerational ASD risk should track the paternal line more strongly than the maternal line when the F0 insult is hypoxic** — because the male germline re-methylation window overlaps the vulnerability window, whereas oocytes are largely quiescent during this period.

## 10. Evidence Gaps and Limitations

1. **No direct study has tested whether preeclampsia-related hypoxia alters the fetal male germline epigenome.** All evidence is extrapolated from undernutrition (Radford 2014, Dutch Hunger Winter) and environmental stressor studies.

2. **The Y chromosome's epigenetic vulnerability to oxygen disruption is mechanistically plausible but experimentally undemonstrated.** This is an inference from the structure (no homologous recombination) and the identity of surviving genes (chromatin modifiers).

3. **Two-parent convergence has not been tested in a controlled design.** The Sandin et al. (2016) additive parental age finding is the closest evidence.

4. **Translation from animal models (mouse, rat) to human is uncertain.** The timing of germline development differs between species, and human spermatogenesis is substantially longer and more complex than murine.

5. **The "head start" hypothesis for paternal age is speculative.** While logically consistent with Jenkins (2014) data, no study has compared epigenetic drift rates in sperm from men with and without adverse gestational histories.
