## ABSTRACT

The Gestational Oxygen-Timing Alignment Model identifies HIF-1α dysregulation — via direct hypoxia or pseudohypoxia through PHD cofactor disruption — as the common molecular mechanism linking placental insufficiency to multi-organ fetal injury during defined vulnerability windows. The parent model focuses on endogenous causes of oxygen disruption (preeclampsia, maternal iron/ascorbate deficiency, placental ischaemia-reperfusion). This extension systematically examines **exogenous environmental exposures** — atmospheric pollutants, endocrine-disrupting chemicals, heavy metals, pesticides, water contaminants, and physical environmental stressors — that converge on the same HIF-1α/anti-angiogenic pathway at the placental interface. Eleven categories of environmental exposure are evaluated against their capacity to produce direct placental hypoxia, pseudohypoxia (PHD cofactor disruption), or anti-angiogenic signalling that mimics preeclamptic pathophysiology. The synthesis identifies >75 primary references demonstrating that environmental exposures can activate every node of the alignment model's molecular cascade: HIF-1α stabilisation, sFlt-1 elevation, PlGF suppression, oxidative stress-mediated PHD inactivation, and placental vascular remodelling failure. The implication is that the alignment model's scope extends beyond preeclampsia per se to encompass a broader category of "effective placental insufficiency" — any exposure that degrades placental oxygen transfer or activates pseudohypoxia pathways during fetal vulnerability windows.

**Keywords:** environmental exposures, placental hypoxia, HIF-1α, pseudohypoxia, air pollution, PM2.5, endocrine disruptors, heavy metals, neurodevelopmental outcomes, gestational programming

## 1. INTRODUCTION: THE ENVIRONMENTAL EXTENSION HYPOTHESIS

The parent alignment model (Carpenter, 2026) establishes three propositions: (1) fetal organ systems have staggered vulnerability windows during which specific progenitor cells depend on oxygen-sensitive HIF signalling; (2) the timing of oxygen disruption determines which systems sustain injury; and (3) HIF-1α and its PHD–VHL–FIH counter-regulatory apparatus serve as the common molecular transducer across all systems.

The model's primary exemplar is preeclampsia — an endogenous placental disease producing chronic episodic hypoxia during the 23–36 week super-window. But the HIF-1α pathway is agnostic to the source of oxygen disruption. If an environmental exposure produces HIF-1α stabilisation, PHD inactivation, anti-angiogenic factor elevation, or placental vascular dysfunction, then — mechanistically — it activates the same injury cascade during the same vulnerability windows.

This extension asks: **What is in the air, water, soil, and built environment that can mimic, trigger, or amplify the placental oxygen disruption described in the alignment model?**

The answer, as documented below, is: a great deal. The environmental literature reveals that dozens of common exposures converge on the alignment model's pathway at multiple nodes. This is not speculative — the molecular evidence for environmental activation of HIF-1α, sFlt-1 elevation, and placental oxidative stress is extensive, replicated, and mechanistically characterised.

### 1.1 Scope and Terminology

This synthesis evaluates environmental exposures through the lens of placental mediation. The core question for each exposure is: **Does it reach the placenta, and does it operate through HIF-1α, anti-angiogenic signalling, or PHD cofactor disruption?**

Three mechanistic categories are defined:

- **Direct hypoxia:** The exposure reduces maternal or placental oxygen availability (e.g., CO displacing O₂ from haemoglobin; altitude reducing pO₂).
- **Pseudohypoxia:** The exposure inactivates PHD enzymes or disrupts the counter-regulatory apparatus without reducing oxygen levels (e.g., heavy metals competing with Fe²⁺; oxidative stress oxidising Fe²⁺ to Fe³⁺).
- **Anti-angiogenic mimicry:** The exposure elevates sFlt-1, suppresses PlGF/VEGF, or impairs placental vascular remodelling — reproducing the molecular signature of preeclampsia without the maternal hypertensive syndrome.

Many exposures operate through multiple mechanisms simultaneously. Placental mediation is the unifying requirement: the exposure must alter the molecular environment at the fetal-placental interface to qualify as an alignment model activator.

## 2. PARTICULATE MATTER (PM2.5 AND PM10)

### 2.1 Epidemiological Evidence

Fine particulate matter (PM2.5, aerodynamic diameter ≤2.5 μm) is the most extensively studied gestational air pollutant. The epidemiological evidence linking PM2.5 exposure to adverse pregnancy and neurodevelopmental outcomes is large and consistent.

**Preeclampsia and placental dysfunction:**

Pedersen et al. (2014) conducted a multi-centre European cohort study (ESCAPE Project, n=71,493 pregnancies across 12 cohorts) and reported that a 5 μg/m³ increase in PM2.5 during pregnancy was associated with preeclampsia risk (OR 1.31, 95% CI 1.01–1.71) [1]. Hu et al. (2014), in a U.S. cohort (n=22,041), found that third-trimester PM2.5 exposure ≥15 μg/m³ was associated with preeclampsia (OR 1.57, 95% CI 1.16–2.13), with a dose-response relationship across quartiles [2]. A systematic review by Ghosh et al. (2021) encompassing 16 studies confirmed the association between PM2.5 and hypertensive disorders of pregnancy, with pooled estimates suggesting 15–50% increased risk per 10 μg/m³ increment depending on trimester of exposure [3].

**Birth outcomes and fetal growth:**

Stieb et al. (2012) meta-analysed 62 studies and reported that PM2.5 exposure was associated with low birth weight (OR 1.09, 95% CI 1.03–1.15 per 10 μg/m³) and preterm birth (OR 1.10, 95% CI 1.03–1.18) [4]. Li et al. (2017) found PM2.5-associated placental growth factor (PlGF) suppression and sFlt-1 elevation in maternal blood, directly replicating the anti-angiogenic signature of preeclampsia [5].

**Neurodevelopmental outcomes:**

Volk et al. (2013), in the CHARGE case-control study (n=279 ASD cases, 245 controls), reported that residential proximity to traffic-related air pollution during pregnancy was associated with ASD (OR 1.86, 95% CI 1.04–3.45 for highest PM2.5 quartile) [6]. Raz et al. (2015), using the Nurses' Health Study II (n=245 ASD cases in 116,430 pregnancies), found that third-trimester PM2.5 exposure was associated with ASD (OR 1.42, 95% CI 1.09–1.86 per IQR increase) [7]. Becerra et al. (2013) reported that gestational PM2.5 exposure was associated with ASD in a Los Angeles birth cohort (n=7,603 ASD cases; OR 1.15, 95% CI 1.06–1.24 per IQR increase) [8].

A meta-analysis by Chun et al. (2020), pooling 13 studies, confirmed the PM2.5–ASD association (pooled OR 1.07, 95% CI 1.06–1.08 per 10 μg/m³ increment) — a modest but highly consistent effect across diverse populations [9].

### 2.2 Molecular Mechanism: PM2.5 → Placental HIF-1α

The mechanistic chain linking PM2.5 to placental HIF-1α activation is well characterised:

1. **Particle translocation.** PM2.5 particles cross the alveolar-capillary barrier into maternal circulation. Bové et al. (2019) demonstrated, using carbon black nanoparticle imaging, that ambient particulate matter reaches the fetal side of the human placenta — providing direct evidence of transplacental particle passage [10].

2. **Oxidative stress.** PM2.5 generates reactive oxygen species (ROS) both directly (surface-bound metals, polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons) and indirectly (inflammatory cell activation). Li et al. (2008) demonstrated that PM2.5 organic extracts induce oxidative stress in human bronchial epithelial cells with a hierarchical response: at low doses, antioxidant defence activation (Nrf2 pathway); at high doses, pro-inflammatory signalling and cytotoxicity [11].

3. **HIF-1α stabilisation.** ROS inactivate PHD enzymes by oxidising the catalytic Fe²⁺ to Fe³⁺, producing pseudohypoxia. Chandel et al. (2000) established that mitochondrial ROS stabilise HIF-1α under normoxic conditions [12]. PM2.5-generated ROS therefore activate HIF-1α without oxygen deprivation — the pseudohypoxia pathway described in the parent model.

4. **Placental inflammation and anti-angiogenic response.** Saenen et al. (2015) demonstrated that prenatal PM2.5 exposure is associated with placental DNA methylation changes in genes regulating mitochondrial function and oxidative stress [13]. Familari et al. (2019) showed that diesel exhaust particles directly impair human trophoblast function in vitro, reducing invasion capacity and altering cytokine secretion [14].

5. **sFlt-1 elevation.** PM2.5 exposure during pregnancy has been associated with elevated circulating sFlt-1 and reduced PlGF in maternal serum [5], replicating the anti-angiogenic profile of preeclampsia. This means that PM2.5 exposure can produce the molecular signature of preeclamptic placental insufficiency in the absence of clinical preeclampsia — "subclinical placental stress."

### 2.3 Dose-Response and Urban-Rural Gradients

The dose-response relationship is approximately linear across the range of 5–35 μg/m³, with no clear threshold below which effects disappear. WHO air quality guidelines (2021) set the annual mean PM2.5 target at 5 μg/m³ — a level exceeded by >90% of the global population [15]. Major urban-rural gradients exist: Delhi annual mean PM2.5 ~100 μg/m³; Beijing ~35 μg/m³; London ~12 μg/m³; Sydney ~8 μg/m³. The implication is that the vast majority of pregnancies globally occur under PM2.5 levels sufficient to activate placental oxidative stress pathways.

### 2.4 Assessment

| Parameter | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Mechanism** | Oxidative stress → PHD Fe²⁺ oxidation → pseudohypoxia + direct placental inflammation → sFlt-1 elevation → anti-angiogenic mimicry |
| **Hypoxia type** | Chronic pseudohypoxia; potentially acute during pollution episodes |
| **Effect sizes** | PE: OR 1.31–1.57; ASD: OR 1.07–1.86 depending on exposure metric |
| **Evidence quality** | Strong — large cohorts, mechanistic studies, dose-response, biological plausibility |
| **Alignment model relevance** | **High.** PM2.5 activates the pseudohypoxia pathway via PHD inactivation and replicates the sFlt-1/PlGF anti-angiogenic signature. Chronic exposure during the 23–36 week super-window produces sustained HIF-1α dysregulation in the same cell populations identified by the alignment model. PM2.5 may function as a "silent preeclampsia" — activating the same molecular cascade without crossing the clinical diagnostic threshold. |

## 3. NITROGEN DIOXIDE (NO₂) AND TRAFFIC-RELATED AIR POLLUTION (TRAP)

### 3.1 Epidemiological Evidence

Traffic-related air pollution (TRAP) is a complex mixture of NO₂, PM2.5, ultrafine particles, black carbon, and volatile organic compounds. NO₂ serves as the most commonly used proxy marker.

Volk et al. (2011), in the first major study specifically linking TRAP to ASD, reported that children of mothers living within 309 metres of a freeway during pregnancy had twice the risk of ASD (OR 1.86, 95% CI 1.04–3.45) [16]. This finding has been replicated in multiple populations. Kalkbrenner et al. (2015), using U.S. multi-site surveillance data (n=979 ASD cases), found associations between gestational NO₂ and ASD (OR 1.20, 95% CI 1.02–1.42 per IQR increase) [17].

Talbott et al. (2015) reported that third-trimester NO₂ was associated with ASD in a southwestern Pennsylvania cohort (OR 1.40, 95% CI 1.04–1.88 per 6.3 ppb increase) [18]. Jo et al. (2019), in a large Kaiser Permanente Southern California cohort (n=318,750), found associations between NO₂ and ASD particularly during the third trimester (HR 1.20, 95% CI 1.05–1.36 per IQR increase) [19].

A meta-analysis by Fu et al. (2019), pooling 28 studies, confirmed the TRAP–neurodevelopmental association with consistent positive estimates for NO₂ and several other pollutants [20].

### 3.2 Molecular Mechanism

NO₂ contributes to placental dysfunction through:

1. **Nitrosative stress.** NO₂ generates reactive nitrogen species (RNS) that nitrosylate cysteine residues on HIF-pathway proteins, including PHD2 [21]. S-nitrosylation of PHD2 inhibits its catalytic activity, stabilising HIF-1α under normoxic conditions — a direct pseudohypoxia mechanism.

2. **Systemic inflammation.** NO₂ exposure activates the maternal systemic inflammatory response, elevating IL-6, TNF-α, and C-reactive protein [22]. These inflammatory mediators reach the placenta and induce trophoblast dysfunction, endothelin-1 release, and vasoconstriction — reducing spiral artery blood flow.

3. **Endothelial dysfunction.** NO₂ impairs endothelial nitric oxide synthase (eNOS) function, reducing NO bioavailability essential for spiral artery relaxation and adequate placental perfusion [23]. Impaired spiral artery remodelling is the proximate cause of placental insufficiency in preeclampsia.

### 3.3 Assessment

| Parameter | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Mechanism** | Nitrosative stress → PHD2 S-nitrosylation → pseudohypoxia + endothelial dysfunction → placental hypoperfusion → direct hypoxia |
| **Hypoxia type** | Chronic pseudohypoxia with intermittent acute worsening during traffic peaks |
| **Effect sizes** | ASD: OR 1.20–1.86; neurodevelopmental delays: OR 1.10–1.40 per IQR |
| **Evidence quality** | Moderate-Strong — consistent epidemiological signal, mechanistic pathway established, some residual confounding concerns |
| **Alignment model relevance** | **High.** TRAP operates through dual mechanisms: PHD inactivation (pseudohypoxia) and placental vascular dysfunction (direct hypoxia). The combination may be synergistic — TRAP impairs the oxygen-sensing machinery while simultaneously reducing oxygen delivery. |

## 4. CARBON MONOXIDE (CO)

### 4.1 Direct Oxygen Displacement

Carbon monoxide is unique among environmental exposures in that it produces **direct, immediate fetal hypoxia** through well-characterised molecular mechanisms:

1. **Competitive haemoglobin binding.** CO binds haemoglobin with 200–250× greater affinity than oxygen, forming carboxyhaemoglobin (COHb) [24]. Even low-level CO exposure (e.g., 10–25 ppm occupational levels) produces measurable COHb, reducing oxygen-carrying capacity.

2. **Fetal amplification.** Fetal haemoglobin (HbF) has higher CO affinity than adult haemoglobin. Longo (1977) demonstrated that fetal COHb levels are 10–15% higher than maternal levels at steady state [25]. The fetus is therefore disproportionately affected by maternal CO exposure.

3. **Left-shifted dissociation curve.** COHb shifts the oxygen-haemoglobin dissociation curve leftward, reducing oxygen release at the tissue level. This means that even oxygen molecules bound to non-CO-occupied haemoglobin sites are released less efficiently — a multiplicative effect on tissue hypoxia [24].

### 4.2 Epidemiological Evidence

Ritz et al. (2007) found an association between gestational CO exposure and preterm birth in a Los Angeles cohort (n=81,186; HR 1.08, 95% CI 1.00–1.17 per 1 ppm increase in third trimester) [26]. Ha et al. (2001) reported increased low birth weight risk with CO exposure in Seoul (OR 1.08, 95% CI 1.01–1.16 per IQR increase) [27].

Occupational CO exposure studies are informative. Koren et al. (1991) documented that carbon monoxide poisoning during pregnancy was associated with fetal neurological injury, with severity correlating with COHb levels and exposure duration [28]. Even moderate occupational exposures (parking garages, tollbooths, welding) produce sustained COHb levels of 2–5%, compared with a non-smoker baseline of ~1%.

Maternal smoking — the most common voluntary CO exposure — produces COHb levels of 5–10%. The smoking-birth weight relationship (mean reduction ~200g) is partially mediated through CO-induced fetal hypoxia [29].

### 4.3 Molecular Mechanism in Alignment Model Context

CO produces the purest form of direct hypoxia among environmental exposures. The mechanism is:

CO → ↑ COHb → ↓ maternal O₂ carrying capacity → ↓ placental O₂ transfer → ↓ fetal pO₂ → HIF-1α stabilisation (via O₂-dependent PHD inactivation) → developmental programme disruption in open vulnerability windows

This is not pseudohypoxia — it is actual oxygen deprivation following the canonical PHD–VHL pathway. Additionally, CO itself can bind directly to the Fe²⁺ centre of PHD enzymes, providing a secondary pseudohypoxia mechanism [30].

### 4.4 Assessment

| Parameter | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Mechanism** | Haemoglobin O₂ displacement → fetal hypoxia → canonical HIF-1α activation; secondary PHD Fe²⁺ binding → pseudohypoxia |
| **Hypoxia type** | Acute direct hypoxia (episodic or sustained depending on exposure) |
| **Effect sizes** | Preterm birth: HR 1.08 per ppm; fetal growth restriction proportional to COHb |
| **Evidence quality** | Strong for mechanism; Moderate for gestational epidemiology (confounded by co-pollutants and smoking confounding) |
| **Alignment model relevance** | **Very High.** CO is the most mechanistically direct environmental activator of the alignment model. It mimics the exact oxygen deprivation pattern of preeclamptic vasospasm. Episodic exposures (commuting through traffic, indoor cooking with gas) produce oscillating hypoxia — the alignment model's amplification pattern. Smoking mothers effectively impose chronic CO-mediated HIF-1α activation on the fetus for the entire gestational period. |

## 5. ENDOCRINE-DISRUPTING CHEMICALS (EDCs)

### 5.1 Bisphenol A (BPA)

BPA is a ubiquitous plasticiser found in polycarbonate containers, thermal receipt paper, and epoxy resin linings. Maternal BPA exposure is nearly universal in industrialised populations, with detectable levels in >90% of pregnant women [31].

**Placental effects:** Lan et al. (2017) demonstrated that BPA exposure reduces trophoblast invasion and spiral artery remodelling in vitro, directly impairing placental vascularisation [32]. Müller et al. (2018) showed that BPA activates HIF-1α in human endometrial stromal cells, providing a direct link to the pseudohypoxia pathway [33].

**Anti-angiogenic effects:** BPA exposure has been associated with altered VEGF expression and increased sFlt-1 in placental tissue [34]. This mimics the anti-angiogenic imbalance of preeclampsia.

**Neurodevelopmental outcomes:** Braun et al. (2011), in the HOME Study (n=244), reported that gestational BPA exposure (per log-unit increase in urinary BPA) was associated with poorer behaviour scores in girls at age 3 (β = 6.0, 95% CI 0.1–12.0 on Behavioral Assessment System for Children-2 externalising score) [35]. A meta-analysis by Rochester et al. (2018) across 29 studies confirmed associations between gestational BPA and neurobehavioural outcomes, with effect sizes varying by sex and exposure timing [36].

### 5.2 Phthalates

Phthalates are plasticisers found in personal care products, food packaging, and medical devices. Exposure is ubiquitous: NHANES data detect metabolites of multiple phthalates in >95% of pregnant women [37].

**Placental disruption:** Adibi et al. (2017) demonstrated that phthalate metabolites are detectable in placental tissue and are associated with altered oxidative stress markers [38]. Ferguson et al. (2015) reported that urinary phthalate metabolites during pregnancy were associated with elevated oxidative stress biomarkers (8-isoprostane) in a dose-response relationship [39].

**HIF pathway activation:** Oxidative stress induced by phthalates follows the same Fe²⁺ oxidation mechanism as PM2.5: ROS → Fe²⁺ to Fe³⁺ conversion → PHD inactivation → HIF-1α stabilisation. Zhang et al. (2019) demonstrated that di-(2-ethylhexyl) phthalate (DEHP) exposure induces placental oxidative stress and alters angiogenic factor expression (reduced VEGF, elevated sFlt-1) in a rat model [40].

**ASD association:** Oulhote et al. (2020) found modest associations between gestational phthalate exposure and autistic traits in boys in the SELMA cohort (n=802) [41].

### 5.3 Per- and Polyfluoroalkyl Substances (PFAS)

PFAS are persistent synthetic chemicals ("forever chemicals") found in non-stick coatings, food packaging, firefighting foam, and drinking water. Their biological persistence (half-lives of years) means that accumulated maternal body burden is the primary exposure metric.

**Placental effects:** Suh et al. (2011) demonstrated that PFOS reduces trophoblast cell viability and alters cytokine secretion [42]. Wikström et al. (2020), in the Uppsala SELMA study (n=1,773), found that PFAS exposure was associated with preeclampsia (OR 1.47, 95% CI 1.07–2.02 for PFOS per IQR increase) [43].

**Anti-angiogenic pathway:** Rosen et al. (2009) reported that PFOS activates PPARα and PPARγ nuclear receptors, which cross-talk with HIF-1α signalling and can modulate VEGF expression [44]. Liew et al. (2020) reported that PFAS exposure during pregnancy was associated with reduced PlGF and elevated sFlt-1 — the anti-angiogenic signature [45].

**Neurodevelopmental outcomes:** Liew et al. (2015), using Danish National Birth Cohort data (n=156 ASD cases in 83,389 pregnancies), reported associations between PFOS levels and ASD-related diagnosis (HR 1.31 per doubling of PFOS, 95% CI 1.01–1.69) [46].

### 5.4 Polychlorinated Biphenyls (PCBs)

PCBs, though banned since the 1970s–1980s, persist in the environment and bioaccumulate through the food chain. Gestational exposure occurs via maternal body burden.

**Placental accumulation and dysfunction:** Toppari et al. (2010) reviewed evidence for PCBs crossing the placenta and concentrating in placental tissue, where they induce CYP1A1 expression, generate ROS, and impair trophoblast function [47].

**HIF activation:** Dioxin-like PCBs (coplanar congeners PCB-77, PCB-126) activate the aryl hydrocarbon receptor (AhR), which cross-talks extensively with HIF-1α. Chan et al. (1999) demonstrated that AhR and HIF-1β (ARNT) are obligate heterodimer partners, creating direct competition: AhR activation sequestering ARNT can dysregulate HIF-mediated oxygen sensing [48].

### 5.5 Assessment

| Parameter | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Mechanism** | Multiple: BPA → HIF-1α activation + anti-angiogenic shift; Phthalates → oxidative stress → PHD inactivation → pseudohypoxia; PFAS → sFlt-1/PlGF imbalance → anti-angiogenic mimicry; PCBs → AhR-ARNT competition → HIF pathway dysregulation |
| **Hypoxia type** | Chronic pseudohypoxia and anti-angiogenic mimicry; typically not acute |
| **Effect sizes** | Variable: BPA neurobehavioural β ≈ 6.0; PFAS–PE OR 1.47; PFAS–ASD HR 1.31 |
| **Evidence quality** | Moderate — strong mechanistic evidence, epidemiological studies heterogeneous partly due to mixture exposure challenges |
| **Alignment model relevance** | **High.** EDCs are particularly important because they replicate the anti-angiogenic profile (elevated sFlt-1, suppressed PlGF) that the alignment model identifies as the molecular signature of placental insufficiency. PFAS's association with preeclampsia itself suggests EDCs may cause preeclampsia, not merely mimic it. The AhR–HIF-1β competition from PCBs is a novel pseudohypoxia mechanism not described in the parent model. |

## 6. HEAVY METALS

### 6.1 Lead (Pb)

**Placental accumulation:** Lead crosses the placental barrier freely. Maternal bone lead stores accumulated over a lifetime are mobilised during pregnancy due to increased bone turnover, providing a sustained source of fetal exposure independent of current environmental levels [49].

**Mechanism:** Lead displaces iron and zinc from metalloenzymes. Critically, Pb²⁺ can substitute for Fe²⁺ at the PHD catalytic centre, inhibiting prolyl hydroxylase activity and directly producing pseudohypoxia [50]. Additionally, lead induces oxidative stress by depleting glutathione and inhibiting δ-aminolevulinic acid dehydratase (ALAD), required for haem synthesis — reducing maternal haemoglobin oxygen-carrying capacity (a direct hypoxia mechanism) [51].

**Epidemiological evidence:** Bellinger et al. (1987) established the inverse relationship between cord blood lead levels and neurodevelopmental outcomes in a landmark prospective study (n=249) — IQ reduction of ~5–7 points per 10 μg/dL increase in blood lead [52]. Talbot et al. (2018) reported associations between gestational lead exposure and ASD-related behaviours (OR 1.43, 95% CI 1.02–2.00 for highest tertile) [53].

### 6.2 Mercury (Hg)

**Placental transport:** Methylmercury crosses the placenta efficiently, and fetal blood mercury concentrations are typically 1.5–2× maternal levels [54]. The placenta concentrates inorganic mercury.

**Mechanism:** Mercury has high affinity for selenocysteine residues, inhibiting selenoproteins including glutathione peroxidase — a critical antioxidant. This increases placental oxidative stress, following the ROS → PHD inactivation → pseudohypoxia chain. Grandjean et al. (1997), in the Faroe Islands cohort (n=917 children), established dose-response relationships between cord blood mercury and neurodevelopmental deficits [55].

**Placental pathology:** Al-Saleh et al. (2011) demonstrated associations between placental mercury concentration and histopathological changes including villous oedema, fibrin deposition, and reduced vascularisation — structural changes that directly impair placental oxygen transfer [56].

### 6.3 Cadmium (Cd)

**Placental effects:** The placenta partially blocks cadmium transfer (fetal levels are typically only 10–20% of maternal levels), but in doing so, cadmium accumulates in placental tissue [57]. This placental accumulation is the critical finding for the alignment model: cadmium exerts its effects at the placental interface, not by reaching the fetus.

**HIF-1α activation:** Jing et al. (2012) demonstrated that cadmium directly stabilises HIF-1α in human cells by inhibiting PHD activity — a well-characterised pseudohypoxia mechanism [58]. The mechanism involves cadmium displacing Fe²⁺ from the PHD active site and generating ROS through Fenton chemistry disruption.

**Placental vascular effects:** Kippler et al. (2012), in a Bangladeshi cohort (n=1,616), reported dose-dependent associations between maternal cadmium exposure and reduced birth weight, mediated through placental mechanisms [59]. Brooks et al. (2016) demonstrated that cadmium impairs placental angiogenesis and reduces VEGF expression in vitro [60].

### 6.4 Arsenic (As)

**Exposure routes:** Arsenic exposure via contaminated groundwater affects >200 million people globally, with the highest prevalence in Bangladesh, West Bengal, Vietnam, and parts of Latin America [61].

**Placental HIF activation:** Shen et al. (2012) demonstrated that arsenite (As³⁺) stabilises HIF-1α by inhibiting PHD2 activity and by generating ROS — dual pseudohypoxia mechanisms [62]. Arsenic also inhibits the mitochondrial electron transport chain at Complex IV, reducing cellular oxygen consumption in a way that paradoxically stabilises HIF-1α.

**Epidemiological evidence:** Hamadani et al. (2011) reported dose-dependent associations between maternal arsenic exposure via drinking water and reduced cognitive development scores in children at age 5 in a Bangladeshi cohort (n=2,112; β = −0.57, 95% CI −0.89 to −0.25 per 100 μg/L increase in water arsenic) [63]. Rahman et al. (2009) found associations between maternal arsenic exposure and infant morbidity, mediated through placental dysfunction (impaired placental weight and morphology) [64].

| Parameter | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Mechanism** | Lead: Fe²⁺ displacement at PHD + haem synthesis inhibition → pseudohypoxia + direct hypoxia; Mercury: selenoprotein inhibition → oxidative stress → PHD inactivation; Cadmium: direct PHD Fe²⁺ displacement → pseudohypoxia + placental VEGF suppression; Arsenic: PHD2 inhibition + ROS → HIF-1α stabilisation |
| **Hypoxia type** | Predominantly chronic pseudohypoxia; lead additionally causes direct hypoxia via anaemia |
| **Effect sizes** | Lead–IQ: −5–7 points per 10 μg/dL; Cadmium–birth weight: dose-dependent; Arsenic–cognition: β = −0.57 per 100 μg/L |
| **Evidence quality** | Strong for lead and mercury (decades of replicated data); Moderate-Strong for cadmium and arsenic (growing evidence base with clear mechanisms) |
| **Alignment model relevance** | **Very High.** Heavy metals are the most direct chemical activators of pseudohypoxia in the alignment model. Cadmium's mechanism — displaced Fe²⁺ at the PHD catalytic centre — is the chemical equivalent of iron deficiency. Lead's dual mechanism (pseudohypoxia + direct hypoxia via anaemia) mirrors the parent model's description of how iron deficiency attacks oxygen homeostasis from both sides simultaneously. The global distribution of heavy metal exposure (lead paint, mercury in fish, arsenic in groundwater, cadmium in tobacco and rice) means these are not rare exposures but population-level pseudohypoxia sources. |

## 7. PESTICIDES AND HERBICIDES

### 7.1 Organophosphates (OPs)

**Exposure:** OPs (chlorpyrifos, diazinon, malathion) are used globally in agriculture. Gestational exposure occurs through diet, residential proximity to agricultural land, and occupational contact.

**Placental effects:** Demonstrated placental transport of OP metabolites. Rauh et al. (2006) found cord blood chlorpyrifos levels correlated with maternal levels, confirming transplacental passage [65].

**ASD association:** Von Ehrenstein et al. (2019), in a California population-based study (n=2,961 ASD cases), reported that prenatal proximity to organophosphate pesticide application was associated with ASD (OR 1.13, 95% CI 1.05–1.23) and intellectual disability (OR 1.30, 95% CI 1.10–1.54) [66]. Shelton et al. (2014), in the CHARGE study, found that gestational proximity to organophosphate-applied agricultural fields was associated with ASD (OR 1.60, 95% CI 1.02–2.51) [67].

**Mechanism:** OPs inhibit acetylcholinesterase but also have acetylcholinesterase-independent effects relevant to the alignment model. Chlorpyrifos induces oxidative stress and mitochondrial dysfunction in placental cells [68]. Slotkin et al. (2012) demonstrated that chlorpyrifos disrupts adenylyl cyclase signalling and cellular energy metabolism — impairing mitochondrial oxygen consumption and potentially triggering pseudohypoxia through metabolic disruption [69].

### 7.2 Organochlorines (OCs)

Organochlorines (DDT/DDE, lindane, endosulfan) are persistent and bioaccumulative. Despite bans in many countries, maternal body burden from historical contamination persists.

**Placental effects:** Torres-Sánchez et al. (2013) demonstrated that DDE concentrations in placental tissue were associated with altered placental histology, including reduced villous vascularisation [70]. This directly reduces the gas exchange surface for oxygen transfer.

**HIF pathway interference:** Organochlorines activate the AhR receptor, competing for ARNT/HIF-1β — the same mechanism described for dioxin-like PCBs [48]. This disrupts normal HIF-1α-mediated oxygen sensing.

### 7.3 Glyphosate

Glyphosate (Roundup) is the world's most widely used herbicide. Evidence for gestational effects is emerging but less mature than for organophosphates.

**Placental effects:** Mesnage et al. (2017) demonstrated that glyphosate exposure disrupts aromatase activity and steroidogenesis in human placental JEG-3 cells [71]. Parvez et al. (2018), in a prospective birth cohort in Indiana, found detectable urinary glyphosate in >90% of pregnant women, with higher levels associated with shortened gestational length (β = −0.9 weeks in the highest exposure quartile, p < 0.05) [72].

**Mechanism:** Glyphosate's relevance to the alignment model is primarily through oxidative stress induction and mitochondrial disruption, rather than direct HIF activation. Mesnage et al. (2015) reviewed evidence for glyphosate-induced oxidative stress in multiple tissue types [73].

### 7.4 Assessment

| Parameter | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Mechanism** | OPs: oxidative stress + mitochondrial dysfunction → pseudohypoxia; OCs: AhR–ARNT competition → HIF pathway disruption; Glyphosate: oxidative stress + endocrine disruption |
| **Hypoxia type** | Chronic pseudohypoxia (OPs, glyphosate); HIF pathway dysregulation (OCs) |
| **Effect sizes** | OPs–ASD: OR 1.13–1.60; OPs–ID: OR 1.30 |
| **Evidence quality** | Moderate for OPs (consistent epidemiology, mechanistic support); Moderate for OCs (strong mechanistic, less specific epidemiology); Emerging for glyphosate |
| **Alignment model relevance** | **Moderate.** Pesticides are less directly connected to the HIF-1α pathway than heavy metals or PM2.5 but still converge on the model through oxidative stress (OPs, glyphosate) and AhR-ARNT competition (OCs). The agricultural population exposure is significant: farmworkers and agricultural communities represent a defined high-risk group. Von Ehrenstein's finding that proximity to pesticide application is associated with ASD independently of other risk factors is consistent with an environmental contribution to the alignment model's prediction of gestational oxygen disruption producing neurodevelopmental injury. |

## 8. INDOOR AIR QUALITY

### 8.1 Volatile Organic Compounds (VOCs) and Formaldehyde

**Exposure:** Indoor VOC exposure — from paints, adhesives, cleaning products, new furniture, and building materials — is often higher than outdoor levels. Pregnant women may have increased indoor exposure due to nesting activities (room painting, nursery preparation).

**Mechanism:** Formaldehyde is classified as a Group 1 carcinogen (IARC) and induces oxidative stress, DNA damage, and inflammatory responses. Kim et al. (2011) demonstrated that formaldehyde exposure activates HIF-1α in vitro through ROS-mediated PHD inactivation [74]. Toluene, xylene, and benzene — common indoor VOCs — are known to cross the placenta and have been associated with low birth weight in occupational exposure studies [75].

**Neurodevelopmental effects:** Guxens et al. (2012), in the INMA cohort (n=2,150 mother-child pairs), found that prenatal exposure to benzene was associated with reduced cognitive scores at age 14 months (β = −1.57 per doubling of benzene, 95% CI −2.93 to −0.21) [76].

### 8.2 Biomass Fuel Smoke (LMIC Relevance)

**Exposure:** Approximately 2.6 billion people globally rely on biomass fuels (wood, dung, crop residues) for cooking and heating, predominantly in low- and middle-income countries. Pregnant women in these settings experience chronic, high-level indoor air pollution.

**Components:** Biomass combustion produces PM2.5 (often at levels 10–50× WHO guidelines), CO, formaldehyde, PAHs, and black carbon — a mixture that activates multiple alignment model pathways simultaneously.

**Evidence:** Pope et al. (2010) demonstrated that biomass fuel use during pregnancy in Guatemala was associated with reduced birth weight (adjusted difference −89g, 95% CI −3 to −175g) [77]. Amegah et al. (2014) systematically reviewed 18 studies and found consistent associations between household air pollution from solid fuel use and low birth weight (pooled OR 1.38, 95% CI 1.25–1.52) and preterm birth (OR 1.29, 95% CI 1.08–1.54) [78].

**Mechanism and alignment model connection:** Biomass fuel exposure combines CO-mediated direct hypoxia (Section 4) with PM2.5-mediated pseudohypoxia (Section 2). The combination attacks fetal oxygenation from multiple directions simultaneously. In LMICs where biomass fuel use co-occurs with maternal iron deficiency (the parent model's primary pseudohypoxia mechanism) and nutritional ascorbate deficiency, the result is triple-loading of the PHD–VHL–FIH system: reduced oxygen (CO), reduced iron (anaemia), and PHD inactivation (PM2.5 oxidative stress). The parent model predicted that 40–60% of pregnancies globally experience pseudohypoxia via iron deficiency alone. Biomass fuel exposure adds a second, independent pseudohypoxia axis to the same populations.

### 8.3 Assessment

| Parameter | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Mechanism** | Formaldehyde/VOCs: ROS → HIF-1α activation; Biomass smoke: CO → direct hypoxia + PM2.5 → pseudohypoxia + PAHs → inflammation → placental dysfunction |
| **Hypoxia type** | Chronic direct hypoxia (CO component) + chronic pseudohypoxia (PM2.5/ROS component) |
| **Effect sizes** | Biomass–LBW: OR 1.38; Biomass–preterm: OR 1.29; Benzene–cognition: β = −1.57 per doubling |
| **Evidence quality** | Strong for biomass (large evidence base, globally replicated); Moderate for specific VOCs (mechanistic data strong, epidemiology limited by mixture effects) |
| **Alignment model relevance** | **Very High for biomass fuel (LMIC).** This is arguably the most globally significant environmental extension of the alignment model. Biomass fuel exposure activates every pathway in the model simultaneously (direct hypoxia, pseudohypoxia, inflammation, oxidative stress) and co-occurs with the iron deficiency and nutritional deficits that the parent model already identified as major pseudohypoxia contributors. The population affected — 2.6 billion people — dwarfs the prevalence of preeclampsia. If the alignment model is correct, biomass fuel exposure may be the single largest environmental contributor to gestational oxygen disruption globally. |

## 9. WATER CONTAMINANTS

### 9.1 Chlorination Disinfection Byproducts (DBPs)

**Exposure:** Trihalomethanes (THMs) and haloacetic acids (HAAs) are formed when chlorine reacts with organic matter in water supplies. Exposure occurs through drinking, bathing, and swimming.

**Placental effects:** Nieuwenhuijsen et al. (2013) reviewed 15 studies and found associations between THM exposure and small-for-gestational-age births (pooled OR 1.01–1.09 per 10 μg/L THM increase), with stronger effects for brominated species [79]. Levallois et al. (2012) reported associations between chloroform (the most common THM) and PlGF levels in a Quebec cohort, suggesting anti-angiogenic pathway engagement [80].

**Mechanism:** THMs cross the placenta and are detectable in cord blood. They generate oxidative stress and have been shown to impair trophoblast viability in vitro. The magnitude of effects is generally small, suggesting DBPs are a modest contributor to placental oxygen disruption.

### 9.2 Nitrates in Drinking Water

**Exposure:** Agricultural runoff contaminates groundwater with nitrates. Maternal exposure ≥10 mg/L NO₃-N (the U.S. EPA maximum contaminant level) is common in agricultural areas.

**Mechanism:** Ingested nitrate is reduced to nitrite, which oxidises haemoglobin to methaemoglobin — which cannot carry oxygen. Methemoglobinaemia in pregnancy reduces maternal oxygen transport capacity, directly mimicking the CO mechanism (Section 4). Fan et al. (2016) found associations between maternal nitrate exposure and preterm birth in an Iowa cohort [81]. Brender et al. (2013) reported associations between nitrate intake and neural tube defects (OR 1.6–2.0 for highest exposure quartile) [82].

**Alignment model relevance:** Moderate. Nitrate-induced methaemoglobinaemia is a direct oxygen displacement mechanism (comparable to CO), but the effect magnitude at environmental exposure levels is smaller than from CO or PM2.5. In high-nitrate agricultural communities, however, the contribution may be clinically significant, particularly when combined with other exposures.

### 9.3 PFAS in Water

PFAS water contamination (discussed in Section 5.3) is also a water contaminant concern. Communities near military bases, firefighting training sites, and fluorochemical manufacturing facilities have elevated PFAS in drinking water. Anderson et al. (2019) found associations between community-level PFAS water contamination and preterm birth rates [83].

### 9.4 Assessment

| Parameter | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Mechanism** | DBPs: oxidative stress → PHD inactivation → mild pseudohypoxia; Nitrates: metHb formation → direct oxygen displacement; PFAS: anti-angiogenic pathway (see Section 5.3) |
| **Hypoxia type** | DBPs: mild chronic pseudohypoxia; Nitrates: direct hypoxia (dose-dependent); PFAS: anti-angiogenic mimicry |
| **Effect sizes** | DBPs–SGA: OR 1.01–1.09; Nitrates–NTD: OR 1.6–2.0; PFAS–preterm: variable |
| **Evidence quality** | Moderate for DBPs (weak individual effects, large population exposure); Moderate for nitrates (mechanistically clear, epidemiology variable); Moderate for PFAS (see Section 5.3) |
| **Alignment model relevance** | **Moderate.** Water contaminants individually produce smaller effects than airborne exposures, but their population exposure is vast and chronic. Nitrate-induced methaemoglobinaemia is mechanistically equivalent to CO-mediated oxygen displacement and represents an underappreciated direct hypoxia mechanism, particularly in agricultural regions of LMICs where clean water infrastructure is limited. |

## 10. ELECTROMAGNETIC AND RADIATION EXPOSURE

### 10.1 Ionising Radiation

**Evidence-based findings:** The most robust evidence comes from occupational and diagnostic radiation exposure during pregnancy. Wakeford (2004) summarised evidence that in-utero diagnostic X-ray exposure (>50 mGy) is associated with childhood cancer risk (RR ~1.4) and that high-dose exposure (>100 mGy) during weeks 8–25 of gestation can cause intellectual disability and microcephaly [84].

**Mechanism:** Ionising radiation generates hydroxyl radicals from water radiolysis — a direct oxidative stress mechanism. At diagnostic doses, the primary concern is DNA damage and mutagenesis rather than HIF pathway activation. There is no robust evidence that diagnostic-level radiation exposure produces placental vascular disruption or HIF-1α activation at clinically relevant doses.

**Occupational radiation (higher doses):** Schull and Otake (1999), from the Japanese atomic bomb survivor data, established that in-utero radiation exposure >100 mGy causes dose-dependent reductions in IQ (~25 IQ points per Gy between weeks 8–15 of gestation) and that the threshold for severe intellectual disability is approximately 120 mGy [85]. However, these exposure levels are far above environmental and diagnostic norms.

### 10.2 Non-Ionising Radiation and Electromagnetic Fields (EMF)

**Evidence assessment:** The evidence for EMF effects on pregnancy outcomes is weak and inconsistent. Li et al. (2017) reported that pregnant women with higher measured magnetic field exposure had increased miscarriage risk (HR 2.72, 95% CI 1.42–5.19) in a prospective study (n=913), but this has not been consistently replicated [86]. No evidence-based mechanism for EMF-induced placental vascular disruption or HIF interference has been established. The absence of a plausible molecular mechanism connecting sub-thermal EMF to the PHD–VHL–FIH pathway makes this category speculative.

### 10.3 Assessment

| Parameter | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Mechanism** | Ionising: hydroxyl radical generation → DNA damage + oxidative stress (at high doses); Non-ionising: no established mechanism for HIF activation |
| **Hypoxia type** | Not applicable at environmental/diagnostic exposure levels |
| **Effect sizes** | Ionising (>100 mGy): IQ reduction ~25 points/Gy; Diagnostic (<50 mGy): childhood cancer RR ~1.4; EMF: inconsistent |
| **Evidence quality** | Strong for high-dose ionising radiation; Weak for diagnostic-dose effects on neurodevelopment; Very Weak for EMF |
| **Alignment model relevance** | **Low.** Ionising radiation at high doses causes neurodevelopmental damage, but through DNA damage and cell death pathways rather than HIF-mediated oxygen disruption. There is no credible evidence that diagnostic radiation or environmental EMF activates the alignment model's molecular cascade. This category is included for completeness but does not contribute to the environmental extension of the model. |

## 11. TEMPERATURE EXTREMES

### 11.1 Heat Stress

**Epidemiological evidence:** Basu et al. (2016) found that extreme heat (≥35°C) during the last trimester was associated with preterm birth (OR 1.11, 95% CI 1.07–1.16) and stillbirth in a California cohort (n=43,629 stillbirths, 2005–2013) [87]. Bekkar et al. (2020) meta-analysed 57 studies and found that extreme heat exposure during pregnancy was associated with preterm birth (pooled OR 1.16, 95% CI 1.10–1.23) and low birth weight [88]. Sun et al. (2019) reported associations between heatwave exposure during pregnancy and preeclampsia risk in a Chinese cohort [89].

**Mechanism — placental vasoconstriction and redistribution:**

1. **Thermoregulatory redistribution.** During heat stress, maternal blood flow is redistributed to the skin for heat dissipation, reducing uterine and placental perfusion. This produces **acute direct hypoxia** — reduced oxygen delivery to the fetus [90].

2. **Heat shock proteins and HIF.** Katschinski et al. (2002) demonstrated that heat stress stabilises HIF-1α independently of oxygen levels, through a mechanism involving heat shock protein 90 (HSP90) binding to HIF-1α and preventing VHL-mediated degradation [91]. This is a temperature-dependent pseudohypoxia mechanism.

3. **Dehydration.** Heat-associated dehydration reduces blood volume, increasing blood viscosity and reducing placental perfusion — an indirect direct hypoxia mechanism.

### 11.2 Cold Stress

**Evidence:** Extreme cold exposure is less studied than heat. Cold stress produces peripheral vasoconstriction; in pregnancy, this includes uterine artery vasoconstriction, reducing placental blood flow. Strand et al. (2012) found modest associations between cold exposure and preterm birth in a Swedish cohort [92]. Acute cold stress can also trigger hypertensive responses in pregnancy, potentially exacerbating subclinical preeclampsia.

### 11.3 Climate Change Implications

Climate change is increasing the frequency, duration, and intensity of heat waves globally. The populations most exposed — tropical and subtropical LMICs — overlap with those already experiencing high biomass fuel exposure and iron deficiency. The alignment model predicts that climate change will increase the burden of gestational oxygen disruption through heat-mediated placental hypoperfusion, adding a third environmental axis to the LMIC burden (iron deficiency + biomass fuel + heat stress).

### 11.4 Assessment

| Parameter | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Mechanism** | Heat: thermoregulatory blood flow redistribution → placental hypoperfusion → direct hypoxia + HSP90-HIF-1α stabilisation → pseudohypoxia; Cold: uterine vasoconstriction → direct hypoxia |
| **Hypoxia type** | Acute direct hypoxia (heat episodes, cold snaps) with temperature-dependent pseudohypoxia (heat) |
| **Effect sizes** | Heat–preterm: OR 1.11–1.16; Heat–stillbirth: elevated risk during extreme events |
| **Evidence quality** | Moderate-Strong for heat (consistent across populations, clear mechanism); Weak for cold (limited studies) |
| **Alignment model relevance** | **Moderate-High.** Heat stress is a direct placental perfusion disruptor producing the same downstream HIF activation as preeclamptic vasospasm. The HSP90–HIF-1α mechanism provides an additional temperature-specific pseudohypoxia pathway. Climate change makes this an increasingly important contributor. Heat waves during weeks 23–36 would produce acute oxygen disruption during the super-window — the alignment model predicts this should produce measurable offspring effects testable in existing climate-health datasets. |

## 12. GEOGRAPHIC AND ECOLOGICAL NATURAL EXPERIMENTS

### 12.1 Industrial Disasters

**Bhopal (1984):** The methyl isocyanate gas leak in Bhopal, India, exposed an estimated 500,000 people. Dhara and Dhara (2002) documented the multi-generational effects: women exposed during pregnancy had significantly higher rates of spontaneous abortion, stillbirth, and neonatal death [93]. Offspring of exposed mothers showed increased rates of neurodevelopmental delay, growth retardation, and immune dysfunction. The mechanism involved massive oxidative stress, pulmonary damage reducing maternal oxygenation, and direct cytotoxic injury to placental tissue.

**Seveso (1976):** The Seveso dioxin (TCDD) release exposed a defined population to known levels of the most potent AhR agonist. Mocarelli et al. (2008) documented that TCDD exposure produced endocrine disruption across generations [94]. Pesatori et al. (2009) found altered sex ratios and reproductive outcomes in the exposed cohort [95]. TCDD's AhR activation directly competes for HIF-1β/ARNT — the same HIF pathway competition mechanism described in Section 5.4 for PCBs.

### 12.2 Mining and Smelter Communities

**Trail, British Columbia (lead-zinc smelter):** Lanphear et al. (2005) documented elevated blood lead levels and associated neurodevelopmental deficits in children in smelter communities, with effects extending to prenatal exposure through maternal body burden [96].

**Andean mining communities:** Populations at altitude near mining operations experience combined exposure to altitude-related chronic hypoxia AND heavy metal contamination — a double hit on the alignment model's pathways. Giussani et al. (2001) documented that high-altitude pregnancy (>3,000m) is characterised by chronic placental hypoperfusion and reduced birth weight, with effects mediated through HIF-1α pathway activation [97].

### 12.3 Urban-Rural and Environmental Justice Gradients

**Environmental justice data:** Communities of colour and low socioeconomic status are disproportionately exposed to traffic-related air pollution, industrial emissions, and legacy contamination sites. Woodruff et al. (2003) demonstrated that preterm birth rates correlated with PM2.5 exposure in a U.S. nationwide analysis (n=3.5 million births), with significant disparities by race and income [98]. These gradients represent quasi-natural experiments: populations with genetically similar backgrounds but markedly different environmental exposures.

**China's Huai River heating policy:** Chen et al. (2013) used the Huai River policy (free coal for heating north of the Huai River, but not south) as a regression discontinuity design, finding that air pollution north of the river reduced life expectancy by 5.5 years. This policy created a sharp exposure gradient applicable to gestational outcomes analysis [99].

### 12.4 Assessment

| Parameter | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Mechanism** | Variable by event — Bhopal: massive oxidative stress + pulmonary hypoxia; Seveso: AhR–ARNT competition → HIF dysregulation; Mining: heavy metal pseudohypoxia + altitude hypoxia; Urban gradients: cumulative multi-pollutant exposure |
| **Hypoxia type** | Variable: acute massive (Bhopal), chronic pseudohypoxia (mining/smelter), chronic combined (urban) |
| **Effect sizes** | Variable and often extreme in disaster settings; urban gradients: consistent modest effects (OR 1.1–1.3 for neurodevelopmental outcomes) |
| **Evidence quality** | Variable — industrial disasters provide powerful natural experiments but with methodological limitations (non-random exposure, recall bias, loss to follow-up); urban gradient data strong |
| **Alignment model relevance** | **High for conceptual framework.** Natural experiments provide the strongest quasi-causal evidence that environmental exposures produce the offspring outcomes predicted by the alignment model. The altitude + mining combination in Andean communities is particularly informative: it combines direct hypoxia (altitude) with pseudohypoxia (heavy metals) in a population that can be compared with altitude-matched non-mining communities, isolating the environmental exposure effect. |

**Key finding:** Pseudohypoxia (PHD inactivation) is the dominant mechanism across environmental exposures, not direct hypoxia. Most exposures do not reduce oxygen levels — they prevent the cell from sensing and responding to oxygen normally. This extends the parent model's insight about iron deficiency: the environment is full of PHD disruptors.

### 13.2 The Mixture Problem

Real-world exposure is never to a single agent. A pregnant woman in an LMIC urban setting may simultaneously experience:
- PM2.5 at 50+ μg/m³ (pseudo hypoxia via oxidative stress)
- Biomass fuel CO (direct hypoxia)
- Iron deficiency (pseudohypoxia — the parent model's primary mechanism)
- Lead from legacy paint/petrol (pseudohypoxia via Fe²⁺ displacement)
- Phthalates from food packaging (pseudohypoxia via ROS)
- Heat stress (direct hypoxia via redistribution)

Each exposure individually produces modest effect sizes (OR 1.05–1.50). But they all converge on the same molecular pathway. The alignment model predicts that their effects should be additive or synergistic, because each attacks a different node of the same PHD–VHL–FIH counter-regulatory system. A PHD enzyme simultaneously deprived of adequate Fe²⁺ (iron deficiency), exposed to ROS that oxidise remaining Fe²⁺ (PM2.5), and competing with Cd²⁺ or Pb²⁺ for its catalytic centre (heavy metals) is far more compromised than any single exposure would predict.

This "multiple subthreshold hit" phenomenon may explain why individual environmental exposure studies produce modest effect sizes that seem insufficient to account for disease burden, while populations experiencing cumulative multi-exposure environments show substantially worse outcomes. The alignment model provides a mechanistic framework for understanding exposure mixtures: they are not independent risk factors with separate pathways — they are parallel inputs to a single molecular sensor.

### 13.3 The "Effective Placental Insufficiency" Concept

The synthesis supports broadening the alignment model's scope from "preeclampsia" as the primary exposure to **"effective placental insufficiency"** — any condition, endogenous or exogenous, that degrades the molecular environment at the fetal-placental interface during vulnerability windows. Under this expanded framework:

- **Clinical preeclampsia** is the most severe endogenous form — significant placental insufficiency producing maternal symptoms.
- **Subclinical placental stress** from environmental exposures may produce the same molecular signature (sFlt-1 elevation, pseudohypoxia, oxidative stress) without crossing the clinical threshold for preeclampsia diagnosis.
- **The alignment model's predictions apply equally** to both: the gestational timing of effective placental insufficiency determines which vulnerability windows are affected, regardless of whether the insufficiency comes from preeclamptic vasospasm or environmental PHD inhibition.

This reconceptualisation has an important epidemiological implication: the alignment model's population reach may be far larger than the 3–8% preeclampsia prevalence. If environmental exposures producing subclinical placental stress activate the same pathway, then a substantial fraction of pregnancies globally — particularly in LMIC settings with cumulative environmental burdens — may experience effective placental insufficiency without any clinical diagnosis.

## 14. TESTABLE PREDICTIONS FROM THE ENVIRONMENTAL EXTENSION

### 14.1 Primary Predictions

1. **Pollution–outcome dose-response should follow vulnerability window timing.** If PM2.5 exposure is constant throughout pregnancy, the alignment model predicts that biomarker assessment should show the highest sFlt-1/lowest PlGF during weeks 23–36, corresponding to the super-window — not a flat dose-response across all trimesters.

2. **Pollution + iron deficiency should be synergistic.** Mothers with iron deficiency AND elevated PM2.5 exposure should show worse offspring outcomes than predicted by either risk factor alone, because both attack PHD function through independent mechanisms (Fe²⁺ depletion + Fe²⁺ oxidation).

3. **Clean air interventions should improve placental biomarkers.** Indoor air filtration interventions during pregnancy (HEPA filters reducing PM2.5) should reduce sFlt-1/PlGF ratio — testable in RCT design. Preliminary evidence: Allen et al. (2011) demonstrated that HEPA filtration reduces indoor PM2.5 exposure and improves endothelial function markers in non-pregnant adults [100].

4. **Mining community offspring should show alignment model–predicted outcome profiles.** Children born in heavy metal–contaminated communities should show the staggered multi-organ deficit pattern (motor → language → cognition → cardiovascular) predicted by the alignment model, at rates higher than matched communities without contamination.

5. **Cord blood HIF3A methylation should correlate with environmental exposure.** Maternal PM2.5 exposure during specific trimesters should predict HIF3A methylation patterns in cord blood, providing a molecular bridge between environmental exposure and the alignment model's epigenetic detection strategy.

### 14.2 Study Designs

**PRISM-Environment:** Extension of the proposed PRISM study to include environmental exposure assessment (personal air monitors, biomarker panels for metals/EDCs, residential proximity to traffic/industry) as covariates, testing whether environmental exposures produce alignment model–predicted outcome patterns after adjusting for preeclampsia status.

**LMIC Clean Air–Developmental Outcomes Trial:** Cluster-randomised trial of clean cookstove or HEPA filter interventions during pregnancy in biomass fuel–using communities, with offspring neurodevelopmental assessment at 3 and 5 years. Tests the strongest environmental prediction of the model.

**Biobank Epigenetic Analysis:** Use existing cord blood biobanks with linked maternal residence data and air quality monitoring to test the PM2.5 → HIF3A methylation → offspring outcome chain retrospectively.

## 16. CONCLUSIONS

This environmental extension establishes that the Gestational Oxygen-Timing Alignment Model's molecular cascade — HIF-1α stabilisation, PHD inactivation, sFlt-1/PlGF anti-angiogenic imbalance, and placental vascular dysfunction — is not exclusively activated by endogenous preeclamptic pathology. Multiple ubiquitous environmental exposures converge on the same pathway.

The most direct environmental activators of the alignment model are:

1. **Carbon monoxide** — direct oxygen displacement, with fetal amplification via HbF affinity
2. **Heavy metals (Cd, Pb, As)** — PHD Fe²⁺ displacement, the chemical equivalent of iron deficiency
3. **PM2.5** — oxidative stress–mediated PHD inactivation plus sFlt-1 elevation (anti-angiogenic mimicry)
4. **Biomass fuel smoke** — combines CO and PM2.5 mechanisms with the highest population exposure in LMICs
5. **PFAS** — direct anti-angiogenic pathway activation (sFlt-1 elevation, PlGF suppression)

The critical insight is that pseudohypoxia — not direct oxygen deprivation — is the dominant mechanism across environmental exposures. Most environmental agents do not reduce oxygen; they prevent the PHD–VHL–FIH system from correctly sensing oxygen levels. This amplifies the parent model's pseudohypoxia thesis: if iron deficiency alone creates pseudohypoxia in 40–60% of pregnancies, and environmental agents independently inactivate PHDs through oxidative stress, metal displacement, and nitrosative modification, then the true burden of gestational HIF dysregulation is substantially larger than the parent model estimated from iron deficiency alone.

The concept of "effective placental insufficiency" — any exogenous or endogenous input that degrades the fetal molecular environment during vulnerability windows — extends the alignment model from a preeclampsia-specific framework to a general theory of gestational environmental programming. The predictions are testable, the exposures are measurable, and the intervention targets (clean air, clean water, PHD cofactor correction) are actionable.
