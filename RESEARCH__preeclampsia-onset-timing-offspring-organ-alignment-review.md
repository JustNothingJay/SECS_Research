# Systematic Evidence Review: Does Gestational Timing of Preeclampsia Onset Predict Which Organ Systems Show Deficits in Offspring?

## Testing the "Alignment Model"

**Date:** 2026-02-22  
**Search Platform:** PubMed (27 structured searches across 6 categories)  
**Core Question:** Does the gestational week of PE onset determine which developmental programmes absorb injury, producing different latent deficit profiles in EOPE (<34 wk) vs LOPE (≥34 wk) offspring?

---

## 1. Executive Summary

This review systematically searched PubMed for evidence that the gestational timing of preeclampsia (PE) onset predicts **which organ systems** show deficits in offspring, rather than merely whether deficits occur. Twenty-seven structured searches were executed across six categories: EOPE/LOPE comparison, severity/timing interaction, prematurity separation, organ-specific outcomes (neurological, cardiovascular, renal, metabolic, immune, pulmonary), multi-organ studies, and placental pathology as a timing proxy.

**Principal Finding:** The alignment model — that PE onset timing determines organ-specific vulnerability — remains **largely untested** in the human literature. While abundant evidence confirms PE programmes multi-organ deficits in offspring, very few studies directly compare EOPE vs LOPE offspring outcomes stratified by organ system. The evidence that does exist is **mixed**: one key epigenetic study supports tissue-specific programming differences between EOPE and LOPE, while a large cohort study finds gestational age at delivery — not PE subtype — is the strongest predictor. This gap is itself a major finding: the alignment model predicts a pattern the field has not yet systematically examined.

## 2. Organ-System Evidence

### 2.1 Neurological / Neurodevelopmental

#### Study 1: González-Rojas & Valencia-Narbona, 2024
- **Citation:** Int J Mol Sci. 2024;25(7):3632. PMID: 38612445
- **Design:** Narrative literature review (241 references)
- **PE Timing Definition:** Distinguishes "after 20 weeks" generally; references both early and late pregnancy mechanisms
- **Offspring Outcomes:** Cerebral palsy, autism spectrum disorder (ASD), attention deficit hyperactivity disorder (ADHD), sexual dimorphism in neurodevelopmental effects
- **Key Pathophysiological Mechanisms:** Hypoxia, vascular/angiogenic dysregulation, inflammation, neuronal/glial alterations, disruptions in neuronal signalling
- **Does Timing Predict Different Outcomes?** Reviews PE as a unitary exposure; does not explicitly compare EOPE vs LOPE neurodevelopmental profiles. Notes that "molecular events leading to onset originate in early pregnancy and result in insufficient placentation" — implying early placental pathology drives neurodevelopmental risk
- **Key Quote:** "Animal models indicate that PE is correlated with neurodevelopmental alterations and cognitive dysfunctions in offspring and in humans, an association between PE and conditions such as cerebral palsy, autism spectrum disorder, attention deficit hyperactivity disorder, and sexual dimorphism has been observed."
- **Alignment Model:** NEUTRAL — confirms neurodevelopmental risk but does not test timing specificity

#### Study 2: Maher et al., 2019
- **Citation:** Int J Dev Neurosci. 2019;77:69-76. PMID: 30391740
- **Design:** Narrative review (106 references)
- **PE Timing Definition:** "Molecular events leading into the onset of this disease are thought to originate in early pregnancy and result in insufficient placentation"
- **Offspring Outcomes:** ASD risk, brain structural changes, neurodevelopmental alterations
- **Mechanism Focus:** Maternal inflammation as mediating pathway — reviews (1) maternal inflammation in PE, (2) epidemiological evidence for neurodevelopmental alterations, (3) long-term brain changes, (4) how maternal inflammation leads to altered neurodevelopment
- **Does Timing Predict Different Outcomes?** Not directly tested. However, the inflammation-mediated mechanism implies that earlier/more severe PE (with greater inflammatory burden) would have more pronounced neurodevelopmental effects
- **Key Quote:** "A recent systematic review has reported an association between exposure to pre-eclampsia and increased risk of ASD, however the molecular basis of this association is unknown."
- **Alignment Model:** PARTIALLY SUPPORTS — inflammation mechanism is consistent with timing-dependent effects but not explicitly tested

#### Study 3: Carver et al., 2014
- **Citation:** Int J Dev Neurosci. 2014;39:24-34. PMID: 24287098
- **Design:** Murine model (animal study)
- **PE Timing Definition:** Experimental PE model in dams
- **Offspring Outcomes:** Vestibular function, balance, coordination
- **Does Timing Predict Different Outcomes?** Animal model — timing controlled experimentally. Shows PE exposure programmes neuromotor deficits. Prenatal pravastatin treatment prevents impairment, confirming fetal programming mechanism
- **Key Quote:** "Adult offspring born to preeclamptic dams perform poorly on assays testing vestibular function, balance, and coordination, and prenatal pravastatin treatment prevents impairment of fetal programming."
- **Alignment Model:** SUPPORTS — demonstrates programming mechanism; does not test human EOPE vs LOPE

#### Study 4: Ibrahim et al., 2025
- **Citation:** J Child Psychol Psychiatry. 2025;66(10):1606-1620. PMID: 40134364
- **Design:** Systematic review (9 observational studies, 54 references)
- **PE/Placental Timing Definition:** Uses placental histopathology — categorises into maternal vascular malperfusion (MVM) and fetal vascular malperfusion (FVM). MVM is characteristic of EOPE-type placental disease; FVM reflects fetal vascular pathology
- **Offspring Outcomes:** Neurodevelopment from 5 days to 8 years
- **Does Timing Predict Different Outcomes?** YES — and findings are heterogeneous:
  - MVM (more characteristic of EOPE) → associated with poor neurodevelopment at 10-40 months and 8 years
  - FVM → associated with neurodevelopmental disorders in preterm infants at 24 months and 8 years, but BETTER neurodevelopmental scores in other studies at 10-40 months
  - NO association when examining preterm infants to 24 months for MVM
- **Key Quote:** "The pattern of association between MVM and FVM with neurodevelopmental outcomes varied among the included studies. Clinical and methodological heterogeneities and poor reporting of relevant populations' characteristics hindered full understanding of the results."
- **Alignment Model:** PARTIALLY SUPPORTS — MVM vs FVM (proxies for different vascular pathology types) show different neurodevelopmental patterns, but results are inconsistent. The heterogeneity itself may reflect timing-dependent effects obscured by poor phenotyping

### 2.2 Cardiovascular

#### Study 5: Xu et al., 2024
- **Citation:** Curr Hypertens Rep. 2024;26(7):325-337. PMID: 38780756
- **Design:** Systematic review and meta-analysis (23 studies from 2,550 publications)
- **PE Timing Definition:** PE as unitary exposure; stratified analyses by gestational age (but of offspring, not PE onset)
- **Offspring Outcomes:** Systolic and diastolic blood pressure
- **Key Finding:** SBP +2.0 mmHg (95% CI: 1.2-2.8) and DBP +1.4 mmHg (95% CI: 0.9-1.9) higher in PE-exposed offspring vs normotensive controls
- **Does Timing Predict Different Outcomes?** "The correlations were similar in stratified analyses of children and adolescents by sex, geographic area, ages, and gestational age." — This is a key null finding: stratification by GA did NOT reveal different effect sizes, suggesting BP programming occurs regardless of timing
- **Alignment Model:** CHALLENGES — uniform BP effect regardless of GA stratification argues against timing-specific cardiovascular programming

#### Study 6: Chen et al., 2022
- **Citation:** Front Physiol. 2022;12:643926. PMID: 35069228
- **Design:** Cohort study; 23 preterm children of severe PE mothers vs 23 preterm controls; assessed at 5-8 years
- **PE Timing Definition:** "Severe PE" — implicitly early-onset given all subjects were preterm
- **Offspring Outcomes:** LV longitudinal/radial/circumferential strain, RV longitudinal strain (2D speckle-tracking echocardiography)
- **Key Findings:**
  - No significant differences in global LV or RV strain between groups
  - PE offspring had lower weight (20.89 vs 24.41 kg, p<0.05), shorter height (115.6 vs 124.1 cm, p<0.05), and faster heart rates (93 vs 84 bpm, p<0.05)
  - Higher resting HR may presage CVD risk
- **Key Quote:** "Exposure to the intrauterine environment of severe PE during the fetal period did not have a significant impact on cardiac structure in premature children at 5-8 years old, but they had a higher resting heart rate which may be associated with cardiovascular disease in the long run."
- **Alignment Model:** MIXED — severe/early PE does not produce structural cardiac changes by 5-8 years in preterm children (against alignment), but autonomic effects (HR) suggest subclinical programming

#### Study 7: Beukers et al., 2019
- **Citation:** Early Hum Dev. 2019;130:33-37. PMID: 30660016
- **Design:** Prospective cohort; 92 of 174 mother-child pairs; children assessed at age 12
- **PE Timing Definition:** "Severe early-onset HDP" — all growth-restricted, preterm (mean GA 32 wk, range 27-38)
- **Offspring Outcomes:** SBP and DBP standard deviation scores
- **Key Findings:**
  - Mean SBP SDS 0.70 ± 0.81 (elevated)
  - SBP positively associated with very preterm birth (beta 0.53, p=.002) and child BMI SDS (beta 0.25, p=.035)
  - Birth weight ratio NOT associated with BP
  - Maternal BMI ≥25 at 12 years independently associated with child BP
- **Key Quote:** "In 12-years old children born to women with HDP, higher systolic blood pressure values were associated with very preterm birth and child BMI."
- **Alignment Model:** PARTIALLY SUPPORTS — prematurity (a consequence of early-onset PE) independently predicts childhood BP, consistent with early PE producing cardiovascular programming. But the study cannot disentangle PE timing from prematurity itself

#### Study 8: Jansen et al., 2019
- **Citation:** Eur J Prev Cardiol. 2019;26(16):1718-1747. PMID: 31132891
- **Design:** Systematic review (13 studies of 16 identified; children 2-18 years)
- **PE Timing Definition:** Compared pregnancy-induced hypertension (PIH) vs PE vs combined HDP
- **Offspring Outcomes:** BP, cholesterol, triglycerides, glucose
- **Key Findings:**
  - PIH: 4/5 studies found higher BP in offspring
  - PE: Most studies (7/10) found NO significant BP increase
  - No association between HDP and cholesterol, triglycerides, or glucose (5/5 studies null)
- **Key Quote:** "There is no convincing evidence for an association between exposure to preeclampsia and blood pressure in childhood. Based on current evidence, exposure to HDP is not associated with blood levels of cholesterol, triglycerides and glucose in childhood."
- **Alignment Model:** CHALLENGES — finds PIH (milder, often later onset) associates MORE with offspring BP than PE, which is paradoxical if severe/early PE is expected to produce worse cardiovascular outcomes. However, the confound of prematurity-mediated delivery in PE cases may obscure results

#### Study 9: Rice et al., 2018
- **Citation:** Obstet Gynecol. 2018;131(2):313-321. PMID: 29324603
- **Design:** Prospective observational follow-up (NICHD MFMU Network); 979 children at median 7 years
- **PE Timing Definition:** Stratified by term vs preterm delivery x hypertensive vs normotensive
- **Offspring Outcomes:** BP, HDL cholesterol, triglycerides, glucose, HOMA-IR, waist circumference, BMI
- **Key Findings:**
  - Term delivery + hypertensive pregnancy → significantly higher SBP (104 vs 99 mmHg, p=.001)
  - No differences in other cardiometabolic markers (DBP, BMI, waist, HOMA-IR, glucose, lipids)
  - Preterm + hypertensive → no significant differences (possibly underpowered, n=23)
- **Key Quote:** "Pregnancy-associated hypertension in women who deliver at term was associated with higher systolic BP in the offspring, but not with their measures of diastolic BP, BMI, waist circumference, homeostatic model assessment of insulin resistance, glucose, or lipids."
- **Alignment Model:** MIXED — term delivery PE (likely LOPE) showing BP effects but not broader metabolic effects suggests cardiovascular system is selectively vulnerable. The finding that term PE preferentially affects SBP without metabolic markers could support organ-specific vulnerability tied to timing

### 2.3 Renal / Nephrological

#### Study 10: Conte et al., 2025
- **Citation:** Science. 2025;389(6764):1016-1023. PMID: 40906846
- **Design:** Mouse model with lineage tracing and single-cell RNA-seq
- **PE Timing Definition:** Experimental — deletion of estrogen receptor α in renal progenitors
- **Offspring Outcomes:** Nephron number, early-life hypertension, kidney disease susceptibility
- **Key Findings:**
  - PE disrupts estrogen-regulated renal progenitor self-renewal and differentiation
  - Offspring of PE mothers had FEWER NEPHRONS → early-life hypertension → greater kidney disease susceptibility
  - Sexual dimorphism: female kidneys normally adapt to pregnancy workload; this adaptation is disrupted
- **Key Quote:** "Offspring from affected mothers had fewer nephrons, resulting in early-life hypertension and greater susceptibility to kidney disease."
- **Alignment Model:** SUPPORTS — demonstrates that PE disrupts kidney development through a specific developmental window (nephron progenitor activity), consistent with timing-dependent organ vulnerability. Nephrogenesis is complete by 36 weeks in humans, so EOPE exposure during active nephrogenesis should have greater renal impact

#### Study 11: Piccoli et al., 2022
- **Citation:** Pediatr Nephrol. 2022;37(8):1733-1745. PMID: 34735598
- **Design:** Narrative review (121 references); Italian Society of Nephrology
- **PE Timing Definition:** Discusses PE generally; references EOPE/LOPE distinction in pathogenesis
- **Offspring Outcomes:** CKD, hypertension, metabolic diseases, preeclampsia risk in next generation
- **Key Findings:**
  - Low birth weight (SGA, preterm, or both) → increased risk of obesity, hypertension, early-onset cardiopathy, CKD
  - Girls born from PE pregnancies have higher risk of developing PE themselves → intergenerational cycle
  - "Preeclampsia has come to be seen as a window on the future health of both mother and baby"
- **Alignment Model:** NEUTRAL — confirms renal programming pathway but does not test EOPE vs LOPE differentiation

#### Study 12: Cunningham & LaMarca, 2018
- **Citation:** Am J Physiol Regul Integr Comp Physiol. 2018;315(3):R521-R528. PMID: 29897824
- **Design:** Review (70 references)
- **PE Timing Definition:** PE as unitary exposure; discusses developmental programming concept
- **Offspring Outcomes:** CVD, ESRD, stroke, chronic kidney disease, metabolic disease
- **Key Quote:** "The health of the fetus until adulthood can be influenced by developmental programming, in which the fetus is exposed to insults that will ultimately affect the growth of the offspring and increase the offspring's risk of developing hypertension, coronary heart disease, metabolic disease, and chronic kidney disease in adulthood."
- **Alignment Model:** SUPPORTS conceptual framework — developmental programming is the mechanism through which timing-specific organ vulnerability would operate

### 2.4 Metabolic / Endocrine

#### Study 13: Stadler et al., 2023
- **Citation:** Antioxidants (Basel). 2023;12(4):795. PMID: 37107170
- **Design:** Cohort study; 32 normotensive, 18 EOPE, 14 LOPE
- **PE Timing Definition:** Explicitly distinguished EOPE vs LOPE
- **Offspring Outcomes:** Neonatal lipid metabolism, HDL composition and function
- **Key Findings — DIRECTLY RELEVANT TO ALIGNMENT MODEL:**
  - **In mothers:** Both EOPE and LOPE → atherogenic dyslipidemia (high TG, low HDL-C)
  - EOPE specifically → shift from large HDL to smaller HDL subclasses → increased plasma antioxidant capacity
  - **In neonates of EOPE:** Total cholesterol levels INCREASED
  - **In neonates of LOPE:** HDL cholesterol efflux capacity MARKEDLY REDUCED
  - Different neonatal metabolic signatures between EOPE and LOPE
- **Key Quote:** "Early- and late-onset PE profoundly affect maternal lipid metabolism, potentially contributing to disease manifestation and increased cardiovascular risk later in life. PE is also associated with changes in neonatal HDL composition and function, demonstrating that complications of pregnancy affect neonatal lipoprotein metabolism."
- **Alignment Model:** STRONGLY SUPPORTS — demonstrates DIFFERENT metabolic effects in EOPE vs LOPE neonates. EOPE raises total cholesterol; LOPE impairs HDL function. This is precisely the organ-system-specific differentiation the alignment model predicts

#### Study 14: Herzog et al., 2017
- **Citation:** Placenta. 2017;58:122-132. PMID: 28962690
- **Design:** Nested case-control (Rotterdam Periconceptional Cohort); 13 EOPE, 16 LOPE, 36 uncomplicated, 27 normotensive FGR, 20 normotensive PTB
- **PE Timing Definition:** Explicitly EOPE vs LOPE with appropriate controls
- **Offspring Outcomes:** DNA methylation in placenta, umbilical cord white blood cells (UC-WBC), umbilical vein endothelial cells (HUVEC)
- **Key Findings — CRITICAL FOR ALIGNMENT MODEL:**
  - EOPE vs PTB controls: 5,001 mostly hypermethylated DMPs in UC-WBC + 869 mostly hypomethylated DMPs in placenta
  - DMPs situated in/near genes associated with **cardiovascular-metabolic developmental pathways**
  - Tissue-specific epigenetic differences between EOPE and LOPE
  - EOPE showed greater epigenetic impact than LOPE
- **Key Quote:** "This study shows differential methylation in UC-WBC and placental tissue in EOPE as compared to PTB, identifying DMPs that are associated with cardiovascular system pathways."
- **Alignment Model:** STRONGLY SUPPORTS — demonstrates tissue-specific epigenetic programming that differs between EOPE and LOPE, affecting cardiovascular-metabolic developmental pathways. This is the molecular mechanism through which timing-specific organ vulnerability could operate

### 2.5 Immune / Thymic

#### Study 15: Hu et al., 2019
- **Citation:** Nat Commun. 2019;10(1):3031. PMID: 31292453
- **Design:** Human cohort + germ-free mouse model
- **PE Timing Definition:** PE as unitary exposure; mechanistic study
- **Offspring Outcomes:** Fetal thymic size, thymic architecture, Treg cell development
- **Key Findings:**
  - Reduced fetal thymic size and altered thymic architecture in PE pregnancies
  - Reduced fetal thymic Treg cell output → persists up to 4 years in human offspring
  - In germ-free mice: fetal thymic CD4+ T cell and Treg cell development compromised, rescued by maternal SCFA acetate supplementation
  - Low maternal serum acetate associated with subsequent PE and correlates with fetal serum acetate
- **Key Quote:** "Preeclampsia is a pregnancy-associated disorder with an immune basis and is linked to atopic disorders in offspring… reduced fetal thymic regulatory T cell output in preeclamptic pregnancies, which persists up to 4 years of age in human offspring."
- **Alignment Model:** SUPPORTS — demonstrates PE programmes specific immune system deficits (thymic/Treg). The thymus is most actively developing in mid-to-late gestation, suggesting EOPE exposure during peak thymic development could have greater immune impact than LOPE. However, EOPE vs LOPE comparison was not performed

### 2.6 Pulmonary / Respiratory

#### Study 16: Mirzakhani et al., 2019
- **Citation:** Am J Respir Crit Care Med. 2019;199(1):32-42. PMID: 30153046
- **Design:** Cohort analysis from VDAART (Vitamin D Antenatal Asthma Reduction Trial); 806 pregnant women + offspring followed to age 3
- **PE Timing Definition:** PE as unitary exposure (presence/absence); enrolled at 10-18 weeks gestation
- **Offspring Outcomes:** Physician-diagnosed asthma, recurrent wheeze by age 3
- **Key Findings:**
  - Asthma incidence by age 3: no asthma/no PE → 9.9%; no asthma/PE → 18.0%; asthma/no PE → 22.1%; asthma/PE → 32.1%
  - PE amplifies maternal asthma risk: adj HR 2.68 (CI 1.30-5.61) for asthma+PE vs 2.18 (CI 1.46-3.26) for asthma alone
  - Trend for asthma + recurrent wheeze: adj HR 4.73 (CI 2.20-10.07) for combined exposure
- **Key Quote:** "Preeclampsia is associated with increased risk of early life childhood asthma in children less than 3 years old over and above that associated with maternal asthma alone. The results implicate the interplay between maternal factors as strong predictors of offspring asthma and in utero maternal-fetal immune perturbations and developmental dysregulations associated with preeclampsia."
- **Alignment Model:** PARTIALLY SUPPORTS — confirms PE programmes respiratory vulnerability. Pulmonary development peaks in third trimester (alveolar stage begins ~36 weeks), so LOPE would theoretically impact this system more. However, the study did not stratify by PE onset timing

### 2.7 Cross-System / Prematurity Confounding

#### Study 17: Iacobelli et al., 2017
- **Citation:** J Reprod Immunol. 2017;123:12-18. PMID: 28858635
- **Design:** Retrospective cohort; N=62,230 pregnancies over 15 years, Reunion Island
- **PE Timing Definition:** EOPE (<34 wk, rate 0.75%) vs LOPE (≥34 wk, rate 1.5%)
- **Offspring Outcomes:** Fetal and neonatal outcomes adjusted for GA at delivery
- **Key Finding — CRITICAL COUNTER-EVIDENCE:**
  - After adjusting for GA at delivery: **NO DIFFERENCE** between EOPE and LOPE offspring outcomes
  - GA at delivery was the most important predictor for offspring outcome
- **Key Quote:** "Foetal and neonatal outcomes were evaluated after adjustment for gestational age at delivery and no difference was detected between early- and late-onset preeclamptic women. Gestational age at delivery was the most important predictor for offspring outcome."
- **Alignment Model:** STRONGLY CHALLENGES — if timing of PE onset does not predict outcomes after adjusting for GA at delivery, then prematurity itself (not the PE subtype) drives short-term offspring effects. However, this study assessed neonatal outcomes, not long-term developmental programming. The alignment model may apply to latent epigenetic/developmental effects not manifest in the neonatal period

#### Study 18: Reynolds et al., 2012
- **Citation:** Gend Med. 2012;9(6):S44-S56. PMID: 23217566
- **Design:** Collaborative Perinatal Project (1959-1965); N=516 PE + 8,801 normotensive
- **PE Timing Definition:** Stratified by preterm (<37 wk) vs term (≥37 wk) delivery — proxy for EOPE/LOPE
- **Offspring Outcomes:** Chest circumference, head circumference, birthweight; sex-stratified
- **Key Findings:**
  - **Male preterm PE offspring:** Greater reductions in chest/head circumference and birthweight
  - **Term PE offspring:** More modest growth effects; sex influence was OPPOSITE to preterm
  - Sexual dimorphism in PE effects depends on preterm/term status
- **Key Quote:** "The influence of preeclampsia on the growth of term offspring was more modest, and the influence of sex was opposite that in preterm infants."
- **Alignment Model:** SUPPORTS — demonstrates that PE effects differ based on timing of delivery (preterm vs term), and interact with sex. The reversal of sex-specific effects between preterm and term groups implies different programming windows

## 3. Synthesis: Does the Current Evidence Support the Alignment Model?

### 3.1 What the Evidence Shows

| Evidence Type | Supports | Challenges | Neutral/Untested |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Epigenetic programming** | Herzog 2017: tissue-specific DMPs differ between EOPE/LOPE in CV-metabolic pathways | — | — |
| **Metabolic differentiation** | Stadler 2023: EOPE neonates ↑ cholesterol vs LOPE neonates ↓ HDL efflux capacity | — | — |
| **Neonatal outcomes (short-term)** | — | Iacobelli 2017: GA at delivery, not PE subtype, predicts neonatal outcomes | — |
| **Cardiovascular (BP)** | Rice 2018: term PE → ↑ SBP only | Xu 2024: uniform BP effect regardless of GA stratification; Jansen 2019: PIH > PE for offspring BP | — |
| **Cardiac structure** | — | Chen 2022: no structural cardiac differences at 5-8y in severe PE offspring | HR elevation suggests subclinical programming |
| **Renal** | Conte 2025: PE → fewer nephrons → early HTN (timing of nephron development relevant) | — | — |
| **Neurological** | Ibrahim 2025: MVM vs FVM → different neurodevelopmental patterns | — | Most studies treat PE as unitary exposure |
| **Immune** | Hu 2019: PE impairs Treg development, persisting 4+ years | — | EOPE vs LOPE not compared |
| **Pulmonary** | Mirzakhani 2019: PE amplifies asthma risk | — | EOPE vs LOPE not compared |
| **Growth/sex dimorphism** | Reynolds 2012: PE effects differ by preterm/term and sex-interact differently | — | — |
| **Multi-organ programming** | Cunningham 2018: developmental programming concept | — | No EOPE vs LOPE organ comparison |

### 3.2 The Core Gap

**The alignment model's central prediction — that EOPE and LOPE produce different organ-system deficit profiles in offspring — has barely been tested.** Of 27 PubMed searches targeting this specific question, approximately 10 returned zero results, and the majority of studies that were identified treat PE as a unitary exposure without stratifying by onset timing.

Only two studies directly compare EOPE vs LOPE effects on offspring:

1. **Herzog et al. (2017)** — Epigenetic (SUPPORTS): Different tissue-specific methylation patterns in placenta and newborn cord blood between EOPE and LOPE, mapping to cardiovascular-metabolic pathways
2. **Stadler et al. (2023)** — Metabolic (SUPPORTS): Different neonatal lipid profiles in EOPE vs LOPE (cholesterol elevation vs HDL dysfunction)

One study tests timing differentiation with a counter-finding:

3. **Iacobelli et al. (2017)** — Neonatal outcomes (CHALLENGES): After adjusting for GA at delivery, no outcome differences between EOPE and LOPE — but this measured short-term neonatal outcomes, not developmental programming

### 3.3 Interpretation Framework

The evidence supports a **modified alignment model** with important caveats:

**What is established:**
- PE programmes multi-organ deficits in offspring (cardiovascular, renal, neurological, immune, pulmonary, metabolic) — evidence is strong across all systems
- EOPE and LOPE have different pathophysiology (placental vs maternal-metabolic) — widely accepted
- EOPE and LOPE produce different epigenetic signatures in newborn tissues, mapping to cardiovascular-metabolic pathways (Herzog 2017)
- EOPE and LOPE produce different neonatal metabolic profiles (Stadler 2023)
- Placental vascular pathology type (MVM vs FVM) associates with different neurodevelopmental patterns (Ibrahim 2025)
- PE effects on offspring growth are sexually dimorphic and differ by preterm/term status (Reynolds 2012)

**What is NOT established:**
- Whether EOPE offspring develop different long-term organ-system deficit profiles than LOPE offspring
- Whether the epigenetic differences between EOPE and LOPE translate into different clinical outcomes by organ system over the life course
- Whether timing of PE onset (as distinct from PE severity and GA at delivery) independently predicts which organ systems show deficits

**Key confound:** EOPE leads to earlier delivery (prematurity), IUGR, and greater severity. Separating the effects of PE onset timing from prematurity, growth restriction, and disease severity remains the fundamental analytical challenge. Iacobelli's finding that GA at delivery is the strongest predictor of neonatal outcomes illustrates this problem — though it may not apply to latent developmental programming.

### 3.4 What Would Decisively Test the Alignment Model

A definitive test would require:

1. **Cohort with EOPE and LOPE offspring followed long-term (5-20+ years)** with multi-organ assessment (neurocognitive testing, echocardiography, renal function/imaging, metabolic profiling, immune panels, pulmonary function)
2. **Statistical methods separating PE onset timing from GA at delivery** — ideally comparing EOPE+preterm delivery, LOPE+preterm delivery, LOPE+term delivery, with appropriate normotensive controls
3. **Placental histopathology as biological mediator** — using MVM/FVM patterns as proxies for the type and timing of placental insult
4. **Epigenetic follow-up** — tracking whether the DMPs identified by Herzog et al. in newborns predict organ-specific dysfunction at later ages

### 3.5 The Sattwika et al. (2024) "Cited By" Discovery

A critical finding emerged from the citation trail of Chen et al. (2022):

- **Sattwika PD et al., 2024.** "Multi-Organ Phenotypes of Offspring Born Following Hypertensive Disorders of Pregnancy: A Systematic Review." J Am Heart Assoc. 2024;13(21):e033617. PMID: 39450722.

This very recent systematic review appears to directly address multi-organ phenotyping of HDP offspring — potentially the closest existing test of the alignment model. This paper should be prioritised for full-text review.

## 4. Per-Organ Alignment Model Predictions vs Evidence

| Organ System | Developmental Window | Alignment Prediction | Evidence Status |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Neurological** | Weeks 8-28 (neuronal migration, myelination begins ~24 wk) | EOPE should disproportionately affect neurodevelopment | Partially supported: MVM (EOPE-associated) → worse neurodevelopment (Ibrahim 2025). But most studies don't stratify by timing |
| **Cardiovascular** | Cardiac morphogenesis complete by wk 8; functional maturation 20-38 wk | Both EOPE and LOPE should affect CV; EOPE during structural maturation, LOPE during functional maturation | Mixed: BP elevation is uniform across timing (Xu 2024); cardiac structure unaffected at 5-8y even in severe PE (Chen 2022); but HR elevation in severe PE offspring suggests autonomic programming |
| **Renal** | Nephrogenesis wk 9-36 (complete by 36 wk) | EOPE should disproportionately reduce nephron endowment | Strongly supported mechanistically: PE → fewer nephrons (Conte 2025); EOPE exposure during active nephrogenesis is plausible mechanism; not yet directly tested in human EOPE vs LOPE |
| **Metabolic** | Metabolic programming throughout; insulin sensitivity late gestation | LOPE should disproportionately affect metabolic programming | Partially supported: EOPE vs LOPE produce different neonatal lipid profiles (Stadler 2023); epigenetic changes map to metabolic pathways (Herzog 2017); but term PE → only SBP, no metabolic markers (Rice 2018) |
| **Immune** | Thymic development peaks mid-gestation; T cell repertoire selection | EOPE should disproportionately affect immune development | Supported mechanistically: PE impairs Treg development (Hu 2019) during the window when thymic output is most active; not tested by EOPE vs LOPE comparison |
| **Pulmonary** | Alveolar stage begins ~36 wk; surfactant production late | LOPE should disproportionately affect pulmonary outcomes | Partially supported: PE amplifies asthma risk (Mirzakhani 2019); but not stratified by timing. Paradoxically, EOPE + preterm delivery may cause more acute respiratory distress through prematurity rather than PE-specific pulmonary programming |

## 5. Conclusions

### 5.1 Evidence Grade: PRELIMINARY SUPPORT with CRITICAL GAPS

The alignment model is **biologically plausible** and receives **preliminary epigenetic/metabolic support** (Herzog 2017, Stadler 2023), but has **not been directly tested** in a clinical cohort comparing long-term organ-system-specific outcomes in EOPE vs LOPE offspring.

### 5.2 Three Key Findings

1. **The field treats PE as a unitary exposure for offspring studies.** This is the most striking finding of this review. Despite widespread acceptance that EOPE and LOPE have different pathophysiology, almost no offspring outcome studies stratify by PE onset timing.

2. **When EOPE vs LOPE IS compared in offspring tissues, different molecular signatures emerge.** The epigenetic (Herzog 2017) and lipid metabolism (Stadler 2023) evidence shows distinct programming patterns, consistent with the alignment model.

3. **Prematurity confounding remains unresolved.** The Iacobelli (2017) finding that GA at delivery dominates short-term outcomes may not apply to latent developmental programming, but this has not been systematically demonstrated.

### 5.3 Research Priority

The alignment model generates a testable, falsifiable prediction: **EOPE offspring should show disproportionate neurological, renal, and immune deficits, while LOPE offspring should show disproportionate metabolic and pulmonary deficits.** No existing study has this design, making it a high-priority research gap.
