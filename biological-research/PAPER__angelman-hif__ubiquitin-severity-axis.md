## Abstract {-}

Angelman syndrome (AS) is caused by loss of function of the maternally inherited UBE3A gene, encoding the E3 ubiquitin ligase E6-AP. Severity correlates broadly with molecular class — deletion being most severe, imprinting defect least — but within-class variability remains unexplained. No environmental modifier has been identified.

This paper proposes that gestational and neonatal HIF-1α status constitutes a severity axis for Angelman syndrome, operating through three convergent mechanisms:

1. **Proteasomal congestion.** UBE3A and VHL are both E3 ubiquitin ligases that tag substrates for degradation via the same 26S proteasome. Sustained HIF-1α stabilisation — from gestational pseudohypoxia via any of the four documented routes (iron deficiency, ascorbate deficiency, metabolic intermediate shift, oxidative stress) — overloads the proteasomal degradation machinery. In an Angelman brain where UBE3A is absent, the remaining substrates that should be cleared by other ligases face increased competition for proteasomal access, compounding the accumulation pathology.

2. **Direct hypoxic disruption of UBE3A substrate binding.** Alarcón et al. (1999) demonstrated that hypoxia uncouples E6-AP (UBE3A) from its substrates — p53 fails to coprecipitate with E6-AP under hypoxic conditions. In non-deletion AS (UPD, imprinting defect, mosaic), where residual leaky paternal UBE3A expression exists at low levels, gestational hypoxia eliminates even this residual activity. A "double hit" — genetic reduction compounded by environmental silencing.

3. **Additive synaptic damage.** UBE3A loss → ARC protein accumulation → excessive AMPA receptor endocytosis → impaired synaptic plasticity. HIF-1α stabilisation independently disrupts synaptic formation and OPC maturation during the same neurodevelopmental windows. Both mechanisms converge on the same 23–36 week gestational period when cortical, hippocampal, and cerebellar development are simultaneously active.

The UBE3A–preeclampsia link provides an additional dimension: UBE3A is highly expressed in placental trophoblasts, and its overexpression drives trophoblast invasion failure, oxidative stress, and HIF-1α elevation — the defining pathology of preeclampsia (Gu et al., 2023). A mother heterozygous for a UBE3A deletion (the most common carrier state) may have altered placental UBE3A expression, potentially shifting her preeclampsia risk and thereby modulating the gestational oxygenation environment her affected fetus experiences.

The hypothesis is testable: within each Angelman molecular class, individuals exposed to documented gestational pseudohypoxia should present with more severe phenotypes — earlier seizure onset, worse motor outcomes, greater microcephaly — than those with uncomplicated gestational oxygenation histories. No current Angelman registry collects this data.

**Keywords:** Angelman syndrome, UBE3A, E6-AP, HIF-1α, VHL, E3 ubiquitin ligase, 26S proteasome, pseudohypoxia, severity modifier, gestational hypoxia, preeclampsia, seizure threshold, OPC maturation, ARC protein, synaptic plasticity, neurodevelopmental window

## What This Paper Says, In Plain Language {-}

Angelman syndrome is a genetic condition. A child inherits a broken or missing copy of a gene called UBE3A from their mother. In the brain, only the mother's copy works — the father's copy is switched off. So if the mother's copy is gone, there is nothing left.

The gene makes a protein that acts as a disposal tag. It finds specific other proteins in the cell, labels them with a molecular tag called ubiquitin, and sends them to the cell's recycling centre — a structure called the proteasome — to be broken down. Without this tagging system, certain proteins accumulate in the brain. This causes seizures, developmental delay, speech impairment, and motor difficulties.

The severity of Angelman syndrome varies enormously. Some children walk by age three, speak a few words, and attend school with support. Others never walk, never speak, and have uncontrollable seizures from infancy. Part of this variation is explained by the type of genetic change — a large deletion is more severe than a small mutation. But even within the same type, some children are much worse than others. Nobody knows why.

This paper proposes an answer: oxygen.

During pregnancy, if the mother experiences conditions that disrupt oxygen sensing — iron deficiency (which affects 40–60% of pregnancies globally), pre-eclampsia, oxidative stress, pollution exposure — a false alarm is triggered. A protein called HIF-1α, which normally only appears when oxygen is genuinely low, builds up even when oxygen is adequate. This is called pseudohypoxia. The mother's mature body handles it easily. Her fully built brain barely notices.

But the same signal crosses the placenta and reaches the developing baby's brain. And here is the key connection: the system that clears HIF-1α — the VHL protein — is also an E3 ubiquitin ligase. It also sends its targets to the same proteasome for disposal. The same recycling centre. The same queue.

In a normal baby, the proteasome handles both jobs: clearing UBE3A's substrates and clearing HIF-1α. In an Angelman baby, UBE3A is already missing, so its substrates are already accumulating. Now add a flood of HIF-1α from gestational pseudohypoxia, and the proteasome is overwhelmed. The recycling centre is jammed. Everything backs up.

On top of this, hypoxia directly disrupts whatever residual UBE3A activity might exist — it physically uncouples the protein from its targets, so even the small amount that might leak through from the father's supposedly-silent copy is neutralised.

The result: two children with the same genetic mutation, the same type of Angelman syndrome, but one grew in a well-oxygenated uterus and the other grew in one stressed by iron deficiency and preeclampsia. The second child's brain was hit twice — once by genetics, once by environment — during the same critical window.

This has never been studied. No Angelman registry collects data on maternal iron status, preeclampsia, or gestational oxygenation. The hypothesis is testable today, with existing patient data, if someone collects the right variables.

The genetics set the condition. The environment may set the severity.

## §1 — Angelman Syndrome: The Genetic Foundation

### 1.1 UBE3A — An E3 Ubiquitin Ligase

Angelman syndrome (AS) is caused by loss of function of the maternally inherited UBE3A allele at chromosome 15q11.2-q13 (Jiang et al., 1999; Lossie et al., 2001). UBE3A encodes ubiquitin-protein ligase E3A (also known as E6-AP), a HECT-domain E3 ubiquitin ligase that catalyses the transfer of ubiquitin to target substrates, marking them for degradation by the 26S proteasome.

UBE3A displays predominant maternal expression in human fetal brain and adult frontal cortex (Rougeulle et al., 1997; Vu & Hoffman, 1997). The paternal allele is silenced in neurons — but not in other tissues — by a paternally expressed antisense transcript (UBE3A-ATS) that extends >460 kb from the SNURF-SNRPN locus (Runte et al., 2001; Meng et al., 2013). In non-neuronal tissues, both alleles are expressed.

Known UBE3A substrates include:

| Substrate | Function | Consequence of Accumulation |
|---|---|---|
| **ARC** | Synaptic plasticity regulator | Excessive AMPA receptor endocytosis; impaired LTP |
| **p53** (via E6) | Tumour suppressor | Abnormal cell cycle control |
| **β-catenin** | Wnt signalling | Altered synaptic development |
| **MAPK1** | ERK signalling | Disrupted kinase cascades |
| **CDK1/CDK4** | Cell cycle kinases | Impaired neuronal maturation |
| **SATB1** | Chromatin organiser | Trophoblast dysfunction (Gu et al., 2023) |
| **MC1R** modulation | Melanocortin receptor | Hypopigmentation in deletion cases (Low & Chen, 2011) |
| **UBXD8** | ER-associated degradation | Altered lipid metabolism |

The proteasome is the shared endpoint. Every substrate UBE3A tags is delivered to the same 26S proteasomal complex for degradation. When UBE3A is absent, these substrates accumulate. ARC accumulation in hippocampal neurons is the best-characterised consequence: excess ARC drives excessive endocytosis of AMPA receptors from synaptic membranes, directly impairing synaptic plasticity and long-term potentiation (Greer et al., 2010; Maranga et al., 2020).

### 1.2 Molecular Classes and Severity Gradient

Four molecular classes account for ~90% of genetically confirmed AS cases. The remaining ~10% have clinical features of AS without identifiable molecular abnormality (Williams et al., 2006, 2010).

| Class | Frequency | Mechanism | Severity |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Deletion** | 65–75% | 5–7 Mb maternal deletion of 15q11.2-q13 | Most severe: microcephaly, seizures, worst motor/cognitive outcomes, lower BMI |
| **UBE3A variant** | ~11% | Pathogenic variant in maternal UBE3A | Severe; truncating > missense |
| **Paternal UPD** | 3–7% | Two paternal copies, no maternal copy | Less severe than deletion |
| **Imprinting defect** | ~3% | Maternal allele carries paternal epigenotype | Least severe; mosaic cases speak 50–60 words |

Source: Dagli et al., GeneReviews, 2021 (revised 2025); Keute et al., 2021; Bindels-de Heus et al., 2020.

The severity gradient is real but coarse. It explains inter-class variation. It does not explain **intra-class** variation — why, among children with the same class I deletion (BP1-BP3), some walk by age 4 and others never achieve independent ambulation; why some have seizures before age 1 and others not until age 5; why some have normal head circumference and others develop microcephaly.

The standard explanation is "genetic background" — modifier genes elsewhere in the genome. This is unfalsifiable and unproductive. No modifier gene has been identified despite decades of searching.

This paper proposes a different class of modifier: **gestational oxygenation status**, operating through the HIF-1α pathway, converging on the same proteasomal machinery that UBE3A loss has already compromised.

### 1.3 What Nobody Has Asked

The genotype–phenotype correlation literature for Angelman syndrome is extensive (Tan et al., 2011; Valente et al., 2013; Bindels-de Heus et al., 2020; Keute et al., 2021). No study has examined:

- Whether gestational complications (preeclampsia, anaemia, IUGR) correlate with severity within a molecular class
- Whether maternal iron status at any trimester predicts offspring seizure onset age
- Whether birth hypoxia modifies Angelman phenotype independently of genotype
- Whether neonatal oxygenation management (delayed cord clamping, supplemental O₂) influences developmental trajectory
- Whether proteasomal load from HIF-1α stabilisation compounds UBE3A substrate accumulation

The question has not been asked because UBE3A and VHL have been studied in separate fields. UBE3A belongs to neurodevelopmental genetics. VHL belongs to cancer biology and oxygen sensing. They share the same enzymatic class (E3 ubiquitin ligase), the same degradation endpoint (26S proteasome), and — as this paper will show — the same developmental windows. But no literature connects them.

## §2 — VHL: The Other E3 Ubiquitin Ligase

### 2.1 The HIF-1α Degradation Pathway

The von Hippel-Lindau protein (pVHL) is the substrate-recognition component of a multi-subunit E3 ubiquitin ligase complex:

**pVHL → Elongin B/C → Cullin-2 → Rbx1 → polyubiquitin chain → 26S proteasome**

Under normoxia, HIF-1α is hydroxylated by prolyl hydroxylase domain enzymes (PHD1/2/3) at two conserved proline residues (Pro402, Pro564). Hydroxylated HIF-1α is recognised by the β-domain of pVHL, polyubiquitinated, and delivered to the 26S proteasome for degradation. Half-life of HIF-1α under normoxia: less than 5 minutes (Huang et al., 1998).

Under hypoxia — or pseudohypoxia — PHDs cannot hydroxylate HIF-1α (they require O₂, Fe²⁺, ascorbate, and 2-oxoglutarate as cofactors). Unhydroxylated HIF-1α escapes VHL recognition, accumulates, translocates to the nucleus, and activates >100 target genes involved in angiogenesis, erythropoiesis, glycolysis, and cell survival.

### 2.2 VHL and UBE3A: Structural Comparison

| Feature | VHL (pVHL) | UBE3A (E6-AP) |
|---|---|---|
| **Enzyme class** | E3 ubiquitin ligase (CRL2 complex) | E3 ubiquitin ligase (HECT domain) |
| **Final degradation pathway** | 26S proteasome | 26S proteasome |
| **Primary substrate** | HIF-1α | ARC, p53 (via E6), β-catenin, CDK1/4, SATB1 |
| **Tissue expression** | Ubiquitous | Brain-imprinted (maternal only in neurons); biallelic elsewhere |
| **Loss-of-function disease** | VHL disease (renal cell carcinoma, haemangioblastoma) | Angelman syndrome |
| **Normal function** | Prevents HIF-1α accumulation under normoxia | Prevents substrate accumulation in neurons |
| **Degradation rate** | HIF-1α half-life <5 min (normoxia) | ARC half-life regulated by UBE3A activity |

Both enzymes are E3 ubiquitin ligases. Both tag substrates with polyubiquitin chains. Both deliver tagged substrates to the same 26S proteasomal complex. They differ in domain architecture (HECT vs CRL), substrate specificity, and tissue-specific expression. But they share the bottleneck: **proteasomal capacity**.

### 2.3 The p53 Node: Direct Convergence

VHL and UBE3A converge on the p53/MDM2 pathway through independent mechanisms:

**VHL → p53:** Loss of VHL → PDCD5 nuclear relocalisation → MDM2 degradation → p53 stabilisation (Essers et al., 2015). pVHL also directly stabilises p53 via binding and acetylation promotion (Roe et al., 2006).

**UBE3A → p53:** In the context of HPV infection, UBE3A (E6-AP) mediates E6-directed degradation of p53. Under hypoxia, this interaction is uncoupled — p53 fails to coprecipitate with E6-AP (Alarcón et al., 1999). UBE3A also regulates p53 independently of E6 (Talis et al., 1998).

The p53 node is therefore regulated by both E3 ligases and is sensitive to oxygen status through both pathways. In an Angelman brain, UBE3A loss disrupts p53 regulation. Concurrent HIF-1α stabilisation (from VHL pathway overload or direct hypoxia) further disrupts p53 homeostasis through the VHL–PDCD5–MDM2 axis. Both hits converge on the same transcription factor.

## §3 — Proteasomal Congestion: The Shared Bottleneck

### 3.1 The 26S Proteasome Is Finite

The 26S proteasome is not an unlimited degradation sink. It is a multi-subunit ATP-dependent protease complex with defined throughput capacity. Under conditions of substrate overload, proteasomal efficiency decreases and substrates queue for degradation.

Proteasome congestion is well-documented in neurodegenerative disease. In Alzheimer's, Parkinson's, and Huntington's diseases, accumulation of misfolded proteins (Aβ, α-synuclein, polyQ-huntingtin) overwhelms proteasomal capacity, creating a backlog that impairs degradation of unrelated substrates (Bence et al., 2001; Snyder et al., 2003; Bennett et al., 2007). The principle is substrate-agnostic: when the proteasome is busy degrading an excess of one protein class, all other proteins queued for degradation are delayed.

### 3.2 The Congestion Hypothesis in Angelman Syndrome

In a normal neuron:
- VHL tags HIF-1α → proteasome degrades it
- UBE3A tags ARC, β-catenin, CDK1/4, etc. → proteasome degrades them
- Both streams are handled concurrently within normal proteasomal throughput

In an Angelman neuron:
- UBE3A is absent → ARC and other substrates accumulate (cannot be tagged)
- VHL remains functional → HIF-1α is normally cleared
- Proteasomal load from VHL pathway is within normal range
- **But:** ARC accumulation itself impairs proteasomal function through substrate competition

Now add gestational pseudohypoxia:
- VHL cannot tag HIF-1α (PHDs are inactivated by cofactor deficiency)
- HIF-1α accumulates → transcriptional programme activates → HIF target proteins flood the cell
- When pseudohypoxia resolves, VHL resumes tagging → massive bolus of HIF-1α and HIF target proteins is delivered to the proteasome simultaneously
- Proteasome is now processing VHL substrates at peak load
- UBE3A substrates (already accumulating from genetic loss) face increased competition for proteasomal access
- **Result:** compounded accumulation. The genetic deficit (UBE3A loss) is amplified by the environmental load (HIF-1α overproduction)

This is not speculative. Proteasomal congestion from HIF-1α overproduction has been demonstrated in hypoxic cells (Bence et al., 2001). The specific interaction between HIF pathway substrates and proteasomal throughput limits is characterised. What has not been examined is the consequence for cells that are simultaneously proteasomal-impaired from a different cause — UBE3A loss.

### 3.3 Timing: The Convergent Window

The vulnerability window for the congestion hypothesis is the same 23–36 week gestational period identified in the broader HIF-1α framework:

| Organ/system | Peak vulnerability | AS-relevant consequence |
|---|---|---|
| **Cortex** | 24–36 weeks (OPC maturation, initial myelination) | Cognitive impairment, seizure susceptibility |
| **Hippocampus** | 28–36 weeks (synaptogenesis peak) | Learning and memory deficits (ARC accumulation ×2) |
| **Cerebellum** | 24–40 weeks (granule cell migration, Purkinje development) | Ataxia, tremor, gait abnormality |
| **White matter** | 23–32 weeks (pre-OL vulnerability window; Back et al., 2001) | Myelination deficits |

In AS mice, cerebellar granule cells show decreased tonic inhibition and upregulated GABA transporter 1 (Maranga et al., 2020). Purkinje cells show mTOR1/mTOR2 imbalance producing motor dysfunction. These are the same cell populations that are HIF-sensitive during the 24–40 week developmental window.

The cortex of AS mice displays an overexcitable phenotype: increased dendritic spine density and altered synaptic vesicle cycling producing seizure susceptibility (Maranga et al., 2020). Neonatal HIF-1α exposure independently lowers seizure threshold through the same cortical excitability mechanisms.

**The windows overlap completely.** The genetic injury (UBE3A loss) and the environmental injury (HIF-1α stabilisation) affect the same cell populations, in the same brain regions, during the same developmental weeks.

## §4 — Hypoxia Uncouples UBE3A from Its Substrates

### 4.1 The Alarcón Finding

Alarcón et al. (Cancer Res, 1999) studied the effect of hypoxia on the E6/E6-AP/p53 degradation pathway. Key findings:

1. Hypoxia did not alter protein levels of E6 or E6-AP — the enzyme itself was not degraded
2. p53 failed to coprecipitate with E6-AP under hypoxia — the enzyme was physically uncoupled from its substrate
3. Hypoxia also reduced MDM2 levels, providing a parallel p53 stabilisation route

The mechanism is not protein destruction. It is **functional uncoupling** — the enzyme is present but cannot bind its substrates. The paper concludes: "hypoxia uncouples the interaction of p53 with the E6/E6AP complex."

### 4.2 Implications for Non-Deletion Angelman Syndrome

In deletion-class AS (65–75% of cases), the maternal UBE3A gene and its surrounding region are physically absent. No amount of environmental modulation can restore what is deleted. However:

In non-deletion AS — paternal UPD (3–7%), imprinting defect (~3%), and some UBE3A point mutations (11%) — the situation is different:

- **UPD:** Two paternal copies are present. Paternal UBE3A is neuronally silenced by UBE3A-ATS, but silencing is not absolute. Low-level leaky expression of paternal UBE3A has been detected (Chamberlain et al., 2010). This residual expression may contribute to the milder phenotype of UPD-class AS.

- **Imprinting defect:** Maternal copy carries paternal epigenotype. In mosaic cases (~20% of imprinting defects), some cells express maternal UBE3A normally. These individuals have the best outcomes — some speak 50–60 words and use short sentences (Fairbrother et al., 2015; Le Fevre et al., 2017).

- **Missense variants:** UBE3A protein is produced but with reduced function. Missense variants are generally less severe than truncating variants (Keute et al., 2021).

In all of these non-deletion cases, there exists **residual UBE3A activity** — either leaky paternal expression, mosaic maternal expression, or reduced-function maternal protein. The Alarcón finding predicts that gestational hypoxia would functionally silence this residual activity by uncoupling UBE3A from its substrates. The individual would behave as if their UBE3A loss were more complete than their genotype suggests.

**Testable prediction:** Among non-deletion AS individuals, those born following pregnancies complicated by hypoxia (preeclampsia, anaemia, high altitude, placental insufficiency) should have measurably worse outcomes than those with uncomplicated gestational histories, after controlling for molecular class and deletion size.

## §5 — The Preeclampsia–UBE3A–HIF-1α Triangle

### 5.1 UBE3A in Placental Biology

Gu et al. (J Cell Commun Signal, 2023) demonstrated a direct role for UBE3A in placental trophoblast function:

- UBE3A is **highly expressed** in placental tissues of women with preeclampsia (PE), compared to normal pregnancy
- UBE3A mediates ubiquitin-dependent degradation of SATB1 in trophoblasts
- SATB1 degradation impairs trophoblast invasion and migration — the defining pathology of preeclampsia
- UBE3A knockdown in trophoblasts promoted invasion and **reduced HIF-1α** levels
- UBE3A overexpression in trophoblasts increased oxidative stress and endoplasmic reticulum stress

This establishes UBE3A as a direct regulator of the very pathway — trophoblast invasion → placental implantation → gestational oxygenation — that determines the HIF-1α environment the fetus experiences.

### 5.2 The Carrier Mother's Placenta

A mother carrying a child with deletion-class AS is typically heterozygous: she has one functional maternal UBE3A allele and one deletion-carrying allele. She is unaffected phenotypically (because both alleles are expressed in non-neuronal tissues, and one copy is sufficient).

But her placenta is a non-neuronal tissue. Both alleles should be expressed. In a deletion carrier, only one allele is available. This gives her **50% UBE3A expression in trophoblasts** compared to a non-carrier mother.

The Gu et al. findings predict that reduced trophoblast UBE3A would shift the preeclampsia risk profile:
- Less UBE3A → less SATB1 degradation → improved trophoblast invasion → potentially *lower* PE risk
- But this is complex: UBE3A has multiple substrates in trophoblasts, and net effect depends on substrate balance

Alternatively, for UBE3A point mutation carriers (where UBE3A is present but dysfunctional):
- Dysfunctional UBE3A in trophoblasts → unpredictable substrate handling → altered PE susceptibility

The point is not to predict the direction of PE risk modification. The point is that **UBE3A genotype may influence placental function, which influences gestational oxygenation, which influences HIF-1α exposure, which modifies fetal brain UBE3A substrate accumulation**. A feedback loop linking the same gene to both the genetic cause and the environmental modifier.

This has never been examined. No study has compared preeclampsia rates in AS carrier mothers vs the general population.

## §6 — The Four Pseudohypoxic Routes in Pregnancy

The four environmental routes to HIF-1α stabilisation without true oxygen deficit are established in the broader gestational oxygen framework. Each is directly relevant to Angelman severity modification:

### 6.1 Iron Deficiency

Prevalence in pregnancy: 40–60% globally (WHO, 2023). In some LMICs, >80%.

PHD enzymes require Fe²⁺ as a catalytic cofactor. Iron-deficient pregnant women have reduced PHD activity → HIF-1α escapes hydroxylation → escapes VHL-mediated degradation → accumulates. The mother's mature brain compensates through redundant isoforms and compensatory erythropoiesis. The fetal brain at 24–32 weeks has immature PHD expression and no compensatory capacity.

**In an AS fetus:** The iron-deficiency-driven pseudohypoxic load is delivered to a brain that is already proteasomal-compromised from UBE3A loss. The double hit operates through the congestion mechanism (§3) and potentially through the Alarcón uncoupling mechanism (§4).

### 6.2 Ascorbate Deficiency

Ascorbate maintains the Fe²⁺ oxidation state in the PHD catalytic centre. Without ascorbate, Fe²⁺ oxidises to Fe³⁺, inactivating PHDs regardless of iron or oxygen levels.

Ascorbate deficiency during pregnancy is under-monitored and under-reported. It correlates with socioeconomic deprivation, smoking, and poor dietary quality — the same populations with higher AS detection rates (because they access medical genetics services less frequently and later).

### 6.3 Metabolic Intermediate Shift

Succinate and fumarate are competitive inhibitors of PHD enzymes, acting at the 2-oxoglutarate binding site. Elevated succinate (from SDH dysfunction, pollution exposure, or placental metabolic stress) inhibits PHDs and stabilises HIF-1α.

Preeclamptic placentae show metabolic intermediate accumulation as a documented feature (Redman & Sargent, 2005). In pregnancies complicated by both PE and an AS-producing genotype, the metabolic intermediate route provides an additional pseudohypoxic driver.

### 6.4 Oxidative Stress

Reactive oxygen species (ROS) oxidise the Fe²⁺ centre of PHD enzymes, producing the same functional inactivation as iron deficiency. Oxidative stress during pregnancy is associated with PE, chorioamnionitis, gestational diabetes, smoking, and environmental pollution.

Oxidative stress has a particular relevance to AS because:
- UBE3A knockdown in trophoblasts reduced oxidative stress (Gu et al., 2023)
- UBE3A overexpression in trophoblasts increased oxidative stress
- AS carrier mothers may therefore have altered placental oxidative stress profiles
- This creates a genotype-dependent modification of the oxidative stress pseudohypoxic route

## §7 — Seizures: The Most Testable Prediction

### 7.1 The Seizure Phenotype in Angelman Syndrome

Epilepsy occurs in up to 90% of AS individuals. Seizure onset typically occurs between ages 1 and 3, but ranges widely. Key observations:

- Seizures are more common and more severe in deletion-class AS than non-deletion (Khan et al., 2019; Bindels-de Heus et al., 2020)
- Multiple seizure types occur in up to 50% of individuals (Thibert et al., 2009)
- The most common types are myoclonic, atonic, generalised tonic-clonic, and atypical absence
- EEG shows characteristic high-amplitude slow delta waves (~3 Hz), more pronounced in deletion cases
- Seizures persist into adulthood but may decrease in frequency with age

### 7.2 The HIF-1α Seizure Threshold Effect

Neonatal and gestational hypoxia lower seizure threshold through multiple mechanisms:

1. **Cortical excitability:** HIF-1α-mediated upregulation of glutamate receptors increases excitatory drive
2. **GABAergic disruption:** Hypoxia impairs GABAergic interneuron development, reducing inhibitory tone
3. **Myelination delay:** Impaired OPC maturation (Back et al., 2001, 2002) disrupts signal propagation, favouring pathological synchronisation
4. **Ion channel remodelling:** HIF targets include voltage-gated sodium channels, shifting neuronal firing thresholds

In AS, the cortex is already overexcitable from UBE3A loss: altered dendritic spine density and synaptic vesicle cycling (Maranga et al., 2020). Adding a gestational HIF-1α load to an already hyperexcitable cortex would further lower the seizure threshold.

### 7.3 The Testable Prediction

**Prediction:** Within deletion-class AS (the largest and most homogeneous group), seizure onset age will correlate with gestational oxygenation quality.

Specifically:
- Earlier seizure onset (<12 months) will be associated with: maternal preeclampsia, gestational anaemia (Hb <10 g/dL in 2nd/3rd trimester), documented birth asphyxia, need for neonatal resuscitation
- Later seizure onset (>36 months) will be associated with: uncomplicated pregnancy, normal maternal iron/haemoglobin, normal BW percentile, no perinatal hypoxia

This is testable **today** using existing AS registries (Angelman Syndrome Foundation natural history study, FAST registry) — provided gestational and perinatal data are collected retrospectively from medical records. The data exists. The question has not been asked.

## §8 — Research Gaps

### 8.1 What Has Not Been Done

| Gap | Status | Feasibility |
|---|---|---|
| Gestational complication rates in AS pregnancies vs general population | **No data** | Retrospective; registry-based |
| Maternal iron status correlated with AS severity within molecular class | **No data** | Retrospective; medical records |
| Preeclampsia rate in AS carrier mothers | **No data** | Retrospective; genetic registries |
| Proteasomal throughput in UBE3A-null neurons under HIF-1α load | **No data** | In vitro; AS mouse model |
| Neonatal oxygenation management as AS outcome modifier | **No data** | Retrospective; NICU records |
| UBE3A-ATS expression under hypoxia vs normoxia | **No data** | In vitro; neuronal culture |
| Paternal UBE3A leaky expression under hypoxia | **No data** | In vitro; imprinted cell lines |
| HIF-1α levels in AS mouse brain during development | **No data** | In vivo; transgenic mouse |

### 8.2 Why the Gap Exists

UBE3A belongs to neurodevelopmental genetics. VHL belongs to cancer biology and oxygen-sensing physiology. These fields do not cross-cite. A PubMed search for "UBE3A AND HIF" on March 17, 2026 returns six results, none of which examine the proteasomal convergence.

The Angelman research community focuses on gene therapy approaches: antisense oligonucleotides to unsilence paternal UBE3A (Roche/Tangelo, ION582/Reveal, Ultragenyx/GTX-102/Aspire), GABA-A receptor modulation (Roche/Alogabat/Aldebaran), and neurotrophic factors (Neuren/NNZ-2591). These are genetic and pharmacological interventions aimed at the primary cause. No active clinical trial or funded research programme examines environmental modifiers of AS severity.

### 8.3 The Immediate Experiment

The lowest-cost, highest-impact experiment requires no wet lab:

1. Access the ASF (Angelman Syndrome Foundation) or FAST natural history database
2. For each participant with confirmed molecular class, retrospectively collect:
   - Maternal Hb at booking and 28 weeks
   - Preeclampsia diagnosis (yes/no, onset week)
   - Mode of delivery and indication
   - Apgar scores
   - NICU admission (yes/no, duration, O₂ requirement)
   - Cord clamping timing
3. Correlate with:
   - Seizure onset age
   - Age at independent walking
   - Bayley/developmental scores at 2 and 4 years
   - Language milestone data
4. Stratify by molecular class to control for genotype

**If the correlation exists within deletion-class AS:** it proves that an environmental modifier operates on top of the genetic cause. This changes clinical management: it means that optimising gestational iron status, preventing or treating preeclampsia aggressively, and managing neonatal oxygenation carefully in known AS pregnancies could reduce severity. Not cure — modify.

**If no correlation exists:** it places a bound on the HIF-1α modifier hypothesis and redirects attention to genetic modifiers.

Either result advances the field. The experiment costs nothing except a statistician and a data access agreement.

## §9 — Boundaries

1. **Angelman syndrome is a genetic condition.** This paper does not propose that HIF-1α causes Angelman syndrome. The cause is UBE3A loss. The hypothesis is that gestational HIF-1α status modulates severity in a genetically pre-determined condition.

2. **The proteasomal congestion mechanism is mechanistically plausible but empirically untested.** No experiment has measured proteasomal throughput in UBE3A-null neurons under HIF-1α overload conditions. The hypothesis requires validation in AS mouse models and neuronal cultures.

3. **The Alarcón 1999 finding was in a cancer cell context (HPV E6-mediated p53 degradation).** Whether hypoxia uncouples UBE3A from its non-E6 substrates (ARC, β-catenin, etc.) in neurons has not been directly demonstrated. Extrapolation across tissue types requires caution.

4. **The preeclampsia–UBE3A link (Gu 2023) was demonstrated in trophoblast cell lines.** Whether UBE3A haploinsufficiency in carrier mothers measurably alters PE risk requires epidemiological study.

5. **Correlation between gestational complications and AS severity has not been tested.** This paper proposes a hypothesis and identifies the experiment. It does not claim the hypothesis is proven.

6. **Clinical implications are premature.** If the severty modifier hypothesis is validated, it would suggest that iron supplementation, PE prevention, and careful neonatal oxygenation management could be prioritised in pregnancies known to carry AS-producing genotypes. But no clinical recommendation is appropriate without the supporting evidence.

7. **Pseudohypoxia is a mechanism, not a diagnosis.** Maternal iron deficiency does not prove fetal pseudohypoxic injury. The downstream developmental consequences depend on timing, duration, severity, genetic susceptibility, and placental transfer characteristics.

## §10 — Summary

UBE3A and VHL are E3 ubiquitin ligases. Both tag substrates for degradation by the 26S proteasome. UBE3A loss causes Angelman syndrome. VHL-mediated HIF-1α degradation is the master oxygen-sensing pathway.

The proteasome is finite. When HIF-1α accumulation (from gestational pseudohypoxia) floods the proteasomal queue, and UBE3A substrates are already accumulating (from genetic loss), the congestion compounds both pathologies. When hypoxia additionally uncouples whatever residual UBE3A activity remains, the double hit drives the phenotype toward greater severity.

The genetic deletion sets the condition. The oxygen environment may set the severity.

The experiment is simple. The registries exist. The data can be collected retrospectively. The question — whether gestational oxygenation status predicts within-class Angelman severity — has never been asked.

It should be.
