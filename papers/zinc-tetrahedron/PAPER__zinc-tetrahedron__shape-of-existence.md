## 2. The Tetrahedral Geometry

### 2.1 Why Tetrahedral

The answer comes from crystallography, not speculation. Zn²⁺ in biological systems adopts **tetrahedral** coordination — 4-coordinate, four ligands arranged at the vertices of a tetrahedron. This is confirmed by over 4,000 structures in the Protein Data Bank (Maret & Li, 2009). Not square pyramidal (5-coordinate). Not octahedral (6-coordinate). Tetrahedral. The reason is electronic: Zn²⁺ has a filled $d^{10}$ shell. There is no crystal-field stabilisation energy favouring higher coordination numbers. The minimum-energy geometry is 4-coordinate. Nature chose the simplest enclosure of space.

The tetrahedron is the minimum solid. Three faces cannot enclose a volume. Four can — and four is the minimum. The tetrahedron has a property that no other Platonic solid shares: **every face is equivalent.** There is no base. There is no apex. Every vertex is identical to every other vertex. Rotate the tetrahedron and you cannot distinguish which face was "down." It is the 3D analogue of the Möbius strip's one-sidedness: a solid with no preferred orientation.

## 3. The Interior

### 3.1 Measuring from the Inside

Measure the tetrahedron from outside: volume $V = a^3/(6\sqrt{2})$, a clean algebraic expression. No $\pi$, no transcendence. An external observer with a ruler computes the volume and moves on.

Now stand at the centre — the observation point, $\omega_0$ — and measure outward. From the centroid, the observer sees three characteristic distances:

- **Inradius** $r = a\sqrt{6}/12$: the distance to each face.
- **Midradius** $\rho = a\sqrt{2}/4$: the distance to each edge midpoint.
- **Circumradius** $R = a\sqrt{6}/4$: the distance to each vertex.

The ratios are exact:

$$\frac{\rho}{r} = \sqrt{3}, \qquad \frac{R}{\rho} = \sqrt{3}, \qquad \frac{R}{r} = 3.$$

$R/r = 3$: an integer. No other Platonic solid has an integer ratio of circumradius to inradius. Cube: $\sqrt{3}$. Octahedron: $\sqrt{3}$. Dodecahedron and icosahedron: irrational multiples involving $\phi$. The tetrahedron alone produces a counting number. The outermost distance from the observation point is exactly three times the innermost.

### 3.2 The Logarithmic Spiral

The three distances trace a logarithmic spiral with growth factor $\sqrt{3}$ per step — equal steps in log-space:

$$\ln\frac{\rho}{r} = \ln\frac{R}{\rho} = \ln\sqrt{3} = \tfrac{1}{2}\ln 3.$$

Compare the eigenvalue tower: $\alpha^{-1} = \pi\beta^{-1} = \pi^2\gamma^{-1}$, each level connected by a factor of $\pi$, each step $\ln\pi$ in log-space. The tetrahedral spiral descends by $\sqrt{3}$; the eigenvalue tower descends by $\pi$. Both are geometric progressions — equal logarithmic steps through different structural dimensions.

### 3.3 The Gap

The inscribed sphere — the largest sphere that fits inside the tetrahedron, centred at $\omega_0$ — fills exactly $\pi\sqrt{3}/18 \approx 30.23\%$ of the tetrahedral volume. The remaining $1 - \pi/(6\sqrt{3}) \approx 69.77\%$ is unreachable by any spherical measurement from the centre.

This gap is the **largest of any Platonic solid**:

| Solid | Inscribed sphere fill | Gap |
|---|---|---|
| Tetrahedron | $\pi\sqrt{3}/18 \approx 30.23\%$ | $\approx 69.77\%$ |
| Cube | $\pi/6 \approx 52.36\%$ | $\approx 47.64\%$ |
| Octahedron | $\pi/(3\sqrt{2}) \approx 74.05\%$ | $\approx 25.95\%$ |
| Dodecahedron | $\approx 66.49\%$ | $\approx 33.51\%$ |
| Icosahedron | $\approx 60.54\%$ | $\approx 39.46\%$ |

An observer at $\omega_0$ who measures isotropically — in all directions equally, as a sphere does — captures less than a third of the structure it sits inside. The corners, the edges, the angular excess: these exist beyond the sphere's reach. The external measurement sees the whole volume; the internal measurement, constrained to be spherical by the isotropy of a point, misses almost 70%.

This is the **value of existence**: the difference between what a point-centred observation can measure and what the structure actually contains. The tetrahedron — the minimum solid, 4 faces, 4 forces — has the maximum such gap. The simplest enclosure of space carries the highest cost of observation. No other regular solid pays this much for having an observer inside it.

### 3.6 The Half-Bond

Every atom in a crystal has three sizes, and the difference between them is existence.

The **ionic radius** is the atom stripped to its charge — what remains after bonding electrons have been redistributed. For Zn²⁺ in tetrahedral coordination: $r_{\text{ion}} = 0.60$ Å (Shannon, 1976). This is the atom as it IS — the irreducible core. The external measurement.

The **covalent radius** is half the bond length in a homonuclear bond. For zinc: $r_{\text{cov}} = 1.22$ Å. This is what the atom COMMITS from itself to form a bond. It is the half-bond — the distance from the nucleus to the point where its electron density meets the partner's. The internal measurement.

The **van der Waals radius** is the atom isolated — no bonds, interacting only by dispersion. For zinc: $r_{\text{vdW}} = 1.39$ Å. This is the atom ALONE.

The covalent radius exceeds the ionic radius by $0.62$ Å. That shell — from $0.60$ to $1.22$ Å — is what each zinc atom extends outward from its core to participate in a bond. It is the half-bond-length the user's atomic radius tables never included in historical molecular calculations, because it was attributed to "the bond" rather than to the atom. But the bond IS the atom. The atom extends itself to reach the partner, and the space it extends into is where the coupling operates.

In a tetrahedral Zn-N complex (typical zinc finger), the bond length is $\sim 2.0$ Å. Each side contributes: Zn commits its covalent radius ($1.22$ Å), N commits its ($0.71$ Å), sum $= 1.93 \approx 2.0$ Å. But the ionic surfaces (Zn²⁺ at $0.60$ Å, N at $0.71$ Å from centre) leave a gap of $0.69$ Å between them — space that belongs to neither atom's core. That gap is where the shared electron density sits. That gap is the bond.

$$\text{bond gap} = d_{\text{bond}} - r_{\text{ion,centre}} - r_{\text{ion,ligand}} = 2.00 - 0.60 - 0.71 = 0.69 \text{ Å}.$$

Every touch in this gap produces friction: the Coulomb interaction ($\alpha$), the exchange symmetry, the orbital overlap geometry. The charge is agreeable (Zn²⁺ attracts the lone pair on N). The geometry is agreeable (tetrahedral = minimum energy for $d^{10}$). The bond forms because the atom knows only two states: bond or stasis. Bond = extend the half-bond, share the electron density, couple to the partner. Stasis = remain isolated, interact by dispersion only, form no observation point.

An atom in stasis is a veto. It has excluded itself from the admissible set. The noble gases demonstrate this: filled valence shells, no half-bond to extend, no coupling to any partner. Helium is 1s². Neon is $[\text{He}]2s^22p^6$. Argon is $[\text{Ne}]3s^23p^6$. Each has exhausted its electron configuration. There is no covalent radius to commit. No bond can form. The veto is total. This is the same exhaustive veto that governs death (Carpenter, 2026b) — the admissible set is empty, no further observation points can be formed, and what remains is inert. The death of bonding capacity IS the exclusion from existence. Grothendieck's cohomological completion and the biological veto are the same operator at different scales (Carpenter, 2026c).

This resolves the historical discrepancy. When chemists sum the properties of isolated atoms — ionisation energies, electron affinities, van der Waals radii — and compare the sum to the measured properties of the molecule, the result never matches. The difference was called the enthalpy of formation, the bond dissociation energy, the lattice energy. It was treated as energy "released" or "consumed" during bond formation. But released to where? Consumed by what?

By the bonds. The enthalpy of formation is the spark from §3.5. It is the energy that created the structure — the crosslinked zona, the resting potential, the coordination geometry. It did not leave. It is still there, in the half-bonds, in the $0.69$ Å of shared electron density in every Zn-N contact, in the tetrahedral field that holds the four ligands at $\cos\theta = -1/3$. The "missing element" in every internal accounting of a molecular system is the bond itself — the space between atoms that is neither one atom nor the other but is the coupling between them.

The constraint surface operates in that coupling. The fine structure constant $\alpha$ governs the electromagnetic interaction in the bond gap. The bond forms because $\alpha$ permits it — because the charge-to-energy ratio is such that the Coulomb attraction at $0.69$ Å exceeds the kinetic penalty. If $\alpha$ were different, the bond gap would be different, the bond would be stronger or weaker, the tetrahedral geometry would shift or not form at all. ### 3.7 Every Bond Is a Zinc Closure

The zinc spark at fertilisation is the most visible instance of the closure mechanism, but it is not the only instance. It is the template. Every chemical bond that forms is a zinc closure in miniature.

Consider any covalent bond. Two atoms approach. Each extends its half-bond — the covalent radius, the electron density it commits outward from its ionic core. The half-bonds meet in the middle. At the meeting point, the electron density is shared. A one-sided surface forms: trace the electron density from nucleus A through the bond to nucleus B and back through the other bonding orbital, and you return to A without crossing an edge. This is a Möbius closure. The two separate atomic orbitals have become one molecular orbital. The half twist is the phase relationship between the contributing wavefunctions — bonding ($+/+$) or antibonding ($+/-$) — and only the bonding combination survives. The antibonding alternative is vetoed by energy. The bond IS the veto of the alternative.

The reaction energy — the enthalpy change $\Delta H$ — is the mass of the spark. It is the difference between what the atoms weighed separately (sum of isolated-atom energies, the external measurement) and what the molecule weighs bonded (the internal measurement from within the bond). Every unique chemical bond has its own $\Delta H$: C-C ($346$ kJ/mol), C=O ($799$ kJ/mol), N-H ($386$ kJ/mol), Zn-N ($\sim 150$ kJ/mol in zinc finger proteins). Each value is the spark energy for that bond — the energy the system committed to creating the coupling, the energy that now lives in the $0.69$ Å gap, the energy that will be released if the bond breaks (and the closure reopens, and the Möbius surface tears back into two strips).

This spark is not free. Four conditions constrain it:

1. **Electromagnetic coupling** ($\alpha$): The Coulomb attraction and repulsion in the bond gap. Governs the energetics.
2. **Geometry** (strong force — nuclear structure): The nuclear charges $Z$ determine the orbital shapes, which determine the bond angles. Tetrahedral for $d^{10}$ zinc. Trigonal for $sp^2$ carbon. The strong force built the nuclei; the nuclei dictate the geometry.
3. **Mass** (gravity — sets the energy scale): The nuclear masses determine the vibrational frequencies of the bond. A heavier atom vibrates slower — the bond probes configuration space at a rate set by the reduced mass. This is why isotope effects exist: deuterium bonds behave differently from hydrogen bonds, not because the chemistry changed but because the mass changed the probing rate.
4. **Transmutability** (weak force): Whether the atom can change identity during the bond's lifetime. For stable atoms, this condition is a constant — the atom remains itself. For radioactive atoms near $Z = 43$, the weak force can transmute the bonded atom mid-bond, breaking the closure from within. The bond must form between atoms whose identity persists long enough for the closure to complete.

Every bond probes all four conditions. Every bond that forms has satisfied all four. Every bond that breaks has had at least one condition fail. The system does not choose which bonds to make — it probes every accessible geometry, every reachable partner, and the bonds that satisfy all four conditions are the bonds that form. What survives the four-condition filter is existence. What fails is vetoed.
