## Chapter 1: Oligodendrocyte Precursor Cell Biology and Vulnerability

### 1.1 The Cells That Build Myelin

Oligodendrocyte precursor cells (OPCs) are the progenitor cells that differentiate into mature oligodendrocytes — the cells responsible for myelinating axons in the central nervous system. OPCs represent the **largest proliferative cell population in the adult CNS**, constituting approximately 70–90% of proliferating cells (Horner et al., 2000). They comprise 3–4% of cells in grey matter and 8–9% in white matter (Dawson et al., 2003). OPCs persist throughout life and maintain their population through self-renewal (Nishiyama et al., 1999).

OPCs develop through a defined lineage:

```
OPC → pre-oligodendrocyte (preOL) → immature oligodendrocyte → mature myelinating oligodendrocyte
```

OPCs first appear during embryonic organogenesis in ventral ventricular zones, directed by Sonic hedgehog (Shh) signalling and Olig1/Olig2 transcription factors. Multiple waves of OPCs are produced sequentially from different germinal zones (Kessaris et al., 2006).

### 1.2 The Maturation-Dependent Vulnerability Window

This is the area with the strongest direct evidence, primarily from the laboratory of **Stephen A. Back** and collaborators.

**Back et al. (2001)** — "Late oligodendrocyte progenitors coincide with the developmental window of vulnerability for human perinatal white matter injury" (*J Neurosci*). Established that the developmental window when late OL progenitors (preOLs) populate human cerebral white matter aligns with the period of maximum risk for periventricular leukomalacia (PVL).

**Back et al. (2002)** — "Selective vulnerability of late oligodendrocyte progenitors to hypoxia-ischemia" (*J Neurosci*). The landmark finding:

> "The mechanism of perinatal white matter injury involved maturation-dependent vulnerability in the oligodendrocyte lineage. Late OL progenitors were the major OL lineage stage killed by apoptosis, whereas early OL progenitors and more mature OLs were highly resistant."

The death rate was dramatic: **72 ± 6% of preOLs** were killed in ischaemic tissue. The vulnerability window in humans corresponds approximately to **23–32 weeks gestational age**, when periventricular white matter is heavily populated by preOLs.

### 1.3 Arrested Maturation — The Key Mechanism

**Segovia et al. (2008)** — "Arrested oligodendrocyte lineage maturation in chronic perinatal white matter injury" (*Ann Neurol*):

> "Degeneration of preOLs was offset by a **robust regenerative response** that resulted in a several-fold expansion in the pool of surviving preOLs in chronic lesions. **However, these preOLs displayed persistent maturation arrest with failure to differentiate and generate myelin.**"

> "The persistence of a susceptible population of preOLs renders chronic white matter lesions **markedly more vulnerable to recurrent hypoxia-ischaemia.**"

### 1.4 Human Confirmation

**Buser, Maire, Riddle... Back (2012)** — "Arrested preoligodendrocyte maturation contributes to myelination failure in premature infants" (*Ann Neurol*). Confirmed in human premature infant brain tissue (retrospective 1983–2000 and prospective 2003–2010 cases):

> "The total population of OL lineage stages was significantly increased in lesions. This increase coincided with **significant expansion of the preOL pool**."

> "The **primary mechanism of myelination failure involves a disrupted cellular response whereby preOLs fail to differentiate** in diffuse astrogliotic lesions."

### 1.5 White Matter Injury as Dysmaturation

**Back (2017)** — "White matter injury in the preterm infant: pathology and mechanisms" (*Acta Neuropathol*):

> "Diffuse WMI involves maturation-dependent vulnerability of the oligodendrocyte lineage with selective degeneration of late oligodendrocyte progenitors (preOLs) triggered by oxidative stress."

> "Newly generated preOLs fail to differentiate and initiate myelination along their normal developmental trajectory **despite the presence of numerous intact-appearing axons.**"

> "There exists a potentially broad but still poorly defined **developmental window for interventions to promote white matter repair and myelination.**"

**Back & Miller (2014)** termed this a **"primary cerebral dysmaturation disorder"** — the injury is fundamentally a disruption of normal maturation rather than simple tissue destruction.

### 1.6 OPC Oxygen Sensing — The HIF Mechanism

**Wellmann, Bührer & Schmitz (2015)** provided direct experimental evidence that OPCs sense and respond to oxygen levels through HIF (hypoxia-inducible factor) signalling:

- **Simulated hyperoxia** (via HIF deletion) produced "white matter disease resembling cystic PVL within the first 7 days of life."
- **Simulated hypoxia** (via genetic inhibition of HIF decay) produced "arrested OPC maturation and subsequent hypomyelination."

Both directions of oxygen disturbance damage white matter — but through distinct mechanisms. OPCs have **intrinsic oxygen-sensing machinery** that directly modulates their maturation.

### 1.7 Excitotoxic Vulnerability

PreOLs are selectively vulnerable to glutamate through multiple receptor types:

- **Back et al. (1998, 2001, 2002)** — established that preOLs are the primary cellular target of glutamate excitotoxicity in periventricular white matter injury.
- **Dohare et al. (2016)** — AMPA-kainate receptor-induced excitotoxicity contributes to OPC damage and hypomyelination.
- **Zhang Y et al. (2013)** — NMDARs are expressed directly on myelin sheaths; NMDA receptor-mediated excitotoxicity can damage white matter independently of neuronal mechanisms.
- **Al-Griw et al. (2021)** — inhibition of ionotropic glutamate receptor signalling preserved oligodendrocyte lineage and myelination.
- **Volpe (2009)** — described the two-hit model: initial excitotoxic/oxidative injury destroys preOLs, followed by arrested maturation of replacement OPCs. The result is a **persistent myelination deficit** even after the acute insult resolves.

### 1.8 The Injury Sequence

Based on the cumulative evidence (Back et al., 2002; Segovia et al., 2008; Buser et al., 2012; Back, 2017):

1. **Initial insult**: Hypoxia-ischaemia selectively kills preOLs through oxidative stress and apoptosis (72% death rate)
2. **Regenerative response**: Surviving OL progenitors proliferate, producing a several-fold expansion of the preOL pool
3. **Maturation arrest**: The regenerated preOLs fail to differentiate into mature myelinating oligodendrocytes
4. **Persistent vulnerability**: The arrested preOL pool remains susceptible to further hypoxic-ischaemic insults
5. **Result**: Axons remain intact but unmyelinated or poorly myelinated, despite adequate precursor cell numbers

### 1.9 Remyelination Capacity and Limits

Remyelination is possible when axons remain intact. Smith et al. (1979, 1982) demonstrated that even thin remyelinated sheaths restore saltatory conduction. However:

- Remyelinated sheaths are characteristically thinner than developmental myelin, with shorter internodes and higher g-ratio
- OPC differentiation efficiency declines with age
- In chronic lesions, OPCs may be present but unable to mature — differentiation failure, not recruitment failure
- "Unmyelinated fibres and myelinated axons of the mammalian central nervous system do not regenerate" — remyelination requires intact axons (Huebner & Strittmatter, 2009)

Up to **30% of oligodendrocytes in the adult corpus callosum are generated de novo from OPCs over a period of 2 months**, demonstrating ongoing OPC-to-oligodendrocyte conversion even in adulthood, though at reduced rates.

## Chapter 2: White Matter Architecture and Myelination Timeline

### 2.1 The Tracts That Matter

The brain's functional networks depend on white matter tracts for communication between cortical regions. The tracts most relevant to language, social cognition, and motor function — and their vulnerability to perinatal OPC injury — are:

| Tract | Function | Myelination at Birth | Active Myelination Period | Maturation Completion |
|-------|----------|---------------------|---------------------------|----------------------|
| **Arcuate fasciculus (AF)** | Complex syntax, speech production, prosody | Unmyelinated | Ages 3–10+ | ~12–15 years (Friederici, 2017) |
| **SLF-III** | Motor speech planning, imitation | Unmyelinated/early | Ages 5–12 | ~15–20 years |
| **Uncinate fasciculus (UF)** | Word retrieval, social reward, emotional regulation | Unmyelinated/early | Through childhood | **~30 years** (Lebel et al., 2008) |
| **ILF** | Face recognition, visual-emotional processing | Early | Ages 5–8 | ~12 years |
| **IFOF** | Semantic processing, emotion recognition | Early | Ages 6–10 | ~15 years |
| **Cingulum** | Theory of mind, empathy, social error monitoring | Early | Ages 5–20+ | **Peak FA after age 40** (Lebel et al., 2012) |
| **Corpus callosum** | Interhemispheric integration | Variable by region | First decade peak | ~35 years (body) |
| **Corticospinal tract** | Motor coordination | Early | Infancy onward | Variable |

### 2.2 Flechsig's Law — Sequential Myelination

Paul Flechsig established the "Fundamental Law of Myelogenesis": cortical myelination follows a strict sequential order. Motor cortex myelinates first, then somatosensory cortex. The last areas to myelinate are the anterior cingulate, inferior temporal cortex, and dorsolateral prefrontal cortex. Myelination begins in the third trimester (~week 26 gestational age) and continues through the third decade of life.

**Yakovlev & Lecours (1967)** — landmark histological study identifying three myeloarchitectonic zones:
1. **Median periventricular zone** (hippocampus, hypothalamus) — myelinates relatively early
2. **Paramedian/limbic zone** (thalamus, internal capsule) — intermediate
3. **Supralimbic zone** (frontal, parietal, temporal white matter) — longest myelination cycle, continuing into adulthood

### 2.3 Language and Social Cognition Tracts Share the Same White Matter

There is **no white matter tract serving social cognition that is entirely independent of language processing.** The tracts are largely shared or anatomically adjacent, running through the same white matter regions:

| Tract | Language Function | Social Cognition Function |
|-------|-------------------|---------------------------|
| Arcuate fasciculus | Phonological loop, syntax, speech output | Prosody perception, pragmatic communication |
| Uncinate fasciculus | Semantic memory, word retrieval | Emotional regulation, social reward, name-face learning |
| IFOF | Reading, semantic processing | Emotion recognition from faces |
| ILF | Visual word recognition | Face recognition, emotional visual processing |
| Cingulum | Verbal memory | Theory of mind, empathy |
| Corpus callosum | Bilateral language processing | Bilateral social-emotional integration |
| SLF-III | Motor speech planning | Imitation, mirror system, gesture comprehension |

This overlap means a single insult to OPCs — which are distributed throughout the brain's white matter zones — can simultaneously compromise both language and social cognition tracts.

### 2.4 The Perisylvian Vulnerability Chain

The entire perisylvian cortex — Broca's area, Wernicke's area, primary auditory cortex — is supplied by the middle cerebral artery (MCA). The internal watershed zone runs through periventricular white matter precisely where the connecting tracts (AF, SLF) traverse. This means:

1. Both language cortical regions are in MCA territory — the most common location for focal cerebral ischaemia
2. The white matter underlying language cortex sits in one of the brain's most hypoperfusion-vulnerable regions (the periventricular watershed)
3. Periventricular white matter is THE primary target of perinatal hypoxic-ischaemic injury — not cortical grey matter (Back, 2017)

This creates a specific vulnerability pattern: **language cortex may be spared while the white matter connecting it is damaged**.

## Chapter 3: Activity-Dependent Myelination

### 3.1 Neural Activity Drives Myelination

Activity-dependent myelination is established as a causal mechanism, not merely correlational:

**Fields (2015)** — "A new mechanism of nervous system plasticity: activity-dependent myelination." Demonstrated that electrical activity in axons directly regulates myelination. Cited 907 times.

**Gibson et al. (2014)** — "Neuronal activity promotes oligodendrogenesis and adaptive myelination in the mammalian brain" (*Science*). Showed that neuronal activity directly promotes OPC proliferation and differentiation — the causal mechanism. Cited 1,634 times. OPCs receive direct synaptic contacts from neurons and can "undergo cell division while maintaining synaptic inputs" (Kukley et al., 2008).

**McKenzie et al. (2014)** — Demonstrated that blocking new myelination **prevents motor learning**. New myelination is not merely a consequence of learning — it is required for it.

### 3.2 Bidirectionality

Activity-dependent myelination operates in both directions:
- **More activity → more myelin**: Scholz et al. (2009) demonstrated white matter volume changes from motor learning; Sampaio-Baptista et al. (2013) showed increased myelination with motor skill acquisition; Buyanova & Arsalidou (2021) confirmed neuronal activity is the "main driving force for dynamic myelination"
- **Less/abnormal activity → less myelin**: Crombie et al. (2021) showed prenatal stress association with long-term white matter disturbance; Gibson et al. (2014) established the causal link in animal models

### 3.3 OPCs Lose Activity Sensitivity as They Mature

OPCs "appear to lose their ability to respond to synaptic inputs from neurons as they differentiate into mature oligodendrocytes." This creates a window during which OPCs are responsive to activity-based signals — an early window of signal sensitivity that diminishes with maturation.

### 3.4 Implications for Therapy

If therapy provides structured, intensive activation of language circuits, this could:
1. Stimulate axonal activity in partially myelinated or unmyelinated tracts
2. Promote differentiation of arrested preOLs into mature myelinating oligodendrocytes
3. Drive activity-dependent myelination of the arcuate fasciculus and related pathways

**No direct evidence found** for DTI-confirmed white matter changes specifically resulting from speech therapy in children with perinatal white matter injury. The mechanism is established in animal models; the behavioural effects of therapy are documented; the specific myelination bridge has not been measured.

## Chapter 4: Perinatal Insult and OPC Injury

### 4.1 Preeclampsia as a Specific Risk Factor

Preeclampsia creates the conditions for OPC injury through three pathways:

1. **Placental insufficiency → fetal hypoxia**: Abnormal placentation leads to ischaemia-reperfusion at the placental bed, producing systemic oxidative stress. Fetal SpO₂ (normally ~60–70%) drops below the range in which OPC maturation is programmed to occur.

2. **Birth complications → acute hypoxia**: Preeclampsia increases risk of emergency delivery, fetal distress, and neonatal resuscitation — each an acute hypoxic-ischaemic challenge to periventricular white matter.

3. **Premature delivery**: Preeclampsia is a leading cause of medically indicated preterm birth, exposing the developing brain during the peak OPC vulnerability window (23–32 weeks).

### 4.2 The Injury Cascade

The mechanisms of perinatal brain injury are well-characterised (Piešová & Mach, 2020; Rocha-Ferreira & Hristova, 2016):

1. **Excitotoxicity**: Hypoxia elevates extracellular glutamate → NMDA receptor overactivation → calcium overload → cell death. The developing brain's dominance of NMDA receptors increases vulnerability.
2. **Oxidative stress**: Mitochondrial impairment increases ROS production. The preterm brain has lower glutathione peroxidase activity — antioxidant capacity is developmentally inadequate (Panfoli et al., 2018).
3. **Inflammation**: Activated microglia release cytokines that directly depress survival and maturation of oligodendrocytes (Robinson et al., 2005).
4. **Three-phase injury**: Phase 1 (seconds/minutes): energy depletion. Phase 2 (hours/days): reperfusion injury via free radicals and apoptosis. Phase 3 (weeks/months): inflammation and epigenetic changes impairing neurogenesis and synaptogenesis.

### 4.3 Direct Animal Model Evidence

**Wu et al. (2025)** — "Sex-specific neurobehavioral and white matter abnormalities in a mouse model of preeclampsia" (*Exp Neurol*). The single most directly relevant study:

- **Male offspring** showed sex-specific deficits in anxiety, cognition, and social exploration
- White matter analysis revealed: **impaired oligodendrocyte maturation, reduced myelination, and axonal damage**
- Changes attributed to **diminished OPC proliferation**
- Environmental enrichment partially restored deficits

This demonstrates the exact mechanistic chain: preeclampsia → impaired OPC maturation → reduced myelination. The sex-specificity (male > female) is consistent with the higher male prevalence of ASD. Partial restoration through enrichment supports the therapy-as-myelination hypothesis.

### 4.4 Fetal White Matter Damage Begins In Utero

**Zheng et al. (2022)** — Fetal MRI showed decreased corpus callosum length and area in fetuses with preeclampsia-associated growth restriction (*JAMA Netw Open*). The splenium was specifically affected.

**Miller et al. (2007)** — 32% of **full-term** infants with congenital heart disease (chronic mild hypoxaemia) had white matter injury at birth — before any postnatal events (*NEJM*). This establishes that chronic moderate oxygen disturbance, not just acute ischaemia, causes white matter damage.

### 4.5 Vulnerability Hierarchy

The developing brain's regional vulnerability to hypoxia (compiled from Piešová & Mach, 2020; Koundal et al., 2014; McClendon et al., 2014):

1. **Hippocampus** — most frequently cited as the single most hypoxia-vulnerable region (glutamate/NMDA receptor dominance)
2. **Basal ganglia (striatum)** — very high sensitivity; dopamine-acetylcholine imbalance → repetitive/compulsive behaviour
3. **Cerebral cortex** — significant neuronal loss documented
4. **Thalamus** — grouped with basal ganglia in vulnerability
5. **Periventricular white matter** — especially in preterm infants; the OPC-rich zone

## Chapter 5: Subclinical Oxygen Thresholds and the Intermittent Hypoxaemia Problem

### 5.1 Clinical vs Biological Thresholds

Clinical oxygen monitoring defines "hypoxaemia" at SpO₂ below ~90% and treats brief desaturation episodes to ~80% as tolerable. The NeOProM meta-analysis (Askie et al., 2017; 5 trials, 4,965 infants) compared SpO₂ targets of 85–89% vs 91–95% and found:

- Lower target → increased mortality (RR 1.16, +28 deaths per 1,000)
- Lower target → decreased severe ROP
- No significant difference in cerebral palsy, blindness, or major disability

**Critical gap**: No RCT has examined outcomes of repeated episodes **below** 85%. The NeOProM trials compared ranges above 85%. Episodes reaching ≤80% were side effects, not the target being tested.

### 5.2 Intermittent Hypoxaemia (IH) — The Invisible Burden

**Di Fiore et al. (2012)** — SUPPORT sub-study: defined IH as SpO₂ ≤80% for ≥10 seconds. Infants in the lower target group had significantly more IH episodes, clustered with <1 minute between events.

**Di Fiore & Raffay (2021)** — IH events "are associated with a range of poor neural outcomes including **language and cognitive delays, motor impairment**, retinopathy of prematurity, and intraventricular haemorrhage."

**Di Fiore & Vento (2019)** — IH events "are associated with many poor outcomes including cognitive or language delays and motor impairment." Proposed oxidative stress as the mechanistic link.

**Di Fiore et al. (2025)** — "Intermittent Hypoxemia and Neurodevelopmental Impairment at 12 and 24 Months in Preterm Infants" (*Neonatology*) — the most recent longitudinal analysis confirming the association.

Patterns matter: "neonatal rodent models have shown that exposure to short repetitive cycles of hypoxia induce a pathophysiological cascade" (Di Fiore & Raffay, 2021).

### 5.3 Reoxygenation Injury — The Oscillation Problem

**Ramond et al. (2013)** — Intermittent hypoxia produces **greater oxidative damage than sustained hypoxia** of equivalent total duration. Each desaturation-resaturation cycle generates a pulse of ROS from the reoxygenation phase.

**Wellmann et al. (2015)** provide the unifying framework — both directions damage white matter:
- **Hypoxia → HIF stabilisation → OPC maturation arrest → diffuse hypomyelination**
- **Hyperoxia → HIF degradation → OPC death → focal cystic PVL**

Oscillation between these states triggers **both** mechanisms in alternation. Each hypoxic dip activates HIF-mediated maturation arrest; each reoxygenation recovery degrades HIF and generates ROS. The OPC exists in a perpetually destabilised state.

### 5.4 The Antioxidant Gap

**Panfoli et al. (2018)**: "Room air exposure of an organism primed to develop in a hypoxic environment, lacking antioxidant defences, and subjected to hyperoxia, hypoxia, and ischaemia challenges the newborn with oxidative stress production."

The preterm brain is vulnerable not because the oxygen insult is severe, but because **antioxidant capacity is developmentally inadequate**. Events fully buffered in a mature brain may cause uncompensated free radical damage in the preterm.

### 5.5 The Monitoring Gap

Current NICU monitoring typically alarms at sustained SpO₂ <85%. Brief excursions to 80% that self-resolve within seconds are often not recorded or acted upon. Yet:

- IH is defined at ≤80% for ≥10 seconds
- Many events cluster with <1 minute between events
- Each generates a reoxygenation-mediated ROS pulse
- OPCs have intrinsic HIF-mediated oxygen sensing at these levels

The monitoring gap is not that alarms are set too high, but that **the clinical framework treats each brief desaturation as an isolated self-resolving event, rather than as part of a cumulative oxidative burden**.

### 5.6 The Three-Variable Problem

The field is currently trapped in a **two-variable trade-off: eyes vs survival**. Lower oxygen protects the retina but increases death. Higher oxygen improves survival but increases ROP.

**The proposed model adds a third variable: white matter oscillation burden.**

Two infants with identical average SpO₂ can have radically different oscillation profiles:
- Infant A: SpO₂ stable at 91%, zero IH events → minimal ROS burden
- Infant B: SpO₂ averaging 91% but cycling 78→95→80→94 → massive cumulative ROS burden

Current monitoring treats both as equivalent. The model says they are not.

## Chapter 6: The Latent Period — Growing Into Deficit

### 6.1 The Concept

The concept that brain injury in early life may produce deficits that only become apparent later — when the developmental programme attempts to deploy injured circuits — is well-documented.

**From the PVL literature** (Rezaie & Dean, 2002):
> "It is often impossible to identify PVL based on the patient's physical or behavioral characteristics. As the individual develops, the areas and extent of problems caused by PVL can begin to be identified; however, these problems are usually found after an initial diagnosis has been made."

**From paediatric TBI** (Chevignard, Câmara-Costa & Dellatolas, 2020):
> "Childhood TBI can disrupt subsequent brain development, and long-term consequences emerge later."

**Anderson et al. (2009, 2012)** — TBI in early childhood produces "high risk of persisting deficits" measurable only years later; intellectual outcomes showed evolution of deficits over a 5-year prospective longitudinal study.

### 6.2 The "Failure at Demand Spike" Model

White matter tracts damaged at birth are **functionally silent because the neonate doesn't yet need them**. The arcuate fasciculus is unmyelinated at birth even in healthy infants. This creates a specific temporal pattern:

1. **Pre-demand period (birth–2 years)**: Damaged tracts are not yet functionally required. Single-word language proceeds relatively normally via ventral stream and simpler cortical circuits.
2. **Demand spike (~2–3 years)**: Development calls upon the arcuate fasciculus for complex syntax. Damaged OPCs cannot generate adequate myelination.
3. **Visible deficit (~2–4+ years)**: Language delays emerge — not because of a new injury, but because existing damage is unmasked by functional demand.

The uncinate fasciculus study by **Constable et al. (2008)** demonstrated this principle directly: at age 12, children born preterm showed white matter microstructural differences that correlated with verbal IQ. The injury occurred at birth; the functional consequence was measurable over a decade later.

## Chapter 7: Staggered Emergence of Deficits

### 7.1 The Timeline Prediction

Because different tracts myelinate at different rates and serve different functions, a single perinatal OPC insult produces **staggered** functional deficits:

```
PERINATAL INSULT (OPC maturation arrest — global)
    ↓
Reduced pool of myelination-competent oligodendrocytes
    ↓
[STAGGERED EMERGENCE]
    ↓
Age 2–4:  AF under-myelinated  → LANGUAGE DEFICIT (syntax, comprehension)
Age 3–5:  SLF under-myelinated → MOTOR SPEECH PLANNING (apraxia features)
Age 4–8:  ILF under-myelinated → FACE PROCESSING deficit
Age 4–10: Cingulum             → THEORY OF MIND / social cognition
Age 5–15: UF under-myelinated  → SOCIAL-EMOTIONAL regulation
Age 5–20: CC under-myelinated  → INTERHEMISPHERIC INTEGRATION
```

### 7.2 The Motor Deficit Connection

An estimated **60–80% of autistic people have motor signs** including poor muscle tone, poor motor planning, and toe walking (Geschwind, 2009). Motor difficulties including features of "dyspraxia" are highly prevalent.

Motor function relies on **different** white matter tracts (corticospinal tract, cerebellar peduncles, SLF-III) than language or social cognition. Yet motor impairment co-occurs with language and social deficits at extremely high rates. This is strong evidence for a **common white matter cause** rather than independent, domain-specific programmes.

### 7.3 Evidence for 12/15 Tracts Affected Simultaneously

**Wolff et al. (2012)** — prospective infant DTI study of 92 infants (28 later diagnosed with ASD). Infants who developed ASD showed higher FA at 6 months but lower FA at 24 months across **12 of 15 white matter tracts examined**, including ILF, UF, cingulum, arcuate fasciculus, and corpus callosum — both language AND social cognition tracts.

**Shukla et al. (2011)** — Tract-based analysis showed widespread reduced FA across cingulum, ILF, IFOF, uncinate fasciculus, SLF, and corpus callosum. White matter differences in ASD are NOT limited to language tracts but extend to ALL major social cognition tracts.

**Travers et al. (2012)** — DTI meta-analysis: white matter differences become **MORE pronounced with age**, not less. As tracts are supposed to myelinate further in childhood/adolescence, the deficit becomes progressively more apparent.

### 7.4 The Uncinate Fasciculus — Explaining Persistent Social Difficulties

The UF has the **longest myelination window of any white matter tract** (to ~age 30; Lebel et al., 2008). It connects anterior temporal lobe and amygdala to orbitofrontal and prefrontal cortex, supporting social reward, emotional regulation, and name-face learning.

This predicts that social cognition deficits may worsen or become more apparent through adolescence and early adulthood, even as language improves — consistent with clinical observation. The UF's vulnerability to perinatal injury is directly demonstrated:
- Preterm birth → reduced UF FA → correlated with IQ deficits at age 12 (Constable et al., 2008)
- Early socioemotional deprivation → reduced UF FA (Eluvathingal et al., 2006)

## Chapter 8: Regression as Myelination Limit

### 8.1 Regression Is More Common Than Reported

**Ozonoff et al. (2010)** — Prospective study showing declining developmental trajectories in **most** ASD infants, not just the 25% identified by parent report. Parent report underestimates regression because the decline is often gradual, not sudden.

**Ozonoff & Iosif (2019)** — Review confirming that prospective studies detect regression in a larger proportion than retrospective parent report.

### 8.2 The DTI Trajectory Matches the Regression Window

Wolff et al. (2012) — the DTI trajectory (high FA at 6 months, low at 24 months across 12/15 tracts) **coincides exactly with the regression window** (typically 15–24 months). The white matter is measurably changing during the period when skills are being lost.

**Nordahl et al. (2011)** — Brain enlargement was specifically associated with the regressive subtype of ASD.

### 8.3 Metabolic Cost of Myelination

Myelination is metabolically expensive, requiring large amounts of lipid synthesis (Tennekoon et al., 1977). **Kuzawa et al. (2014)** — the brain's glucose consumption peaks at ~66% of resting metabolic rate at age 4–5, creating direct metabolic competition between brain growth and body growth.

**Tepavčević (2021)** — Under metabolic stress, oligodendrocytes respond with process retraction, reduced metabolic rate, and compromised myelination — but preserved cell survival. **Myelination is the first function sacrificed under metabolic constraint.**

### 8.4 Evidence Gap

**No direct evidence found** for a specific "repair mode" in which the brain reallocates metabolic resources toward white matter repair during autistic regression. The concept of activity-dependent OPC recruitment is established; metabolic resource competition is documented; but their specific application to autistic regression remains untested.

## Chapter 9: The Disconnection Model — Cortex Intact, White Matter Disrupted

### 9.1 Classical Foundation

Geschwind (1965) established the concept of "disconnexion syndromes": white matter damage can produce functional deficits WITHOUT cortical damage. The deficit is specific to functions requiring communication between regions, while each cortical region retains its local processing capacity.

### 9.2 ASD as Developmental Disconnection

**Geschwind & Levitt (2007)** — "Autism spectrum disorders: developmental disconnection syndromes" (*Curr Opin Neurobiol*). ASD can be understood as a developmental disconnection syndrome where cortical regions are intact (or enhanced in local processing) but white matter connections between them are impaired. Deficits are primarily in functions requiring cross-regional integration (language, social cognition, executive function).

**Courchesne & Pierce (2005)** — ASD involves excessive local connectivity within cortical regions combined with reduced long-range connectivity between them. Long-range tracts require MORE myelination and are therefore MORE vulnerable to a global OPC supply problem.

### 9.3 Evidence That Cortical Architecture Is Preserved

Multiple lines of evidence support intact cortex with impaired white matter:

- **Enhanced perceptual functioning**: Superior performance on local visual processing tasks (embedded figures, block design) — cortical visual areas function at enhanced levels (Mottron et al., 2006)
- **Intact/superior rote memory**: Savant skills occur at ~10% in ASD vs <1% general population (Treffert, 2009)
- **Uneven cognitive profile**: Strengths in some domains, weaknesses in others — consistent with intact local processing but impaired integration
- **AAC success**: Augmentative communication devices work because individuals CAN communicate when given an alternative output pathway that bypasses impaired white matter speech-output tracts

### 9.4 DTI Signature Consistent with Myelination Failure

**Fletcher et al. (2010)** — DTI of the arcuate fasciculus in autistic adolescents showed significantly increased **radial diffusivity (RD)** without proportional axial diffusivity increase. Increased RD is specifically interpreted as a marker of **reduced myelination** (as distinct from axonal loss, which increases axial diffusivity). This is the DTI signature of **myelination failure with intact axons** — exactly what OPC maturation arrest would produce.

**Zhang F et al. (2018)** — DTI of AF in ASD toddlers with language regression: reduced FA with increased tract volume — consistent with poorly myelinated, swollen tracts rather than tract loss.

## Chapter 10: "Words Are There But Won't Come Out"

### 10.1 The Clinical Pattern

About **25–35% of autistic school-age children are non-speaking or minimally speaking** (Byrne et al., 2024). This occurs in a population where:
- Cortical regions are intact
- Receptive (input) processing is often relatively preserved
- Visual processing is often enhanced

This pattern — intact input processing with impaired output — is the signature of a white matter disconnection affecting output tracts.

### 10.2 Childhood Apraxia of Speech

CAS is defined as the brain having "problems planning to move the body parts needed for speech" while "the individual knows what they want to say." CAS "can occur as a secondary characteristic of other conditions such as AUTISM." Causes include "brain injury" from "prenatal or birth injuries." This directly aligns with the perinatal disconnection model.

### 10.3 AAC as Proof of Cortical Integrity

The success of AAC/PECS in autism supports the disconnection model. The individual CAN communicate when given an alternative output pathway. Some non-speaking autistic individuals demonstrate full linguistic competence through typed or written communication — strong clinical evidence for intact cortical language with impaired output connectivity.

### 10.4 Frustration and Meltdowns

The DSM-5-TR notes that "symptoms may not become fully manifest until later, when social demands exceed capabilities." The demand-exceeds-capacity model is directly predicted by white matter disconnection:

- At low communicative load, partial white matter integrity may suffice
- As demands increase, impaired tracts reach bandwidth limits
- Output degrades or fails entirely → frustration → meltdown

Self-injurious behaviour may function to "express needs, distress, or other messages" — consistent with the disconnection model: when speech-output disconnection prevents verbal communication of distress, alternative (motor-based) expressions emerge.

# PART IV — INTERVENTION MECHANICS

---

## Chapter 11: The Forming-vs-Formed Distinction

### 11.1 OPC Density Declines With Age

OPC density and proliferative capacity decrease with age, creating a biological gradient favouring earlier intervention:

- White matter OPCs "proliferate at higher rates" than grey matter OPCs
- "The rate at which OPCs generate oligodendrocytes declines with age and is greater in white matter than in grey matter"
- OPCs persist throughout life and maintain their population through self-renewal, but differentiation efficiency declines

### 11.2 Two Phases of Opportunity

**"Forming" phase** (tracts actively myelinating):
- Higher OPC density and proliferative capacity
- Active signalling cascades for myelin gene expression
- Activity-dependent myelination operates most efficiently
- Intervention is additive to an already-running process

**"Formed" phase** (tracts completed myelination):
- Lower OPC density; reduced differentiation rate
- Myelin maintenance replaces myelin formation
- Remyelination is possible but produces thinner myelin
- Intervention must initiate de novo myelination rather than supplement ongoing myelination

### 11.3 Molecular Evidence for Age-Dependent Returns

**Gatto & Broadie (2008)** — In Fragile X syndrome, early introduction of the FMRP protein results in "nearly complete restructuring of the synapses" but "is not as effective when introduced into a mature subject and only partially accommodates for the losses." This demonstrates age-dependent diminishing returns of intervention **at the molecular level**.

---

## Chapter 12: Therapy as Activity-Dependent Myelination

### 12.1 Earlier Is Consistently Better

**Lovaas (1987)** — Original intensive ABA study: 40 hours/week before age 4 → 47% achieved "optimal outcome."

**Rogers et al. (2012)** — ESDM RCT: younger age + more hours = better efficacy.

**Estes et al. (2015)** — ESDM effects persist 2 years after treatment ends.

**Fuller et al. (2020)** — ESDM meta-analysis: significant cognition + language improvements.

**Dawson et al. (2012)** — ESDM intervention **normalised ERP brain responses** in autistic children — not just behavioural improvement, actual neural change.

**Fein et al. (2013)** — "Optimal outcome": some children no longer meet ASD criteria after early intervention.

### 12.2 The Mechanism

If therapy drives structured, intensive activation of specific neural circuits, and if activity-dependent myelination (Fields, 2015; Gibson et al., 2014) drives OPC recruitment and differentiation in those circuits, then:

- **Speech therapy** → AF circuit activation → OPC recruitment → AF myelination
- **Social skills training** → UF/cingulum circuit activation → OPC recruitment → social tract myelination
- **Motor therapy** → CST/SLF activation → OPC recruitment → motor tract myelination

This frames therapy not as behavioural conditioning but as **biologically-driven myelination acceleration**.

### 12.3 The 30-Year Window

The uncinate fasciculus myelinates until age 30 (Lebel et al., 2008). This means social cognition interventions have a far longer biological runway than language interventions (AF myelination largely complete by 12–15 years). The protracted UF window explains why social skills training can still be effective in adolescence and adulthood — the biological substrate for activity-dependent myelination remains available.

### 12.4 Evidence Gap

**No study directly demonstrates** that speech therapy drives language tract myelination in autistic children. The mechanism is established in animal models (Fields, 2015; Gibson et al., 2014), the behavioural effects are documented, ESDM normalises brain responses (Dawson et al., 2012) — but the specific myelination bridge has not been measured with pre/post DTI.

# PART V — SUPPORTING EVIDENCE FROM PARALLEL SYSTEMS

---

## Chapter 13: Cross-Domain Recovery Patterns

### 13.1 The Build-Release-Continue Pattern

Recovery across biological systems follows a universal staged pattern:

| System | Fastest Response | Full Recovery | Key Feature |
|--------|-----------------|---------------|-------------|
| Liver | 5 minutes (hemodynamic) | ~30 days (half mass) | Only 10% required for full regeneration |
| Wound healing | Minutes (hemostasis) | Months to >1 year (remodelling) | Type III → Type I collagen replacement |
| Bone | Hours (haematoma) | 3–5 years (complete remodelling) | Woven → lamellar bone |
| White matter | Days (OPC activation) | Weeks to months (incomplete) | Remyelination thinner than developmental |
| Beehive colony | 16 days (emergency queen) | Weeks (worker pipeline) | Age-based task progression (temporal polyethism) |
| Ecological succession | Years (pioneers) | 100–1,400 years | Simple → complex community |

### 13.2 Beehive Parallel

Colony recovery is staged and sequential. When a queen dies, workers select larvae for emergency queen rearing (16 days). Worker bees follow age-based task progression from cell cleaning (days 1–2) through nursing (3–12) to foraging (21–42). If foragers are lost, younger bees accelerate their progression — the hive flexes its developmental timeline to meet functional demand.

This parallels the brain's capacity for compensatory myelination: when one tract is impaired, alternative routing through available white matter infrastructure may accelerate. But as with the hive's emergency queens ("usually smaller and less prolific"), the compensatory pathways may function less effectively than normal developmental pathways.

## Chapter 14: Metabolic Competition and Brain Development

### 14.1 Brain Metabolic Dominance

**Kuzawa et al. (2014)** — The brain's glucose consumption peaks at ~66% of resting metabolic rate at age 4–5, creating direct competition with somatic growth. Brain metabolic peak inversely correlates with body weight gain — when brain glucose demand is highest, body growth slows.

**Peters (2006, 2011)** — "The Selfish Brain: Competition for Energy Resources." The brain demands priority energy via a "brain-pull" mechanism, competing with peripheral organs.

### 14.2 Myelination Under Metabolic Stress

**Tepavčević (2021)** — Under nutrient deprivation, oligodendrocytes retract processes, reduce metabolic rate, and compromise myelination while preserving cell survival. Myelination is the first function sacrificed.

**Rone et al. (2016)** — Oligodendrocytes reduce glycolytic rate under stress, directly impairing myelination capacity.

**Harris & Attwell (2012)** — White matter uses less energy than grey matter per unit volume (no synaptic signalling), but the **process** of myelination is acutely metabolically expensive.

### 14.3 The Frontal-Cerebellar Inverse Correlation

**Carper & Courchesne (2000)** — In children with autism, when frontal lobe volume was enlarged, cerebellar volume was reduced, and vice versa. Direct quote: "decreased size of another, the correlation is clearly not an artefact of variance in overall brain size." The most direct evidence of a possible zero-sum trade-off between brain regions in ASD. Cited 539 times.

### 14.4 Early Overgrowth → Arrested Growth

**Courchesne et al. (2001, 2003, 2004, 2011)** — well-documented trajectory in ASD: unusual brain growth in infancy (white matter overgrowth), followed by premature arrest. "Early overgrowth followed by premature arrest of growth — increased frontal volumes and decreased cerebellar vermal volumes were linked."

---

## Chapter 15: Competitive Plasticity and Parallel Conditions

### 15.1 The Crowding Hypothesis

**Teuber (1974); Stiles (2000); Bates & Roe (2001)** — When early brain injury forces a function to relocate to the contralateral hemisphere, the displaced function competes for neural territory with functions already resident there, resulting in reduced performance of both. Compensation is not cost-free.

**Anderson et al. (2011)** — Challenges the simplistic view that younger brains always recover better. Early injury can produce worse long-term cognitive outcomes than later injury in some domains. Cited 764 times.

### 15.2 ADHD as the Strongest Parallel

**Shaw et al. (2007)** — Cortical grey matter maturation in ADHD is delayed by approximately **3 years** (peak cortical thickness at age 10.5 vs 7.5 in controls), with the delay most prominent in prefrontal regions. Crucially, **the trajectories converge** — ADHD brains eventually reach the same cortical thickness, just later. Cited >2,000 times.

**Damatac et al. (2022)** — Aberrant white matter microstructure was associated with ADHD symptom changes over time, consistent with a delayed-maturation model. **Hyde et al. (2023)** — ADHD + motor difficulties show a distinct maturation pattern in the corticospinal tract.

ADHD provides the **strongest parallel**: grey matter delay (~3 years), corresponding white matter lag, and clinical improvement with age — consistent with eventual myelination catch-up.

### 15.3 Cerebral Palsy (PVL Subtype) as Direct Precedent

PVL directly results from destruction of preOLs (Back et al., 1998, 2001) during the gestational vulnerability window. Children with PVL-type CP often show improvement in motor function during childhood as delayed myelination partially catches up. The CAP trial (Schmidt et al., 2007) demonstrated that reducing perinatal apnoea/hypoxia via caffeine significantly reduced CP rates, confirming the causal link between perinatal physiological compromise and white matter outcomes.

# PART VI — EPIDEMIOLOGICAL AND IMAGING CONVERGENCE

---

## Chapter 16: Preeclampsia → ASD Risk — The Population Evidence

### 16.1 Meta-Analyses

**Dachew et al. (2018)** — Meta-analysis of 10 studies: ASD risk **32% higher** with preeclampsia (RR 1.32, 95% CI 1.20–1.45). (*Br J Psychiatry*)

**Maher et al. (2018)** — 61 studies: HDP → ASD (OR 1.35, 95% CI 1.11–1.64); HDP → ADHD (OR 1.29). (*JAMA Psychiatry*)

### 16.2 Population Cohorts — Key Features

**Maher et al. (2020)** — Swedish cohort, 2,842,230 children. PE → ASD: HR 1.25. **Sibling-matched**: HR 1.17 (effect persists within families). PE + SGA combined: **HR 1.95** (sibling-matched). Effect **stronger with placental dysfunction markers**.

**Sun et al. (2020)** — Norwegian cohort, 980,560 **term births**. PE → ASD: OR 1.29. Association **persists in term births**, eliminating prematurity as a confounder.

**Wang et al. (2021)** — 4,489,044 singletons (Denmark + Sweden). **Dose-response confirmed**: severe PE → ASD HR 1.86 (vs 1.29 for all HDP); severe PE → ID HR 3.99. **Similar in sibling analyses**.

### 16.3 What These Studies Establish

1. Effect persists in sibling analyses — not explained by shared genetics/environment
2. Effect persists in term births — not simply a prematurity effect
3. Dose-response confirmed — severe PE produces stronger effects
4. Effect amplified by placental dysfunction (SGA) — consistent with hypoxia mechanism
5. Broader HDP category shows similar effects — placental insufficiency as common pathway

## Chapter 17: DTI Evidence in ASD — The White Matter Signature

### 17.1 Meta-Analytic Scale

**Yoon et al. (2025)** — 59 studies, 1,750 ASD participants: FA reduced in **8 tracts** (CC, CST, thalamic radiation, AF, IFOF, ILF, SLF, UF); MD increased in **6 tracts**.

**Li et al. (2022)** — 33 studies, 831 ASD participants: All language-associated tracts showed abnormalities. Abnormalities **more prominent in left hemisphere**. **Children with ASD showed more pronounced disturbances than adults** — consistent with a developmental myelination deficit.

**Aoki et al. (2013)** — 14 studies: Significant FA reductions in CC, left UF, left SLF.

### 17.2 The Myelination-Failure Signature

**Fletcher et al. (2010)** — Increased radial diffusivity in AF without proportional axial diffusivity increase. This is the specific DTI marker of **reduced myelination with intact axons** — precisely the signature of OPC maturation arrest.

**Zhang F et al. (2018)** — AF abnormalities present in ASD toddlers at the earliest stage when language regression is observable. Reduced FA with increased tract volume = poorly myelinated tracts, not tract loss.

**Nagae et al. (2012)** — Significant negative correlation between left SLF white matter integrity and clinical language ability (higher MD = worse language).

### 17.3 Tract-Function Mapping

| Tract | DTI Finding in ASD | Functional Domain | Evidence |
|-------|-------------------|-------------------|----------|
| Arcuate fasciculus | ↓FA, ↑MD, ↑RD | Complex syntax, comprehension | Yoon 2025, Fletcher 2010, Zhang 2018, Li 2022 |
| SLF | ↓FA, ↑MD (left > right) | Articulation, phonological processing | Aoki 2013, Nagae 2012, Yoon 2025 |
| Corpus callosum | ↓FA, ↑MD | Interhemispheric integration | Aoki 2013, Yoon 2025; most replicated finding |
| Uncinate fasciculus | ↓FA (left) | Word retrieval, social cognition | Aoki 2013, Ameis & Catani 2015 |
| ILF | ↓FA, ↑MD | Face recognition, visual-emotional | Yoon 2025, Li 2022 |
| IFOF | ↓FA, ↑MD | Semantic processing, reading | Yoon 2025, Li 2022 |
| Thalamic radiation | ↓FA | Sensory relay, attention | Yoon 2025 |
| Corticospinal tract | ↓FA | Motor coordination | Yoon 2025 |

**Ameis & Catani (2015)** — Review of 72 DTI studies: white matter alterations are "indicative of developmental changes in WM organization and potentially myelination."

White matter disruption in ASD is **widespread, not tract-specific** — consistent with a generalised OPC-level disturbance. But severity is **left-lateralised and concentrated in dorsal language stream tracts**, with additional involvement of ventral, interhemispheric, and sensorimotor pathways.

## Chapter 18: White Matter in Preeclampsia-Born Children

### 18.1 The Only School-Age DTI Study

**Figueiró-Filho et al. (2017)** — **The only published DTI study** of school-age children born from preeclamptic pregnancies (10 matched pairs, ages 7–10). Found increased SLF tract volume in the PE group — mirroring the pattern in Zhang et al. (2018) in ASD toddlers. **No direct AF-specific measures** — a critical gap.

### 18.2 Neonatal Trajectory Disruption

**Xing et al. (2021)** — DTI in preterm infants born from PE mothers: the normal positive FA-age correlation was **ABSENT** in the PE group. FA did not increase with age as expected. Consistent with OPC maturation arrest — the developmental programme calls for myelination, but it does not proceed.

### 18.3 The Convergence Test

The key prediction: if DTI patterns of PE-born children and ASD children overlap (particularly ↑RD in AF suggesting myelination failure), it provides structural evidence connecting PE to the ASD-adjacent white matter phenotype. **No study has performed this head-to-head comparison.**

# PART VII — EVIDENCE GAPS AND RESEARCH AGENDA

---

## Chapter 19: Explicit Evidence Gaps

The following specific questions have **no direct evidence** in the current literature. These are stated plainly because the theory's integrity depends on honest acknowledgement of what is demonstrated and what is not.

### Mechanistic Gaps

1. **No study directly measures myelination (vs FA proxy) during autistic regression.** Wolff et al. (2012) used DTI/FA, which is an indirect measure.

2. **No study directly demonstrates activity-dependent myelination of language tracts in response to speech therapy in autistic children.** The mechanism is established in animal models; the behavioural effects are documented; the specific myelination bridge has not been measured.

3. **No study confirms OPC maturation arrest as a causal mechanism in autism.** The vulnerability of preOLs to hypoxic-ischaemic injury is established in PVL (Back et al., 2001); it has not been directly studied in autism.

4. **No study demonstrates a "repair mode"** in which the brain reallocates metabolic resources toward white matter repair during autistic regression.

5. **No study directly compares the effectiveness of therapy during active myelination vs after tract maturation** in autism.

6. **No direct measurement of OPC density in autistic brains** has been published.

### Threshold Gaps

7. **No specific SpO₂ threshold at which OPC maturation arrest begins** has been established in living human neonates. Wellmann et al. (2015) showed OPCs sense O₂ via HIF but the activation threshold is unquantified.

8. **No direct measurement of tissue PO₂ at periventricular white matter** during transient SpO₂ desaturations in human neonates.

9. **No direct evidence** for whether oscillation pattern (desat→resat cycling) or absolute nadir drives more OPC damage. Animal models suggest oscillation; no human confirmation.

### Bridging Gaps

10. **No single longitudinal study** tracking the complete chain: preeclampsia → IH events → OPC arrest → tract-specific myelination failure → language/social deficit in one cohort.

11. **No head-to-head DTI comparison** between PE-born children and ASD children in the same study protocol.

12. **No syntax-specific language assessment** (as opposed to global language scores) in PE-born cohorts. The theory predicts syntax-specific deficits (dorsal stream) with preserved vocabulary (ventral stream).

13. **No study measuring white matter tract FA correlated with meltdown frequency/intensity** or with the "words are there but won't come out" experience in ASD.

## Chapter 20: Testable Predictions

### 20.1 Core Predictions

1. **Temporal prediction**: Language regression/deficit should correlate with reduced AF myelination at ages 2–4; social cognition deficits should correlate with reduced UF myelination at ages 4–8. Testable with longitudinal DTI + behavioural assessment.

2. **Tract-specificity prediction**: White matter deficits in ASD should be tract-specific (corresponding to staggered deficit domains), not globally uniform. If deficits are evenly distributed rather than tract-specific, the staggered-demand model is weakened.

3. **DTI signature prediction**: Children with perinatal risk who develop autism should show ↑RD (myelination failure signature), not ↑AD (axonal loss). The RD/AD ratio differentiates OPC arrest from axonal degeneration.

4. **Intervention prediction**: Activity-dependent therapy should specifically increase FA in targeted tracts — measurable with pre/post DTI. Speech therapy → AF FA increase; social skills training → UF/cingulum FA increase.

5. **Dose-response prediction**: Severe PE should produce worse AF metrics than mild PE, which should produce worse metrics than controls — matching the epidemiological dose-response (Wang et al., 2021).

6. **IH burden prediction**: Cumulative IH events (SpO₂ ≤80%) in NICU records should predict AF integrity at school age, mediating PE → language outcome.

7. **Oscillation Index prediction**: A composite metric of IH frequency × clustering × depth should predict white matter outcomes better than simple average SpO₂ or nadir SpO₂.

### 20.2 What Would Weaken the Model

- If DTI deficits in ASD are uniform across all tracts (no staggered pattern)
- If PE-born children show normal AF development despite documented IH burden
- If the RD/AD ratio in ASD indicates axonal degeneration rather than myelination failure
- If comprehensive post-mortem OPC quantification shows normal OPC maturation in ASD brains
- If therapy-driven FA changes occur equally in formed and forming tracts

## Chapter 21: Study Designs and Dataset Feasibility

### 21.1 Study 1: Retrospective IH Burden → DTI → Language

**Design**: Identify a cohort of children (aged 3–5) monitored with high-resolution SpO₂ as neonates. Perform DTI neuroimaging (AF fractional anisotropy). Administer standardised language assessments (CELF-5, PLS-5).

**Hypothesis**: IH burden predicts AF integrity, and AF integrity predicts language outcome.

**Most viable cohort**: Di Fiore/Hibbs at Case Western (n=175, continuous SpO₂ + ASQ-3 already collected). Requires DTI scanning of surviving participants.

### 21.2 Study 2: Preeclampsia Cohort Comparison

**Design**: Compare IH burden and white matter outcomes between PE-born infants and gestational-age-matched controls.

**Hypothesis**: PE infants show higher IH burden AND worse AF development, supporting in-utero priming.

### 21.3 Study 3: Oscillation Index vs Simple SpO₂

**Design**: Define a composite oscillation metric. Apply retrospectively to existing high-resolution SpO₂ datasets. Compare predictive power for white matter outcomes against average SpO₂, nadir SpO₂, and time below threshold.

**Hypothesis**: The oscillation metric predicts white matter outcomes better than any single SpO₂ metric.

**Advantage**: Purely computational — requires no new data collection, only re-analysis.

### 21.4 The Minimum Viable Study

Contact the Di Fiore team. Access existing SpO₂ data. DTI scan surviving participants at current age. Administer CELF-5. Test: does IH burden predict AF FA? Does AF FA predict language score? Single-centre, ~100 participants, publishable as proof-of-concept.

### 21.5 Dataset Landscape

**No existing dataset contains all three legs** (continuous SpO₂ + DTI + language outcomes). See companion document `RESEARCH__neonatal-dataset-audit-IH-DTI-language.md` for full per-dataset audit (10 datasets evaluated). The most pragmatic path is adding DTI/language follow-up to existing NICU cohorts with SpO₂ data (Pre-Vent, COT, Di Fiore/Rainbow).
